He went to Lindøy in late August, two months after Landa, on a small boat his father lent him for a week. The wind was light from the southwest. The crossing took three hours. Olava had written him a letter at the end of July — short, four lines, in a hand that was firmer than he had expected from a girl her size — saying that her family would be at home for the last week of August and that her father had said it was time he met them. Olav had answered the letter the same evening. He had told her the day he would come.

The letter had been the seventh letter between them since midsummer. They had written, the two of them, every Wednesday and every Sunday — he on Wednesday, she on Sunday — and their parents had read each letter before posting. The letters were in the formal Norwegian that two young people from different islands with marriageable intentions would have written in 1865, in which there was nothing to object to.

Olava's third letter, from August, had ended:

> *The northwest has held nine days. Father is at the boats. Mother says to your mother that her potatoes have done what mine have done, which is nothing.*
>
> *I read your fourth letter twice. The second time was for me, the first time was for the form.*
>
> *The dog at the door is well.*
>
> *Yours, Olava.*

Olav had read the line about the second time twice himself, and had understood, on the second reading, that the rhythm was the thing the rhythm meant.

---

Olav landed at the Lindøy jetty at noon. There was a man at the jetty waiting for him — a man of perhaps sixty-five with a wooden hand. The hand was carved oak. It was lashed to the stump at the wrist with leather and a small iron pin, and the man wore it the way men wear a thing they have gotten used to over forty years of getting used to it. The man stood at the jetty in a clean wool jacket and a clean shirt and a clean cap. He had clearly come down to the jetty an hour before Olav was due.

"Hestby?"

"Yes."

"Lindøy. Come up to the house. I will help you with the chest."

He helped Olav with the chest with the wooden hand. The wooden hand was steadier than Olav had expected, and Olav understood, walking up from the jetty beside the man with the chest between them, that the wood was not a substitute for the missing hand but a tool the man had developed in compensation for it — the way a sailor whose first language is Norwegian becomes, by long use, more precise in his second language than a native is in his first. The wooden hand held the chest's handle in a grip that did not slip.

The Lindøy house stood a quarter mile up from the jetty, on a slope above a small pasture, white-painted with a roof of black slate that the captain had brought back from Wales sometime in the previous decade. There were two horses in the pasture. There was a dog at the door of the house, an old grey-muzzled thing of the kind Norwegian farmers keep for the company more than for the work. The dog watched Olav approach without standing up.

"Inside," said the captain. "My wife has the kettle on. My daughter is at the back garden. Marit is at the schoolmaster's; she will be back at supper. Come."

---

Olava's mother was a small woman of fifty-five with the same grey-channel eyes as her daughter and a mouth that fell easily into a smile that did not, like Captain Sars's, need to be polite to be warm. She put coffee in front of Olav at the kitchen table and bread and butter on a small plate and a piece of brown cheese cut very thin, and she did not, in any of the placing, give the impression that she had been waiting to evaluate the man who would, perhaps, take her daughter from her. She gave the impression that she was a mother feeding a young man who had been on a boat for three hours.

"Your mother is well."

"Yes, ma'am."

"Tell her, when you go home, that I have been thinking of the strawberries we picked at Skjelvik in '49."

"I will."

"I will write to her this autumn. You may tell her also that I will write."

"Yes, ma'am."

She moved on. She did not, having delivered her two pieces of intelligence — the strawberries and the autumn letter — extend the catechism. She let Olav eat his bread and his cheese. She went to the door to speak to the dog, briefly, in a low voice, the way a woman speaks to a dog she has known for fifteen years and considers, in some respects, the most reliable male in the household.

The captain sat across from Olav at the kitchen table and watched him eat. He did not speak. His wooden hand rested on the table beside his living one. The two hands at rest were of a piece — both knuckled, both browned, both veined — except that the wooden one was wood. Olav, as he ate, was conscious of the captain looking at him without being scrutinized by it. It was the look of a man who had decided to do the looking now so that he would not have to do it later.

When Olav had finished the bread and cheese the captain said, *Come out to the boat-shed,* and they went out to the boat-shed.

---

The boat-shed at Lindøy was much like the boat-shed at Finnøy except that everything in it was carved or planed or jointed with the precision of a man who had had thirty years to develop habits about how a boat-shed should be organized when one of his hands was wood. The tools were on pegs in size order. The rope was on cleats by lay. The smell of the shed was the same smell as Olav's father's shed — pine tar, linseed, old salt, mouse — but the air in it was somehow more available, as if the shed had been built for the breath of one man rather than the four men who had used Olav's father's shed in three generations.

The captain set himself on a stool. He pointed to another stool. Olav sat.

"You will write at school, at Stavanger?"

"Yes, sir."

"To my daughter."

"Yes, sir."

"Olava is eighteen."

"I am twenty."

"I know what you are. I have been told what you are. I have been told it twice, by my daughter, who tells me a thing once and then tells me it once more to be sure I understood the first time. She has told me what you are, and I have decided what you are, and that is what we are talking about now."

He held up his wooden hand. He set it down again.

"I lost this at Brest in fifty-seven. I came home, and I did not, after I came home, go back to sea. It was not the hand. It was the year I had at home with the hand. The year was the thing I had not had in twenty years, and I found, when I had it, that I did not want —"

He stopped. He looked at the wooden hand on the bench beside him for a long moment. When he resumed it was not, Olav understood, the sentence he had been about to finish.

"My daughter is going to be your wife, if she chooses to be, and you are going to be a sailor, and the years she has with you are going to be the years she has with you, and the years she has without you are going to be the years she has without you. I do not give advice to young men. I am telling you what the arithmetic looks like from where I sit. Marit will marry the schoolmaster's son next year and she will not, after that, leave Lindøy. Olava will marry you, if she does, and she will leave Lindøy with you, and the Lindøy she comes home to when you are at sea will be the Lindøy of her father with one hand and her mother going through the strawberry letter for the third time. I am not telling you this against you. I am telling you what the daughter you may marry will live."

Olav did not, for some time, answer. The captain did not seem to expect an answer immediately.

"I know about the year I will be at school," Olav said. "I know about the years after."

"You know about them in the way of a man of twenty who has been told. You will know about them in the other way later. That is normal. I am only saying it now so that the saying is on the record. When we sit at the table tonight I will not say it again. We will speak of the weather and the herring and my brother in Drammen who has bought a railway share. You will sleep in the back room over the kitchen. You will not, in the back room over the kitchen, see my daughter at any hour at which my daughter ought not to be seen. I do not say this against you either. I am saying it because it has to be said by someone, and I am the one in this house who says these things."

He looked at his wooden hand. He looked at his living one. He nodded once, the way a man does at the end of a thing he has been preparing to say for some weeks and has now, evidently, said correctly.

"That is what we needed to say," he said. "Now we will go look at the boats."

They went to look at the boats.

---

Olava worked in the back garden until the sun came around to the back of the house and then she came in and made supper. The supper was salt cod with potatoes, and a pickled herring her mother had jarred the previous spring, and a sweet brown bread she had baked that morning. Marit, at the schoolmaster's, came home in time to set the table. The captain said grace. They ate. The captain spoke of his brother in Drammen and of the railway share and of the weather and of a piece of timber that had been lying at the jetty for three weeks waiting for a Stavanger man to come and fetch it. He did not, at supper, look at Olav more than was natural. Olava, across the table from Olav, did not, at supper, look at him more than was natural. They had, by some agreement they had not negotiated, decided to behave at supper the way two young people who corresponded by letter on Wednesdays and Sundays would behave when they were at last in the same room — which is to say, slightly more formally than two young people who had not corresponded at all. The looking, they were saving for after.

After supper Marit washed the dishes and her mother dried them and her father went to the parlor with a piece of carving he had been working on for three weeks and Olava and Olav were left, by the kind of small unspoken stage management that Norwegian households have practiced for a thousand years, in the kitchen with the kettle.

They did not, in the kitchen with the kettle, say very much.

"You came on a fair wind," she said.

"Yes."

"Tomorrow I want to show you the path up to the high pasture. There is a view from the top that I have been wanting you to see since June."

"Yes."

"And the next day, if you are willing, I have a thing I would like to do alone, and I would like you to be at the boat-shed with my father while I do it. I am only saying this so that you do not, when I disappear after breakfast, feel that I have disappeared from you."

"Yes."

"Good."

"Olava."

"Yes."

"What is the thing."

She looked at the kettle. She did not look at him.

"It is a thing about my brother," she said. "I do it once a month. I do it on the day of the month he died. The day of the month is the day after tomorrow. I do it alone. I have done it alone since he died and I do not, even for you, mean to change it. I am telling you only because I do not want you to misunderstand the disappearing."

"I will not misunderstand."

"Good."

She poured the kettle into the cups. She handed him his cup.

They sat at the table for the next hour and they spoke of small things. The kettle cooled. The lamp burned. Outside, the dog at the door barked once at a gull and stopped. Somewhere in the parlor the captain's carving knife made its small steady sound against the wood. The mother had gone to bed. Marit was reading in the room she shared, when Olava was at home, with Olava.

When the kettle had gone cold and the lamp had begun to gutter Olava stood up and took both cups to the basin and washed them. She came back to the table with her hands wet and she did not, quite, wipe them on her apron before she put them, briefly, on Olav's at the table. Her hands were small and the water was warm. She left them there for the count of one breath. Then she withdrew them, wiped them on her apron after all, and said *good night.*

"Good night."

She went up the stair to the room she shared with Marit.

Olav sat at the kitchen table for some time after she had gone. He listened to her footsteps overhead. From the parlor came the small steady sound of the captain's carving knife. The dog at the door sighed once and was silent.

He did not, sitting at the table, allow himself to think about what he had not told Olava. He did not think about the not-telling either. The not-telling, he understood, was a country he was going to have to live in for the rest of his life with this woman if the rest of his life with this woman was the thing they were arranging by letter on Wednesdays and Sundays. The not-telling had a shape. He was not ready to learn the shape tonight.

He went up to the back room over the kitchen. The bed was a narrow one with a quilted coverlet. The window faced the water. He undressed and lay down and watched the channel for some time. The Finnøy hills were across it; he could see, in the dusk, the small dark mass of his own island. He thought of his father standing at the gate, then of his mother in the kitchen.

He went to sleep.

---

On the morning Olava had named, after breakfast, she went out from the house with a small basket and a shawl and walked up the road that went past the Lindøy church. Olav, at the captain's instruction, went with the captain to the boat-shed to help him fit a new garboard plank to a small skiff that had taken a knock the previous winter. They worked at the plank for an hour and a half. The work was steady; the captain talked, more than he had at supper, about Brest in fifty-seven, about the rope that had taken his hand, about the surgeon at the Brest naval hospital who had set the stump (a Frenchman, who had spoken no Norwegian and very little English, and who had been, the captain said, the best surgeon a man had ever had the misfortune to need). They drove the plank. They caulked it. By the time the caulking was done the morning was half gone.

The captain set down his caulking iron.

"Go up the road," he said. "She will be at the churchyard. Do not speak to her. Do not let her see you. Come back."

Olav looked at him.

"I am telling you," the captain said, "what to do, because she will not tell you. She does not know I am telling you. Do not tell her. Go."

Olav went.

---

The Lindøy church was a small white building like the church at Hestby, with the same low stone wall and the same kind of small graveyard behind it. The wall ran along the road. Olav, as instructed, walked up the road on the side of the wall opposite the graveyard, until he could see the headstones over the wall. He stopped behind a hedge.

Olava was at the headstone in the second row.

She was sitting on the grass beside it, with her shawl drawn around her shoulders, with the basket beside her on the grass, with her hands folded in her lap. She was not weeping. She was not praying, either, in any visible sense. She was sitting at the headstone the way a person sits at the bedside of a friend who has been sleeping for a long time and may, perhaps, wake. She was not, Olav understood after the first minute of watching, doing anything that could be done in less than the time she had set aside for the doing of it. She had come to sit. She would sit until she was finished. She would then go.

He watched her for the count of perhaps twenty minutes.

For most of those twenty minutes she did not move. Once, she leaned forward and brushed a leaf off the foot of the stone. Once, she said something — Olav could not hear what — to the stone, and then closed her mouth and was again silent. She did not, in any of the twenty minutes, look up from the stone. She did not know he was there.

He left before she did. He walked back down the road on the far side of the wall and was at the boat-shed when the captain looked up from the second skiff he had begun.

"You went."

"I went."

"You did not let her see you."

"No."

"Good."

The captain handed him a chisel. They went on with the second skiff. The captain did not speak for some time. After perhaps a quarter hour he said, without looking up:

"She does the same thing every month. She does it for an hour. She has done it for four years. She will do it for the rest of her life, or until she does not need to do it any more, and I do not know which it will be. I told you to go because you are going to be her husband and you are going to need to know that she does this. You are going to need to know it without her having told you. There are things wives do not tell husbands. There are things husbands have to know anyway. This is one of those. You will not, in your life with her, ask her about the brother. She will tell you about the brother in the time she chooses, in the words she chooses, and you will not anticipate her. You will only know — because I have told you, today, against her not having told you — that she goes to him once a month for an hour, and that she does not weep there, and that the thing she carries about him is hers. Do you understand."

"Yes."

"Good. Pass me the gauge."

Olav passed him the gauge.

They worked on the second skiff until noon. They did not speak of Olava again that day. At noon Olava came back from the churchyard with the empty basket, and her mother had a meal ready, and they ate, and Olava did not, all afternoon, give the slightest indication that she had been at her brother's grave that morning. She asked Olav about the high pasture. They went up the path that afternoon. The view from the top was as she had said it would be.

---

That night, in the back room of the Lindøy house, Olav lay in the narrow bed and watched the channel. He thought about Olava sitting at the stone. He thought about his own brother's stone behind the Hestby church, which he had not, in twenty-one years, sat at. He understood, in a small confused way, that he had married — was about to marry, would marry, would have married — a woman who had a year more practice than he did at being the surviving sibling of a brother taken by the same illness, and that the year of practice was a thing she would have over him for the rest of their lives, and that the having would be a piece of what kept them married.

He understood, also, that Olava had a part of her life that was not, ever, going to be his to enter, and that the part was small and was hers and was the right size for what it carried.

He did not, that night, think of Haakon. He thought instead about the brig-coil on the peg above his father's workbench at home, and the way it stayed where he had put it.

He went to sleep.
