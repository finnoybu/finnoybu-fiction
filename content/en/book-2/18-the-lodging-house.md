They made Calcutta again at the end of the first week of November.

The bark took her same mooring at the Strand. The rice and indigo that were to be the homeward cargo were already waiting at the agent's warehouse on Clive Street, bought by Brun at the Exchange in October and held under the company's bond through the month while the bark had gone to Rangoon and back. The load was ready. The bark, however, was not: the Bassein approaches had put a small hull problem to the carpenter on the return, a place at the garboard where a plank had worked against its fastenings, and Brun had decided — on the basis of eight hundred miles of the Indian Ocean ahead — to put the *Semiramis* into a small dry-dock on the Hooghly for three days while the plank was refastened and a coat of pitch was laid on the seams either side of it.

The dry-docking began on the Tuesday. The crew, during it, was paid ashore-rations for two nights and given leave to take lodgings in the city at their own expense and on their own time. The bark would not accept them back until Thursday noon.

Olav understood, when Sundberg read the arrangement at the forenoon muster on the Monday, that the two nights ashore were the first real shore-leave of the voyage. Calcutta on the outbound had been three weeks of port work. Rangoon had been four days of port work and one morning at the Shwedagon. The Tuesday and Wednesday of the dry-dock were the first two nights in six months on which the crew would sleep off the ship.

He also understood, without needing to think about it, where he would not stay.

He would not stay at the sailors' lodgings on Bowbazar Road that the forecastle was already arranging. He had kept his distance from the three risks at the first Calcutta stop, and he was not going to abandon the discipline at the second. He had, over the Monday afternoon, enquired at the Norwegian consul's office about the small Christian lodging-house the consul had recommended at his first visit — a house on Russell Street, run by a Danish woman named Mrs. Lauritsen who took Scandinavian sailors and clerks at eight annas a night and served a quiet supper — and had arranged a room for the two nights.

He had not told Sven.

He had not, on three weeks since the fight, told Sven anything. The six feet of deck between them had held. The ordinary business of the watches had gone on. The wordless sharing of the main topsail yard on the morning they had made the Andaman banks had been the extent of their contact. Sven, Olav understood, would be at Bowbazar Road with the forecastle. The forecastle was where a married Bergen man with a wife in Sandviken went ashore on his two nights, because Bowbazar Road was the kind of ashore that a married Bergen man could tell his wife, if the wife asked, that he had been to — it was the ordinary ashore, the cheap ashore, the ashore that had no story in it.

Olav went to Mrs. Lauritsen's on the Tuesday evening.

---

The lodging-house was a two-storey wooden building set back from Russell Street behind a small garden of banana and one old tamarind tree. The ground floor had a parlour and a dining room and Mrs. Lauritsen's own small rooms; the upper floor had four lodger's rooms off a long corridor that had a single oil-lamp at each end. Olav's room was the third along the corridor. It had a bed with a mosquito-net frame, a washstand with a basin and a jug, a small desk under the shuttered window, a chair. The room was clean. The sheet on the bed had the smell of clean sheets in a hot country — a faintness of soap, a faintness of sun-drying.

Olav washed his hands and his face at the basin. He ate the supper Mrs. Lauritsen had laid for him and the other lodger — a Danish clerk of about forty — at the dining room: cold chicken, rice, a pickle, tea. He exchanged three polite sentences with the Danish clerk about the weather. He went up to his room at eight o'clock. He sat at the desk and wrote a page in his small bound book: *Second Calcutta. Dry-dock. Lodgings at Mrs. Lauritsen's, Russell Street. Good room. Good supper. The city at night is not the city at day; I can hear the dogs and the cries of a hawker from the lane behind the house and nothing from the Strand. Nothing to note from the crew. Bassein is in his crate on the bark.*

He closed the book. He put it under the pillow.

He sat for a moment at the desk looking at the shuttered window. Then he stood up to undress for bed.

He had his coat off and his shirt half unbuttoned when the knock came at the door.

---

The knock was a sailor's knock — three at a measured interval, not loud, not apologetic, the knock of a man who was used to being let in on his first knock and did not need to repeat it.

Olav stopped with his hand at the top button.

He went to the door.

He opened it.

Sven was in the corridor.

He was still in his shore coat, the canvas bag over his shoulder that held his ashore kit, his hair flat with the damp of the November evening. The single oil-lamp at the end of the corridor was behind him; his face was half-lit.

"Hestby."

"Sven."

"Mrs. Lauritsen is full downstairs. I asked at the door. She said there is a fourth room. She said she would let me have it at eight annas."

"She said that."

"She said the fourth room's door is the one on the end. She said the key is hers. She said she would bring it up."

"Yes."

"I am taking the room."

"Yes."

Sven did not move.

The corridor was, in the second Olav stood at his own doorway looking at him, full of the small particular pressure that had been absent from the deck for three weeks. The six feet of deck had been a distance arranged in the open air among eighteen men. It had been public. It had been maintained by the public nature of the deck. The corridor was private. There were three other lodgers in the house; none of them was on this floor except the Danish clerk, whose room was at the far end past the fourth. The corridor, at eight in the evening of a Tuesday in November, was two men and an oil-lamp and a closed shutter at each end of it.

"Sven."

"Yes."

"Why are you at Mrs. Lauritsen's."

Sven did not answer at once.

He looked past Olav into the room. He looked at the bed and at the desk and at the chair. He looked back at Olav.

"I was at Bowbazar," he said. "I went with Reuss and Lie to the lodging the forecastle arranged. I was at the supper there. I left after the supper. I could not, as it happened, stay in the building at Bowbazar tonight."

"Why not."

"Because I would have got drunk. I would have got drunk the way the men at Bowbazar were going to get drunk, and I would have done what a man does at Bowbazar when he is drunk, and I would have done it not because I wanted to do it but because I was a man in Calcutta on shore-leave sleeping four feet from a Reuss who was going to do it and a Lie who was going to do it. I did not want to do it. I did not want to be in the building in which it was going to be done. I walked out after supper. I walked down Chowringhee. I saw Mrs. Lauritsen's shingle. I asked about a room."

"You asked without knowing I was here."

"I asked without knowing. I learned it from the landlady when she told me who was in Room Three. I did not know until she told me. I considered leaving then. I did not leave."

"Why not."

Sven looked at him for a beat that was not a beat.

"Because I am tired, Hestby. I am tired of the six feet. The six feet was my piece. I paid for it at the fight. I have kept it. It has been held. But at Mrs. Lauritsen's at eight in the evening on the second week of November after three weeks of it I am tired of it, and I have come up the stairs, and I am standing at your door, and if you close the door I will go to the fourth room and sleep and go back to the bark on Thursday and the six feet will stand for six more months. I will not mention that I was here. I will not, tomorrow on the bark, have come up the stairs. That is what will happen if you close the door."

"And if I do not close the door."

Sven did not, for a second, speak.

Then he said, in the small quiet voice Olav had not heard from him before:

"Then I do not know. Then the next thing is not mine to decide. It is yours."

Olav looked at him.

He looked at the face in the corridor's half-light. He looked at the shoulder of the canvas bag. He looked at the lip where the fight had left a mark in November that had, by now, healed into a thin pale line. He looked at his own hand on the door-frame.

He did not, for a count of breath, move.

He did not, in the count, weigh the thing in sentences. He did not work out what he was going to tell Olava in a letter or what he was going to tell Brun at the quarterdeck or what he was going to be telling himself in the forecastle on the twenty-fifth night of the Indian Ocean passage home. He had done all of those working-outs already, at other moments, in other chapters of this voyage. None of them was the working-out that applied at the corridor at Mrs. Lauritsen's at eight in the evening.

What applied was the ape with his fist around the rice.

He had understood, three weeks ago at the crate on the bark, that the ape did not let go. He had understood, also, that the ape's not letting go was not a moral failing; it was the ape's construction. He had written it in his own hand in his small bound book. He had re-read the paragraph three times since.

He had not understood, until Sven's voice in the corridor, that the ape could close on more than one handful.

He had understood, at Sven's voice, that he had closed on Olava. That was one fist. That was real. That was not in doubt.

He had understood also, at Sven's voice in the corridor, that he had closed, in a different hand, on the man standing at his door.

He had been telling himself, for three weeks, that he had kept the hand open.

He had not kept it open.

The hand had been closed since Biscay. The hand had been closed since the brace-fall. The hand had been closed at the earing and at the banyan and in the fight and in the six feet of deck. The fist had been around something. The something was not Olava. The something was not Haakon. The something was this specific Bergen man standing in the oil-lamp light in the corridor of Mrs. Lauritsen's, in his shore coat, with the canvas bag over his shoulder and the lip that had healed into the pale line.

He stepped back from the door.

He said, in the register he had not used on the bark in three weeks:

"Come in."

Sven came in.

---

Olav closed the door.

He turned the key in the lock because the Danish clerk was in the room at the end and because Mrs. Lauritsen was going to come up the stairs with a fourth-room key in ten minutes. He did not turn the key because he thought anyone in the house would come in; he turned it because locking a door was the small necessary act a man performed when he had decided, without being able to speak the decision aloud, that the room behind the door was going to be a room in which a thing was going to happen that was not going to be interrupted.

Sven set the canvas bag on the floor at the foot of the chair.

He did not take off his coat.

He stood at the foot of the bed.

Olav stood at the door.

The six feet of deck, which had held on the bark since the fight, was the six feet of the room's length between them now, and for the count of a breath neither of them crossed it.

Then Olav crossed it.

---

He did not, afterward, remember which of the small motions of the crossing had been first. He remembered the particulars in pieces rather than in sequence — the feel of Sven's jaw under his hand, which was rough in a way his own was not; the smell of the lodging-house soap that had come off Sven's collar when he had leaned in; the sound of the canvas bag sliding against the chair-leg as one of them had shifted their weight toward it; the warmth of Sven's breath against the corner of his mouth for the count of a breath before the actual touching; the un-hurry of the un-hurry, the un-hurry being the particular thing about it, because neither of them had rushed, neither of them had behaved like a man in a Bowbazar building with a Bowbazar hour, and the un-hurry had been the sign and the seal of the fact that what was happening was not what would have happened at Bowbazar.

What was happening was not the thing the handbooks warned about.

What was happening was not even — Olav understood, at some point in the quiet early portion of it — the thing Sven had been holding the six feet of deck against, which had been the brute thing.

What was happening was the thing the six feet of deck had not, in fact, been holding against. What was happening was the other thing. It was the thing the lee of the galley on the *Sigrid* had opened an edge of in 1876 and had not completed. It was the thing Olav had not, in five years, understood he had been waiting to complete. It was the thing the earing in the Indian Ocean had promised him without his knowing. It was the thing the banyan root had promised him. It was the thing the six feet of deck had promised him by the very fact of its maintenance.

It was, at Mrs. Lauritsen's on a Tuesday in November in a room lit by a single oil-lamp turned low, the thing he had not had in his life.

He had it.

He had it, he understood at some point in the first hour, because Sven had come up the stairs and had stood at the door and had said *if you close the door I will go to the fourth room.* He had it because he had not closed the door. He had it because he had stepped back and had said *come in.* He had it because both of them, in those five words and the step that had followed them, had agreed to be — for the first night and for the second night and for no further nights after that — the men they had not, on the bark or anywhere else, been permitted to be.

He had it.

---

In the small hours, when they were both awake again but not speaking, Sven put his hand on the side of Olav's face in the dark and held it there for perhaps twenty seconds. Then he took the hand away and he said, in the dry flat Bergen register:

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"I want to say this once, and then we will sleep."

"Say it."

"This is two nights."

"Yes."

"Tomorrow is the second. Thursday we go back on the bark. On the bark is the six feet. The six feet will be there because I will put it there and because you will help me put it there, and because there is no other shape the bark can have for the remainder of the voyage. You understand."

"I understand."

"You do not ask me for more than the two nights. I do not ask you. This is not because I do not want more than two nights. This is because two nights is what I can pay for without putting Hanne and the boy through a thing they would not be able to stand. You are engaged to a woman who will not stand it either."

"No. She will not."

"Then the two nights are what we have."

"The two nights are what we have."

"Good."

He did not, after saying it, take his face away from the pillow beside Olav's. He stayed where he was. After a minute his breath lengthened into sleep.

Olav lay in the dark listening to the breath.

He thought: *I am going to have to live with this.*

He thought: *I am going to have to live with this in the particular way Sven is also going to have to live with it, which is that we are going to carry it home to the women and the boys we are going home to, and we are not going to tell them, and we are not going to tell anyone, and we are not going to tell each other except in the six feet of deck that is itself the way we will acknowledge it between us.*

He thought: *this is what the older men mean when they say that a long voyage changes a man. They do not mean the weather or the sights. They mean the two nights.*

He thought: *I am not, tonight, able to regret it.*

He thought: *I do not, tonight, try to.*

He slept beside Sven until the light came grey at the shutters. Then he slept a second time, with Sven's hand at his shoulder the way a hand lay at the shoulder of a man it had been at the shoulder of in the course of a long quiet night, until the sounds of Mrs. Lauritsen beginning the breakfast downstairs reached them, and they got up and dressed and went down.

---

The second night was the same and was different.

It was the same in that the room was the same room and the bed was the same bed and the two of them, at the end of the supper, went up the stairs together without the pretence of the first evening. It was different in that both of them had understood, since waking in the first light of the Wednesday morning, that the second night was going to be the last night, and the last-ness was in every small action of it — in the way Sven washed at the basin before coming to the bed, in the way Olav turned down the single oil-lamp, in the way they talked in the dark, quietly, for almost an hour before either of them moved.

They talked about nothing in particular.

Sven talked about his boy, whose name Olav now learned was Jonas. Sven talked about Jonas at three, who had learned to count to ten in the spring and who said *bestefar* for grandfather in a way Sven had tried to record with small private accuracy. Sven talked about Hanne's mother, who baked a particular kind of bread on Saturdays that Sven had eaten every Saturday of his life. Sven talked about the cliff above his grandparents' farm outside Bergen where he had, at nine, fallen and broken his wrist. He talked, Olav understood, because he wanted Olav to know these things. He talked because this was the only night of his life on which he would be able to tell another man he had lain with these things, and the telling of them was a piece of what a married Bergen man with a wife and a boy was able to give a man he had lain with one time.

Olav listened.

He talked, in return, about Aagot at the tiller of the small boat home from Landa; about his mother's honey-cake on his twentieth birthday; about the hand on his wrist at the foretop of the *Sigrid* in 1876 and the man who had put it there; about the wool coat on the Stavanger quay in June. Sven listened to the list of his own making and did not interrupt.

They did not, after the hour of talking, talk much more.

When the grey light came at the shutters for the second time Olav woke first. He looked at Sven's face in the early light — the face that was, in sleep, the face of a twenty-four-year-old who had been to sea for ten years and had, in the count of two nights at a lodging-house on Russell Street, allowed himself the one thing his life had not allowed — and he thought: *I am going to remember this face in this light for the rest of my life.*

He thought it without drama.

He thought it as a small verified fact, the way a navigator thought a verified fact on the margin of a chart.

He closed his eyes.

He listened to Sven's breath.

When Sven woke they got up and dressed and went downstairs and ate Mrs. Lauritsen's breakfast and paid for the rooms and walked the half-mile to the Strand with their canvas bags over their shoulders. They did not, on the walk, speak. They did not need to. They had, in the two nights, spoken everything the two of them had the language to speak. The speech at the Strand gate would be the speech of two men going back on the bark.

At the customhouse stairs Olav took the first dinghy. Sven took the next. He did not look back to see Sven come down the stairs. Sven did not look forward to watch Olav cross the water. They arrived at the bark a quarter hour apart, the way two common seamen who had not been at the same shore-lodging would have arrived.

On the deck Sundberg was at the forward rail. He looked at Olav. He looked at Sven, coming up a quarter hour later. He looked at them the way a Karlskrona bosun looked at men whom he had perhaps, at some level of his bosun's intuition, also understood. He did not say anything. He did not need to. He had looked, and the looking had been the looking, and the filing had already been done.

The six feet of deck was back.

It would hold, this time, until Bergen.

Both men were committed, now, to the holding.

The bark lifted Calcutta at the noon tide on the Thursday. Banerjee came aboard at the Strand. He piloted her down the Hooghly on the ebb. By the evening of the second day she was past the Sagar pilot-station and heading for the Bay of Bengal, and the homeward leg of the voyage — four months and a half of open water to Bergen, by Brun's schedule — had begun.
