They raised the Hooghly pilot-brig off Sagar Island on the morning of the eighty-fourth day out of Stavanger.

The pilot came aboard at nine — a small brown man of perhaps fifty with an English coat and the particular accent of a Bengali who had been piloting Europeans up the river since the coat was new — and he took his charts into the captain's cabin with Brun and stayed there through a cup of coffee. He came out on deck again at ten bells of the forenoon and looked at the sky and the tide and the bark and said, in English the whole of the quarterdeck could follow if it leaned in:

"Captain. We have three hours of the flood. We make Saugor Roads by noon. We anchor at Saugor for the night. Tomorrow we go up on the second flood. Three days to Calcutta, barring the bore. Conditions are fair."

"Good," Brun said.

And so they began, under a pilot's hand, the long winding passage up the Hooghly that Olav had read about in the grey-covered handbook at Stavanger eighteen months before.

The handbook had said that the Hooghly was the deadliest river in the world for a sailing ship. It had, when he had read it at Mrs. Kindingstad's kitchen table in February 1880, seemed an exaggeration of the kind handbooks were in the habit of making about foreign rivers; a river was a river, and the Thames — which Olav had not seen but had read about — had been handled by sailing ships for three hundred years without losing most of them. The handbook had given him the figures anyway. The James and Mary shoal, four miles of tidal sand below Calcutta, had on its bottom perhaps two hundred European wrecks. The bore — the tidal wave that came up the river on the rising tide twice a month at the springs — could, on a bad day, drive a fully-rigged ship sideways onto a mud bank and leave her there to break up with the ebb. The current ran at up to six knots. The channel moved between monsoons. A Hooghly pilot was the best-paid pilot in Asia; this was not, the handbook had noted dryly, because the pilots had strong unions.

Olav remembered the handbook as the pilot set his course.

---

They made Saugor Roads by noon, as the pilot had said, and anchored in twelve fathoms on a mud bottom. The roads were a wide shallow bay at the mouth of the river with perhaps twenty ships at anchor — most of them European deep-water ships waiting for their pilot or their tide, two of them P&O steamers loading for Singapore, one of them a country brig from Rangoon with teak on her deck. The land around the bay was low and green in the way Olav had not, until this morning, understood green could be low — a density of green at the water's edge that had neither the openness of Norwegian green nor the height of European green but had instead the thick close quality of jungle seen from a ship.

There was a smell on the wind that was not, Olav thought, any smell he had known before. It was not the smell of any sea he had been on. It was the smell of a land he had not yet set foot on — a mix of wet earth and something burning and something else Olav, at twenty-three, did not have the words for. Sven, at the foredeck beside him, stood with his hands on the cap-rail and sniffed once.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"That is the east."

"Yes."

"A man does not, at home, ever smell air of this kind."

"No."

"I have smelled something like it once. A Dutch brig came into Antwerp the summer I was on the *Aalesund* with Halvorsen, and she had been to Batavia, and the mate gave me a stem of clove he had brought back in his pocket, and the clove smelled like that on my palm for a week. This is that smell, only larger."

"Yes."

"We are here," Sven said, which was the sort of thing Sven said when he was registering, without being particular about it, the small fact that a voyage had reached a place it had been going for eighty-four days.

They went to their work.

---

The bore did not come through that night.

The pilot had been right about the tide and right about the weather. They lay at Saugor through a hot still night — the hottest night Olav had been in at sea, hotter than any Biscay summer evening, a heat that the bark's timbers held and that the forecastle would not release until an hour before dawn — and at four bells of the morning watch the second flood came and the pilot, who had slept in the captain's cabin, came on deck and said they would up-anchor on the hour.

They up-anchored. The pilot took them up the river. The river was not like any water Olav had been on. It was narrow — narrower than he had expected from the handbook, hardly wider in places than the *Semiramis*'s length times three — and it was brown, and it ran with a current that the bark, under only a foretopsail and a staysail, had to hold against at a steady eight points off the wind. The land on either side was close. There were villages. There were temples. There were boats — small flat Bengali boats, painted red and blue, with single lateen sails and three or four men on each — passing the bark at a cable's distance with the unhurried manners of men who had made this river their life.

Olav stood at the foremast braces for hours and watched.

He had not, in five years at sea, seen a country. He had seen ports — Lisbon, Wilmington, Cape Town, a dozen coastal places in Norway — and he had seen the water between ports, but he had not seen a country from the deck of a ship passing through it. A port was a place that had been made for the convenience of sailors. A country seen from a river was a place that had not been made for any such convenience. It was simply there, in its own business, moving at its own speed.

The river was also — he understood, at the foremast rail in the hot afternoon — a piece of the world in which he was for the first time unambiguously a foreigner.

At Stavanger he had been a Norwegian in Norway. At Lisbon he had been a Norwegian in Portugal, which was a place that had for four hundred years had Norwegian sailors passing through it and had taken the measure of them. At Wilmington he had been a Norwegian in a country that had been full of the deserting-from-various-things, and his small private deserting had not made him remarkable. At Cape Town he had been a Norwegian in a port arranged by a Dutch administration with an English superstructure, and the port had taken his signature as the signature of a man of the sort of country it knew how to take signatures from.

On the Hooghly he was a Norwegian in a country whose people had no reason, at the riverbank, to know that Norway existed.

He registered this. He did not let the registering expand.

---

On the second night, at Diamond Harbour, a trader came off the bank in a small skiff with a boy at the oars and a lantern at the bow and hailed the bark in better English than the pilot spoke.

"*Semiramis!* Bergen?"

"Bergen," the mate called back.

"I have oranges. I have coconuts. I have mangoes. I have Bombay onions. Who is your steward."

"Torgerson."

"Send Torgerson."

Torgerson came. He leaned over the rail. He dealt in a mixture of English and gestures that had, Olav noted from the main fife rail, the fluency of a steward who had done this a hundred times at a hundred different ports and had long since stopped being surprised by any of it. The trader passed up a basket of mangoes and a string of small bananas and six coconuts and two dozen onions; Torgerson passed down four rupees and some small change in Norwegian øre that Olav was faintly surprised the trader would take, which the trader took.

"Captain buys the mangoes," Torgerson said afterward, coming past Olav with the basket. "The rest is for the forecastle."

"The rest is your doing."

"The rest is part of my contract with the *Semiramis,* Hestby. The steward is permitted to spend the captain's change on the forecastle's supper if it comes to him at a trader's bank in the Hooghly on the second night of an arrival. I have put it on the books."

"Is that in the articles."

"It is in the custom. The captain does not read the custom. The captain reads the books."

"You are going to be the steward on Bergen ships for another twenty years, Torgerson."

"I am going to be the steward on Bergen ships until I am a steward on a ship I own a share of. My chronometer lesson is on Thursday."

"Thursday. I will be ready."

Torgerson went aft with the basket.

---

That night in the forecastle Olav lay in the heat and could not, for some time, sleep. The bark had anchored at Diamond Harbour on a bottom of the kind of sticky river-mud that held an anchor in a vice; she was not going anywhere. The air in the forecastle was heavy and thick and the men around him were sleeping the kind of sleep men slept on their first hot river night — troubled, half-awake, turning — and Olav lay in his hammock with his coat folded under his head and the forecastle's one small scuttle open above him and listened.

There were sounds from the bank he had never heard before. Bird sounds, he thought; or insect sounds. Some of them he did not have a category for. A low rhythmic calling that might have been a frog was somewhere a hundred yards off the bark's starboard beam, and the calling had the particular unvarying quality of a sound that would go on, he understood, all night and every night he was on the river. Another sound — a longer, higher call that he thought might be a bird — came three times in a quarter hour and then stopped.

He thought of Olava.

She would not, for another five months, have the news that he had come up the river. The Calcutta letter he would write over the next four days would not reach her until February. For five months she would only know what the Cape Town letter had told her: that he had lifted for Calcutta on the Friday at the end of July. She would, between now and February, not be able to place him on any chart she could draw.

This was the particular fact, he understood, of being engaged to a man at sea: that the woman ashore lived in a space where the man was a vague figure moving through a geography she could only half imagine, and where the letters when they came were fixed points on a line otherwise drawn in air. Olava had told him, at the Lindøy bench in the Easter week of this spring, that she had made peace with the fact before he had signed the articles. He had believed her then. He believed her now.

He thought: *the woman ashore is one man. The man afloat is another. The marriage is the rope between them.*

He thought: *the rope is a long rope. The rope is made of the letters. The rope is made of the silences between the letters. The rope is made of the things the letters say and the things they do not say. On the day I come home in November of next year, the rope will have been spun by both of us for eighteen months. The marriage will be the rope. I am, tonight at Diamond Harbour on the mud of the Hooghly, spinning my end of the rope.*

He thought: *I am spinning it well enough.*

He listened to the bird that was not a bird.

He slept at last, the half-sleep of a man on his first Indian night.

The next morning they took the third flood up the river to Garden Reach, and at noon the following day they were warped into the anchorage off the Strand at Calcutta, and the *Semiramis,* at the end of the eighty-seventh day out of Stavanger, came to rest on a mooring that would hold her for the next three weeks of her voyage.

Olav, at the foredeck with the rest of the common watch, took off his cap for a moment and looked at the city.

The city was, from the mooring, a long low line of buildings along a river that was wider here than it had been below Diamond Harbour, and the line of buildings had in it the particular composition a man from Northern Europe had not, until this moment, seen — a row of grand white classical fronts, like London or Paris but hotter, broken at intervals by temples with the kind of profile Olav had last seen in a hand-coloured book in the library at the navigation school — and behind the line of buildings, behind it at a depth he could not, from the mooring, estimate, the city rose in roofs and dust into a distance that disappeared into the afternoon haze.

He put his cap back on.

He understood, at the mooring with his cap back on his head, that he had come to a place in the world his father had never seen and would never see, and that his father, who had been to Newcastle and Hamburg and the Azores but not east of the Cape, would read the letter Olav wrote about Calcutta with the same careful attention he read a piece of news from a place he did not know.

He thought of his father reading the letter.

He thought of his mother reading the letter.

He thought of Olava reading it in February.

He went aft to find Captain Brun, who had said, two months before at Cape Town, that Olav would come ashore with him on the first day in the harbor to draft the chart corrections. The first day was today. Olav had the leather folder under his arm. He was ready to be the navigator a second time.
