Olav spent Christmas of 1882 at Lindøy.

It had been agreed, at the kitchen table at Finnøy in the second week of November, that he would. His parents were coming over on the Christmas Eve coaster and would stay through Saint Stephen's; the captain and Mrs. Lindøy had set aside the back room for them; Marit had given up her own room for the days in question to her schoolmaster's-son fiancé's family — the Schmidts, who were coming down from Mosterøy — and would sleep on a cot in her mother's sewing room; Olav would have the loft. This was the arrangement Mrs. Lindøy had been constructing by her own quiet correspondence since November, and by the time Olav came across from Finnøy on the twenty-third of December the arrangement had the finality of a small Lindøy institution.

He came across on the twenty-third in his father's boat alone.

His father would come over the next afternoon with his mother on the coaster. Olav had volunteered to go ahead, and his mother had agreed, because his mother had understood that he wanted to be at Lindøy for the twenty-fourth's preparation rather than arriving at the table on the twenty-fourth with his parents already seated. His mother, as she had been managing to do since the arrival at Bergen in October, understood without being told.

The channel on the twenty-third had a flat low grey sea and a light north wind and the kind of cold a Finnøy–Lindøy crossing had in late December. The boat took the hour and a half he had budgeted for it. He tied up at the Lindøy jetty at a quarter past eleven in the morning. Olava was at the jetty. Her wool coat was the wool coat. Her hair was coming loose under her cap the way it had been coming loose at the tag game at Landa three and a half years before.

She put one hand on his face, briefly, and said:

"Olav."

"Olava."

"Come up."

She did not — and had not, on any of his arrivals at the jetty since the October homecoming — held his face for the full count of breaths. She had held it once only, at the October arrival, for the long count of six. Since then the holding had become, at each crossing, a single-breath gesture at the jetty; a longer gesture had begun to be reserved, by her calibration, for particular evenings. He had understood, by the second week of November, that the economy was hers to keep and that his part was to register it without altering it.

He registered it now.

He followed her up to the house.

---

At the house Mrs. Lindøy had the stove going. The captain was at the kitchen table with his newspaper and his coffee. Marit was not yet home from a morning errand at the neighbour's. The small kitchen smelled of baking — the sour particular smell of a Lindøy Christmas cake, which was a slower bread than an ordinary Christmas bread, and which Mrs. Lindøy had been slow-rising since the Sunday.

Mrs. Lindøy said, at the stove:

"Olav."

"Mrs. Lindøy."

"You are early."

"Yes."

"Your room is ready. Marit will come at noon. Your father and mother tomorrow."

"Yes."

"Have coffee."

"Yes."

He had coffee.

He sat at the kitchen table. The captain, across from him, looked up.

"Hestby."

"Captain."

"You look better."

"I am."

"Two months ashore."

"Two months."

"Good."

The captain did not say more. He returned to his newspaper. Olava was at the window with her own coffee. She did not, at the table, speak either.

The quiet of the Lindøy kitchen at eleven-thirty on the twenty-third of December was the quiet that he had, on the *Semiramis* in the forecastle at night, imagined the kitchen would have when he came home. He had imagined it correctly.

---

The twenty-fourth was Christmas Eve.

Olav's parents came across on the noon coaster. The Schmidts — Eirik's father and mother and his younger sister — came across on the afternoon boat from Mosterøy. The house at Lindøy had, by four in the afternoon, eight people in it and the particular small warmth a Norwegian island house had on the twenty-fourth of December with both stoves going and the candles lit in the parlour and the cake out on the table.

They had supper at six.

The supper was the Lindøy Christmas supper Mrs. Lindøy had been making for her family for thirty years — pickled herring, smoked mutton, new potatoes, flatbread, the slow-rising sour bread she had been baking since the Sunday, and the small honey-cake Marit had made for the Schmidt girl's benefit. The captain said a short grace. His father and Mrs. Lindøy exchanged four polite sentences across the table about a shared cousin. Eirik's father and Olav's father discovered they had both been to Hamburg on different ships in the spring of '71 and fell into a small dry sailor's conversation about a particular Hamburg customhouse clerk who had, according to both of them, been the worst clerk they had dealt with in their respective careers. Marit and the Schmidt girl — who was fourteen — laughed at something Eirik had said. The captain poured aquavit for the adult men and wine for the women. He did not, Olav noted, offer the aquavit to Eirik, who was sixteen and was therefore at the table in the small formal category of *not-yet-an-adult-though-engaged.*

Olava sat beside Olav. Her knee was against his under the table for most of the meal.

He registered the knee without commenting on it.

---

After the supper the family moved to the parlour. The captain read, as he had read in each of the last forty Christmases of his life, a single short chapter from the Gospel of Luke. Mrs. Lindøy sang — the Schmidt girl joining her on the second verse — one Norwegian Christmas song of the old kind that the Lindøy women had been singing on Christmas Eve for three generations. Eirik, to the quiet small amusement of the table, added a tenor line on the third verse he had been teaching himself over the autumn. The captain, when the song was done, said:

"Good."

Which was, at the Lindøy Christmas Eve parlour, the small formal captain's expression of approval that required no further comment.

They went to their rooms at ten.

---

At half past eleven, when the house had gone quiet and the lamps in the parlour had been turned down and Olav's parents were in the back room and Marit was in the sewing room and the Schmidts were in Marit's room and the captain and his wife were in their own room, Olava came up the loft stairs in her nightdress with her wool coat thrown over her shoulders and she stopped at the top of the stairs and she said, quietly, into the dark of the loft:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"I am going down to the back garden for a quarter hour. Come with me."

He put on his coat.

He went down behind her.

---

The garden at midnight on Christmas Eve had the particular small clarity a Norwegian island garden had on a still cold December night — the apple trees black against the stars, the channel a wide dark thing past the bottom of the garden, the lamp in the kitchen window behind them giving only enough light to walk by. The bench was where the bench had been since Olav had first sat on it in June of '80.

They sat on the bench.

Olava pulled her coat tighter and leaned slightly against his shoulder.

They did not, for some minutes, speak.

The stars at Lindøy on the night between the twenty-fourth and the twenty-fifth of December had — in the year Olav had been away — come to be, in his head, the stars he had pictured when he had tried to picture Lindøy from the bark. They were the same stars he had, at various latitudes of the voyage, checked his sighting against. They had been at different declinations at his different latitudes; they had, at this latitude and at this season and on this bench, come around to the arrangement his head had been holding.

He looked up at them.

Olava, beside him, looked up at them too.

After perhaps five minutes she said, without turning her head:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"At Christmas of '79 at the kitchen table at the back of my father's room I said a thing to you. Do you remember it."

"I remember."

"I said that I knew there was a piece of you that I did not have and was not going to have, and that I would agree not to ask about the piece, and that when the carrying got too heavy I was asking you to bring whatever I had to know to me at the moment of the too-heaviness and not before."

"Yes."

"I have been keeping track, in the ledger and in my head and in the folder, of whether the carrying has reached too-heavy."

"Yes."

"It has not."

"No."

"I want to say one thing to you about it tonight. I am not going to ask you a question. I am going to say a thing."

"Say it."

She did not, for a beat, speak.

Then:

"I know there is more in you than there was in June of last year when you went away. I know it the way a woman who has lived with a man's letters for seventeen months knows it. I am not asking what the more is. I am noting that I know. I am noting it to you because I do not want you to carry, on top of the more, the additional small weight of supposing that I do not know. I know. The carrying is yours. The knowing is mine. We are each of us carrying our own side of what the voyage made."

She stopped.

She waited.

Olav did not, for the count of ten seconds, answer.

Then he said, quietly:

"Thank you, Olava."

That was what he said.

He did not ask *how do you know,* because he had understood at the first moment of her speaking that the how was a Christmas morning '79-style question that did not need to be asked. She knew the way she had known at the tag game that he was the man who had got out from under the cough. She knew by looking. She knew by the accumulated small evidence of the seventeen months of the folder. She knew the way a woman of her intelligence and her calibration, engaged for three years to a man at sea, knew the shape of what the man was not saying.

He did not, also, say *there is a drawer with two letters in it now,* because to say that would have been to cross the line she had just drawn at the bench by saying *I am not asking what the more is.* She had drawn the line on purpose. The line was the line the marriage would sit on. His part, at the bench on Christmas Eve, was to honour the line by not crossing it. He honoured it.

What he said — *thank you, Olava* — was what the bench's agreement permitted him to say.

It was, he understood at the bench, the correct answer.

---

They sat for another few minutes without speaking.

The lamp in the kitchen behind them went out. Mrs. Lindøy, the last of the adults up in the house, had banked the fire and gone to bed.

Olava said, at last:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"I am going in."

"Yes."

"I want to say one more thing before I go in."

"Say it."

"The voyage is done. The drawer is where it is. In the spring we will be married at the Hestby church and the two names will go on the parish register in both spellings and the two of us will live the life we are going to live at Finnøy and at Lindøy, and I am going to be the wife of the man who has the drawer and the two letters, and you are going to be the husband of the woman who agreed in the winter of '79 not to ask what is in the drawer. That is what the marriage is. I am telling you because I want you to know, in the night between the twenty-fourth and the twenty-fifth, that I am, at this bench, marrying you as you are and not as a version of you that does not have the drawer. I do not want a version of you that does not have the drawer. A version of you that does not have the drawer would not be you."

He took her gloved hand.

She let him.

He did not, at the holding, speak.

The not-speaking was the only sentence he had left in him that could match what she had just said, and the bench's suspension of the one-sentence rule — which he had not, he now realized, been told of; she had simply, tonight, allowed herself the long sentences because the night was the night — had not included, by his reading of it, his own matching speech. She was giving him the speech. His part was to take it.

He took it.

After a minute she stood up from the bench.

She kissed him — lightly, briefly, on the mouth, for the count of perhaps two breaths; which was the second kiss of their lives, and which was the kiss Olava had decided, at some point she did not announce, that the midnight of the twenty-fourth and twenty-fifth would be the right moment for, after the supper and after the Luke and after her sentence at the bench and before the wedding still three months away — and she walked up the path to the house without looking back.

He sat at the bench for another minute.

Then he too went up.

In the loft he lay in the dark.

He thought, at the ceiling: *she knows. She knows without my having told her. She has told me she knows. She has told me it is enough to know. She is marrying the man with the drawer.*

He thought: *I am marrying the woman who will not ask.*

He thought: *the not-asking is the form. It was the form in '79 and it is the form now and it will be the form for as long as the two of us live the life the bench has just described.*

He thought: *I have, at twenty-three on Christmas Eve, been given the marriage a man does not always get.*

He did not, at the ceiling, allow the thought to expand into anything the ceiling could not bear.

He closed his eyes.

Downstairs the house was quiet. The bench in the garden was empty. The channel outside the kitchen window ran its small cold December tide. The stars — the stars Olav had looked up at for seventeen months at varying latitudes — were the stars over Lindøy at Christmas in the arrangement his head had been holding for them.

He slept.

---

On Saint Stephen's afternoon, after the Schmidts had gone back to Mosterøy on the morning boat and Olav's parents had left for Finnøy on the midday coaster, Olava came down to the jetty with Olav to see him off. He was going back to Finnøy for the ten days of small finishing work at the boat-shed before the coaster-cycle brought him back to Lindøy on a Wednesday.

At the jetty she did not hold his face.

She took the cuff of his coat, as she had at the Stavanger quay in June of '81 and at the Lindøy jetty in the morning departure of the same day, and she held it for a count of two breaths.

She said:

"Go, Olav."

"Yes."

"Be at the boat-shed by evening."

"Yes."

"Come back in ten days."

"Yes."

She let go.

He stepped down into the boat. His father's small boat was his; he took the tiller. He did not, on the crossing, look back at the jetty.

He had decided, at the jetty, that he was not going to. The not-looking was the small personal discipline he had carried since the Stavanger quay in June of '81.

He crossed the channel.

He tied up at the Finnøy jetty in the late afternoon.

He went up to the boat-shed.

---

At the boat-shed he lit the stove. He had not been at the boat-shed since the twenty-third. His father had not, in the four days, been at the shed either. The shed was cold. The stove took perhaps twenty minutes to bring the small space to the kind of warmth a man could work in at the end of December.

He stood at the workbench.

He put his palm, briefly, on the top of the workbench above where the drawer was.

He did not open the drawer.

He had understood, at the bench on the night between the twenty-fourth and the twenty-fifth, that the drawer was not to be opened. Olava had not asked. He had not told. The bench had confirmed what the workbench would, from now on, enact. The enactment was the not-opening.

He took his palm off the top of the workbench.

He took up the plane. He resumed the work on the jamb-board.

The boat-shed at Finnøy at five o'clock on Saint Stephen's evening of 1882 held its twenty-three-year-old navigator at the workbench, and the small drawer under the workbench held the two letters it had held since the second Tuesday of November, and the house up the path held the parents who had said their sentences in October and November, and the channel beyond the path held the Lindøy jetty at which the wool-coat woman had, on the night between the twenty-fourth and the twenty-fifth of December, said — without asking — that she knew.

The knowing was hers.

The carrying was his.

The not-asking was the form.

The form was going to hold for the rest of their lives.

He planed the jamb-board.

The stove burned.

At six his mother called him up to supper.

He went up.
