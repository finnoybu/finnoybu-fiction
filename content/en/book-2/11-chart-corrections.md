Captain Brun went ashore at the inner harbour with Olav in a hired dinghy at two bells of the afternoon watch on the day they moored.

The dinghy was rowed by a thin young man in a white cotton shirt and a cap of the kind the river-boys on the Hooghly wore — flat, round, tied under the chin — and the young man rowed with the particular shortened stroke a man used on a crowded harbour to avoid catching the oars on the cables of the bigger boats. He took them to the customhouse stairs at the Strand. He asked, in English of the sort the Calcutta boatmen spoke, for two annas for the trip. Brun gave him four.

"Captain," Olav said, when the young man had pulled away, "the book said two."

"The book was written in 1868," Brun said. "The prices have risen."

"Yes, sir."

"You will find, in the course of your career, that the books never admit that they have aged. That is one of the uses of a captain. He updates the prices in the book without making a show of it."

He led Olav up the stairs and into the Strand.

---

The Strand at Calcutta in September at three o'clock in the afternoon had a particular weight to it that Olav registered the moment he had come up off the dinghy-stairs and felt the flagstones under his boots. The weight was heat, and it was the density of the crowd, and it was the smell — the Diamond Harbour smell compounded now by the smells of a city, horse and dust and a spice Olav did not know and a cooking smell of some oil — and it was the sheer number of people moving in every direction.

He had not, at Cape Town in July, been in such a press of people. Cape Town had had a few hundred people on any one street. The Strand at Calcutta had several thousand. Porters carrying bales on their heads; clerks in white coats; British officers in khaki; Indian gentlemen in dhotis; women in saris; a trio of Chinese merchants in silk coats; a great many men whose business Olav could not at a glance identify; carts; rickshaws; two bullock-wagons moving against the flow; an elephant.

He looked at the elephant. He did not let his face do anything.

Brun did not stop for the elephant.

They walked perhaps six hundred yards along the Strand to a building in the classical style with *The Bengal Pilots' Office* in gilt and black on its door.

---

The pilots' office was, inside, cooler than the Strand by a measurable degree. The windows were shuttered on the west side; the ceilings were very high; there was a punkah, pulled by a boy on a mat in the corner, that moved the air across the central room in a slow steady wave. Along the walls were bookshelves of charts, and at the far end of the room was a long oak table, and at the table was a man in his late forties with a white moustache and the manner of an Englishman who had not been in England for twenty years.

"Captain Brun."

"Colonel."

"You are on time."

"I had a good pilot."

"Banerjee. He is one of our best. You have someone with you."

"Mr. Hestby. My navigator. He is here to take the chart corrections for our departure."

The colonel looked at Olav. The look was the look of a man who had, in twenty years in Calcutta, looked at many young Europeans who had come up the river for the first time, and who had developed a short grading-scale with which to place them. The colonel placed Olav in perhaps four seconds. He nodded.

"Navigator. Which certificate."

"Stavanger, sir. June of last year."

"Stavanger." The colonel turned to a ledger at his elbow and wrote something. "We do not get many Stavanger certificates here. Most of the Norwegian masters we see are Bergen or Christiania. Halvar Sars is Stavanger, but Sars has not been east of the Cape in twelve years. Who is your examiner of record."

"Professor Nilson, sir. At the school. The examination was by the master of the *Diana,* Captain Sars, as an external examiner."

"Sars. You sailed on the *Diana.*"

"As an able seaman, sir. The summer of '78, Vardøy to Bergen."

"Then you have stood with a man who has taken more noon sights than the whole of Trinity House. I am pleased to make your acquaintance, Mr. Hestby."

"Thank you, sir."

"Come to the chart table."

---

The chart table held a large sectional chart of the Hooghly from Sagar Island to Calcutta, marked in the latest pilotage with coloured pencil.

The colonel led them through the corrections. The James and Mary shoal had shifted, in the last monsoon, by about three hundred yards to the east; the main channel past it now ran closer to the starboard bank than it had in the 1879 survey. The Mayapur bar had built up by two feet at the northern edge and should now be passed on a rising tide only, even by a vessel of the *Semiramis*'s draft. The bends at Hooghly Point and at Fultah had not moved, but a new wreck — a Welsh brig from Swansea lost in the June monsoon — was now on the chart at Fultah at its position on the mud, and the pilots were routing around it on the inbound tide with an extra quarter-cable to spare.

Olav wrote the corrections on the slate he had brought in the leather folder. He wrote them in the small clear hand Nilson had made him practise at Stavanger — the hand a navigator used for chart annotations, with the cardinal directions spelled in full and the distances given in both fathoms and cable-lengths. He did not ask questions; he listened and wrote. When the colonel had finished, he read back the corrections aloud — slowly — and the colonel, at the end, said:

"Good. Mr. Hestby. You will write those corrections onto the ship's chart tonight in the captain's cabin, and on the outbound run in three weeks you will have them in front of you, and you will pilot her down with Banerjee or whichever pilot we send you, and you will, by doing so, have made your first Hooghly descent. The descent is harder than the ascent. The ascent is on the flood; the descent is on the ebb. On the ebb there is less time to recover a mistake. Banerjee will be at your shoulder, but you will be the one who writes the sight at the binnacle. The chart is the chart you have just taken. You have the chart."

"Yes, sir."

"Captain Brun."

"Colonel."

"You did not tell me he was a good one."

"I told you he was my navigator. I do not sign for common seamen I plan to describe to pilots' colonels as *good ones.* I sign for the navigator."

"Fair."

"My discharge of cargo begins tomorrow. I will be at the Exchange at ten o'clock Friday with the invoices. May I leave Mr. Hestby with you for the afternoon to review the Rangoon coastal chart? We will be calling there before we return, and I would like his hand to have worked the approaches before we sail."

"I have Rangoon charts. Bring him."

"Thursday afternoon."

"Thursday afternoon."

They left the pilots' office.

---

On the walk back along the Strand to the customhouse stairs — still under the same heavy three-o'clock weight, still in the same press — Brun did not speak for perhaps two hundred yards.

Then, without stopping:

"You did that well."

"Thank you, sir."

"You did not interrupt the colonel to ask whether a bend was in fathoms or metres. The last young navigator I brought here did. The colonel did not mind the question, but he noted the question, and he placed the young navigator a grade lower than he would have otherwise, and the young navigator did not know he had been placed and was surprised afterward when the colonel's recommendation to the company's agents was milder than he had expected. You did not ask the question because you had read the chart the colonel was working from and you had seen the units at the corner. The reading of the units is a small thing. But it is the small things the Colonel Wightons of the world grade on, because the small things are what separate the navigator who will bring his ship home from the navigator who will lose her at Fultah on an ebb."

"Yes, sir."

"I have two more things to say to you before we reach the stairs, Hestby."

"Yes, sir."

"First. You will, tomorrow morning, write the corrections onto the ship's chart at the chart table in my cabin. Mr. Aarseth is the mate and he has a mate's right to the table. I have told him that you will have it between six bells of the morning watch and four bells of the forenoon. He will not be in your way. The ink and the ruler are in the upper drawer of the chart-table. You will take as long as you need."

"Yes, sir."

"Second. You will, at the signing-off of this voyage in Bergen in November of next year, be signed off as my first mate. You have earned it in the last three months. I have not told you this before because I do not, as a rule, tell a man he has earned a berth before the voyage has ended — too many things can still happen between here and home — but I have decided today that in your case I will tell you. I am telling you now because I do not want you to arrive at Bergen and have the offer surprise you. It will not be a surprise. You are, and have been since Biscay, the man I want at my right hand on the next *Semiramis* voyage. I am telling you so that you can think about your answer between here and home. The voyage will lift the September after we return. It is another East India, eighteen months, the same route. Bergen to Calcutta and back. You think about it."

Olav did not, for ten paces, reply.

He had known, from the supper coil with Sven in Biscay, that the offer was coming. He had worked out, in the two months since, the shape of the offer and the shape of his answer. He had said the answer aloud to Sven at the Biscay rail and he had — in a different form, in a slightly different set of words — written the answer into his Cape Town letter to Olava. The answer was not going to be different, at the customhouse stairs at Calcutta on a hot September afternoon, than it had been in the Biscay dusk.

He said:

"Captain."

"Yes."

"I have a wedding to get to, sir. I am engaged to a girl on Lindøy. The wedding is set for the spring after we come home. The next *Semiramis* voyage is eighteen months. If I take the mate's berth on her, I will not be home to be married."

Brun walked another three paces before he answered.

"I had wondered," he said. "You have not, in the chart-room, ever spoken of her. I had thought it was a possibility. What was her name, Hestby."

"Olava, sir."

"Olava. Old name."

"Yes, sir."

"It is the name my own wife would have been given, had she been born at home. Good name. You are telling me no, then."

"I am telling you no, sir, with thanks that I hope I do not owe at less than the weight I owe them. I would sail with you again, Captain. I will not sail with you again on this voyage. After the wedding, if the company is still making East Indias in a year, and if you are still her captain, and if you are still signing navigators, I will sign for you on a later voyage."

"You are asking me to hold the berth."

"I am asking nothing, sir. I am telling you what I would do if the offer came again in two years, which it will not, because you will have found another man by then. I am telling you so that you understand that the no is not a no about the ship. It is a no about the year."

"A fair distinction."

"Yes, sir."

Brun nodded, once. They reached the customhouse stairs. The dinghy-boy was waiting. Brun gave him another four annas — Olav noted — and they went out onto the brown water of the Hooghly at dusk.

---

In the dinghy, with the bark's mooring a quarter-mile ahead of them and the city's evening lamps coming up along the Strand in a long low line, Brun said, looking at the city and not at Olav:

"Your Olava is a fortunate woman, Hestby."

"Thank you, sir."

"The fortunate part is not that you refused a mate's berth for her. Men refuse mate's berths for women every year, and two years later they wish they had not. The fortunate part is that you refused with the answer you gave. You told me the refusal was about the year, not about the ship. That is the answer of a man who is not lying to himself about what he is refusing. Men who refuse for the wrong reasons grow to resent the women they refused for. Men who refuse with your answer do not. You are going to be the husband your Olava married. That is more than most women who marry sailors get."

"Thank you, sir."

"I will say that once, Hestby, and I will not say it again."

"Yes, sir."

Brun turned his face from the city to the bark. The dinghy slid alongside the hull. The boy held her at the accommodation-ladder. Brun went up first. Olav went up after him, the leather folder with the corrections under his arm and the chart of the James and Mary shoal in his head, and the third day of his Indian Ocean voyage ahead of him.

On the deck Sundberg was at the main fife rail. He looked at Olav. He looked at the folder. He looked at Olav again.

He did not say anything.

Olav went to his berth and laid the folder on his chest and went aft to take the first dog-watch.
