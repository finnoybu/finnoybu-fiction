On the Wednesday morning of the first week home Olav took the small boat across the channel to Finnøy with his father.

They left Lindøy at half past six, Jens at the tiller and Olav at the sheet, on a grey October morning with the wind just north of east at about twelve knots and the sea running a small quarter chop across the channel. The crossing took an hour and twenty minutes. Olav's mother and Olava were at the Lindøy jetty when they pushed off, standing at the boards with their arms folded against the cold, and they watched the small boat until it was a dot under the Finnøy coast, and then — Olav had no doubt, because he had grown up in a house of this kind of mother — they went back up to the kitchen to put the kettle on and talk about him without him being there to listen.

His father did not, for the first forty minutes of the crossing, speak.

Olav did not press him.

He understood that his father had been waiting, at the quay at Bergen and on the coaster south and at the Lindøy supper on the Monday evening and at the breakfast on the Tuesday and at the kitchen coffee on the Tuesday afternoon, for the small private hour at which he was going to say the thing he had been waiting for sixteen months to say to his son alone. The small boat between Lindøy and Finnøy on a Wednesday morning was the hour. It was the first hour of the homecoming in which Olav had been with his father and no one else, and it was also the first hour in which both of them had had something to do with their hands that was not social — the tiller and the sheet of a small boat were not social, in the way the breakfast table and the kitchen had been social — and the hands being on the work would let the mouth, if it wanted to, say the thing.

At about the fortieth minute his father said:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"You are thinner than I was at twenty-three."

"I do not know what you were at twenty-three."

"I weighed a hundred and sixty pounds at twenty-three. I weighed it on the doctor's scale at Stavanger when I signed on for the *Aase* in September of '57. I have the slip at the house in the drawer in the bedroom."

"I weighed a hundred and fifty last week at Bergen on the company scale."

"You will gain it back at home. Your mother has been planning for it since September."

"Yes."

His father fell silent for another minute.

Then:

"Olav. The thing I am going to say is the thing I have been composing for sixteen months. I have rehearsed it in my head at the woodpile perhaps two hundred times. I am not going to rehearse it further. I am going to say it once, and then I am going to let it sit in the boat between us for the rest of the crossing, and I am going to let you take it back to the house with you in whatever shape the taking requires."

"Say it."

"You have been to sea for fifteen months on a deep-water bark. You have been a common seaman and a navigator. You have been signed off by a captain I have known the reputation of for forty years and who on the quay at Bergen on the Saturday morning said to me the sentence *your son will be a master.* I am telling you that I know what the sentence meant. I am telling you that I do not expect you to stay ashore after the wedding. I am telling you that a Hestby who has come down the gangway of a *Semiramis* after an East India voyage is a man who is going to go again, and that I have spent the sixteen months understanding this in a way I was not, in June of '81, able to understand it. I am telling you that when you go again your mother and I will be at the Stavanger quay to see you go, and that Olava will be at the Lindøy jetty with you afterward, and that we will — all three of us — manage the seeing-off the way we managed it in June of '81 and will manage it without making it harder for you than it has to be. I am telling you this because you are, at twenty-three on a Wednesday morning in a small boat between Lindøy and Finnøy, the kind of man a father says this sentence to instead of making him work it out for himself over five years of a first marriage."

He did not, after saying it, look at Olav.

He looked at the tiller and at the set of the sail.

Olav did not, for perhaps thirty seconds, answer.

He understood, at the thirty seconds, what his father had just given him. His father had given him — in the small boat at the fortieth minute — the permission his father had not given himself in 1857 when his father had come home from the *Aase* and had decided, against his own body's knowledge of what his body wanted next, to stay ashore at Finnøy and not go to sea again. His father had chosen the land. His father had not, Olav understood, ever entirely stopped regretting the choice, even though the choice had been the right choice for the family and the right choice for the marriage and the right choice for the small farm and the small boat-shed a Hestby had been running at Finnøy since 1812.

His father was telling him, at the fortieth minute, to not make the same choice.

His father was telling him to go back to sea.

Olav said:

"Father."

"Yes."

"I will go back."

"I know."

"I will not go back in October of '83 on the *Semiramis.* The next *Semiramis* lifts five months after my wedding, and I have decided not to be the kind of husband who leaves his wife of five months for eighteen."

"That is the decision of a man twenty-three years old who is getting married in March. It is the correct decision for that man. I am not asking you to reverse it."

"Good."

"When the year after your wedding is run, you will consider another berth. That year or the next or the one after that. The captain at the Bergen office said he would hold the *Semiramis* reserve for you until October of '83; I do not know what he has said to you privately about later voyages. But you will, I understand, be on some ship within three years of your wedding. I am saying now, in the boat, that I expect it. I do not want Olava or you to have to manage around my silence about it when the time comes."

"Thank you, Father."

"I am not asking for thanks. I am arranging the house for the five years ahead of us."

"Yes."

They did not speak again for the rest of the crossing.

The Finnøy jetty came up at the eightieth minute. His father brought the small boat alongside at the old familiar place at the south side of the jetty, where the post at the bollard had the small notch his grandfather had cut into it in 1843 and that no one had ever replaced, and Olav stepped onto the boards and tied the painter, and his father took down the sail and stowed it, and they went up the path to the house without speaking.

---

His mother was at the kitchen window when they came up to the house.

She did not come to the door to meet them; she waited inside. This was her way. A mother who had been managing a son's voyage from a Finnøy kitchen for sixteen months did not meet the homecoming at the door a second time; she let the homecoming come to her at the stove.

He came to her at the stove.

She was baking bread. The kitchen smelled of the particular Finnøy bread his mother had baked every Wednesday of his life. She looked at him, once, over her shoulder, and she said:

"The loft is ready."

"Thank you."

"Your coat is damp. Take it off and hang it by the door."

"Yes."

"There is coffee."

"Yes."

He hung his coat. He took the coffee. He sat at the kitchen table with the cup in his hands, and his mother went on with the bread, and his father went to the parlour to look at the ledger that had not been looked at in six weeks, and the Finnøy kitchen at ten in the morning of the first Wednesday of his homecoming became — by the quiet small arithmetic of the household — the place he belonged.

---

After the bread was out of the oven and on the cooling-rack his mother said:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"Your father said he would say his sentence in the boat."

"He said it."

"Good. Then we do not need to have a different conversation at the table."

"No."

"I have, also, a sentence of my own. I am not going to say it at the table. I am going to say it at the kitchen door on the Thursday evening when you are going out to the boat-shed. That is the small arrangement I made with myself in August. I will keep it."

"Yes."

"I am telling you the arrangement so that you know the sentence is coming and will not be surprised by it."

"Thank you, Mother."

"Go to the boat-shed."

He went to the boat-shed.

---

The boat-shed had been swept.

His father had been doing the sweeping, Olav understood on entering, on each Saturday of the sixteen months. The sawdust on the floor was fresh. The tools were hung as they had been hung in June of '81. The stove in the corner had wood in the hopper. The workbench along the back wall was the workbench it had been in June, with the small scratches and the worn place at the far end where his father's elbow had rested for thirty years and where his grandfather's elbow had rested for thirty years before that.

The small drawer was under the workbench on the underside, as it had been since 1842.

Olav stood at the workbench.

He did not open the drawer.

He had told himself, at the bedside table in the loft at Lindøy on the Monday night, that he would not open the drawer at the Wednesday morning visit to the boat-shed. He had told himself he would, on the Wednesday, simply stand at the workbench and know the drawer was there and go on with his other business. He had also told himself — being honest — that the telling might or might not hold, because he was not entirely sure, at the Monday night, what the telling was meant to achieve. He had not been sure whether the not-opening was the discipline or whether it was a performance of the discipline.

At the workbench on the Wednesday morning he understood, the way a man understood such things at the bench itself, which it was.

It was the discipline.

It was the discipline because the letter in the drawer had, in the sixteen months since he had put it there, done a particular small work he had not entirely been able to name until now. The work had been to be a thing in a drawer in a boat-shed at Finnøy while he was at sea. The work had required the thing not to be read again, not to be burned, not to be moved; it had required the thing simply to be. The thing had been. The drawer had held.

Opening the drawer now, at the first Wednesday of his homecoming, would be him asking the drawer to do a second work, which was to yield up the thing for a second reading. The drawer was not for that. The drawer was for keeping.

He laid his palm flat on the top of the workbench above where the drawer was.

He kept it there for perhaps five seconds.

He took his palm off.

He went to the other work he had come to the boat-shed for, which was the sorting of the tool rack his father had last seen to in '81 and which had, by the careful small disorder an idle toolrack developed over sixteen months, begun to need its Wednesday attention.

He spent an hour at the rack.

He did not, in the hour, open the drawer.

---

On the Thursday evening his mother said, at the kitchen door, the sentence she had told him she would.

He was on his way out to the boat-shed at six o'clock. She was at the stove. She wiped her hands on the dishcloth and came to the door.

"Olav."

"Yes."

"The sentence."

"Yes."

"Whatever you saw at sea, I do not need to know it. Whatever you did, I do not need to know that either. I have, in sixteen months, stopped expecting to know. Your father has also stopped. Olava, as far as I can tell from her letters to me, never expected to know in the first place, and has, from the engagement onward, made her not-expecting into something I recognize as the thing her grandmother made with it at the same age. You are a man who has been to sea. You have come home. That is the sentence."

"Thank you."

"Go to the boat-shed."

"Yes."

He went to the boat-shed.

He thought, walking down the path, that his mother had said in a single sentence what it had taken his father an hour in a boat to say, and that both had said the same thing, and that the family at Finnøy had therefore formed — in his absence — a consensus about the shape of his homecoming that he had not been consulted on and that he did not need to contest.

He accepted the consensus.

---

On the Saturday of the first week he took the coaster back across to Lindøy with his father — his mother had decided she would stay at Finnøy for the baking and would come over on the Wednesday — and on the Saturday evening at the back-garden bench at Lindøy with Olava he had his first hour alone with her since the arrival.

They sat at the bench.

The light was the October dusk. The channel was grey. The Finnøy coast was a darker grey across it. The wool coat was the wool coat.

Olava said:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"There is an arrangement I want to make for the months before the wedding."

"Say it."

"I want us to speak of the voyage in the small amounts a person can speak of a thing they have lived without the speaking becoming the life. I do not want us to avoid it; avoidance would be worse than any speech. I also do not want us to have long conversations about it. I want, when a thing rises in me at the kitchen table that I wish to ask, to ask it in one sentence. I want you, when a thing rises in you that you wish to tell, to tell it in one sentence. The one-sentence rule is the rule I have decided for us between now and the wedding."

"Yes."

"Good."

"Olava."

"Yes."

"I have a sentence now."

"Say it."

"The small drawer in the workbench at Finnøy is the drawer you knew about at Christmas of '79. It has a letter in it. It has had a letter in it since May of '81. I did not open it on Wednesday when I was at the boat-shed. I have, since Wednesday, been thinking about the fact that I did not open it. I am telling you now that I did not because I wanted you to know I did not."

She did not, for perhaps ten seconds, answer.

Then:

"Thank you, Olav."

"Yes."

"That is the sentence for tonight."

"Yes."

"We will, if you like, have a sentence tomorrow."

"We will have a sentence every Saturday evening at the bench until the wedding. That is what the rule will be between us."

"Yes."

"Good."

They sat for another ten minutes without speaking, and then the lamp came on in the kitchen behind them, and Olava stood up from the bench and put her hand briefly on his shoulder and walked up the path to the house.

He sat at the bench for another minute alone. Then he followed her up.

---

On the Monday of the second week he walked up with his father to the Hestby church at the top of the island.

It was the first time he had been in the churchyard since June of '81. The church was the church it had been. The priest was the priest it had been — Pastor Kleven, seventy-one, who had baptized Olav in March of 1858 and had confirmed him in April of 1872 and had, in the interval, buried both of Olav's grandfathers and one of his grandmothers and had christened three of Olav's cousins and had married two of his uncles. The small stone in the churchyard with *Hestbye* in the period spelling — the spelling the priest still used on the parish register even though the families had moved, one by one across the century, to the modern *Hestby* — was his grandfather Jens Hestbye's, who had died in 1849.

Olav stood at the stone for a minute.

Then he followed his father into the church.

Pastor Kleven was at the altar-end at a small desk with the parish register open. He looked up when they came in. He looked at Olav for a beat.

"Olav."

"Pastor."

"You are home."

"Yes."

"Your father and I have been writing the banns for a fortnight by letter across the channel. We have them ready for your signature."

"Yes."

Kleven turned the register toward him. On the page was a clean entry written in Kleven's old hand, dated October of 1882, stating that the banns of marriage would be read for three consecutive Sundays in Advent between *Olav Hestbye of Finnøy, parish of Hestbye, age twenty-three years and seven months, navigator,* and *Olava Lindøye of Lindøy, parish of Lindøy, age twenty-one years and ten months,* with the marriage to be solemnized at the Hestbye church on the fourteenth of March, 1883.

Olav looked at his name in the old spelling.

He looked at Olava's name in the old spelling.

He had not, in his life, seen either name written this way on a formal register. He had seen the stone at his grandfather's grave and had seen other stones in the churchyard and had seen the parish record at his own baptism certificate, which was in the tin box his mother kept in the kitchen; but he had not, until this afternoon, seen both his name and Olava's in the priest's hand in the register of the church in the form *Hestbye* and *Lindøye.*

The old spelling was the spelling the church had kept.

His father, at his shoulder, did not comment. Kleven, at the desk, did not comment.

Olav took the pen. He signed *Olav Hestby,* in the modern hand, at the line Kleven had indicated. He blotted the signature.

Kleven nodded once.

"That is the signing. Miss Lindøye will sign tomorrow when she is here. Then I will read the banns on the first Sunday of Advent, and on the second, and on the third, and on the fourteenth of March I will marry you at this altar. Is that understood."

"Yes, Pastor."

"Olav Hestby."

"Yes, Pastor."

"I christened you in this church in the spring of '58. I confirmed you in '72. I have, since your departure in June of the year before last, been remembering at the sentence in the confirmation service the line about a man making his own vocation. I have remembered it at each Sunday service. You have, I understand from your father, come home from the *Semiramis* as a man whose captain has said the word *master.* That is the vocation working. I am pleased."

"Thank you, Pastor."

"Go home with your father."

"Yes, Pastor."

---

On the walk down from the churchyard his father did not speak until they were at the gate at the foot of the church path.

At the gate he said:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"Pastor Kleven said *Hestbye* at the desk."

"He did."

"That is the old spelling."

"Yes."

"You signed *Hestby.*"

"Yes."

"You are at liberty to sign either. The church keeps the old. You and I keep the new. Olava will sign the new tomorrow because her father's hand is the modern one as well. But the parish register, after tomorrow, will have the old for both of you in the priest's hand and the new for both of you in your own."

"Yes."

"That is the Hestby."

"Yes."

"It is the Hestby we are. The old in the stone, the new at the signature. The two spellings of one name."

"Yes."

"Both are correct."

"Both are correct."

His father, at the gate, put his hand on the gate-post for a moment. He did not look at Olav.

"Go to your Olava, Olav. She has been waiting at the house since noon."

"Yes, Father."

He went down the hill to the house.

At the kitchen door Olava was at the window looking out. She saw him at the bend in the path. She did not, when she saw him, come to the door. She let him come to her.

He came.

At the door she put one hand on his cheek, briefly, and said:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"The banns are signed."

"They are signed."

"Hestbye and Lindøye in the pastor's hand."

"Yes."

"We are, as of today, the Hestbye and Lindøye of record."

"We are."

"Good."

She took her hand from his cheek.

She went back inside.

He stood at the door a moment longer. Then he went in after her.

The Hestby church on the hill at the top of the island, with the old spelling carved on the stones and the new spelling at his own signature in the register, was — he understood in the kitchen doorway of the house on Lindøy — the shape his life had been walking toward since Stavanger in June of '81, and the shape it was going to close around in five months at the altar where Pastor Kleven was going to put two names together in both spellings at once and make them, by the voice of the parish, a single thing.

He was not, in the kitchen doorway, ready to think further about the closing.

He went in for the supper.
