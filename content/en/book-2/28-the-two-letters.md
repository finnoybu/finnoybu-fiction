The two letters came on the same Tuesday morning in the middle of November.

Olav had been at Finnøy since the Sunday — he had crossed back from Lindøy on the Saturday afternoon boat to help his father rehang the boat-shed's main door, which had sagged over the summer on a hinge that his father had not, at the woodpile work alone, been able to address — and on the Tuesday morning he was at the workbench at eight o'clock with a plane in his hand, smoothing the long edge of a new jamb-board the two of them had cut the previous afternoon.

The Tuesday postman came up the road to the house at half past nine.

He was the postman who had brought the May letter from Shields in 1881 — the same thin man in the dark coat, a year and a half older, the mail-bag slung in the same way. He was at this season a man whose arrival at the house was the house's small Tuesday punctuation, because the postman came up the road to the house only when he had a letter for it and did not come otherwise, and a house whose adult son was newly home from an eighteen-month voyage was a house that got letters on Tuesdays with a certain regularity as the ship's arrival-news made its way through the consular correspondence.

Olav was at the workbench and did not hear the knock. His mother did. She was at the kitchen window. She opened the door; she took the letters in her hand; she stood at the kitchen door for a long moment looking at them.

She came out of the kitchen door.

She came down the path to the boat-shed.

Olav set down the plane.

"Olav."

"Yes."

"Two letters. Both for you."

"Yes."

She held them out. One was in an envelope he knew — the small Norwegian consul's cover from Copenhagen, with the Norwegian consul's stamp at the corner — and the other was in an envelope he had not seen before. It was a slightly smaller envelope, of a Stockholm type, with a Stockholm postmark, and the handwriting on the cover was a hand he had seen once in his life: it had been on a calling card handed to him on a quay corner in Nyhavn in September.

He took both letters from his mother.

He did not, at that moment, open either.

His mother looked at him. She did not, at the looking, press.

"Olav."

"Yes."

"The Copenhagen one is from the consul's correspondence. The other is a Stockholm hand."

"Yes."

"I noticed the Stockholm because the Tuesday postman almost never brings a Stockholm letter. There are perhaps six houses on Finnøy that receive Stockholm mail in a year. We are not one of them."

"No."

"I am not asking about the Stockholm letter, Olav. I am noting that I noticed. The noting is the end of the notice. I am not going to bring it up again."

"Thank you, Mother."

"The kitchen is making coffee. Come up when you are ready."

"Yes."

She turned and went back up the path.

He stood at the workbench with the two letters in his hand.

---

He did not, for perhaps half a minute, move.

Then he sat down on the stool at the end of the workbench with both letters in front of him on the bench. He looked at them. The Copenhagen one was from the consul — it would be, he understood, a routine piece of the bark's post-arrival correspondence; a Norwegian consul sometimes forwarded a small outstanding item to a sailor at his home address after the paid-off had been done, and the *Semiramis* had had consular business at Copenhagen on the homeward that this letter would be the tail of. He knew what was in it without opening it.

The Stockholm one was Anders Lindqvist's reply.

He had written Anders a single letter, as he had promised on the quay corner in Nyhavn: a short letter, two sentences long, posted from Stavanger on the second week of October on his way home from Bergen. The letter had said *Lindqvist — the* Semiramis *made Bergen on the first Saturday. I am at Finnøy. Yours, Hestby.* He had burned the card on the same night of their meeting, as Lindqvist had asked him to burn it; he had committed the address to memory; he had written the two sentences on the thinnest paper he could find and had used no return address beyond *O. Hestby, Finnøy, Stavanger.* He had posted it at the Stavanger post-house from the coaster between Bergen and Lindøy.

He had not, until this Tuesday morning, known whether the reply would come.

Anders had said, at the corner at Nyhavn, *One return answer is what I am asking for.* Anders had said he would file whatever came and would not, after, send anything further.

Anders had, by the Stockholm hand on the cover, sent a return answer.

---

He took the Copenhagen letter first.

The Copenhagen letter was what he had expected. It was a small consular document from Petersen, forwarded by Kvalheim at Bergen, returning to him the duplicate of the bark's chart-corrections he had drafted at Copenhagen on the Tuesday of the homeward. Petersen had kept the original for the company's files and had promised, in a sentence at the end of the Copenhagen work, to send Olav the duplicate. The duplicate was now in his hand. He folded it back into the envelope. He put the envelope on the workbench.

That was the first letter.

He took the Stockholm letter next.

He held it for a moment before he opened it.

He understood, at the holding, that whatever was inside the letter was the last piece of the voyage the voyage was going to ask him to handle. He had handled the signing-off. He had handled the homecoming at the quay at Bergen with his parents. He had handled the coaster south. He had handled the Lindøy jetty and the loft room. He had handled the banns at the Hestby church. He had handled his father's sentence in the boat and his mother's at the kitchen door and Olava's at the back-garden bench and the small Wednesday visit to the workbench at which he had not opened the drawer. The letter from Stockholm was the one piece the voyage had not yet delivered into his hand.

He had known, since the Nyhavn corner, that the piece would come.

He had, on the coaster south and on the first Tuesday home and at the workbench yesterday, half-prepared a small arrangement for how he would take the letter when it came. The arrangement was that he would read the letter once, and then he would decide what to do with it, and the deciding would be the deciding.

He broke the wafer — a plain Stockholm wafer, not the kind a man who corresponded privately with other men would have used — and he opened the letter.

---

The letter was short.

It was three lines, in the formal Swedish of a Stockholm clerk writing to a Norwegian he did not know well. It said:

> *Hestby —*
>
> *I received your line from Finnøy on the second Tuesday of November. I am glad the bark made Bergen. I am well at Stockholm. I send my best wishes for your spring wedding.*
>
> *Yours, Lindqvist.*

He read it.

He read it twice.

He read it three times, slowly, to verify that what he had taken in at the first reading was what the letter had actually said.

What the letter said was — he understood on the third reading — the same thing he had himself said in the letter he had sent. It said: the voyage is over, I received your line, I am glad, I wish you well at the wedding. The *wedding* was the word that closed the correspondence. The mentioning of the wedding was the mentioning of a thing that belonged irrevocably to the rest of Olav's life, and that Lindqvist, in naming it, was handing back to him as the thing the correspondence was not going to touch.

The letter was — by Anders's careful construction of it — the closing sentence that matched the May-'81 three-line letter from Shields in its form.

It was the same kind of letter as Haakon's.

The correspondence from Stockholm, Olav understood as he read the third time, was going to be parallel to the correspondence from Shields: one letter had been written, one reply had come, and the correspondence was now, by mutual unstated discipline, closed.

Anders had done what Haakon had done.

Anders had, in fact, done it faster. Haakon's single letter had sat at Finnøy for fifteen months before Olav had written anything in reply; Anders's answer had come within five weeks of Olav's letter, and had come in the form of a letter that itself declined to ask for further correspondence. The speed was a Stockholm-clerk's speed; the form was the form a man who had been clear at a quay corner at Nyhavn in September had already committed himself to.

Olav folded the letter.

He sat at the stool for a moment.

---

He thought, at the stool, about the thing he had known he would have to do when the Stockholm letter came.

He had known on the coaster south from Bergen that the Stockholm letter, when it came, would go into the drawer under the workbench. He had known it with the small flat clarity he had, on the voyage, applied to most of the things the drawer was going to hold. The drawer had Haakon's letter. It was going to have Anders's letter. The two letters in the drawer were going to be the physical fact of the two correspondences, and the physical fact of the two correspondences was going to be held in the drawer for the rest of Olav's life as the physical counterpart of the interior carrying that was also going to be held for the rest of his life.

He took the letter from Stockholm in his hand.

He folded it once, along the fold Anders had folded it along in Stockholm.

He stood up.

He went to the workbench.

He took hold of the small drawer under the underside of the bench, at the place he had known since he was nine.

He opened the drawer.

---

The drawer was what he had left.

There was the one folded letter in it — Haakon's — where he had laid it in the afternoon of the second Saturday of May, 1881, and where it had been for eighteen months and one week. It had not been moved. The wafer was still closed. The fold was still the fold Haakon had folded it into. The letter sat in the small empty drawer in the small empty way a letter sat in a drawer that had been made, originally, for tobacco.

Olav laid Anders's letter, folded once, beside Haakon's.

He did not, at the laying, arrange the letters.

He did not put one on top of the other.

He laid them side by side with about an inch between them, so that the drawer when he opened it — if he ever opened it again — would show the two letters as two separate objects, and the drawer would not be the drawer of one letter but of two.

He stood at the workbench with the drawer open for perhaps ten seconds.

He thought: *two letters. Sven is not a letter because Sven did not send a letter. The thing that happened with Sven is in me, and is not in the drawer, and will not be in any drawer because the thing that happened with Sven was not a thing that produced a physical object.*

He thought: *the drawer is for the physical objects.*

He thought: *the physical objects are the two letters. Haakon's and Anders's. Both short. Both formal. Both the closing sentences of the two men to whom the drawer belongs.*

He thought: *the drawer is holding.*

He thought: *it will hold.*

He pushed the drawer closed.

---

He sat on the stool at the end of the workbench for perhaps five minutes.

He did not, in the five minutes, feel what he had thought he might feel at the laying. He did not feel grief; he did not feel relief; he did not feel the particular unloading a man sometimes felt at the moment of finishing a long carrying. He felt instead a small flat quiet, of the kind his interior used for the moments in which a decision that had been made over a long time had finally been enacted. The decision had been enacted. The enactment had been the small physical act of opening the drawer, laying the letter, closing the drawer.

The enactment had not been dramatic.

It had not been large.

It had been the small economical motion of the same body that had at the age of twenty-three taught a steward his chronometer, held an earing in the Indian Ocean, written a sheet at a chart-table at Cape Town and burned it in a stove, sat at a corner table in Copenhagen and taken an address and burned the card, and stood at the altar of the Hestby church signing the banns in the modern spelling under a register that kept the old.

The body had done the work of the homecoming.

It had done it without asking him to feel anything he was not prepared to feel.

He took the Copenhagen duplicate off the workbench and slid it into the leather folder that sat in his chest under the loft bed at Lindøy — he would carry it across on the Wednesday — and he closed the chest. He took the plane back off the workbench. He finished the long edge of the jamb-board.

---

At noon his mother called from the kitchen door.

He went up to the kitchen.

His mother had the coffee on and the bread out. She did not ask about the letters. She set a cup in front of him. She said:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"Your father is at the net-house for the afternoon. He will be back at supper."

"Yes."

"I am going to ask you one question. It is not about either letter. It is the question I would have asked on any Tuesday you came in from the boat-shed at noon."

"Ask."

"Are you all right."

He drank his coffee.

He set the cup down.

"I am all right, Mother."

"Good. Then drink your coffee and eat the bread. I will bring out the ham."

She went to the larder.

He drank the coffee. He ate the bread. He had — he registered, at the first bite — a real appetite for the first time since the arrival at Bergen. He had, in the ten days of being home, been eating because his mother had been feeding him, but he had not entirely been hungry. At the Tuesday noon after the two letters he was hungry in the way a man was hungry after a long small piece of work had been finished in the morning.

His mother brought out the ham.

He ate the ham.

He thought, at the table: *the voyage is done. The banns are read three Sundays from now. The wedding is four months from this Tuesday. The two letters are in the drawer. The drawer is closed. The marriage I am going to have will begin at the altar in March with the name on the register in both spellings. The life I am going to have at Finnøy will continue in the years after with the two letters in the drawer in the boat-shed, and the drawer will be at about fifteen feet from my mother's kitchen table, and my mother's kitchen table will be at about two miles across a channel from Olava's kitchen table, and I will be at one of the two tables most days of the rest of my life, and the drawer will hold.*

He thought: *this is how the life is going to be.*

He thought: *it is a good life.*

He thought: *it is the life I chose.*

He asked his mother, at the second cup of coffee:

"Mother."

"Yes."

"How much ham is left."

"Enough for your father and me through the week. Eat what you want now. I can cure another leg next week."

"Then I will have another slice."

She cut another slice.

He ate it.

The kitchen at Finnøy on the Tuesday noon of the middle of November of 1882 held its two occupants in its ordinary small light, and the boat-shed below the kitchen held, in its small drawer at the underside of the workbench, the two physical letters that were the voyage's only surviving physical record, and the channel between Finnøy and Lindøy held the small smack that would, on the Wednesday morning, take Olav back to Olava in the coat at the jetty; and the Hestby church at the top of the island held in its register the banns in the old spelling, with Olav's and Olava's signatures in the new; and the world, at the cup of coffee and the second slice of ham, was the world Olav had, by the small daily discipline of a man who had at twenty-three closed his hand around the rice his life had offered him, arrived at on schedule and intact.
