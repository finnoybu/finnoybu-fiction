The morning watch called at four bells, two o'clock by the cabin clock, and Olav turned out of his hammock in the dark and groped for his boots and his coat on the hook above his chest and went up the companion to the deck.

The *Semiramis* was running. The wind had held from the southwest through the night, and the bark was making, by the feel of her under his boots, nine knots or a little better. The sea ran quiet under her. The Norwegian coast, when he reached the rail, was a darker dark to port, thin and low and a long way off. Skudesnes was behind them. The lamp at Lista light, if they were where he thought they were, would come up on the port bow sometime before dawn.

Sundberg was at the foot of the foremast. Sundberg did not, in the dark, greet him.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"You are early."

"Yes."

"Good."

That was the whole of it. The watch-list was read in silence by no one. The crew took their stations by knowing. Olav took his — at the starboard foretopsail braces — and stood, and listened to the bark, and waited for an order that did not, for some minutes, come.

He registered, at the rail in the dark, that he had not in five years stood on the deck of a ship on the first morning out and felt the thing he was feeling now. On the *Sigrid* he had been eighteen and cold and ignorant of everything. On the *Allegro* north to Vardøy he had been sick. On the *Diana* coming south he had been the cargo more than the crew. On the small coasters the year after his certificate he had been ashore as much as aboard. This morning he was twenty-three, the taller of the two of him that had come down the gangway yesterday, and the bark underfoot was a thousand tons of pitch-pine bound for the Hooghly, and the coast of Norway was falling behind and not coming back for eighteen months, and he was, he found, standing in it.

The mate's voice from the quarterdeck at last: "Up to the topgallant. Loose the main topgallant. Handsomely."

The men went up.

---

By six bells the gray had come up in the east and the Norwegian coast was a line under it. Sundberg had had them sweating up the topgallants, then the royals on the foremast — the main royals stayed furled until the wind decided what it was doing — and then a round of small corrections at the braces as the bark laid her course a point further south. By the time the first real light came on the rigging, Olav had done the work of an hour and a half, and the work had done what work at sea does for a man: it had put him in his body.

The *Semiramis* handled differently from the *Sigrid*. She was longer in the stride, heavier at the helm, slower to answer. The main yards — the course yards — were over eighty feet long; the royal yards were half the weight of the topgallants on the brig. A man climbing her went up further and found the sail smaller when he got there. The footropes were newer than the brig's had been. The jackstay was oak. The ship had been built in Arendal three years before the voyage she was now making, and she felt, under a hand, like a ship still in the first decade of her thinking.

He found, climbing down the topgallant shrouds with Sundberg after the bosun had inspected his gasket, that his own hands remembered a different footrope under them than the one they were touching. His hands remembered the *Sigrid*'s. The hands had, in five years, kept the old footrope's width and its lay and the particular brittle stiffness of the marline that had been on it. The *Semiramis*'s footrope was softer under his fingers and the yard was wider. The hands were learning the new yard while he was watching them do it. He stood for a moment on the main fife rail and looked at them, as if they were someone else's hands he had just been handed.

Sundberg, passing him, did not ask what he was looking at.

---

Breakfast was coffee and bread and a dried sausage at six bells of the morning watch, served out at the galley door by a man named Torgerson whom Olav had not yet spoken to. Torgerson was thirty and missing his left small finger at the second joint. He ladled; he did not talk. The coffee was better than the *Sigrid*'s had been — Captain Brun, Olav gathered, was not one of the particular ones about coffee, but the company provisioner at Bergen had done the work the captain did not insist on — and the bread was not yet bad.

Olav ate standing up against the galley wall. Around him eighteen men ate in the same position, in the small even quiet of a crew who did not yet know each other's voices. A Bergen man five down from Olav's hammock was ahead of him in the line; he passed back along the deck with his coffee and sat on a coil of rope at the windlass and drank without speaking to anyone. The Bergen man's hands, Olav noticed, were the hands of a man who had been to sea at least as long as Olav had. He did not let himself notice anything else.

At eight bells Captain Brun came up. He came up the way he had yesterday morning: clean coat, beard trimmed, the upright carriage of a man who had been an officer for thirty-five years and had never, in any of those years, allowed himself to slouch in front of a junior. He stood on the quarterdeck. He looked at the rigging. He looked at the wake. He looked at the compass. He nodded, once, to the mate.

Then he looked at Olav.

Olav held his eye for the beat it was offered. The captain did not move his face. He did not smile. He did not nod. He looked at Olav the way a man looks at a piece of gear he has himself checked the day before — to confirm that his check had not been in error, and to confirm it without speaking to it. After the beat he looked away. He went to the starboard rail and stood there with his glass.

Sundberg, passing Olav on his way forward, spoke without turning his head.

"He is satisfied."

"Yes."

"That is the most you will get from him on a day when nothing has gone wrong."

"I know."

"You knew it yesterday."

"Yes."

Sundberg went on forward.

---

The afternoon watch took Olav aloft. The mate sent him — it was not Sundberg's decision, though Sundberg was the one who pointed him at the shrouds — to check the main topgallant lifts. The wind had freshened through the forenoon and the mate was not satisfied with the angle of the yard, and a man aloft was what he wanted. Olav went.

He had not, until this afternoon, been at the main topgallant of a deep-water bark. He had been at the truck of a brig; he had been at the main topgallant of a coaster. The *Semiramis*'s main topgallant was a different thing. He came up the main shrouds to the top, climbed over the futtocks, went up the topmast shrouds to the crosstrees, climbed over those, went up the topgallant shrouds to the topgallant yard. He was, by then, perhaps a hundred feet above the deck. The bark was heeling perhaps eight degrees to starboard on the steady southwesterly. The horizon — seen from where he was — was much further away than the horizon he had been looking at from the deck. The coast of Norway was no longer visible from the deck at all; from the topgallant yard, as he turned his head at the lift he had come to check, he could still see a thin blue thing on the port quarter that might have been the Jæren hills. It might also have been a cloud. He did not look at it for long.

He checked the lift. It was tight. He hand-signaled the mate on the deck — thumb down, then flat palm — and the mate waved him down.

He did not go down immediately. He stood on the footrope for perhaps half a minute, with his belly on the yard and his arms hooked through the jackstay, and looked at the sea ahead.

The sea ahead was empty. It was the North Sea, south and west, running toward the English coast that he would pass without seeing. The wind was a steady shape on the water; the bark's bow wave was a small white line at the stem. A gull was following the bark, half a cable astern, lifting and falling with the wind. There was nothing else.

He thought, at the main topgallant yard of a bark he had signed for yesterday, that he had not — in five years at sea, and through one cough, and through Wilmington, and through Vardøy — ever stood at a yardarm and looked at open water that was going to be open water for the next eighteen months. The North Sea was open. The Atlantic past it would be open. The South Atlantic below that, the Indian Ocean after that, the Bay of Bengal at the end of it — they would all be open. There would be land on the port bow in Bergen in November of next year and very little between now and then.

He thought: *I am, for the first time in my life, on a voyage whose endpoint I have chosen.*

He thought: *the voyage I chose is eighteen months of open water.*

He thought, because the body insists on its small symmetries, *I have given Olava the wool-coat morning at the gangway, and I have given myself this.*

He stood another second on the footrope. Then he climbed down.

---

The mate was waiting at the foot of the shrouds.

"Hestby."

"Sir."

"Lift is tight."

"Yes, sir."

"Captain will want a word with you this afternoon."

"Yes, sir."

The mate looked at him for a moment longer than the exchange had required. He was a tall man with a Bergen accent, maybe thirty-five, with the sort of closed face a mate learns to wear by the end of his first year in the berth. His name was Aarseth. Olav had not, until this moment, heard another man say it aloud; he had read it on the articles.

"You checked both beckets at the yardarm before you put weight on the footrope."

"Yes, sir."

"Who taught you that."

"A boatswain on a brig, sir. Years ago."

"Good boatswain."

"Yes, sir."

Aarseth nodded once and turned away.

Olav, coiling the main topgallant buntline at the fife rail, did not let his face do anything. He thought, with the small clean precision a man thinks at sea when he does not want a thought to expand: *Haakon is in my hands and my feet and my watchfulness. Haakon is the reason I am alive to stand on this deck. The debt did not end when he stopped being a man I saw.*

He coiled the buntline. He hung it on the pin. He went forward.

---

The captain did not call for him that afternoon. Whatever the mate had said about Brun wanting a word had been either his own mistake or — more likely — Brun's decision, on further thought, not to need the word. The day ran out its watches. Olav went below at eight bells of the first dog-watch and ate the second meal of the day — the same coffee, a piece of salt beef, more bread — and came back on deck for the second dog and then the first night. At midnight his watch ended.

He lay in his hammock in the dark and the bark settled around him into the long roll she was going to have for the next fortnight at least, and he listened to the eighteen men in the forecastle breathe. The Bergen man — Sven, his name had come up at breakfast when Aarseth had read out the watch-list, and Olav had registered it and had not let himself register that he had registered it — had the hammock one port of his own. Sven's breathing was quiet; he had fallen asleep quickly, or he was pretending he had. Olav had not yet learned the difference between a sleeping Bergen man's breath and a pretending one's. He would, in time.

He thought of the small drawer in the workbench at Finnøy. He had stood at the workbench the evening before he had come down to Stavanger, and he had looked at the drawer without opening it. The letter had been in it for six weeks by then. He had not taken it out to read it again. He had not, also, lit the stove to burn it. It had been in the drawer. It was in the drawer now, with his father going in and out of the boat-shed above it, and his mother going about her work at the house, and the island going through its June at its usual pace, and the letter folded as it had been folded, sealed with the small dark wafer, addressed in the hand that did not write often.

He thought, in the forecastle of a bark making six knots under a topgallant breeze on the first night of a voyage he had chosen, that a letter in a drawer was a specific kind of thing. It was not a letter one had read recently. It was not a letter one had burned. It was a letter one had put somewhere where it would not, for the length of this voyage, be in his hand. The drawer had been the point. The drawer was the point now. The drawer was doing, at Finnøy, what Olava had done at the Stavanger quay with her hands on his face — holding the shape of a thing he could not carry in his hand at sea.

He closed his eyes.

He did not sleep for some time.

When sleep came it came the way it came to men on the first night of a long voyage — not as rest, but as a version of the work that continued in a different form. He dreamed, or half-dreamed, of the topgallant yard and the gull and the empty water ahead, and when the bell rang at four bells for the middle watch he heard it with the part of his mind that had learned to hear bells even when the body was sleeping. The watch was not his. He turned his face into the canvas. The bark rolled once, gently, and settled again.

He was, for the first time he could remember, exactly where he had meant to be.

He slept.
