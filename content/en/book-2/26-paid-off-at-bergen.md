The bark made Bergen on the first Saturday of October, 1882.

She came up Byfjorden on the morning tide with a Bergen pilot at the wheel and the city rising behind the mountains in the slant autumn light, and at two bells of the forenoon she was warped into a mooring at the customhouse quay that was the mooring she had lifted from — Olav understood as he stood at the foredeck with his duffel at his feet — in June of the year before. The mooring-bollards were the same bollards. The crane on the quay was the same crane. The office behind the crane, with *Det Bergenske Damskibsselskab* in gilt on the glass, was the same office.

He was the same man on the foredeck who had been there in June.

He was not, he understood as the gangway went over, the same man.

The difference was not, he thought in the half-second before he stepped onto the gangway, a difference the man on the quay watching him come down would be able to see. The man on the quay — who was his father — would see a twenty-three-year-old in a wool coat and a wool cap coming down the gangway of a bark that had been at sea for fifteen months and nineteen days, and the man on the quay would see him because the man on the quay was his father and had been at the quay since six that morning. The difference between the man going up in June and the man coming down in October would be a difference the father would understand without having to be told, because a father who had done his own East India at twenty-four in 1857 would know what an East India did to a man, and the father would not — Olav had known this for years, though he had not understood he had known it until this morning — require any account of the difference.

His father was at the quay.

His mother was at the quay.

Olava was not at the quay.

Her absence had been agreed at Christmas of '79. She had written him in her Cape Town letter — which he had read at the Cape two months ago — that she would be at Lindøy. She was not going to come to Bergen for the arrival. She was going to wait at Lindøy for him to come down on the coaster. The agreement had been that the homecoming at the Bergen customhouse belonged to his parents and that the homecoming at the Lindøy jetty belonged to her, and that the two homecomings were separate and would not be combined.

Olav had, on the bark, thought about whether he wished her to come.

He had thought about it at the Cape on the homeward and at the Channel and at Copenhagen, and he had decided, at each thinking, that he did not. The reason was the reason that had been the reason at Christmas of '79. The homecoming at Bergen was a ship's homecoming and was crowded and public and the business of a customhouse quay, and the homecoming at Lindøy was the wife-to-be's, and the keeping of the two apart was the small gift the geography allowed his particular kind of engagement.

He came down the gangway.

---

His father was at the foot of the gangway.

His father was — Olav noted in the half-second before the embrace, because even at twenty-three with fifteen months of deep water in his pocket he was the son who registered things about his father before he allowed himself the embrace — older than he had been in June. The hair at the temples was more grey. The coat was the same coat he had been wearing in June at the Stavanger quay. The hand he put out to Olav was the hand that had been put on Olav's neck at the quay in Stavanger in June, and the hand was, Olav registered, a little less strong at the grip.

Jens Hestby said:

"Olav."

"Father."

"You are thin."

"I have been thinner."

"You have been eating."

"I have been eating. The tea as promised, and the sausage, and the coconut when we had it."

"You will eat better at home."

"Yes."

His mother was at his father's shoulder. She said nothing at first. She put both her hands on the sides of his face — not as Olava had, at the cheekbones, but lower, at the jaw — and she held his face for perhaps six seconds. She did not, at the holding, make a sound. She let him go. She took his duffel from his shoulder without asking and set it at her feet and took his arm.

"Olav," she said.

"Mother."

"We are at the Bergen quay. We will be at the coaster for Stavanger on the Monday tide. You have two nights in Bergen. You will sleep at the rooming house in Øvregaten. Your father has the booking."

"Yes."

"The captain is expecting you at the company office at eleven. Then you are ours."

"Yes."

His mother, Olav understood in the moment of the arm-taking, had read the Cape Town letter and had read the Copenhagen-forwarded short letter and had read every letter through the seventeen months of the voyage, and had been managing — at Finnøy and in her correspondence with Olava and in the half-dozen letters she had herself sent to him and that he had read at Cape Town and at Calcutta and at Saint Helena — a particular small campaign of quiet care he had not, until this morning at the quay, allowed himself to imagine the shape of. His mother had been the campaign's general. His mother had decided, in June of '81, that the voyage was going to be survived, and she had done the work a mother did at Finnøy to make the deciding stand.

She was, at the quay, claiming her result.

He did not, at the quay, contest the claim.

He let her take his arm.

---

At eleven he was at the company office.

It was on the second floor of a stone building a hundred yards up from the customhouse — not the office he had written to in '80 for berths, which had been an agent's office on the lower floor, but the office of *Det Bergenske Damskibsselskab* itself, where the directors and the chief clerks did the company's actual business. Brun was in a chair at the chief clerk's desk. The chief clerk was a Bergen man of about fifty-five named Johansen with spectacles and a watch-chain. Petersen from Copenhagen — having come up on an earlier steamer — was in a second chair. A young clerk was at a side table with the articles.

They signed Olav off.

The signing-off took twenty minutes. The wage — common seaman's wage at the agreed rate across fifteen months and nineteen days — came to 1,042 kroner and some change. The company paid it out in cash in a linen pouch Olav had brought for the purpose. Johansen counted the coins twice, because Johansen was a Bergen company clerk of his generation and because counting twice was how that generation signed off a common seaman. He slid the pouch across the desk. Olav picked it up. He signed the articles at the bottom line.

"Hestby."

"Yes, sir."

"The captain has told me you will not take the next *Semiramis.*"

"No, sir."

"You understand the striking at Copenhagen."

"Yes, sir. The captain explained it at Stormgade."

"Good. Then I will not press the point. The company will note you as a common seaman and navigator who completed one East India voyage at the rank of common seaman on the bark *Semiramis* in 1881–82 and who took no further berth with the company at that time. The company will be willing, in future, to consider any application you make for a mate's berth on any of its ships, provided the application is made through its agents and not less than twelve months after this signing-off. Do you understand that condition."

"Yes, sir."

"Twelve months from this Saturday is the first Saturday of October, 1883. You may apply at any time on or after that date. The company's regard for a man who completes an eighteen-month voyage as a common seaman does not lapse because he declines the next berth. The regard waits."

"Thank you, sir."

"You are, Hestby, a man whom Brun tells me I should be watching. I am watching. That is all I will say at the desk today."

"Yes, sir."

Johansen stood. He put out his hand. Olav shook it.

Brun did not, at the signing-off, speak. He waited at the second chair while the formal business was completed. When Johansen had sat down again and Petersen had begun gathering the ledgers, Brun stood.

"Hestby."

"Sir."

"A word."

"Yes, sir."

---

They went out of the office onto the stone stair.

They stood on the landing. Through the window Olav could see the customhouse quay below them, the bark at her mooring, the gangway still rigged, Sundberg on the quarterdeck with his list, the other common seamen who had not yet been signed off queued at the customhouse gate.

Brun put his hands behind his back. He did not look at Olav when he spoke.

"Hestby. I will say three things to you on this landing and I will not repeat any of them."

"Yes, sir."

"First. You have been the navigator of the *Semiramis* for fifteen months and nineteen days. You have been the navigator in practice, not in contract. I have allowed this. I have written nothing of it in my captain's book, because writing it would have required me to alter your articles in ways that were not, in the circumstances of the voyage, necessary or appropriate. The fact, however, stands. You were the navigator. I am telling you so in private on a landing because I will not, in the company's books, have told you, and a man whose captain has let him do a trade without paying him for it is owed, at minimum, the telling of the fact by the captain."

"Thank you, sir."

"Second. You will be offered the next *Semiramis* berth in October of '83 if you apply. You will be offered it at a first mate's wage. I have told Johansen this morning, with Petersen as witness, that I will hold the reserve in my own name against your application, which means that the company cannot assign the berth before the first Saturday of October, 1883, to another man if you apply on or after that date. The holding of the reserve is a thing a captain does for perhaps one man in his career. It is not an obligation. It is my choice."

"Thank you, sir."

"Third. You are going to get married in the spring. You are going to stay ashore — or near-ashore — for some years after the wedding. You may take the *Semiramis* berth in October of '83, or you may not. You may sail with me again, on some later voyage, or you may not. The arithmetic of what you will take and when is a thing I cannot, at this landing, predict. I am not asking you to predict it. I am telling you, instead, the one thing a captain can tell a navigator at the end of a voyage which a navigator had a right to know. That thing is this."

He looked at Olav for the first time on the landing.

The grey eyes had, at the hour of the landing, the quality he had given the name *satisfied* to, on the North Sea morning after the first storm.

"You will be a master, Hestby. Before you are forty. You will have your own ship and you will sign your own articles and your own captain's book. You will be a good master. I have watched four men do what you did in the last fifteen months. Three of them became masters. The fourth died in the South Atlantic in '69. I do not say this to flatter you; I say it because it is the small fact a captain who has been to sea for forty years owes the one navigator in fifteen months who has done what you have done. It is what I was told, by my own first master, in 1846. It is what I am telling you in 1882. That is the line."

Olav did not, for a moment, speak.

He had understood, at the word *master,* that Brun had said the thing that was the single piece of praise Brun would ever give him and would never take back. He had understood — at the word, and at Brun's eye — that the thing was given because it was true, and that the givenness of it was a thing Olav was going to carry for the rest of his professional life whether he took the *Semiramis* berth or not.

He said, at the landing:

"Thank you, Captain."

Brun nodded, once.

"Go to your parents, Hestby. The coaster lifts on the Monday tide. I will not see you again unless you apply for the berth. If you apply, I will see you on the deck of the *Semiramis* in October of '83. If you do not, we are done."

"Yes, sir."

"Good voyage home."

"Good voyage on the next, sir."

"Yes."

Brun turned and went back into the office.

Olav stood on the landing for perhaps twenty seconds.

Then he went down the stairs.

---

At the customhouse gate his father and mother were waiting.

They took him along Strandgaten to the rooming house at Øvregaten. The rooming house was Scandinavian-clean and respectable, with a landlady of his mother's age who had served Jens Hestby coffee twice in the forty-eight hours of her waiting, and who had — Olav saw in the landlady's face at the door — decided, before she had met him, that she was going to approve of him because his mother had sat at her kitchen table and had drunk her coffee. He let her approve. He let his mother show him the room.

He put his duffel on the narrow bed.

His father, at the door of the room, said:

"Olav."

"Father."

"Your captain said two words to me this morning at the quay when he came down off the bark. He said *your son will be a master.* He then said he would not repeat the remark, and he went into the office. I am telling you he said it. I am not making a remark of my own about it. I am telling you because I do not think it is a thing a father keeps from a son when a captain has passed it to him."

"Thank you, Father."

"You are to come down to the dining room at one. We will have a meal. Your mother has been at the butcher."

"Yes."

"Put away your duffel. Wash. Come down."

He put away the duffel.

He washed at the basin.

He sat for perhaps a minute on the edge of the narrow bed with his hands on his knees, in the small Scandinavian rooming-house room on Øvregaten, with 1,042 kroner in a linen pouch under the bed and the captain's word on the landing in his head, and he did not, in the minute, do anything with either. He let both be. He let the minute be a minute in which a twenty-three-year-old Norwegian navigator was at the first Bergen rooming-house of the first homecoming of his first deep-water voyage, and he did not, at the minute, ask the minute to mean anything further.

He went down to the meal.

---

On the Monday morning he took the coaster south.

His parents came with him. His father had closed the boat-shed at Finnøy for ten days. His mother had closed the kitchen at Finnøy for ten days. The coaster took them down the coast in the October weather — grey, clear, cold — and they ate sandwiches his mother had brought from the rooming-house and drank coffee from a flask his father had brought from Bergen. The Norwegian coast unrolled. At six in the evening the coaster called at a small jetty on Rennesøy; at nine it called at Stavanger; at ten it called at Tau. At Tau they got off. They took the Lindøy smack across the channel in the last light.

The Lindøy jetty came up.

Olava was at the jetty.

She was in the same wool coat she had been in on the Stavanger quay in June of '81, sixteen months ago.

Marit was at her shoulder.

Her father, Captain Lindøy, was at the end of the jetty with his wooden hand in his pocket and his good hand at his hip.

Her mother was at the house with the lamp lit.

Olav came ashore.

He went to Olava.

She did not, this time, wait for him to cross. She crossed to him.

She put both her hands on his cheekbones.

She did not kiss him — not here, not on the jetty, not in front of his parents and her father and Marit and the boat-boys at the smack — because the kiss at the jetty was a thing she had decided, in June of '81 at the Stavanger quay, she would do only at the departures and not at the returns.

She held his face for the count of perhaps six breaths.

Then she let go.

"Olav."

"Olava."

"You are home."

"I am home."

She did not say anything else at the jetty.

She took his hand — his hand, this time, not his arm — and she walked him up the jetty toward the house. His parents walked behind them. Marit walked behind his parents. Her father walked at the jetty rail, watching, and then fell in at the rear.

At the door of the house his mother, the captain's wife, said:

"Olav."

"Mrs. Lindøy."

"You are staying here tonight."

"Thank you."

"In the loft room."

"Yes."

"Come in."

He went in.

---

In the loft room that night, after the supper, after the kitchen-table conversation in which the captain had said four sentences and Marit had said six and his mother had said two and Olava had said one and his father had said three and his mother-in-law-to-be had said the rest, he sat on the edge of the narrow bed in the loft with the window open onto the channel.

Olava had gone to her own room an hour ago.

Marit had gone to hers.

His parents were at the spare bed in the back. The captain was in the parlour with a small glass of aquavit and the newspaper. His mother-in-law was in the kitchen banking the fire.

The house at Lindøy in the October of 1882 was the quiet house Olav had carried in his head for fifteen months as the house he was coming home to.

He sat at the edge of the bed.

He took from the inside of his coat the small bound book. He opened it to the page he had ended the Saint Helena entry on. He turned to the next clean page. He did not, for a long moment, write.

Then he wrote:

> *Home. Lindøy. The loft room. Olava at the jetty in the wool coat. Both hands on my face for six breaths. No kiss. The kiss belongs to the departure, not the return. My father thinner than in June of '81. My mother older. The captain steady. Brun said at the landing at the company office that I will be a master before forty. He said it once. I am writing it once. The drawer in the boat-shed at Finnøy is a two-hour smack-crossing from here. I will be at it on Wednesday morning. I will not, on Wednesday morning, open it. I will stand at the workbench. I will know it is there. The drawer is where the drawer has been.*
>
> *The voyage is done.*
>
> *I am twenty-three years and seven months old.*
>
> *The spring wedding is five months away.*
>
> *I have come home.*

He blotted the page.

He closed the book.

He put it into the small bedside drawer of the loft room, where — he understood, in the putting — the book was going to live for the next five months, until the wedding, and where he was going to add to it, in the weeks between now and the wedding, the small sentences of an engaged man at home.

He blew out the candle.

He lay on his back in the loft bed.

He listened to the house.

The house was the house at Lindøy. The house had in it his parents in the back room, and Olava and Marit in theirs, and the captain in the parlour, and the captain's wife in the kitchen, and the small autumn October quiet of a Norwegian island house at ten in the evening.

Olava was a wall and a staircase and half a corridor away.

She would remain, tonight, a wall and a staircase and half a corridor away. The wedding, in March, was the night on which the wall and the staircase and the half a corridor would not be the distance between them. That was five months from now.

He could, he understood at the ceiling of the loft, wait five months.

He had waited sixteen.

Five was the smaller figure.

He slept.
