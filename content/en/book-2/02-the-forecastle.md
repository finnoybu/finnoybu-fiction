The watch-list was read out by Aarseth at seven bells of the morning watch on the second day.

It gave Olav the names he had been collecting from the ship's articles in pieces since he had signed them at Stavanger. There were eighteen common seamen. Five were Norwegian, three Swedish, two Danish, two German, one Finnish, one English, one a man from Åland whose speech was Swedish enough for Sundberg and Olav had not worked out what for Aarseth, one a man from Stjernerøy whose name was Kinn and whose face Olav thought he might have half-known as a boy on a Landa jetty. The Norwegians were the Stjernerøy man, a Finnøy-accented man from Rennesøy, a man from the northern Lofoten whose first name was Ivar, a Trondheim man on the mate's watch, and — Olav's watch, five hammocks down from his own in the larboard bank of the forecastle — Sven Haugen of Bergen.

The list had nothing more to say about Sven Haugen than his name, his age (twenty-four), his height (five foot ten), his birthplace (Bergen), and his home port. The articles had not asked whether he was married; articles did not. The list said *deck* where a man's trade might have said *sailmaker* or *carpenter* or *cook,* which meant Sven was what the *Semiramis* called him — a deckhand, nothing more, nothing less, a man paid seventy kroner a month to pull and coil and climb.

Olav did not, when the list was folded away and the watch dismissed to its work, know why he had registered Sven Haugen's name twice while the names of the other seventeen men had gone past him once. He only registered that he had registered it, and that the registering had happened without his permission.

He put the registering where he put the other things that happened without his permission.

---

The first chance came on the afternoon of the second day. Sundberg put them on re-coiling the starboard brace falls, four men at the work, Sven and Olav at the same coil. The wind had eased through the forenoon and the bark was making seven knots under topsails and topgallants, the English coast a hundred miles off the starboard beam and falling behind hour by hour. The coils had been made up quickly at Stavanger by men who had not expected to be graded on them; Sundberg wanted them redone before the bark got any further out to where a brace-fall coming loose at a critical moment would be more expensive than it was to-day.

Sven coiled the brig way — over-and-under, two-and-two, against the lay. Olav had coiled the brig way in his father's boat-shed at Finnøy as late as the previous month; he knew the look of a coil made by a brig man. He coiled the brig way beside him.

Sven noticed. He did not, for a while, say anything. They finished the first coil. They moved to the second.

"Brig man."

"Once."

"Not since?"

"Coastal. Two years. Then a school."

"A school."

"Navigation."

Sven considered this. The coil came through his hands warm from the sun. He did not, at the word *navigation,* speed up or slow down.

"And you are at the brace fall."

"Yes."

"By choice."

"Yes."

"You have a reason."

"I have."

"You are not going to tell me the reason at the second coil."

"No."

"Good. I would not have trusted the man who told me at the second coil."

He finished the coil. He set it on the pin. He moved to the third.

"Haugen."

"Yes."

"Bergen."

"Yes. Sandviken. If you know it."

"I have walked it twice."

"Then you know where I live."

"Where you live or where your people live?"

"Both. I have a wife there. I have a boy. The boy is three. The wife is Hanne. She is a cousin of half the Sandviken carpenters. I am telling you all of this because you will hear it in bits and pieces on this voyage and I would rather you had it in one piece from me."

Olav took the coil. He put it on the pin.

"Haugen. My name is Olav Hestby."

"I know."

"How."

"The mate read out the watch-list at seven bells. I was paying attention."

"I was paying attention and I did not catch half the names."

"I had been waiting for the Finnøy man. A boy I knew on the *Aase* in '74 was from Finnøy and he said Hestby was a church on his island."

"It is the church on my island."

"Then your name is the church."

"Yes."

Sven did not, at that, say anything. He moved to the fourth coil. The small dry recognition was still in his face. It was not unkind. It was the recognition of a man who had learned, in eight years at sea, that a man's name and a man's church and the lay of a man's coil all carried information about him that the articles did not.

They finished the braces.

---

At four bells of the afternoon dog-watch the steward rang for supper — salt beef, boiled potatoes, a cup of watery tea — and the eighteen men of Olav's watch ate in the shelter of the galley wall. Olav sat on a coil of rope with his plate on his knee. Sven came and sat beside him without asking. Not close; a hand's width of coil between them.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"Your first deep-water voyage."

"My first deep-water voyage."

"Mine is my fourth. I did Antwerp twice. I did Buenos Aires once. This is my first east of the Cape."

"Then we are each other's first in something."

"Yes."

They ate. The salt beef was salty. The potatoes were boiled to the point of giving up. The tea was the kind of tea a steward gives a crew when he does not expect to be thanked for it.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"There is a thing about a first Indian Ocean voyage I want to say to you, because I want to be the man who says it and not the mate or the bosun."

"Say it."

"A man's first Indian Ocean voyage is the voyage in which he either decides the sea is a life or decides the sea is a season. Most men do not know which until they are in it. I have watched it happen to four men I have known. Two of them made captains. Two of them went ashore when they got home and did not put a foot on a deck again. You cannot tell, from the first day out, which of the two a man is. The Indian Ocean tells him. It does not tell him kindly."

Olav considered this.

"Which were you."

"I was neither. I was the third kind. The kind that makes the voyage and comes home and goes back out because his wife and his boy and his rent in Sandviken require it. I am not a captain. I am not a man going ashore. I am a man who is doing what is in front of him because it pays seventy kroner a month and because I am good at it. That is a third kind. It is worth knowing it exists, because the mate and the bosun will not tell you about it."

"Thank you, Haugen."

"Sven."

"Sven."

"That is all I wanted to say today."

He ate the rest of his supper. When he was finished he handed his plate to the boy at the galley door and went forward to the windlass, where he leaned on the bitts and watched the wake until the bell went for his watch.

---

At eight bells the sun was still an hour above the horizon; they were south enough now that the long Norwegian June of Olav's childhood had flattened into a more ordinary midsummer dusk. The mate's watch took the deck. Olav's watch had until midnight.

He stood for some minutes at the forward rail. The coast that was not the Norwegian coast — the low dim shape of the Dutch-German bank, he thought, though he would not have sworn to it — was a long way off the port beam. The water ran past the stem in a kind of quiet he had not yet, on the *Semiramis*, learned the particular name for. On the *Sigrid* a quiet like this had been called the *good-boy quiet* by a Danish hand Olav had forgotten the name of; on the small coasters at home he had never been far enough off land for the sea to make this kind of quiet at all.

The quiet was the quiet of a bark in open water, on a fair wind, at dusk. A man who could name the kinds of quiet a ship made was, he thought, a man who was going to be a navigator whether he carried a certificate or not. He had been carrying the certificate now for fourteen months. He was, on the deck of a deep-water bark bound for Calcutta, being asked by the quiet itself whether he had earned it.

He did not, at the forward rail, answer the question.

He went below.

---

In the forecastle, in the dark, Olav listened to Sven breathe.

He did not, in the listening, let himself listen for anything but breath. He had decided, at the second coil of brace fall in the afternoon, that he was going to know Sven Haugen better than the articles knew him. He had decided, at the supper on the coil of rope, what that knowing was going to be: the knowing of a man whose wife and son were in Sandviken and whose hands coiled the brig way and whose face had the small dryness a man wears when he has been to sea long enough to recognize another man but not long enough to be surprised by anything.

The knowing was going to have a shape. The shape was the shape of a thing he had learned in 1876 to put in a drawer.

The drawer on the *Semiramis* was not the drawer at Finnøy. The drawer at Finnøy had a real letter in it, under a workbench, above the stove his father had not lit in June. The drawer on the *Semiramis* was the larger drawer — the interior one, the one Olava had named on Christmas morning the second winter before — and the larger drawer was, on the second day of an eighteen-month voyage, being reopened.

He had not expected it to be reopened on the second day.

He had expected the reopening to take some weeks; he had expected, at his desk in the loft in February, that whatever was going to happen to him on the *Semiramis* would happen slowly, in the long weeks of the Indian Ocean, at a pace he could register and store. The second day was not the long Indian Ocean. The second day was the Skagerrak. And yet, at a coil of brace fall with a Bergen man's hands moving in the brig way beside his own, the drawer had opened a crack.

He closed his eyes.

He was going to have to close it the way he had learned to close it, which was the way a man closes a drawer when the closing is the work of five years of practice. He closed it.

He registered, in the closing, that the closing had been easier than he had expected. He registered also that the closing was never the end of the story — that a drawer once opened a crack does not, in the life of a man who has learned how drawers work, ever be closed to the degree it was closed before.

He turned his face into the canvas.

He thought of Olava. He thought of her hand on the cuff of his coat at the Lindøy jetty. He thought of her face on the Stavanger quay in the noon light with Marit a step behind. He thought of the kiss. He thought of the count of three. He thought of the wool coat in the noon light. He thought: *I have brought her with me. She is in my face and my hands and my watchfulness. She is the reason I am on this bark and not on another. The drawer I have just half-opened will stay half-open or closed according to what I decide. The deciding is mine.*

The thought was not untrue.

It was also not the whole truth.

The whole truth was that he had, on the second day out, felt the drawer at the words *brig man* from a Bergen man coiling rope beside him, and the whole truth was that the feeling had not stopped at the words.

He did not, in the dark, allow himself to finish the thought.

He turned over.

The bark rolled under him. Five hammocks down Sven's breath was quiet and steady. The forecastle snored.

He slept.
