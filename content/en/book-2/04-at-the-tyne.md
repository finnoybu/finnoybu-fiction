The *Norden* made North Shields on the Tuesday evening tide in the second week of July.

Haakon had stood the harbour watch from the forepeak with a rope's end in his hand and the pilot's whistle in his ear, and when the hawsers went over at the coal staithe he set down the rope and took off his cap and wiped his face and stood for a moment looking at the town he had by now made twenty-three times. Shields in July was not a beautiful thing. The sky above the Tyne mouth was the colour of a coin that had been kept in a pocket for a long time, and the air — at least the air near the coal staithes — carried the particular mineral cough of a port that existed to move twenty thousand tons of Durham coal a week and was not, for anyone's benefit, going to pretend otherwise.

He put his cap back on.

The mate of the *Norden,* a man from Molde who had been Haakon's mate for four voyages now and was tolerable, came to the rail beside him.

"Two nights," the mate said.

"Two nights."

"The company man will want us turned round by Thursday morning."

"I will have her ready."

"I know."

The mate went forward. Haakon stayed at the rail for another minute, looking at the staithe and the loading crane and the two other coal ships tied up along the quay — a barque from Sunderland, a brigantine from Whitby, both English — and he worked out, the way he always worked out at the arrival of any port, what his time ashore was going to be.

A bath. Clean clothes from his chest. A meal at the sailors' ordinary the *Norden*'s crew always took at the corner of Clive Street, where the Norwegian consul's man came through on Wednesday nights and brought letters. A walk on the Fish Quay if the weather held. One pint of English beer at the Low Lights. Back to the *Norden* before midnight. Sleep in his berth aboard ship, because a boatswain who slept ashore in a coal port was a boatswain who had something to hide, and Haakon, at thirty, had nothing he was willing to hide ashore.

That was Wednesday.

Thursday morning they would turn the *Norden* round and take her back to Bergen with a load of Durham for the gasworks, and the coal would feed the Bergen lamps through the autumn, and Haakon would be on the Tyne again by the first week of September.

That was the life he had.

It was a small life. He knew it was a small life. He had known it at twenty-seven when he had signed for the *Norden* the first time, and he had known it at twenty-eight when he had renewed, and he had known it at twenty-nine when the company had offered him the boatswain's berth at a slightly better wage and he had taken it because the slightly-better wage was what his small life, at that point, had been in a position to accept. A man who had spent the second half of his twenties on a coal-coaster between the Tyne and Bergen was a man who had made certain choices — or, more exactly, had failed to make certain other choices — and Haakon had been honest with himself about those choices for as long as he could remember.

He went below to change out of the harbour-watch coat.

---

At the sailors' ordinary on Clive Street he ate mutton and potatoes with five other men off the *Norden* at the long table by the window, and he drank a cup of coffee that was hot if not honest, and at a quarter past eight a man came in through the street door with a canvas bag over his shoulder and the accent of Bergen's Sandviken on his first word.

"Norwegians?"

"Norwegians," said the mate, without looking up.

"Consul's bag."

The consul's man set the canvas bag on the end of the table. He was a thin man of about forty, with the stooped shoulders of a clerk who walked more than he sat, and he knew the routine. He untied the bag. He called names. He passed letters across the table to the men they were for. When the bag was empty he tied it shut and sat down opposite Haakon and ordered coffee.

Haakon had not, this voyage, had a letter. He had not expected one. He had no one at Hogganvik who wrote to him regularly — his mother wrote once in the spring and once at Christmas; his brothers did not write at all; his oldest brother's wife had written him once, four years ago, to tell him his father had died — and he had arranged his small life, by his late twenties, such that the absence of letters was not, on most voyages, a thing he registered as a loss.

The consul's man drank his coffee in three gulps. He looked at Haakon.

"Berg. The *Norden.*"

"Yes."

"You were with a Bergen man named Halvorsen on the *Kvikne* six years ago."

"I was."

"Halvorsen is in the port this week. He is on the *Aalesund* at the Low Lights. He asked me to tell any *Norden* man I saw that he was aboard. He has news from Bergen he wanted to pass to someone he trusted."

"About what."

"He did not say."

Haakon considered this.

He did not, as a rule, go looking for men he had sailed with six years ago in ports. He had learned, at twenty-five, that men one had sailed with were men one had known at a particular age in a particular place, and that what one had known of them then was not always what they had become since. Halvorsen had been a quiet Bergen hand, married, with the good sense not to drink more than two beers in any port. News from Bergen in July, passed from him to Haakon at the *Aalesund* on the Low Lights, was likely to be a death — a cousin of somebody's, a ship lost, a captain gone ashore with the gout. It was not going to be an adventure.

But he had nothing else to do this evening.

"I will walk down," he said.

"He will be glad of it."

The consul's man finished his coffee and went out into the street with his bag, and Haakon paid for his mutton and his coffee at the counter and walked down Clive Street toward the Low Lights.

---

The Low Lights was a tavern at the base of the lighthouse at North Shields that had been a sailors' place since at least 1830 and possibly since 1790, the kind of tavern where the floor sloped toward the door and the windows were small and the English the landlord spoke was a Tyneside that a Norwegian could half-follow if the Norwegian knew his nautical vocabulary in the other language. Haakon knew his. He came in, ordered a pint of the house's mild beer, asked after *the Bergen man off the* Aalesund, and was pointed to a corner table where a man of his own height and four years more was sitting with a pipe and a glass of the same beer.

"Halvorsen."

"Berg."

"Consul's man said you were here."

"I asked him to tell the *Norden.* I was not sure which of you would come."

"Only me this trip. Schulz is up in Newcastle on a carpentry errand. Olsen is asleep aboard."

"Then you."

"Yes."

Halvorsen gestured at the stool. Haakon sat. They drank for a minute without speaking — the way men who had sailed together once and had not seen each other in six years drink, without the pretence of being closer than they were — and then Halvorsen set his glass down and said, in the flat tone of a man giving a piece of information he has been carrying for a week:

"There is a bark out of Bergen called the *Semiramis.*"

"I know her."

"You know her?"

"I know of her. A Sars office man told me in March the ship was fitting for an East India voyage."

"Then you have probably already heard the other thing."

"What other thing."

"There is a man from Finnøy on her articles. Common seaman. Certified navigator."

Haakon did not, for some seconds, speak.

He had, at the word *Finnøy,* set his glass down on the table without meaning to. He had set it down because the hand that had been holding it had lost, for the length of a breath, its interest in the holding of the glass, and the glass had gone down the way a glass goes down when a hand releases it without falling. It had not fallen. It had not spilled. It had only, on the scuffed wood of the Low Lights corner table, come to rest with a small click the Tyneside landlord would not have registered and Halvorsen, across the table, did register.

Halvorsen did not, in the registering, say anything. He drank.

"Hestby," Haakon said at last.

"Hestby. Yes."

"Olav."

"Olav Hestby. They left Stavanger on the second Wednesday of June. The *Semiramis.* Captain Brun. Cargo of Norwegian timber to Calcutta, returning with rice and indigo. Voyage of eighteen months."

"How do you know."

"I was at the Sars office in Bergen three weeks ago to deliver a parcel. The clerk knew me. I asked after ships in for the Calcutta trade because my brother has been thinking of signing for one. The clerk told me the *Semiramis* had just lifted. He told me the articles. He read me two names. Hestby was the first. The second was a man named Haugen."

"And you heard *Hestby* and you remembered."

"I heard *Finnøy* and I remembered. You had written him, in May. I was at the Bergen customhouse the morning you dropped the letter at the Norwegian post, Berg. I did not, at the time, speak to you. I let the post be the post. But I saw the address."

Haakon picked up his glass.

He drank a long drink.

He set the glass down again, this time with the small deliberate care he had not used the first time.

"Why are you telling me this, Halvorsen."

Halvorsen considered the pipe. He tapped it against the edge of the table, knocking the ash into the tray. He did not look at Haakon.

"Because you wrote him once, and the writing looked to me like the writing of a man who had reason to write. Because I sailed with you on the *Kvikne* in '75 and I thought then that you were the kind of man the world was going to underpay for its entire length, and I did not, in the six years since, have occasion to change my mind. Because it is a small thing to pass to a man I sailed with that the man he wrote to is on an eighteen-month voyage to Calcutta. You can make of it whatever you like. I am not asking."

"No."

"I am not asking, Berg."

"I know."

They sat for another minute without speaking. The tavern filled around them — the Wednesday evening crowd of colliers and coasters' men and one old Dutch boatswain who was a fixture — and the noise rose and the landlord shouted some pleasantry in Tyneside that even Haakon's half-knowing did not catch, and Halvorsen at the end of the minute said, without raising his voice:

"I sail Friday."

"Good voyage."

"You."

"Thursday morning. Bergen."

"Then we will not see each other again this port."

"No."

"Well, then."

Halvorsen set down his pipe and his glass. He stood. He put out his hand. Haakon took it. Halvorsen's hand was the hand of a man who had been to sea for twenty years and had the particular cool weight such a hand had. They shook. Halvorsen put on his cap and went out into Clive Street.

Haakon sat alone at the corner table and finished his beer.

---

He walked up Clive Street toward the quay in the July dusk.

The street was the street he had walked twenty-three times. The lamps were being lit by the same lamplighter. The smell of the Tyne was the same particular mineral smell. The two English children who always played at the bottom of the street at this hour were playing there now with a hoop. Haakon walked past them without looking at them, and he turned the corner onto the quay and he stopped.

He stopped because he had not, in the six weeks since he had dropped the letter at the Bergen customhouse, thought about the letter with the part of himself that had actually written it. He had thought about it in the way he thought about things he had done and put away — as a small completed task, a thing filed, a thing that did not require further attention. The letter had been the gesture. The gesture had been made. The making had closed the small account that had opened when he had heard from the Sars office that Hestby was engaged.

He had not expected the account to open again.

The account had, at the Low Lights with Halvorsen's news, opened again.

He stood on the quay at the top of Clive Street in the July dusk and he worked out, the way he worked out everything, what the opening had changed.

It had changed this: the letter had been a gesture to a man he had understood to be at home. *Home* had been the word he had written the letter to. The man at home was a settled man — a navigator-certificate man, betrothed, presumably setting up a household with his girl from Lindøy. The gesture had been small and formal and final: *I send my best to you both.* It had been the kind of sentence a man wrote when he was closing a door on his own side of a matter he had never opened.

The man on the *Semiramis* was not a settled man. The man on the *Semiramis* was a man who had signed for eighteen months at sea five weeks before his wedding and had chosen, in doing so, a voyage that would take him through ports of a kind Haakon himself had worked for most of his life. The man on the *Semiramis* was not closing the door on his side of the matter. The man on the *Semiramis* was, whether he had admitted it to himself or not, leaving the door half-open on the walk out.

Haakon understood this, on the Shields quay in the July dusk, without needing to think it through in sentences. He understood it because he had, himself, walked out of the door of a man's life at twenty-one with the door half-open behind him, and he had understood even then what the half-open door meant, and he had been the one who had, six months later in Lübeck, closed the door from his side by walking off the ship without leaving a word. He had known, at twenty-one, what a half-open door was. He was not going, at thirty, to fail to recognize one.

He thought: *I could write a second letter.*

He thought: *I could write to Calcutta care of the Norwegian consul. I could write to Cape Town, care of the shipping agent. I could write to the captain of the* Semiramis *with a letter to be handed to the navigator-common-seaman. I could do any of these. None of them would be difficult.*

He thought: *what would I say.*

He walked to the end of the quay. He stood at the rail that faced down the Tyne toward the bar. The water was running out on the ebb. A Danish brig was being warped out into the stream by a tug; a pilot's punt was pulling back toward the shore. The lamps on the Fish Quay had come on.

He thought, at the rail: *I would say* I heard you are at sea again. *I would say* I write to say I heard. *I would say* I do not need an answer. *I would say* if a letter comes from you at Shields when this one reaches you, I will read it. *I would say* if no letter comes, I will not ask why.

He thought: *that is the second letter.*

He stood at the rail for a long time with the second letter in his head.

Then he thought, with the small flat clarity in which he thought most things:

*The first letter was the door closed from my side. The second letter would be the door opened from my side. I closed the door at twenty-one because I understood, at twenty-one, that the door was not a door I had the right to keep open. I have not, in the nine years since, changed my mind about whether I have the right.*

*The* Semiramis *has sailed. He has sailed. The voyage is eighteen months of open water. He has chosen it. The choosing was his. My writing a second letter to him while he is at sea is not the giving of a thing — it is the taking of a thing. It is taking, from a man on a voyage of his own choosing, the attention he has decided to spend on the voyage. I do not have the right to take that attention. I did not have it at twenty-one. I do not have it at thirty.*

*The second letter is the one I do not send.*

*The not-sending is the form.*

He stood at the rail another minute. He watched the Danish brig clear the bar. The tug let go and turned back upstream.

He went back to the *Norden.*

In his berth he took out his pencil and the small bound book he kept for the harbour-watch log and he opened it to a clean page and he wrote, across the top, *11 July 1881, North Shields,* and underneath, in the smaller careful hand he used for himself:

> *Heard at the Low Lights. The Semiramis lifted Stavanger 2nd week June. Hestby aboard. Calcutta-bound. 18 months.*

He looked at what he had written.

He wrote, underneath it, the single word he did not, as a rule, write in his logbook:

> *Good.*

He closed the book.

He turned down the lamp.

He lay on his back in the berth for some time without sleeping, and when he slept he slept the small broken sleep of a man who had made a decision in the July dusk on the quay of a port he had made twenty-three times, and who had decided, without knowing he had decided, that he was going to carry, for eighteen months, the knowledge that a man at sea east of the Cape of Good Hope was a man he still, at thirty, thought about at four bells of the morning watch when the rest of the world was asleep.

The knowledge was not, he understood, a letter.

But he had put it where he put letters.

The drawer was old.

The drawer had room.
