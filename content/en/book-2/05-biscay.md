They made the Bay of Biscay on the fifteenth day out of Stavanger.

Biscay in July was not the Biscay of the winter stories Olav had grown up with at the boat-shed at Finnøy. The Biscay his father had spoken of was a winter Biscay — a bay that had, in his father's phrase, *eaten more Norwegian captains than the Lofoten has herring* — and the summer Biscay, as the *Semiramis* came down into it under a steady nor'wester, was a more ordinary water. The sea was a long low green-gray roll. The wind held. The bark made nine knots hour after hour. The French coast was well off the port beam and not coming into sight. The captain, at eight bells of the forenoon watch on the first day of the crossing, set a course that would take them three hundred miles off the Iberian corner and into the trades by the third week.

The work on deck was the work of a bark running fair on a long leg. Sundberg had the watches holystoning. The carpenter had the topsail yards down on deck for a refit of the iron strops; the sailmaker had his needles out at a patch on the main course that had given at the cringle in the North Sea blow. The steward had the water-casks out of the hold to check for the first leak of the voyage, which he had not yet found and did not expect to find for another month. The *Semiramis* was, Olav understood, a ship settling into her voyage the way a body settles into the third day of a long walk — sore in the places it expected to be sore, finding the stride.

He had been, since the weather, going aloft more than the watch-list required of him. He had not, in the first two days after the storm, registered that he was doing it. By the fourth day he had registered it; by the fifth he had registered that Sundberg had registered it too and was not commenting. The bosun's not-commenting was the way the bosun's approval showed itself on the *Semiramis,* and Olav had accepted the not-commenting for what it was, which was the nearest thing to praise a man on the mate's watch was going to get out of a Karlskrona Swede of fifty.

It was on the eighth day of the Biscay crossing that Olav noticed the boy.

---

The boy was the youngest common seaman on the bark.

His name was Sevrin Odegaard; he was sixteen; he was from Ålesund; he was on the mate's watch, which meant Olav had been seeing him, on and off, at the changing of the watches and at the mess, for three weeks. The *Semiramis* was his first ship. He had done, before signing for her, one summer on a coast-smack out of Ålesund at fourteen and another at fifteen, and those summers had been his preparation for a thousand-ton deep-water bark bound for Calcutta. It was not, Olav understood on the day he noticed him, nearly enough preparation.

The boy was thin the way a boy who has not finished growing is thin. He was tall for his age and had not yet put on the chest that would make him, in two years or three, a man of ordinary size. His hands were large and pink and raw at the knuckles. His face had the kind of set seriousness that a boy adopts when he has worked out that serious is the face that is going to keep him out of trouble on a ship where he is the youngest. The face was, Olav registered, the face he himself had been wearing at eighteen on the *Sigrid.* It was a face that did not yet know what a face could do.

What Olav noticed, on the eighth day, was that the boy was afraid of the chronometer.

He did not, of course, know at first that the fear was of the chronometer. He only noticed, at six bells of the forenoon watch, that Sevrin Odegaard was standing at the quarterdeck rail with a slate in his hand and a pencil in the other, and that he was writing a number, and then rubbing it out with his thumb, and then writing it again, and then looking at the chronometer in the companionway and rubbing the number out a third time. The boy was on the watch that the mate had assigned to read the chronometer at the hourly check. It was a small duty. It was the kind of duty a mate gave a young man to teach him what a chronometer was. It was not a duty anyone, at six bells of the forenoon watch on the eighth day in Biscay, had any reason to be afraid of.

The boy was afraid of it.

Olav watched him from the main fife rail while he pretended to be coiling. The boy wrote. The boy rubbed out. The boy looked at the chronometer. The boy wrote. Aarseth was at the weather rail, his back to the companionway, not attending. The boy had perhaps another four minutes before the mate would turn and ask for the reading.

Olav crossed to the quarterdeck rail.

"Odegaard."

The boy looked up at him. The face was the serious face, but at the serious face there was now a thin line of sweat at the hairline that had not been there at eight bells.

"Hestby."

"You have not got it down yet."

"No."

"What does it read."

"I do not know whether I have read it right. The second hand is — I cannot tell whether I have it before or after the minute mark."

Olav considered this for a beat.

Then he climbed the companionway, looked at the chronometer himself, came back down, took the slate from the boy's hand without asking, wrote the reading on it in his own hand — hour, minute, second, the three numbers in the small clear column a navigator wrote them in — and handed the slate back.

"That is the reading."

"How do you know."

"I just took it. The second hand was at eleven past the minute mark when I looked. I noted the minute mark at the same moment I noted the second hand. The reading is the reading I wrote."

"But how did you know which to read first."

Olav understood, at the question, what the boy's difficulty was. The boy had been reading the chronometer the way one reads a clock — eye going to the hour first, then the minute, then the second — and in the half-second the eye took to move from hour to minute, the second hand had moved, and by the time he reached the second hand he could no longer be certain which minute it belonged to. He was reading a chronometer with the method for a kitchen clock. The method did not work. It was not going to work. It was not going to work at six bells of the forenoon watch on a bark running nine knots in Biscay, and it was not going to work at any bell on any ship anywhere for the rest of his life.

"Odegaard."

"Yes."

"You read a chronometer backwards. The second hand first. You fix the second hand by eye and you hold it in your head, and you move to the minute and read it and hold it, and the hour you read last because the hour is the slowest and the hour does not move between the two glances your eye has taken. If you read forwards you will always lose the second. If you read backwards you will catch it. You practice the backwards reading on the cabin clock when you are below and the clock is not important. By the time you read the chronometer at six bells tomorrow, the backwards reading will be the reading your eye does without your asking it."

The boy looked at him.

"I was not taught this at the signing."

"No. The mate does not teach it. The mate assumes a man will learn it."

"How did you learn it."

"A man at a navigation school in Stavanger told me in my first week. He told me after he had watched me do exactly what you are doing now. He told me because I had not asked and he had seen that I was not going to ask."

"He was a good man."

"He was Halvdan Nilson. He was a good man."

The boy took his slate. He looked at the column of numbers in Olav's hand.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"Will you be on the deck tomorrow morning when I read the chronometer at six bells."

"I will be forward."

"Will you come aft at five bells and stand by."

"Odegaard. If I come aft at five bells, the mate will see me come aft, and he will see that I am coming aft to stand by you, and he will understand that I have been teaching you. The mate does not mind that I have been teaching you. But the mate also does not reward the teaching. He assumes a man learns what he needs to learn from whom he needs to learn it, and the teaching is the teaching, and the teaching does not need to be seen. I will not come aft at five bells. You will read the chronometer at six bells the backwards way I have told you, and you will write the reading on the slate, and the reading will be right. If the reading is wrong you will come and find me on the forecastle at seven bells and we will read it again. It will not be wrong."

The boy nodded.

Aarseth turned at the weather rail. He did not, Olav noted, look at him and the boy; he looked at the water off the beam. Aarseth had registered. Aarseth was not attending. The teaching was the teaching; the mate did not, as Olav had told the boy, reward it, and the mate also did not interfere with it, and the mate's not-interfering was its own small piece of the *Semiramis*'s particular way.

Olav went forward.

---

At seven bells the next morning the boy did not come to find Olav on the forecastle.

Olav understood this the way Sundberg would have understood it — by the boy's absence — and he did not, for a moment, let himself feel anything about the absence. He went about his work. He coiled two lines. He went to the main fife rail for the eight bells muster. The boy was not at the muster; the boy was at the quarterdeck rail, at Aarseth's elbow, with his slate in his hand, and the slate had on it a column of three numbers in a hand that was not Olav's, and the mate was reading the column and nodding, and the mate, for the first time in Olav's observation of him, was smiling — a small, closed, mate's smile that did not reach the eyes any more than Brun's did, but that was, in its way, the mate's equivalent of the bosun's not-commenting.

Olav did not, at the muster, let his face do anything.

He did, however, understand that he had — at six bells of the forenoon watch on a Thursday in Biscay, with a slate and a chronometer and a sentence about reading backwards — crossed a line the shape of which he had not, before that moment, known he was going to cross.

The line was the line between the man being taught and the man teaching.

He had crossed it, he understood, at twenty-three. He had crossed it without a ceremony. He had crossed it in the same way Sars had crossed it when he had taught Olav to read a chart on the *Diana*, and the way Nilson had crossed it when he had told him about reading the chronometer backwards, and the way his father had crossed it when he had stood behind the eleven-year-old Olav in the boat-shed at Finnøy and put his hands on the eleven-year-old's hands at the caulking mallet and said *you will take the mallet and I will hold the iron and we will see whether you are a boy who can work a seam.*

He was, on the morning of the ninth day in Biscay, on the far side of that line from the side he had been on.

---

That evening at the windward rail, at the hour he had begun to think of as his evening hour — the quarter hour between the relief of the second dog-watch and the supper bell, when the crew thinned on deck and the captain was below and a man could have a few minutes at the rail without anyone expecting anything of him — Sven came and stood at the rail a foot to his left. They did not, for some minutes, speak. The Bay of Biscay ran under the bark in the long roll it had kept for a week now. The sky was the pink of an evening that was going to produce, by midnight, the kind of cloudless stars men who navigated by them thanked their saints for.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"You taught the boy."

"Yes."

"Aarseth is pleased."

"Yes."

"Brun will be pleased when Aarseth tells him. Brun will be pleased at supper tonight, when Aarseth tells him at the cabin table, and he will not mention it to you, and you will not know that he is pleased. But he will be."

"I know."

"You are on the chart already, Hestby. You are on the chart he keeps in his head of who is going to be on this bark in November of next year and in what berth. You are on that chart tonight at a place you were not on it at eight bells of this morning."

"Yes."

"I will ask the next question once, and then I will not ask it again."

"Ask."

"Are you going to take the mate's berth on the next *Semiramis* voyage when Brun offers it to you at Bergen in a year and a half."

Olav looked out at the sea.

He had not, in the six weeks since he had signed the *Semiramis* articles, asked himself the question in those words. He had asked himself the easier question — *will I have earned the berth* — and he had answered the easier question with the small private *yes* a man gave himself at twenty-three when he had decided what his body was going to do next. He had not asked the harder question. The harder question was the one Sven was asking. The harder question included Olava, and the drawer at Finnøy, and the wool coat on the Stavanger quay, and the eighteen-month gap between the *Semiramis* that was running tonight and the *Semiramis* that would lift again a year after Olav came home. The harder question was about the kind of man Olav had, in the last seven weeks, been discovering himself to be.

"No," he said.

Sven did not, for a moment, react.

Then, slowly, he nodded.

"Good."

"Why good."

"Because a man who said yes without having the woman ashore in the sentence would be a man I would not want to sail with twice. You did not, in the answer, have to work out whether to say her name. She was in the no. That is the kind of no a man lives by. That is enough for me."

He nodded at the sea once more. Then he turned from the rail and went forward to the supper.

Olav stayed at the rail another minute.

He did not, at the rail, think about the harder question any further than he had already thought about it. He had given the answer he would have given at eight bells of this morning and at eight bells of the morning he had signed the articles, and the giving of it aloud to Sven in the Biscay dusk had not changed the answer. It had only made the answer a thing that had now been said in the world, by his own mouth, to another man who had asked.

He thought: *I have said it. It is now a thing that has been said.*

He turned from the rail and went forward to his supper.
