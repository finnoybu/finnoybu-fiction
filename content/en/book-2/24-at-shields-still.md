Haakon made North Shields on the Friday evening tide in the second week of August, 1882.

It was the twenty-eighth time he had made Shields on the *Norden.* He had, in the thirteen months since the Shields call at which he had learned of the *Semiramis*'s departure, made the port twelve more times — six outbound with Bergen coal, six homeward with Durham coal — and in the thirteen months the particular private shape of his life had become what he had, at the Low Lights in July of '81, allowed himself to understand it was going to become for the foreseeable run of years: a life arranged around the twice-monthly crossings of the North Sea, around the small routine of his two nights at Shields and his three at Bergen, around the quiet upkeep of a boatswain's berth on a coal-coaster the owners had no reason to change and he had no reason to leave.

He was thirty-one.

He had, at thirty-one, the small private fact that he had carried for the thirteen months. The fact was a man at sea on a bark named the *Semiramis* who was — by any pilot's reckoning anywhere — in the last quarter of his voyage. He had carried the fact the way he had understood, at the July quay at Shields in '81, that he was going to carry it: without telling anyone, without writing anyone, without allowing the carrying to alter the visible shape of his life in any particular the *Norden*'s mate or the company clerks or the *Norden*'s captain or any of the men at the Low Lights would have noticed.

He had, in the thirteen months, succeeded at the carrying.

The carrying had cost him — he was honest about this, in the dry Hogganvik register he used for himself — four nights of broken sleep at Shields over the thirteen months and perhaps half a dozen at Bergen, and had cost him an afternoon in November when a Norwegian schooner at the Tyne mouth had, at a brief moment, been mistaken by him for a bark, and a morning in March when a clerk at the Bergen customhouse had, passing, said the word *Semiramis* in a conversation about an entirely different ship of the same name — there had been a steamer in a P&O fleet of that name, he had learned. These had been the costs. They had been, for a man of thirty-one who had been honest with himself about his own construction for a long time, small costs.

The carrying was, in the way the drawer in him handled such things, the ordinary operation of a drawer already accustomed to the weight of a similar letter.

---

At the Low Lights on the Saturday evening of this Shields he went in at his ordinary hour.

The tavern was the same. The landlord was the same. The Dutch boatswain who was a fixture was at his fixture corner. Haakon ordered his pint of the mild. He took it to a table near the window where the light from the street-lamp fell on the wood and gave a man reading a paper — which Haakon, this evening, was not — enough to read by.

He had been at the table perhaps twenty minutes when a Stavanger deckhand off a small Norwegian galeas called the *Tordenskjold* — a man Haakon knew by sight but not by name — came in with two friends and passed Haakon's table on the way to the bar. The Stavanger man, passing, nodded at Haakon.

"Berg."

"Stavanger."

"We are in two days."

"Crossing good?"

"Fair."

The Stavanger man had made the bar and had his pint when he came back past Haakon's table and stopped.

"Berg."

"Yes."

"A thing you may want to know."

"What thing."

"A friend of mine came off the Dover pilot-cutter yesterday and brought a sheet of the ships in the Channel over the last fortnight. He knows I read the lists. I read his list this morning at the Stavanger. The bark *Semiramis,* Calcutta timber-trade, northbound, passed Beachy Head Thursday of last week. She will be north of the Dogger by now on this wind. Bergen by the end of the month or thereabouts."

Haakon did not, for a beat, answer.

"How do you know I care about the *Semiramis.*"

"Halvorsen told me in July of last year that you did. I was at the Low Lights the evening he came to see you. I was three tables away. I did not, at the time, put it together with a name. Halvorsen told me later, at Buenos Aires in the autumn, when I was on the *Harald* and he was on the *Aalesund,* that the *Norden* boatswain had had a look on his face at the word *Semiramis* that he had never seen on the *Norden* boatswain's face at any word in ten years of knowing him. He said — Halvorsen — that if I ever saw you in any port and happened to have news of the *Semiramis* I should pass it."

"Halvorsen said that."

"He said that. I am passing it."

"Thank you."

"That is all, Berg. Good evening."

He went back to his two friends at the bar.

Haakon sat at the table with the Stavanger man's news.

---

He did not, for some minutes, lift his pint.

He thought about the news in the flat register he used for most things. *Semiramis* past Beachy Head on a Thursday last week. North of the Dogger by now. Bergen by end of August. The voyage Hestby had signed for in June of '81 had, by the arithmetic of a Stavanger deckhand's hand-delivered list, another two to three weeks to run. Bergen at the end of the month meant the *Semiramis* would be at her mooring at the Bergen customhouse perhaps six days before the *Norden*'s next Bergen call. Haakon would be in Bergen — by the *Norden*'s schedule — at the beginning of September. He would be in a room in Sandviken a half-mile from the customhouse quay on the days the bark lay there.

The geography was the geography it had been for thirteen months.

The proximity was the proximity it had been for thirteen months.

What had changed, in the Stavanger deckhand's fifteen seconds at the table, was only the time. The proximity was now — by an end-of-the-month arrival at Bergen and a first-week-of-September arrival of the *Norden* — going to overlap, for six days, in the same port.

He thought about the overlap.

---

He thought first about going to the bark.

He thought about it in pieces. A man going to a bark in Bergen on the third day of her unloading, at the customhouse quay, would be seen by the customhouse clerks and by the bark's own watch and by whoever happened to be on the quay that morning. He could go as a boatswain off the *Norden* making a courtesy call on another Norwegian ship at her quay — there was nothing irregular about such a call; boatswains made courtesy calls — and he could ask at the rail for the navigator. The navigator would come up from below. They would shake hands at the rail. They would speak for perhaps two minutes. The courtesy call would, from the customhouse clerks' and the bark's watch's point of view, be the ordinary sort of call.

Haakon thought about the two minutes.

He thought about what the two minutes would be.

He did not, in the thinking, arrive at a two minutes he could bear.

The problem was not that the two minutes would be awkward or uncomfortable; those he had borne more than once in his life. The problem was that the two minutes would be a thing that had *happened* — that had existed in the world with other men as witnesses — and that the having-happened of it would be a fact in Hestby's life, and in Hestby's forthcoming marriage, that Hestby had not asked for.

Hestby had not asked for a courtesy call.

Hestby had, in May of '81, sent three lines from Shields and had said *I send my best to you both,* and Haakon had understood those five words — at the time and since — to have been the closing of an account between them. Hestby had closed his side. Haakon had not, in a year and a month, reopened his. He was not, at thirty-one at the Low Lights with a Stavanger deckhand's news in his head, going to reopen it now.

The two minutes at the rail would be an opening.

The opening would be — by his own careful calibration of what a man who had known what he had known at the Wilmington salt-hold and what Haakon had known on the *Sigrid* in the lee of the galley had been carrying for six years — the kind of opening a newly engaged man arriving home from an eighteen-month voyage did not need to be made to handle.

Hestby had enough to handle at the customhouse quay without the additional courtesy call of a boatswain he had last seen on a brig in 1876.

He was not, at the quay in September, going to go.

---

He thought then about writing a letter.

He thought about writing it that night at the *Norden,* sealing it, and leaving it at the Bergen customhouse on the Friday for the bark to pick up at her arrival. The letter would be shorter than the May-'81 letter. The letter would say, in its entirety: *Hestby — your bark is northbound. I learned it at Shields. I am at Bergen the week after your arrival and will not, as a matter of my own discipline, come to the quay. I welcome you home. Yours, Berg.*

He thought about that letter.

He thought about it longer than he had thought about the quay.

The letter, unlike the quay, would not require a response from Hestby. The letter, unlike the quay, would not be a thing that had happened with witnesses. The letter would be four lines in a boatswain's hand left at a customhouse and picked up by a navigator coming ashore in Bergen, and the navigator would read it once, and the navigator would — Haakon had no doubt — put it into whatever the navigator's equivalent was of Haakon's own tin box under his hammock, and the letter would have, as its first and last work, the transmitting of the information that Haakon had not, in the hour he had been able to make the quay, come.

The letter would be, in other words, a note of explanation for a non-visit.

And the non-visit, Haakon understood at the Low Lights window-table, was not a thing that required explanation.

He had written the May-'81 letter because a man he had known at twenty-five whom he now heard was engaged was a man he had been for some months unable to think of without the small fact of having written nothing, and the writing had closed that fact. He had, at thirteen months' distance, no such fact on him. There was no unwritten letter that was making his silence heavy. The silence was, on the contrary, the exact instrument his thirteen months of carrying had required. Writing a letter to explain the silence was the kind of gesture that would draw attention to the silence and, in drawing attention, change the silence's shape.

A silence that had held for thirteen months was a silence that was its own statement.

The correct reply to a voyage of eighteen months was to let the voyage end at its own quay without a boatswain's letter lying at the customhouse office to meet it.

He was not going to write.

---

He thought, at the window-table with the decision made, one more thing.

He thought about what Hestby would, in the not-receiving of a letter and the not-seeing of a figure at the quay, understand.

Hestby was, by any reading of the May-'81 letter and the thirteen months since, not a man who required gestures. Hestby would, arriving at the Bergen customhouse in early September, look at the quay as a man arriving home always looked at a quay — for his parents, for his fiancée if she were there, for the faces of the family who had come to meet him — and he would not, in the looking, be expecting to see Haakon. Haakon had not, in May of '81, said he would be there. Haakon had not, in the year since, sent a second letter that might have said he would be there. The quay would be the quay of Hestby's family and of Hestby's fiancée, if she had come. The absence of Haakon at the quay would not, to Hestby, be an absence; it would simply be the quay's ordinary composition, which did not include a coal-coaster boatswain Hestby had not asked to have there.

Haakon did not need to write a letter to tell Hestby what Hestby was already going to know.

Hestby was going to know it by the shape of the quay.

The shape of the quay was going to speak for him.

---

He lifted his pint.

He drank off the rest of it.

He sat at the table for another quarter hour without ordering a second. He watched the men at the bar. The Dutch boatswain in his fixture corner was doing what he always did, which was drinking a single pint very slowly for the length of the evening while reading a Dutch-language newspaper three days old. The landlord was wiping glasses. The Stavanger deckhand and his two friends had got their second pints and were laughing about something the landlord had said. The Low Lights was, in the sum of its parts, the tavern it was every Saturday Haakon had been there in five years.

He paid for his pint.

He went out.

He walked up Clive Street in the August dusk. The lamplighter was at the corner at the top of the street. The English children at the bottom were at their string game. He turned onto the quay.

He stood at the rail that faced down the Tyne toward the bar for perhaps five minutes.

He thought, at the rail: *Hestby will be in Bergen in three weeks. I will be in Bergen in four. Our ports will overlap for six days. I will not go to the quay. I will not write a letter. I will, in the six days, stay at my quarters in Sandviken and I will do the work I do at Bergen and I will not, once, walk past the customhouse. If the* Semiramis *is still at her mooring at the end of the six days I will, on the Wednesday of my turnaround, take the Bergen train for an overnight to a sister at Voss I have not visited in eight months. I will return to the* Norden *on the Thursday morning at the Bergen quay via the other side of the customhouse. The bark will be gone by then. She will not be gone forever; Brun will lift her for the next East India in September of next year and she will, for the rest of my life, come and go at Bergen at intervals. Each time she comes I will be at sea or at Shields or at Voss or at some other place that is not the Bergen customhouse. I will arrange the rest of my life — for as long as the* Semiramis *is a ship the company sails — around the not-being at the Bergen customhouse when she is in.*

He thought: *that is the arithmetic from here.*

He thought: *it is an arithmetic I am capable of.*

He turned from the rail.

---

At the *Norden* he did not, as he had in July of '81, write anything in the small harbor-log he kept.

He did not need to.

The log was for thoughts a man felt the need to set down in his own hand because otherwise he would forget them, and what he had decided at the rail was not a thing he was going to forget. The decision was the decision; the geography of the rest of his life was — by the simple small logistical means of arranging to be elsewhere every time the *Semiramis* was at Bergen — going to be built from the decision without requiring further decisions along the way.

He turned down the lamp.

He lay on his back in his berth.

He looked up at the beams.

He slept, eventually, a sleep that was not the broken sleep of the thirteen months of carrying but a different sleep — the sleep of a man who had, at a Saturday evening in August at the Low Lights, learned the time of a homecoming and had, in the space of a pint and a walk up Clive Street, arranged his own position with respect to that homecoming for the rest of his life.

The *Norden* lifted Shields on the Tuesday morning tide.

Haakon was at his boatswain's place. The Tyne went down under her bow. The crossing was ordinary. The *Norden* made Bergen on the Friday evening.

The *Semiramis* did not make Bergen for another eight days after that — Brun had held her at Copenhagen for orders longer than the schedule had allowed for, which the Bergen customhouse was told by a Copenhagen cable when the *Norden* was at her Bergen quay — and Haakon, in the interim, had taken the Voss train on the Wednesday as he had planned and had walked on the Thursday morning back to the *Norden* along the back side of the customhouse, on the Strandgate, on the inland side of the harbor, from which he had not, for the whole of his walk, been able to see the customhouse quay or any ship tied at it.

He boarded the *Norden* at the stern gangway.

She lifted Bergen on the Thursday evening tide for her twenty-ninth Shields run.

At no point in the eight days at Bergen had Haakon seen the bark *Semiramis* of Bergen, and at no point had the bark *Semiramis* of Bergen seen him.

The not-seeing was, he understood on the quarterdeck of the *Norden* as she cleared the outer light for the Tyne, the form the thirteen months of silence had taken at the moment the silence had been tested. The silence had held. The form had been correct.

He went about his watch.

He did not, at the watch or afterward in the forecastle or in his berth or in the small private minutes he allowed himself each evening, think of Hestby again that crossing.

He understood, without needing to say so to himself, that he would think of him again in his life.

Just not that crossing.
