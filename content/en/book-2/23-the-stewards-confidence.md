Torgerson came to the chart-table of the captain's cabin on the Thursday evening of the second week out of the Cape.

It was their usual evening. The steward's galley lesson on the homeward had, since the first week, migrated into the captain's cabin by Brun's permission — the galley bench had been, at night in the South Atlantic, the warmest place on the bark because of the stove, and Torgerson had been unwilling to teach a chronometer on a surface that was also the surface at which he gutted the next morning's fish — and the chart-table at the captain's cabin had become, with Brun's quiet approval, the place the two of them met at four bells of the first night-watch on Thursdays.

On this Thursday evening Torgerson came in with the slate under his arm and, under the slate, a second piece of paper Olav had not before seen him bring.

"Hestby."

"Torgerson."

"I have the week's sights."

"Good. Show me."

They worked the week's sights. Torgerson had, on each day of the week, taken a noon sight at the galley door with the old sextant Brun had lent him — the sextant Brun used on the bark's second quadrant, kept in the chart-cabinet, of the kind a master mariner kept as his second instrument against the loss of his first — and had compared each sight to Olav's and to Brun's and had noted, in the small careful column at the right-hand of the slate, the errors. The errors had been, this week, smaller than the errors of the first week out of the Cape. The largest had been forty seconds of arc. The smallest had been zero, which was to say that on the Tuesday Torgerson had taken a sight identical to Brun's.

Olav nodded at the column.

"You are getting the reading."

"I have been getting the reading for three weeks."

"You are getting it consistently."

"Consistent is the new piece."

"Yes. What is the paper."

Torgerson took the paper from under the slate. He laid it on the chart-table.

It was not, Olav saw at once, a ship's paper. It was not a chart-correction or a sight-log or a page from the mate's book. It was a printed sheet — a thin sheet of the kind the advertisements at the back of the Norwegian shipping papers at Stavanger came on — folded once, a little yellowed at the edges, bearing at the top the name of a firm Olav did not know and below the firm's name a heading: *For Sale, Half-share in the Coast-smack* Hjemmet, *Arendal to Kragerø Run.*

Torgerson did not, immediately, say anything.

He was waiting, Olav understood, for the sheet to be read.

Olav read it. The sheet advertised a half-share in a small Arendal coastal smack of about twenty tons, currently making the Arendal–Kragerø run with timber and small goods; the smack was five years old; she had two hands on her; the asking was four thousand kroner; the seller was the current majority owner, a man named Egenæs, who was looking to retire from small-boat owning and return to his farm. The sheet had been printed in Arendal in April of the previous year.

Olav looked up.

"Torgerson."

"Yes."

"Where did you get this."

"A Danish clerk at the Cape Colonial Bank put it into my hand on the Friday at the cargo office. He is a nephew of Egenæs. He knew I was an Arendal man. He said his uncle had been talking about the advertisement for the last year and had found no buyer he cared for, and that his uncle would renew the advertisement for a further year at the end of the month. He said that a Scandinavian steward who had been saving his wages for ten years could — if he wanted — go to Arendal at the end of the next year and present himself to Egenæs, and that Egenæs would consider the offer."

"He gave you this at the bank."

"He gave me this at the bank. He said the uncle had told him to pass the sheet to any honest Scandinavian he met at the Cape Colonial who might be looking."

"Are you, Torgerson, looking."

Torgerson considered the sheet on the chart-table.

He did not answer at once.

"I have saved," he said, at last, "four thousand two hundred kroner in ten years of stewarding. The saving has been the work of a man who does not go to the lower streets of ports. I have had no wife. I have had no rent; I have lived aboard and on the company's advance at ashore-seasons. The saving is in a Bergen bank in the ledger of a cashier I have, at three different occasions across the ten years, asked for my balance. I have not, until last Friday, had a thing I wanted to do with the saving. I was saving, I had told myself, against a time at which a thing I wanted to do would present itself. The thing has, perhaps, presented itself at the Cape Colonial."

"A coastal smack."

"A half-share. The other share is the son of Egenæs. The son is a steady man. I have made enquiries about him; my Danish clerk at the Cape has confirmed the son is steady. The boat is sound. The route is a route an Arendal man knows. I have — I understand, reading the sheet a second time — the option of ending my fifteen years at sea a year from now and becoming a man who goes between Arendal and Kragerø and sees his own boat from his own window on the mornings he is at home."

"That is the thing you have come to tell me."

"That is the thing I have come to tell you."

Olav did not, for a moment, speak.

---

He looked at the sheet on the chart-table. He looked at Torgerson's face across from him. Torgerson's face, at the lamp over the table, was the face it had been in the first week — closed, careful, the steward's face — and yet at the mouth was the small thing Torgerson's mouth did when it had almost, but not quite, smiled: the single quarter-inch of rise at the left corner that had also appeared on the day Olav had insisted on the two-and-two trade of the tobacco.

The rise was the steward's equivalent of the kind of thing another man would have written a letter of.

Olav registered it.

He registered, also, that Torgerson had come to him with the advertisement because Torgerson had understood, in the three months of Thursdays at the chart-table and in the seven months of the voyage before that, that Olav was the man on the bark Torgerson was going to tell this to. Torgerson was not going to tell it to Brun at the quarterdeck, because Brun was the captain and the captain did not wish to be told a steward was leaving until the articles of the next voyage required the telling. Torgerson was not going to tell it to Sundberg, because Sundberg was the bosun and would take the information on the bosun's terms, which were useful but unsentimental. Torgerson was going to tell it to Olav because Olav had, over three months of Thursdays, become the man on the bark whose interest in Torgerson had been the interest of a man who considered the steward's life a serious matter.

Olav understood this.

He understood, also, that the advertisement had arrived in the same half-month at which he himself had — at the chart-table on the Thursday night eight days ago — burned a sheet in a stove in this same cabin.

He understood that the two events were not unrelated.

---

"Torgerson."

"Yes."

"You have decided."

"I have decided that I will go to Arendal at the end of the next voyage and call on Egenæs. Whether the offer is still good at that time I cannot know. Whether Egenæs accepts me as a partner at that time I cannot know. What I can know is that I am going to Arendal. I am also going to give notice, at Bergen in November of this year, that the *Semiramis* is my last voyage. I will sail out the rest of 1882 and I will sign off at Bergen and I will go to Arendal in December. This is the decision."

"Has the captain been told."

"Not yet. I will tell him at the end of this homeward. I am telling you tonight because the decision is the kind of decision a man wants one other man to know about before the captain does."

"Yes."

"You are that man."

"Yes."

They did not, for a moment, speak.

Olav looked at the sheet on the chart-table. He looked at the column of sights on the slate — Torgerson's week of noon sights, four of them within ten seconds of Brun's, one of them identical. He looked at Torgerson's hands on the table, which were the hands of a steward who had been gutting fish and ladling coffee for ten years and which were, at the advertisement, already partly the hands of an Arendal smack-owner.

He thought, at the chart-table: *I have taught a man his chronometer and his sight, and the teaching has been the thing that, along with a Danish clerk's chance gesture at a bank at the Cape, has taken the man out of stewarding and back to Arendal. The teaching has not been the only thing; the saving was the condition. But the teaching has, at some level, been the piece that allowed him to imagine the half-share as a thing he could run, because a man who owns half of a small smack needs to be able to take his own sights on a run up the Skaggerak, and a steward who could not take his own sights would have been a half-owner who had to depend on his son-in-law's son for the navigation. I have given him, at the Thursdays, the independence a man needs to own a half-share of a smack.*

He thought: *I have been giving away the things I was given. I have been doing it on this voyage since Biscay. The giving has been — I am only now understanding — the thing that, in the aggregate, is going to be the shape of my own coming-ashore when it comes.*

He thought: *it is not coming at Bergen in November. It is not coming at the spring wedding. It is, perhaps, coming at a port down the road — five years, ten years — when I have taught enough to have given the bark everything I was given, and when the ledger in me has, by the daily small arithmetic of giving, run down to the amount a man needs to be able to come ashore without feeling that he is withdrawing before he has repaid.*

He thought: *the decision is not mine to make on a Thursday night in the South Atlantic. The decision is a decision I am going to make over a decade of Thursdays. Torgerson has made his over ten years. I will make mine over ten more.*

---

He said aloud, after the minute:

"Torgerson."

"Yes."

"You are going to be a good partner to Egenæs. You are going to be a better partner than Egenæs thinks you are going to be. You are going to make the Arendal–Kragerø run with a small certain competence, and you are going to eat your wife's cooking when you have a wife in Arendal, and you are going to own a share of your own smack for the rest of your life. That is what I see, at the chart-table tonight, in the paper."

"Thank you."

"There is a thing I want to ask you."

"Ask."

"Will you, at Arendal, write to me once a year at Christmas until one of the two of us is dead. I would like the annual letter from an Arendal smack-man who was once my steward on a bark."

Torgerson looked at him for a beat.

He did not, at the beat, say anything.

Then he said, in the small quiet voice he had used for the telling of the decision:

"I will."

"Good."

"I have not written a Christmas letter in twelve years."

"You will write this one."

"Yes."

"Good. We will leave it there."

Torgerson folded the advertisement. He put it inside his shirt. He picked up the slate. He turned toward the cabin door.

He stopped at the door.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"Will you come ashore one day."

Olav considered the question.

"I will come ashore one day, Torgerson."

"At Finnøy."

"At Finnøy."

"With the girl at Lindøy."

"With the girl at Lindøy."

"That is the answer I expected."

"It is also the answer I expect to give in ten years."

"Yes. That is the difference between a man who is going to come ashore in ten years and a man who is going to come ashore never. The first answer is the same as the second, but the first has a *in ten years* in the back of the mouth that the second does not."

"Yes."

"I have the *ten years* in the back of my own mouth tonight."

"I know."

"Good."

He went out of the cabin.

---

Olav stayed at the chart-table for another fifteen minutes.

He did not, in the fifteen minutes, work on any paper. He looked at the lamp; he looked at the stove; he looked at the chart of the South Atlantic under his hand. The chart had on it the bark's estimated position at noon — a small pencilled cross on the fair-copy at about thirty-six degrees south and nine degrees east — and the pencilled cross was, tonight, the mark of a man who had taught a steward enough navigation to allow the steward to buy a half-share in an Arendal smack, and the pencilled cross was also the mark of a man who had understood, at the Thursday chart-table in the captain's cabin on the homeward leg of his first East India voyage, that his own coming-ashore was a decision he had just given himself permission to be making for the next ten years.

He had, until tonight, thought of the coming-ashore as a single event at a future date.

He understood now that it would not be a single event. It would be a gradient. It would start, at Bergen in November of this year, with his refusal of the next *Semiramis* berth. It would continue with the spring wedding at Hestby. It would go on with whatever voyage he took after the wedding, and with whatever voyage he took after that, and would end — at some year in the 1890s — with the last berth he signed for, and with the settling at Finnøy in a household he and Olava would by then have made, and with whatever small boat he would, at thirty-eight or forty-two, have bought a share of at the Stavanger harbour.

The gradient would be the shape of the rest of his life at sea.

He had not, at twenty-three, understood that a man's life at sea could be a gradient. He had thought it would be the line his father had had — fifteen years in, one day out, never again — because his father had chosen to leave the sea in a single hard moment rather than in a long slope. His father's leaving had been an event. His own, he understood at the chart-table tonight, was going to be a different shape entirely.

He blew out the lamp.

He went out of the cabin.

---

On the quarterdeck Aarseth the mate looked up at him.

"Long chart-work, Hestby."

"A long lesson, sir."

"The steward's."

"Yes, sir."

"The steward is a quick learner."

"Quicker than I had expected."

"Hmm."

Aarseth did not pursue it. The mate had, in the eleven months of the voyage, come to respect Olav without ever giving the impression that the respecting had been a thing that required him to change his manner; he was the same closed tall Bergen man he had been at the gangway in June of the previous year, and the respecting lived, such as it was, entirely in the not-interfering-with-Olav's-chart-table-hours.

Olav nodded at him. He went forward to his own rail-place.

The bark was running seven knots on the port tack. The wind had held from the southeast since the Cape. The South Atlantic trade was steady. The next landmark was Saint Helena, twenty-two days out.

He looked at the dark ahead.

He thought: *I will write the Saint Helena letter when we sight it. I will write it short. I will not, at the Saint Helena chart-table, have to burn a third sheet. The third sheet was a Cape Town sheet. I am not, at Saint Helena, going to need to write it again.*

He thought: *the burning I am going to practise, from here to Bergen, is the burning of the sheets the voyage does not require me to send. I will do it at Saint Helena. I will do it at the Channel. I will do it at Copenhagen. I will do it on the homeward deck when a letter to Haakon in my head asks to be written to paper, and I will do it when a letter to Sven asks to be written, and I will do it when a letter to Anders — a name I do not yet know, if there is such a man in a port ahead — asks to be written.*

He thought: *burning is the fourth kind of seamanship.*

He thought: *I had not, at twenty-three, known the four.*

He thought: *I know them now. The three are the work, and the watching, and the teaching. The fourth is the burning.*

He went below.

He slept.

Five hammocks down Sven slept the even breath of the dark. Bassein in his crate on the foredeck slept with his paws folded under his chin. Torgerson in the galley bunk slept the new sleep of a man who had, that evening, told one other man of a decision he had been carrying for ten days.

The bark ran on north-northwest on the South Atlantic trade, toward Saint Helena and toward the Channel and toward Bergen, with the particular small peace a homeward ship had in the middle of her Atlantic crossing when the wind had held for a fortnight and the watches had settled and the men had all decided, each in his own way, what his own coming-ashore was going to look like when he stepped off her in November at the quay he had stepped onto her from in June of the year before.
