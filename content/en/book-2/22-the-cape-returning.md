They lay at Cape Town for six days on the homeward.

The refit this time was lighter than it had been in July — water, a little coal for the galley, a minor adjustment to the foremast truss that had worked on the long crossing — and the Danish agent Dahl, at his office in the same stone building, took Olav's signature on the homeward consignment manifest with the small courteous dispatch of a man who had already formed his opinion of the navigator in July and had not, this time, anything more to add. Brun went ashore on his own business at the Exchange on three of the days. He took Olav with him on the fourth, to the Colonial Bank on Adderley Street, to present a draft and to be introduced, *for future reference,* to the Scandinavian clerk who handled such drafts for bark captains of Brun's kind.

Olav shook the clerk's hand. He was a Swede of forty-five named Alm. He gave Olav a card.

"Mr. Hestby."

"Yes."

"If you come back to Cape Town as the master of your own ship in ten years, you will present your drafts at this bank. You will present them to my successor, if I have had one, or to me, if the bank has kept me. The card is for the keeping. Put it in the leather folder you are going to keep your whole career's small papers in."

"Yes. Thank you."

"You are welcome."

Brun, on the walk back to the customhouse stairs, did not comment on the card. He did not need to. The card had been the Swede's way of passing on, at a twenty-minute handshake, the small welcome Brun had given at the customhouse stairs in July — a welcome to a trade, given without ceremony, with the understanding that the man being welcomed was going to be around for forty more years.

Olav put the card in the folder that evening.

---

On the Thursday evening — their fifth at the Cape — Olav climbed to the quarterdeck at eight bells of the first dog-watch with the small bound book under his arm and asked Aarseth for leave to use the chart-table of the captain's cabin.

"For?"

"A letter, sir."

"An hour."

"Yes, sir."

"Take it."

He took the hour.

The captain was at the Cape Town Chamber on Adderley Street until the second bell of the night-watch; the mate was at the forward rail. The cabin was empty. The lamp over the chart-table was trimmed. The smell of the cabin was the particular smell of a captain's cabin in a port — a cleaner smell than the forecastle's, the air shifted every morning by the steward, a little sandalwood from the chest in the corner that had come aboard at Calcutta as a gift from the Dahl-office clerk.

Olav set the small bound book on the table beside him. He did not open it. He set his writing case in front of him. He opened the case. He took out the Cape Town letter-paper — three sheets, because Mrs. Lauritsen was not going to appear in the letter but other things of the last five months were, and three sheets was what he had decided, on the outbound Atlantic last week, that the homeward Cape letter would be — and he took out the pen.

He wrote for forty minutes.

What he wrote, on the first sheet, was the part of the homeward Cape letter that was simple. The passage had been sixty-four days. The ship was sound. The crossing had been the longest of the voyage. The Cape had been the landmark. He had, at the topgallant jackstay on the sixty-third day, looked at the mountain a second time. He had looked at it for her. He had felt, at the looking, the turn of the voyage. He was — by the calendar — forty-nine days from Bergen.

What he wrote on the second sheet was the portion about Saint Helena, which they would pass in a fortnight. He would not, Brun had said at the Adderley Street bank, put in; the bark would run past Saint Helena to the south on the trade, perhaps sighting it at a range of thirty miles if the weather was clear. Olav wrote that he would write, anyway, a letter to Olava at Saint Helena — that the letter would go on the first ship he could hand it to at a sighted pilot or a passing mail steamer — and that the letter would be a short one, because the body of the homeward letters belonged to the Cape and to Copenhagen and not to the small intervening ports.

What he wrote on the third sheet was the part he had decided, at the chart-table in the forty-minute hour, that the homeward Cape letter needed to have.

He wrote:

> *Olava. There is a sentence I have been waiting for the right letter to write.*
>
> *I have, on this voyage, lived beside a Bergen man called Haugen whose hands coil the brig way and whose face has the small dryness I have not, in my life, seen on another man at sea. He has been — with his wife and his boy in Sandviken and with my engagement at Lindøy — the one other man in the forecastle I have come, over the ten months, to know at the depth a man comes to know another man on a bark at the end of the earth. He has kept, from his side, every discipline a married Bergen man with a Sandviken wife and a three-year-old boy has kept on every voyage he has made. I have kept, from mine, every discipline a man engaged at Lindøy would be expected to keep.*
>
> *I am not writing you about him because the ledger in your drawer asks for it. I am writing because he has been, for ten months, the closest man to me on this bark, and a letter of mine to you from the Cape on the homeward that does not name him is a letter that holds back a piece of the voyage from you which you have agreed, at Christmas of '79, not to ask about.*
>
> *I am giving you his name because the giving is the agreement I can honour from my side.*
>
> *I am not going to give you more than the name. The more is mine.*
>
> *You will understand this or you will not. I do not write to ask for your understanding. I write because I have decided, at a chart-table at Cape Town on a Thursday evening in May, that the name is the thing you should have, and that the thing I do not give is the thing you have agreed I do not have to give.*
>
> *That is the sentence.*
>
> *The rest of the letter is on the other sheets.*
>
> *Yours, Olav.*

He read the third sheet once.

He set the pen down.

He thought, at the chart-table in the lamplight, about the sheet.

He thought: *I have told her the name. The name is what the agreement permits me to tell her. The name is less than what Brun said, at Calcutta in October, was the answer of a man who was not lying to himself about what he was refusing. I am not lying. I am also not telling the whole truth. The whole truth is a thing she has agreed I will not tell her until the moment, if it ever comes, at which she asks.*

He thought: *the sheet tells her that the name exists. The name existing is the thing the ledger has been waiting to register. The sheet is, for her, the closing of the gap between the thing she has been noting and the thing she has been noting.*

He thought: *she will read the sheet. She will not ask for more. She will put the letter in the folder. The folder will have, at the next entry, the name. The name will sit in the folder beside Hamre's postcard and the leather wafers and the Cape Town and Calcutta letters and my certificate, and the folder will, in June of this year, hold the fact of the name and no more than the fact.*

He thought: *I am writing the letter I promised, in the winter of '79, I would write when the voyage required it. The voyage required it. I am writing it.*

He sat for perhaps three minutes at the chart-table without moving.

He read the third sheet a second time.

He read it a third.

At the third reading he understood, with the small flat clarity his interior used for the thoughts it had already half-arrived at, that the sheet was not a sheet he was going to send.

He understood this not because the sheet was untrue. The sheet was true. He understood it because the sheet was a unilateral revision of an agreement he had made, with Olava, at the back bedroom at Lindøy in the Christmas week of '79 and had reaffirmed at the back garden bench in the autumn of '80 and had stood up at the Stavanger quay under the year before. The agreement had been that she would not ask, and that he would carry, and that the silence between them about the piece of him she had named and he had not would be the shape of the marriage.

The sheet was, in the clearest possible terms, the breaking of that silence — or the beginning of the breaking. It was him telling her the name. She had not asked for the name. The agreement was that she would not ask. His giving it to her without her asking was, he understood at the chart-table, as much a violation of the agreement as his denying it would have been had she asked.

The agreement worked in both directions.

He had understood this, at some level, at the writing; but he had written the sheet anyway, because the writing of it had been a piece of the hour's thinking, and because some part of him had wanted — for a short hour at a chart-table at the Cape — to imagine the letter he might send if the agreement had been different.

It was not different.

He took the third sheet in both hands. He folded it once. He folded it again. He tore it into four pieces along the folds. He tore the four pieces into eight. He got up from the chart-table and he crossed to the small stove the steward kept going in the captain's cabin in port, and he opened the stove-door, and he fed the eight pieces into the low flame one at a time.

He watched them burn.

He closed the stove-door.

He went back to the chart-table.

---

He sat down.

He took a new third sheet from his writing case.

He wrote, in a hand that was steadier than the hand of the first third sheet:

> *Olava, the homeward Atlantic from here is about forty-nine days by Brun's count. The South Atlantic trade is the fair one. We may sight Saint Helena in the middle of June; if so, I will try to put a short letter aboard a mail-ship passing, or leave one at a pilot if the bark makes the pilot cutter at all. Then the English Channel in the middle of August, and Copenhagen for orders in September, and Bergen at the end.*
>
> *I think of you at the kitchen table at Lindøy when I write. I do not need to say so in every letter. I am saying so in this one because the voyage is turning home and the Cape is the turning, and the sentence has been in me since the morning I came down off the topgallant on the sixty-third day and saw the mountain for the second time.*
>
> *Yours, Olav.*

He blotted it.

He folded the three sheets — the first, the second, the new third — together. He put them in an envelope. He sealed the envelope with the red wafer. He wrote on the cover *Miss Olava Lindøy, Lindøy by Stavanger, Norway.* He wrote in the upper left *Bark Semiramis, Cape Town, May 1882.*

He put the letter in the inside pocket of his coat.

He blew out the lamp.

He went out of the cabin.

On the quarterdeck the mate looked at him once and nodded. Olav nodded back. He went forward to his own rail-place.

The Cape was, beyond the bark's bowsprit, dark against the late-May sky. The mountain had its cloud on it tonight — the *tablecloth* of the summer wind Dahl had described — and the cloud was moving slowly off the western face in the particular way the tablecloth moved at this hour. The harbour around the bark had perhaps fifteen other ships tonight. Two of them were lit at the rigging. The rest showed only their binnacle-lamps.

He stood at the rail.

He did not, at the rail, second-guess the third sheet.

He had made the decision at the chart-table. The decision had been the decision. The second-guessing, at twenty-three, was the thing that unmade the decisions a man had taken with his full attention. He did not unmake this one.

He thought of Olava reading the sheet.

He did not, in the thinking, allow himself to see her face at the reading. He allowed himself only the small private fact that she would be at the kitchen table at Lindøy on a Tuesday or a Wednesday in the second week of July, with the letter from Cape Town in her hand; that she would read the third sheet four times as she read everything four times; that she would put the letter in the folder afterward; that the folder would not, in the putting, have the weight it would have had if he had told her more.

The folder would have the name.

The folder would have the fact that he had given her the name.

The folder would have, in its own quiet way, the architecture of a marriage that had agreed — at Christmas '79, at the Lindøy bench in November '80, at the gangway at Stavanger in June '81, and now at a chart-table at Cape Town in May '82 — that this was the shape the marriage was going to have.

The architecture held on paper.

It would hold, he had to believe, in the forty-nine days between tonight and the Lindøy jetty in November.

He went below.

---

He gave the letter to the Norwegian consul at Cape Town the next morning.

The consul was a different consul from the one in July — Dahl's office was not the consul's office, though the two worked together — and this consul was a younger man, a Bergen-educated clerk of about thirty with a neat mustache and a slow English. He took the letter. He read the cover. He stamped it.

"Mr. Hestby."

"Yes."

"Three sheets."

"Three sheets."

"A fair letter."

"A letter the voyage needed."

The consul nodded, briefly, in the way a clerk nodded at a remark he understood and did not need explained. He put the letter in the outbound tray.

"First P&O steamer is Tuesday. Your letter will be in Southampton by the third week of June and in Bergen by the first week of July. Lindøy by the end of the second week."

"Thank you."

"My regards to Miss Lindøy."

"I will give them."

He went out.

On the walk back to the customhouse stairs he did not hurry. He stopped at the top of Wale Street, where there was a view down to the harbour, and he looked at the bark and at the mountain behind the bark and at the small busy business of the Cape Town working day around him. He thought: *I have done the hardest piece of the homeward's interior work, which was not the writing of the letter I sent but the burning of the letter I did not. The burning was the keeping of the agreement. I will, at the next hardest piece at Saint Helena in a month, do the burning again if the hour at the chart-table produces another third sheet. The burning is, from here to Bergen, a skill I am going to practise.*

He went down to the stairs.

The dinghy-boy took him back to the bark.

The *Semiramis* lifted Cape Town on the Monday morning tide.

She caught the southerly as she cleared the outer light and set her course north-northwest for the South Atlantic trade, and by noon of the first day she was a hundred miles off the Cape with the mountain a blue thing on the horizon behind her, and Olav — at the forenoon watch, at the foretopgallant loosing the royal — looked aft once at the mountain and understood, in the looking, that the third landmark of the voyage had been passed, and that the last landmark before the Norwegian coast was going to be Saint Helena in the middle of June, and that the landmark after that was the English Channel in the middle of August, and that the landmark after that was Copenhagen in September, and that after Copenhagen there were no more landmarks — only the coast of Norway coming up off the port bow on a late-autumn morning, and the bark turning for Bergen, and the voyage ending at the very quay she had lifted from in June of the year before.

He shook out the royal.

He came down to the deck.

He went to his watch.

---

In the forecastle that night he lay in his hammock and listened to the bark.

She was running eight knots on the port tack. The southerly had steadied. The Cape was behind her. The homeward Atlantic was ahead. The forecastle breathed its eighteen breaths — seventeen of the sleeping kind and one of the pretending-kind he had come, by now, to recognize as Sven's when Sven was awake but did not wish to be — and Olav, at twenty-three in the dark of a bark on the homeward at the end of the fourteenth month of the voyage, slept the good clean sleep of a man who had written his hardest letter and had given it to the right consul and had, in the giving, done the piece of the voyage's work that had most been at his chest.

The letter was on its way to Lindøy.

The architecture of the homeward was holding.

Forty-eight days to Bergen now.

He slept.
