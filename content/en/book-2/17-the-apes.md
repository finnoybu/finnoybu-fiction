They lifted Rangoon on the last Friday in October.

The passage from Rangoon to Calcutta was not the long passage the outbound had been — only eight hundred miles of the Bay of Bengal, up across to the Hooghly — and the bark made it in twelve days on a quarter wind, the southwest monsoon by now faded into the quieter north-easterlies that would carry her across the Bay and then, after the second Calcutta stop and the loading of the last of the homeward cargo, down the Indian Ocean on the return.

The twelve days were the first twelve days of the voyage after the fight.

The six feet of deck held. At supper Sven sat at the windlass; Olav sat at the foot of the foremast. At the foredeck they worked on separate coils unless Sundberg put them on the same yard; the only yard they shared, in the twelve days, was the main topsail on the morning they made the Andaman banks, and the sharing was wordless, and the sharing ended at the deck without the exchange that would have ended such a sharing in the weeks before the fight.

Olav did not, in the twelve days, speak to Sven except about the work.

He had understood, in the first two days after the fight, the thing Sven had done and the reason Sven had done it. He had understood it in pieces — a small clarity at the forward rail at two in the morning of the day after, a second piece at the galley at Torgerson's bench the next evening when Torgerson had, without asking, set a cup of coffee in front of Olav and gone back to his dishes — and he had arrived, by the end of the fourth day, at the full shape of it. The knife had not walked. Sundberg had looked at Sven's chest for two seconds longer than the others because Sundberg had been telling Sven a thing without words. The fight had been the reply.

Olav had considered, at the fourth day, whether to tell Sven that he had worked it out.

He had decided against telling him.

He had decided against it because Sven had, by his own three days of calculation before the fight, arranged a particular small architecture — a six-foot distance enforced by a filed bosun's judgment — and the architecture's whole point was that the two men inside it would live inside it without speaking of it. To tell Sven he had worked it out would be to crack the architecture from his own side. Sven had paid, with a knuckle and a filed log-entry, for the architecture. Sven was not, Olav had understood, asking to be relieved of it. He was asking to be allowed to use it.

Olav allowed.

He also — this was the harder piece — understood that his own entering into the architecture was, from his side, its own form of what Sven had entered into from his. The keeping of the six feet was the keeping that made the discipline two men's discipline instead of one's. He had entered because he had worked out that Sven needed him to.

The working-out had felt, at twenty-three, like a thing he had not known he was going to be asked to do when he had signed for the bark. He did it anyway.

---

On the fifth morning of the passage a small flotilla of country-boats came off the port side of the bark from a place the chart called only *Bassein approaches* — a mangrove coast at the top of the Andaman Sea — and the boats came because they knew the *Semiramis* was passing and because the boats had something to sell.

What they had to sell was, as it turned out, coconuts.

There were eight boats. Each had two men at the oars and one at the stem holding up a cluster of coconuts on a stave for the bark to see. They came under the bark's quarter and shouted up in a mixture of English and Burmese, and Torgerson the steward came to the rail with the mate and they dealt, by the mate's permission, for sixty coconuts at two annas the six.

The coconuts came aboard in baskets lowered on a line.

They came up onto the deck, and the steward and the galley-boy and two of the common seamen on the mate's watch sat around them to husk them for the forecastle's afternoon meal, and in the second basket that came aboard — Olav, passing at the coaling-port on his way aft, registered — were not sixty coconuts but fifty-nine coconuts and a small Burmese monkey.

The monkey had come aboard with the coconuts.

It was a small one — perhaps the size of a large cat — with a pale face and a long tail and the particular darting awareness of a monkey that had lived on a Burmese riverbank its whole life and had, in the last half hour, been lifted onto a very large wooden thing and did not know what the wooden thing was. It was tied by one ankle to a coconut frond inside the basket. The tying, Olav realized, had not been an accident of the loading. One of the Burmese boatmen had put the monkey in the basket.

The steward stood up from his husking and looked at the monkey.

"Captain," he called aft, in the tone a steward used for a decision that was not his.

Brun came forward. He looked into the basket. He looked at the galley-boy. He looked at the mate. He looked at the monkey.

"Who sold us the monkey."

"The last boat, sir," the mate said. "The boatman went back to his boat after the deal was struck. He did not say anything about the monkey. The basket came up with the monkey in it. The boats have pulled off."

Brun looked at the bark's quarter. The boats were, in fact, already twenty yards off and pulling back toward the mangrove line. The boatmen were not looking at the bark. They were pulling steadily and without the looking-back of men who had made a legitimate trade.

"They have given us the monkey," Brun said. "Either as a gift or as a joke or because the man who owned the monkey did not want the monkey any more. The trade is done; I am not chasing the boats."

He looked at the monkey a moment longer.

"Mr. Hestby."

"Sir."

"You are on the watch. You have the monkey. Find it a place and a ration. Keep it away from the cabin. I do not want it in any line of sight that will make it into my quarterdeck. Report to me at four bells of the afternoon watch with your arrangement."

"Yes, sir."

The captain went aft.

---

The monkey's arrangement, by four bells of the afternoon watch, was a small crate Olav had asked the carpenter to knock together, set on the larboard side of the foredeck behind the windlass where the forecastle wall shaded it from the afternoon sun. Olav had, with Odegaard's help, tied the monkey to a ring-bolt on the crate with a length of cord that gave him perhaps three feet of radius and kept him from reaching any line of actual rigging. The monkey had been given half a coconut, two pieces of ship's biscuit, and a tin can of fresh water. The monkey had drunk the water and was, when Olav went to make his report to the captain, working patiently at the half-coconut with small clever hands.

Brun received the report at the quarterdeck without expression.

"Name the monkey, Hestby."

"Sir?"

"Give him a name. A monkey without a name on a ship becomes *the monkey* in the log, and a ship's log with the word *the monkey* in it is a log that has a man with insufficient imagination keeping it. Give him a name."

Olav considered for a beat.

"Bassein, sir."

"Bassein."

"For the waters."

"Acceptable. Log him as Bassein. His ration is your responsibility. His cleanliness is also your responsibility. If he escapes, he is your responsibility. If he bites a man, he is your responsibility. I will not feed him. The crew will not feed him unless you instruct them to. Is that understood."

"Yes, sir."

"Dismissed."

The captain, Olav registered on the way back forward, had permitted himself — on the word *Bassein* — a very small and very quickly buried twitch at the right corner of his mouth. It had not been a smile. It had been the furthest thing the captain's face had done toward a smile since the chart-room in March.

Olav filed the twitch.

---

In the days that followed, the monkey became a piece of the ship.

The forecastle called him *Bassein* in the log and *Bas* in private. He was cleverer than any of the crew had expected and less trouble than any of them had feared. He ate coconut, ship's biscuit, the rinds of the lemons the mate kept in a jar in the galley, and — three times in the first week — a piece of salt beef a common seaman had dropped at supper that Bas had come to the end of his cord to reach. He did not, in the first week, bite anyone. He did not, Olav was pleased to note, try very hard to escape. He seemed to understand — to the degree a small Burmese monkey tied to a ring-bolt could be said to understand — that the bark was going somewhere, and that wherever it was going would be somewhere the bark also was, and that remaining in the crate was the option that put him on the bark.

Olav came to feed him each morning at six bells.

He fed him half a coconut.

He opened the coconut with a deck-knife — not Odegaard's; his own — by splitting the husk in four and prising the flesh loose. He set the coconut half in front of Bassein. Bassein took it in both hands and settled in a particular way against the side of the crate that Olav came, in the first three mornings, to recognize as Bassein's morning posture: left hand on the coconut half, right hand picking at the white flesh, the tail coiled against the wood of the crate, the eyes on Olav.

On the fourth morning, feeding him, Olav thought of the metaphor.

He had read about it — the metaphor, not the creature — at Mrs. Kindingstad's in the winter of '80, in a book about the trade routes of the East India companies that had been published at London in '68. The book had described a trick the forest people of the Malay used to catch small apes like Bassein: they cut a hole in a coconut, emptied the coconut of its milk, filled it with a sweet rice, and left the coconut on the jungle floor. The hole was large enough for a small dry ape-hand to enter, but not large enough for an ape-hand that had closed around the rice and had therefore made itself, in the closing, a fist. The ape reached in. The ape closed his hand. The ape could not, having closed his hand, withdraw it. The ape could have let go of the rice and been free; the ape, in every known instance, did not let go of the rice. The forest people came at their leisure and took the ape.

Olav had read the metaphor in 1880 as a parable about desire.

He had read it, at the time, as a parable about ordinary human appetites — drink, women, the small fatal appetites Sundberg had later summarized at Calcutta — and had filed it as one of those small morbid literary observations that men of the period put into travel books.

He looked, on the fourth morning at the windlass with Bassein working the coconut flesh in front of him, at the ape.

He thought about the metaphor again.

He thought: *the metaphor is not about drink and women. The metaphor is about any closing fist. The metaphor is about the thing a man has reached into that he cannot, without letting go of the thing, withdraw. The metaphor is not moral. The metaphor is mechanical. An ape does not fail to withdraw because the ape is weak. The ape does not withdraw because the ape is made in such a way that the ape's hand does not open while the rice is still in it. A man does not let go of what he has closed on because he is not made to let go of it. He is made to close.*

He thought: *I have closed on this voyage. I have closed on the work, on the chart, on the mate's berth I will not take, on the wool coat at the quay. I have closed on these. They are the rice. I cannot withdraw from the voyage with those still in my closed fist; I can only finish the voyage with them. I am the ape. The voyage is the coconut. The rice is the life I have chosen.*

He thought: *Sven has closed too. He has closed on the wife and the boy and the congregation at Sandviken Church. He has closed on his own particular rice. That is why the fight. That is why the six feet. Sven is the ape who understood, earlier than the metaphor-maker thought an ape could understand, that the rice was the rice. He did not let go. He also did not let his hand be taken. He closed on the rice and fought the metaphor itself.*

He thought: *I have closed on the rice too. My rice is Olava and the Hestby church and the workbench drawer that I have not opened. I am not, at twenty-three, letting go of the rice. I am also not, at twenty-three, letting my hand be taken. That is what this voyage has become. That is the thing the metaphor did not describe, because the metaphor was written by a man who did not understand that the ape could, at the moment the forest people reached for him, also be a man with his own six feet.*

He gave Bassein the coconut.

Bassein ate.

Olav stayed at the crate for another minute.

---

That evening in the forecastle he wrote, in a small bound book he had bought at the Asiatic Society stall on the Friday before Rangoon — not a letter, not a log, a book of his own that had no one's name on it and that had been intended for chart-work notes and that had, on this voyage, begun to fill with sentences — he wrote the paragraph he had thought at the crate in the morning.

He wrote it because he wanted to know, in ten years, that he had thought it.

He wrote it in the careful hand he used for his own notes.

He wrote, at the end:

> *The ape is only caught by the fist if the forest people come. An ape alone in the jungle with a fist around the rice is only an ape with a long patient afternoon. The forest people are what turn the fist into the trap. I am not sure, at twenty-three, who the forest people are on this voyage. The forest people are not Brun. They are not Sundberg. They are not Sven. They are not Olava. They may be no one. It may be that there are no forest people on this voyage, and the voyage is only a long patient afternoon, and I will come home in November with my fist still around my rice, and I will sit at the supper table at Finnøy with my father across from me and eat the meal my mother has cooked, and my fist will open at the table because the fist only needs to be closed while I am at sea.*
>
> *That is what I hope.*
>
> *I do not know if it is true.*

He blotted the page.

He closed the book.

He put it into his sea-chest under the leather folder.

He slept.

The bark ran on up the Bay of Bengal on the quiet north-easterly, Bassein asleep in his crate with a half-eaten coconut between his paws, the six feet of deck between the foot of the foremast and the windlass maintained in the dark by the invisible architecture two men on the bark had, between them, built and would — for six more months at least — hold.
