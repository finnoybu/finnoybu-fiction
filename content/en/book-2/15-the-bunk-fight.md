The knife went missing on the Tuesday.

It was the Ålesund boy Odegaard's knife — a small bone-handled deck-knife his father had given him the morning he signed for the *Semiramis* in April, with *S.O.* scratched on the handle in the father's hand — and the boy had kept it in the leather pouch he kept in his sea-chest, and when he had opened the chest at eight bells of the forenoon watch on the Tuesday to find his spare wool shirt the knife had not been in the pouch.

He had looked for it for the quarter hour before he had said anything. Then he had come, because Odegaard was the boy he had been since Biscay and had by now worked out where his small allegiances on the bark lay, to Olav at the main fife rail.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"My knife is gone."

"Gone when."

"Gone today. It was in my chest on Monday evening when I went up for the last dog-watch. It was not in my chest at eight bells this morning."

"You have looked."

"I have looked at every fold of the shirts. It is not there."

Olav considered.

A knife going missing from a common seaman's chest on the eighty-sixth day out of Stavanger, in port at Rangoon, was not an ordinary event. It was also not an extraordinary one. Men lost knives; knives fell out of pouches into corners of chests; a knife that was not in the pouch might be at the bottom of the chest under a folded blanket and not have been found by a boy who had been looking in the wrong place. Olav had, in his first year on the *Sigrid,* lost a knife for a fortnight before he had found it inside the folded sock it had been kept in, and the loss had been entirely his own.

But the boy had looked. The boy was not a careless boy about his knife. The boy's knife had his father's initials on the handle, and the boy was sixteen, and the knife had been a gift, and a boy with a gift-knife did not, in Olav's reading of Odegaard, mis-fold the knife into a sock.

"Odegaard."

"Yes."

"Go check again. Check the bottom of the chest under everything. Take everything out. Put it back in. If it is not there after you have done that, come back and find me. Do not speak of the knife to anyone else in the forecastle yet."

"Yes."

The boy went below.

---

An hour later, at the changing of the watches, he came back up.

"It is not there."

"You are certain."

"I have taken everything out. I have checked the pouch. I have checked inside the folded shirts. I have checked inside my boots, in the coat pockets, in the cap. It is not in the chest. It is not in my hammock. It is not on the deck where my chest sits. It is not in my coat."

Olav thought.

There were eighteen common seamen on the bark. Of the eighteen, five shared Odegaard's side of the forecastle — the port bank — and of those five, two had chests on either side of Odegaard's. The two were a Trondheim hand named Lie who had not, on ninety days at sea, given Olav any reason to form an opinion of him; and Sven.

Olav did not, at the fife rail, permit himself to form an opinion about whether the knife had been taken by anyone at all.

"Odegaard."

"Yes."

"I will ask Sundberg. He will know what to do. A lost knife in a forecastle is a bosun's business."

"The bosun will call a muster."

"He will call a muster. It is not, in itself, a disgrace. It is the forecastle's standard procedure for a lost knife. You have not done anything wrong by losing it. You also will not do anything wrong by telling the bosun about it."

"I know."

"Go to the bosun."

The boy went to the bosun.

---

Sundberg did what bosuns of his kind did: he called a forecastle muster at four bells of the afternoon watch.

He called it for Olav's watch — which was the watch the knife had gone missing from, and which was the watch in which the boy's chest was — and he conducted it the way he conducted every such muster on every such bark on which he had been bosun, which was with the men standing in a line along the forecastle bulkhead and their chests open on the deck in front of them. He went along the line. He looked in each chest. He did not handle the men's belongings; he looked, and he moved to the next chest. He did not say anything.

When he came to Sven's chest he looked into it for a moment longer than he had looked into the others.

Olav, standing at the end of the line in his own watch's place with his own chest open at his feet, watched this. He watched it with the small flat attention of a man who had not, until this moment of the muster, let himself form an opinion about the knife and who was not going to form one now on the basis of an extra two seconds of a bosun's look.

Sundberg did not speak. He moved to Lie's chest.

He looked in Lie's chest.

He looked in the remaining chests.

He came to the end of the line.

"Hestby."

"Bosun."

"The knife is not in any chest in the forecastle."

"No."

"I will write it as missing. The boy will have the cost refunded against the company at Bergen. That is the procedure."

"Yes."

"Muster is dismissed."

The men closed their chests. They went back to the deck. The watch resumed.

---

At six bells of the afternoon watch the knife turned up.

It turned up in the galley.

Torgerson the steward had been cleaning the small bench where he gutted fish for the crew's supper, and the knife had been underneath the bench, wedged between the bench and the bulkhead on the galley floor. He brought it to the main fife rail with a wry look Olav had by now come to know.

"Hestby."

"Torgerson."

"The boy's knife."

"Yes."

"It was under my bench. I cannot, at this hour, tell you how it got there. It is not a knife that walks. It is not a knife a man mislays in a galley a common seaman does not enter. A man put it there. A man came into the galley this morning between four bells and six bells and put it under the bench."

"Why."

"I do not know why. I am the steward. I know what was moved in my galley. I do not know who came in between four bells and six bells. I was at the captain's cabin for the second breakfast service. A man who wanted to put a knife under my bench would have had the hour."

"Did you see anyone coming out of the galley."

"I saw no one. The galley door is at the bulkhead; a man coming out from behind me as I came up the companionway would not have been seen. The door was not latched when I came back; I had latched it when I left. But a man who is careful about a latch is a man who is careful about being seen."

Olav took the knife. He looked at it. The boy's initials — *S.O.* — were on the handle.

He understood, with the flat clarity of a man who had been on the bark for ninety days, that the knife had not been mis-placed. The knife had been taken out of Odegaard's chest and placed under the galley bench. The taking and the placing had been a deliberate act by someone on board, done in the hour between the muster being called and the muster being held. The purpose of the placing had been to have the knife found — after the muster had declared the knife not in any chest — in a place that put the knife in an ambiguous position, a position that would force a second muster or a second investigation, a position that was the kind of small petty trouble a bored crew in port could produce if one of its members was sufficiently sour and sufficiently clever.

Olav did not know which of the eighteen men had done it.

He had, however, the small cold register that the doing had been a kind of provocation, and that the provocation was the kind of provocation a man made at another man when he wanted, for his own small obscure reasons, to force a fight.

"Torgerson. Thank you. I will take this to Odegaard. Do not, at this hour, say to anyone where the knife came from. Say only that it was found on the deck."

"Yes."

---

He gave the knife back to Odegaard at the windlass.

"Where was it."

"On the deck under a coil. It had fallen out of your chest during the muster. Do not think about it further."

"Hestby —"

"Odegaard. Do not think about it further. The knife is yours. The knife has come back to you. The rest of the story is the bark's story, not yours. Go back to your work."

The boy went back to his work.

Olav, at the windlass, stood for a moment.

---

At eight bells of the afternoon watch Sven came forward from the aft coils and stopped at the main fife rail where Olav was pretending to check the set of the mainsail brace.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"The knife was found."

"Yes."

"Where."

"Under Torgerson's galley bench."

"That is not where a boy loses a knife."

"No."

"Then who put it there."

"I do not know."

Sven did not answer at once. He looked out across the deck at the empty mid-afternoon. Three Burmese lighters were alongside the larboard taking the last of the rice. The mate was on the quarterdeck making a small adjustment to the binnacle-compass for the warmer air; Sundberg was forward at the capstan; most of the crew was below or at the far end of the deck. Olav and Sven were, for the moment, in the narrow quiet a foredeck had at that hour in a port.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"I will ask you one question."

"Ask."

"Do you think it was me."

Olav did not, for a moment, speak.

He had not let himself think that it had been Sven. He had registered, during the muster, that Sundberg had looked into Sven's chest for a moment longer than the others, and he had registered, in the two hours since, that some part of him had stayed at Sundberg's two-seconds longer and had not moved on from them. He had not, however, decided that Sven had taken the knife. He had decided that he was not going to decide. The not-deciding was the discipline of a man who had been on the bark ninety days and had made a drawer of his not-deciding.

Sven was asking him to open the drawer.

"Sven."

"Yes."

"I have not let myself think it was you."

"You have not let yourself."

"I have not."

"But the thought has been there."

"The thought has been there."

"Then the answer is yes."

"The answer is no, Sven. The answer is I did not decide. I let the two seconds Sundberg took at your chest be the two seconds they were. I did not turn the two seconds into a decision. I am not going to turn them into a decision now. If you are telling me that you did not take the knife, I will tell you that I did not decide that you did. If you are telling me that you did take the knife, I will ask you why, and I will listen. Those are the two answers."

"I did not take the knife."

"Then that is the first answer."

Sven did not move.

He was standing at the fife rail perhaps a hand's width from Olav. The hand's width was the hand's width Sven had held since Biscay at the supper coil — the hand's width that was the public measure of two men who had decided, for reasons the watch-list could not describe, that they were going to be to each other what they were going to be to each other at a public distance only. The hand's width had, on ninety days, become a shape they both held to.

The fight started at that hand's width.

It did not start with a word. It started with Sven's hand — the left one, the one that had been on Sven's hip — coming up and pushing Olav's shoulder. The push was not a full push. It was the push a man gave another man when he was not sure, for the count of one breath, whether the other man was the man he had been at the banyan root. The push was a question.

Olav understood it as a question.

He pushed back.

He pushed back because the fight was, at that hand's width, the fight he had been, without knowing it, waiting to have with Sven since the earing on the royal yard in the Indian Ocean — not a fight about the knife, which both men knew had not been Sven's, but a fight about what neither man had been able, in ninety days, to put into a sentence.

The push went to a grapple. The grapple went to a short hard flurry of three or four blows that were not aimed and that came, from Olav, from the shoulder Sven had pushed, and that came, from Sven, from the elbow Olav had caught. They were on the deck for perhaps thirty seconds. Olav's knuckle was split at the second joint. Sven's lip was open. They came apart, both of them half against the foremast and half against each other, and they stood at arm's length breathing hard.

Sundberg was at the forward rail. He had not, Olav registered, intervened. He had watched the fight for its thirty seconds from the rail without moving, which was what a bosun of his kind did when a fight between two good hands was a fight that had been coming for a while and had, in being had, ended a thing that the two hands had not been able to end by other means. Now, at the ending, he came forward at an unhurried pace.

"Hestby. Haugen."

"Bosun."

"Bosun."

"The fight is over."

"Yes."

"It will not happen again."

"No."

"It will not happen again because next time I will pull both of you from the watch and put you on water-casks for a week and write it in the log. This time I will not. This time I will say you were working out a small unclearness between you and were using the means men at sea use for working out small unclearnesses. Agreed?"

"Agreed," Sven said.

"Agreed," Olav said.

"Good. Get cleaned up. Go back to your work. Do not speak of it at supper. Do not let the forecastle speak of it. By eight bells tomorrow it is a thing that did not happen. Is that understood."

"Yes, bosun."

"Yes, bosun."

"Good."

He turned and went aft to the capstan.

---

Sven and Olav did not, through the rest of the afternoon watch, speak.

They worked at their separate parts of the foredeck. Olav's knuckle bled for perhaps ten minutes; he tied his kerchief around the hand and went back to coiling. Sven's lip swelled. Odegaard from across the deck looked once at both of them and did not, Olav noted, ask.

At supper Sven did not come to the coil of rope where he usually sat beside Olav. He took his plate to the windlass and sat on the bitts and ate alone. Olav took his plate to his usual coil and sat on it and ate alone.

The hand's width of coil between them, that evening, became something larger than a hand's width. It became the six feet of deck between the coil and the windlass.

Olav understood that the six feet was the form Sven had decided, for this evening, that the hand's width was going to take.

He did not, in the registering, feel wronged.

He felt, instead, that a thing had been fought out between them that neither of them had known was there to be fought, and that the fighting-out had been, for both of them, a releasing of a pressure that had been building since the earing in the Indian Ocean. The releasing had not been pretty. The releasing had cost a knuckle and a lip. But the releasing had been a thing both of them had needed, and Sven — Sven the married Bergen man with the wife and the boy in Sandviken, Sven who had held the banyan root for him at the creek on Saturday — had done the releasing in the only way the two of them had the language for.

After supper Olav went to his watch.

Late that night, in the forecastle, in the dark, Sven was already asleep when Olav climbed into his hammock. Or Sven was pretending he was already asleep. Olav did not, at this distance, know which. He did not need to know. The six feet of deck at supper had said what it had said. The forecastle dark did not need to add to it tonight.

He slept.

The bark lay at her creek mooring in the Burmese dark, the rice going aboard by lantern light, ninety-two days now since Stavanger, and the two men five hammocks apart slept the unmatched broken sleep of men who had that afternoon, at the foot of the foremast of a bark at Rangoon, had a fight neither of them would speak of again and both of them would, for the rest of their lives, remember the feel of.
