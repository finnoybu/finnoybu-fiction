On the Saturday afternoon the steward asked the mate for permission to send four of the watch to a small bathing-beach the Burmese porters had told him of, half a mile upstream on the Pazundaung creek where the water ran clean and cold off a spring in the bank.

The mate gave the permission. Sundberg chose the four. He chose Sven and Olav and Odegaard the Ålesund boy and a German common seaman named Reuss who had been quiet and competent for ninety-eight days. He gave them three hours.

They went in a hired country-boat at two bells of the afternoon watch. The Burmese boatman pulled them upstream against the weak tide. The creek narrowed above the timber-yards. The water, which at the bark's mooring had been the brown of a river handling a city, went by degrees to a green-brown and then to a clear green as they came up past the last of the timber-rafts. On a bend a half-mile above the bark the boatman put them in at a low bank under a banyan and said, in the English he had, *spring from the bank, cold water, good for washing, I wait here one hour,* and he tied up and lit a small clay pipe.

They stripped at the bank.

The stripping was the stripping of four sailors at a hot foreign river — no ceremony, no modesty beyond what sailors observed with each other, coats and shirts and wool trousers in a heap by the banyan's roots — and they went into the water in the order that men on a bathing-break went in. Reuss first, because Reuss was the oldest. Odegaard next, because Odegaard was the youngest. Olav third. Sven last, which meant Sven was the man on the bank while the other three were in the water, and which was — Olav would only later register — the position a Bergen man of Sven's generation often took at a river, because Sven was the man who could swim least well and Sven knew it and did not make a show of the knowing.

The water was cold.

It was the first cold water Olav had felt since Bergen harbour in early June. He stood in it to his waist for a moment with his palms on the surface and let the cold do what cold did for a body that had been, for four months, in nothing cooler than the bark's drinking-water. He ducked his head. He came up. His hair was flat. His eyes were stinging. He drew a breath he had not, in the weeks since Cape Town, quite allowed himself.

Reuss and Odegaard were downstream by thirty feet, working their way across a shallow towards the far bank. Sven was just behind Olav on the deeper side, stepping into the pool, the water at his knees.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"I do not swim well."

"You stay at the edge, then."

"I stay at the edge."

He came up to Olav's waist-deep place. He stood. He looked out across the water toward the far bank and the jungle beyond it. His chest was pale under the sun, the line where the sun had browned his neck and his forearms a sharp one against the white of the rest. Olav registered the line. He also registered that he was not going to let the registering do anything it had not done at the earing on the royal yard in the Indian Ocean. The registering was a fact about his body. The body's facts did not, at a bathing-beach under a banyan in a country in which he was a foreigner, require him to act on them.

He pushed off from the bottom and swam out into the pool.

---

He had been in the water perhaps five minutes when the bottom gave under him.

What had been a pool of three feet deep — the depth he had been walking in at the waist — became, two strokes out, a pool of eight or ten or he did not, in the first confused second, know how many feet. The current was also stronger here than it had been at the bank. The Pazundaung was not a fast creek, but it was a creek with holes in its bed, and the holes were the holes a man from Finnøy who had swum all his life in the channels off the island would have understood about if he had been paying attention, and Olav had not, for the first three strokes, been paying attention. He was, at the fourth stroke, no longer at his depth.

He put his head down to swim and the current turned him.

It did not turn him much. It turned him perhaps fifteen degrees off the line he had been swimming. But the fifteen degrees took him out of the line he had expected to be on, and the unexpectedness made his arms do the thing arms did in water that was not doing what a man expected — they grabbed for purchase instead of stroked — and the grabbing used air the stroking would not have used, and by the time he had rearranged his limbs to the proper stroke again he was down a breath and the current had carried him another ten feet past the bank.

He went under.

Under the water the green was darker than he had expected. He opened his eyes for a second and saw the suspended silt the Pazundaung carried from its upriver banks — long fine strands of it, drifting in the current — and he closed his eyes and kicked for the surface, and the surface came up slower than it should have.

His head broke.

His arms came up. He took a breath. The breath was half water; he coughed. The current, he understood in the two seconds he had his head above the water, was taking him past the banyan bank and into the middle of the creek, toward a line where the green of the jungle came down to the water on the far side and where the creek ran faster under the shadow of the overhanging trees.

He tried to turn toward the bank. He was not strong enough to turn toward the bank; the current had him.

He understood, for the count of one breath before he went under a second time, that he was going to drown at the bathing-beach of a creek called Pazundaung, half a mile above the bark *Semiramis* on which his sea-chest and his leather folder and his promise to Olava and his Cape Town letter in his own hand and the Calcutta chart-corrections he had written at the captain's desk all lay, and that the drowning was going to happen in perhaps thirty seconds, and that he was going to go down without having had, as far as he could remember, any last thought except the registering of the thirty seconds.

He went under.

---

A hand took him at the wrist.

The hand took him from above. It took him as he was going down the second time. The hand was strong at the thumb and it did not hesitate in the taking; the wrist was held and the shoulder was pulled, and Olav's head came up out of the water by force and his body followed, and he was pulled — the pulling was the steady one-armed pulling of a man who was himself only half in the water and who had his other hand, Olav understood a moment later, on the trailing root of the banyan that came out underwater at the pool's edge.

The hand was Sven's.

Sven had come out into the deeper water after him. Sven had come further than he should have come, given that he did not swim well; Sven had come to the edge of the deeper pool and had, when Olav had gone under the second time, grabbed for him by instinct rather than by ability. The root of the banyan had been the only thing that had allowed Sven to do it — if the root had not been there, Sven would have gone under with Olav and they would both have gone down the creek together — and Sven had known, in the two seconds of the grabbing, that he was using the root.

He held Olav by the wrist until Olav's feet found the bottom of the pool at the shallower shelf below the bank.

Olav stood. He coughed water. He coughed more. Sven released his wrist, and the two of them waded the three strokes back to the bank, Sven with his other hand still on the root and Olav supported on Sven's shoulder in a hold that was, at the moment, the only hold either of them was capable of.

At the bank they went down onto the warm mud. Olav coughed up the last of what he had swallowed. Sven sat beside him on the bank with his arms across his knees.

Reuss and Odegaard were running back up the shallow toward them. Reuss was shouting something in German. The boatman, under the banyan, had stood up and dropped his pipe and was coming out into the water with his trousers hitched up.

Olav lifted his head.

"I am —"

"Do not talk yet," Sven said. "Cough. You have water in your chest. It will come out if you cough."

Olav coughed. More water came out.

Reuss reached them. Reuss was a large man; he put both his hands under Olav's arms and helped him sit up. Odegaard — the boy — was standing at the bank with his face white. The boatman stood in the shallow, his pipe still in his mouth now that he had picked it up again, and looked at all of them.

"He went under," Sven said, in Norwegian, to Reuss.

"I saw," Reuss said.

"The pool drops. There is a hole."

"I saw."

"He is all right."

"Yes."

Reuss looked at Olav. Olav nodded.

"I am all right," Olav said, because it was the thing that needed saying.

"Good," Reuss said. "I will put the boy over there." He indicated Odegaard, who was, by now, quietly crying at the bank, the way a sixteen-year-old cried when he had watched a man almost drown and did not, at sixteen, have the equipment for any other response. Reuss went to the boy. He put a hand on the boy's shoulder. He said something in German that Olav did not catch but that had the register of an older man calming a younger one.

Sven stayed on the bank with Olav.

---

After a minute Olav's chest had cleared. He was able to sit without leaning on Sven's shoulder. The sun was warm on his skin; the mud was warm; the cold of the water was draining out of his muscles into the sun. He understood, at the bank, that he was not going to die that afternoon. He also understood that he had, for the count of two breaths, been a man who was going to die that afternoon, and that the not-dying was a thing Sven had done with a reach of his arm and a grip on his wrist.

Sven did not, in the silence that followed Reuss's going to Odegaard, say anything.

Olav turned his head. He looked at Sven. Sven's hair was wet at the ends where the water had come up to his neck. The small scar Olav had not previously noticed on Sven's collarbone was white in the sun. Sven was looking at the opposite bank of the creek.

"Sven."

"Yes."

"You went into the water."

"Yes."

"You do not swim."

"I do not swim well. I can hold a root."

"Why did you come."

Sven considered this. He did not, Olav noticed, look at him.

"I came because you had gone under," he said. "I do not know what else the question wants. You had gone under and the boy was not going to reach you in time and Reuss was too far downstream. I came because I was there and I could hold the root."

"Yes."

"It is not a thing a man thinks about."

"No."

"It is a thing a man does before he thinks. We have been over this, Hestby. A man who at a fouled earing does not think does not at a bad pool think. That is all this was."

Olav did not, for a moment, answer.

He had understood, at the second stroke of the water when he had felt the bottom go out, that he was in a kind of trouble his body had not prepared him for. He had understood, at the hand on his wrist, that the trouble had been ended. He had not, between those two moments, understood whose hand it had been. He had known — afterward, in the coughing on the mud — that the hand had been Sven's; he had known from the voice.

He thought: *Sven went into water he cannot swim in to take my wrist at a root.*

He thought: *Sven has said it was the same thing as the earing.*

He thought: *it was not the same thing as the earing. The earing was a thing two hands could do. The root was a thing one man did for another who could not, at that moment, do it for himself.*

He thought: *Sven knows it was not the same. Sven is calling it the same because the not-same is a thing he does not want us to have to hold between us.*

He did not say any of this aloud. He said, after a moment:

"Thank you, Sven."

"Yes."

"I know what you did."

"I did what a man does at a creek."

"You did what a particular man does at a creek."

Sven did not answer.

He stood up. He went to his clothes at the banyan. He began dressing with the small even motions of a man who was, in the dressing, putting an hour at a Burmese river into the small careful order the bark's routine required it to be put into before he came back aboard. Reuss came up with Odegaard, whose face was still pink at the eyes but who had stopped crying. They dressed too.

Olav dressed.

---

On the country-boat back to the bark none of the four spoke.

The boatman sculled them down on the running tide. The timber-yards came up. The bark's long black hull came into view at the mooring. Sundberg was at the forward rail; he had been waiting, Olav thought, for the four of them to come back. He looked at them as the boat came alongside. He looked at Olav. He looked at the wet at Olav's hair. He looked at Sven. He looked at Sven's hands.

He did not ask.

The boatman held the country-boat at the accommodation-ladder. Odegaard went up first. Reuss next. Olav went up third. Sven came last.

At the top of the ladder Sundberg spoke, just to Olav, quietly enough that the rest of the crew did not hear.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"Did you go under."

"I went under."

"Haugen pulled you out."

"Haugen pulled me out."

"I will write it in the log as *incidental mishap at bathing, Pazundaung creek, 15 October 1881, all hands returned safely to the ship.* That is what the log will say. That is what it needs to say."

"Yes."

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"A man who is pulled out of a river by another man does not, in his life, ever not know it."

"No."

"Good. Go below. Dry your hair. Come up for the first dog-watch."

Olav went below.

---

He lay in his hammock for the half-hour before his watch.

He did not, in the half-hour, allow his body to replay the under-water green or the current or the going-under. The body had already registered those things and had, in the replaying, the risk of making them into a story the body would be unable to forget at the moments it needed to forget. He allowed the body to be where it was — in a hammock, dry, with his hair damp at the ends, with his wool shirt clean — and he let the body take the half-hour as rest.

He allowed his mind a single thought.

The thought was: *a man who is pulled out of a river by another man does not, in his life, ever not know it.*

He put the thought in the place where he put the thoughts he was going to live with for a long time.

The place was already crowded. It had in it the lee of the galley on the *Sigrid* in '76. It had in it Thomas Engelstad's face at the tar-barrel at Wilmington. It had in it the hand on his wrist at the hammock on his first night out of Stavanger. It had in it Sars's eggs. It had in it the folded sheet in the workbench drawer at Finnøy. It had in it the kiss on the Stavanger quay and the wool coat in the noon light.

It had, now, Sven's hand at his wrist at the banyan root.

The place did not, he noticed, complain of the additions. The place was the size of a man's life and a man's life had, at twenty-three, only begun to fill it.

He went up for his watch.
