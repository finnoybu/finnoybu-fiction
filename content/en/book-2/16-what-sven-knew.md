Sven had known since the second day.

He had known since the second day and he had been honest about the knowing, with himself, in the small dry register in which he was honest about most things, for the four months since. It was not a knowledge he was proud of having and it was not a knowledge he was ashamed of having. It was a knowledge, like the knowledge he had of his own unwell swimming and of his small uneven left eye and of his father's opinions about Lutheran ministers, that he carried with him because it was a fact about him that he had not been given the option of not knowing.

The knowledge was this: that at the second coil of brace-fall on the second day out of Stavanger, when the new hand from Finnøy had coiled the brig way beside him and the small thing that happened in Sven's chest at the sight of the brig coil had not been the small thing that happened in his chest when Reuss coiled the brig way beside him or when Kinn from Stjernerøy coiled the brig way beside him, he had understood that the voyage was going to be a voyage in which he was going to have to keep a particular discipline he had kept twice before in his life and had, on the basis of the previous keepings, understood the shape of.

He had kept it twice.

The first time he had kept it had been at nineteen, on a brig out of Bergen to Antwerp in the summer of '76, when a Dutch second mate had spent three weeks looking at him across a deck the way the Dutch second mate had looked at him, and Sven — twenty, newly married to Hanne, his first child not yet even a possibility in his wife's body — had kept the discipline by the crude means of not going ashore with the Dutch second mate at Antwerp and of not, after the voyage, going aboard any Dutch ship again. The Dutch second mate had not, he was sure, been particularly taken with him. The Dutch second mate had been particularly taken with a version of himself he could not find on his own ship. Sven had been an opportunity. Sven had declined to be one.

The second time he had kept it had been at twenty-one, on a bark out of Bergen to Buenos Aires in the spring of '78, when an Irish carpenter four years older than Sven had, at a gale in the South Atlantic, dropped a hammer down a companionway that had nearly taken Sven's head off at the base of the ladder, and Sven — bending to pick up the hammer — had looked up the companionway into the Irish carpenter's face and had understood in the count of one breath that the Irish carpenter had been, in some small way he himself had perhaps not understood at the time of the dropping, relieved that the hammer had not taken Sven's head off. The Irish carpenter's relief had, for the rest of the voyage, been a weight Sven had been aware of and had, at Buenos Aires and back, not engaged. The carpenter had gone back to Cork at the end of the voyage. Sven had written him a letter of good wishes that he had never sent, because sending it would have been engaging, and had burned the letter at the galley fire at Bergen on the morning he had signed off the bark.

The discipline had, on both occasions, worked.

The working had been — Sven thought, in the forecastle on the night after the fight at the foot of the foremast of the *Semiramis* with a cut lip and a knuckle he had split against Hestby's cheekbone — the small private working of a married Bergen man with a wife and a boy and a mother-in-law who had opinions about him and a congregation at Sandviken Church who had, since he was baptized there in 1857, been the congregation he intended to be buried in. The discipline had held him inside a life that was the life he had chosen, the life he intended to have, the life his wife and his boy and his mother-in-law and the congregation at Sandviken Church had all been under the impression he was having.

The discipline was, he understood at thirty days out of Stavanger this voyage, not going to hold for eighteen months on the same bark with Hestby.

He had understood this with the small flat clarity he used for most things.

He had understood, in the forecastle on the thirtieth night, that the understanding did not mean that the discipline was going to break. The discipline might hold. The discipline had held twice before. The discipline had held for a thirty-five-year-old Irish carpenter over four months at a southern latitude in a small ship with nowhere to go ashore that would have mattered. The discipline could, in theory, hold for a twenty-three-year-old Finnøy navigator over eighteen months at a larger latitude in a larger ship with several ports to go ashore at where it would matter.

What he had understood — in the forecastle on the thirtieth night — was that the holding, this time, was going to cost him something the previous holdings had not cost him.

The Dutch second mate had been a man Sven had liked the look of and had not got to know.

The Irish carpenter had been a man Sven had got to know a little and had liked a little and had not, by the end, found himself at a particular grief about.

Olav Hestby was different.

Olav Hestby was a man Sven had already, by the thirtieth day, known more of than he had known of either of the previous two, and had found, in the knowing, more of a set of particulars he liked than he had found with either of the previous two, and had discovered was going to be, for eighteen months, the man he ate supper with and worked brace-falls with and climbed royal yards with and — this had been the hardest to discover — drank coffee beside in the gray dawn while the rest of the forecastle slept.

The particulars were not the particulars of the bed. The particulars of the bed were a possibility the discipline existed to prevent, and Sven had never made the mistake of letting himself linger on the bed's particulars. The particulars that undid the discipline were the other ones. The way the Finnøy man coiled. The way he watched the captain take a noon sight. The small flat private *yes* he gave at questions other men would have given a long careful answer to. The way he had, in Calcutta, refused the mate's berth for his Olava without having to think about the refusing. The way he had, at the banyan, said *thank you, Sven* in a register Sven had not been spoken to in, by another man, since he was twelve.

It was the last one — the register — that Sven had known, on the bank at Pazundaung creek with Hestby coughing the water out of his chest, was going to be the particular the discipline would not hold against.

The register was a thing no married Bergen man who had kept the discipline twice had any defense against.

---

He had thought about this for three days.

He had thought about it on the walk up to the Shwedagon on the Thursday. He had thought about it at the gate. He had thought about it on the stairs coming down. He had thought about it in the country-boat back to the bark and in the evening on deck and in his hammock that night. He had thought about it in the creek on Saturday, before the river, and after the river in the country-boat back. He had thought about it in his hammock on Saturday night and at breakfast on Sunday and in the galley service on Monday morning while he cleaned the stewed plums the captain had been served.

He had thought about it, on Monday afternoon, for long enough to reach a decision.

The decision was this: that he was going to provoke a fight with Hestby.

He was going to provoke it because a fight with Hestby was the one thing Sven could do that would insert, between the two of them, the small public distance Sundberg had already been watching them fail to maintain. Sundberg had seen. Sundberg had looked at Sven's chest at the Tuesday muster for two seconds longer than the others not because Sundberg had thought Sven had taken the knife — Sven knew Sundberg had not thought that — but because Sundberg had been telling Sven, in the bosun's oblique way, that the bosun had registered what was growing at the fife rail and at the coil of rope, and that the bosun would, if the registering reached any further, have to act on the registering.

Sundberg had told him, with the two seconds, that he was Sven's problem to solve.

Sven had agreed, at the two seconds, that he would solve it.

The fight was the solving.

---

The knife was a piece he had not worked out at the beginning.

He had known that Odegaard's knife was in the boy's chest because Odegaard had shown it to him on the second week out of Stavanger at the forecastle supper, with the father's initials scratched on the handle, and Sven had made the small courteous noises an older hand made when a younger hand showed him a gift-knife. He had not, at that supper, planned on using the knife for anything.

He had, on the Monday evening after deciding to provoke the fight, worked out that he needed a pretext that would bring Hestby and him into a short open confrontation on the foredeck in front of Sundberg. A simple insult would not do; a simple insult between two men in a forecastle was a thing the forecastle would work out without the bosun involved, and Sven did not want the forecastle to work it out. He wanted Sundberg to be the man who closed the matter. The matter being closed by Sundberg would be the matter being put on the bark's books as *closed by the bosun,* which was a filing a Karlskrona bosun only filed when he had decided the filing was worth his time, and which would be — Sven understood — the strongest public statement that would be made about Hestby and Sven on the foredeck for the remainder of the voyage.

He had gone into Odegaard's chest while the boy was at breakfast on the Tuesday morning. He had taken the knife. He had gone to the galley at five bells of the forenoon while Torgerson was at the captain's cabin on the second breakfast. He had latched the galley door behind him, because a man who was careful about a latch was a man who was careful about being seen. He had wedged the knife under Torgerson's bench.

He had come back up.

He had, through the rest of the morning, done his work without looking at Odegaard or at Hestby.

Odegaard had, at eight bells of the forenoon, opened his chest and found the knife gone. Odegaard had gone to Hestby. Hestby had gone to Sundberg. Sundberg had called the muster. The muster had happened. The knife had been found under the galley bench. Torgerson had taken it up to Hestby.

Hestby had come to Sven at the fife rail two hours later.

Sven had asked him the question he had planned to ask. The question had been the wrong question, in the sense that Hestby had refused the question's premise and had turned the question back on Sven the way Hestby, over the four months, had turned several of Sven's questions back; but the question had served its purpose, because Hestby had said *the thought has been there* and Sven had been able, truthfully, to say *then the answer is yes.*

He had pushed Hestby's shoulder.

Hestby had pushed back.

They had fought. They had fought the way two good hands on a foredeck fought — briefly, not aimed, with the small efficiency of men who did not, on their fourth month at sea together, want to break each other's hands — and the fight had ended with Sven's lip open and Hestby's knuckle split, and Sundberg had come forward and had closed the matter with the formula Sven had been counting on.

*Agreed.*

The agreed had been the agreement. The agreement had been that the fight had been a working-out of a small unclearness. The working-out had been filed. The filing had been the bosun's filing.

Sven had got what he had come for.

---

At supper he sat on the windlass bitts and ate alone, and Hestby sat on the coil of rope at the foot of the foremast and ate alone, and the six feet of deck between them was the six feet of deck Sven had made it his business, that afternoon, to put there.

He had not, he registered at the windlass with his bread at his mouth, felt particularly good about the fight.

He had felt — he registered, chewing — that he had done a thing he had to do, and that the doing had cost him the small particular pleasure of Hestby's company at the coil of rope, and that the cost was the cost the discipline required him to pay if he was going to, at Bergen in November of next year, walk back off the bark to Sandviken to Hanne and the boy and not be the man he had been at Antwerp five years ago or at Buenos Aires three years ago — a man, that is, who had kept the discipline and had still come home half-unmade by the keeping.

The half-unmaking was what he was trying to avoid.

The half-unmaking was what came when the discipline held against all the bed-particulars and against most of the register-particulars but not against one or two of the register-particulars, and when the man came home and sat at the supper table with his wife and his boy and understood, with the particular flat clarity he used for most things, that one or two of the register-particulars was still in him — was in him in the way a small stone is in a boot, is only noticeable at certain steps, is perfectly invisible at all the other steps of a life, but is, at those certain steps, there — and that the having of it in him meant that the man he had been for the other man was a man his wife would never meet and his boy would never meet, and that the man his wife and his boy would meet for the rest of his life was a man whose shape was just slightly different from what it would have been if the other man had never come up on the voyage.

Sven had the stone in the boot from the Irish carpenter.

He had not, on three or four occasions in the three years since Buenos Aires, been able to entirely forget that he had the stone.

He did not want a second stone.

The fight, he had understood three days ago, was the one intervention that could prevent a second stone. The six feet of deck at supper on the evening of the fight was the fight's dividend. The six feet would become three feet again at some point in the next six months — he could not, at twenty-four, hold six feet on a bark for six months — but the three feet would never, from this evening onward, become the hand's width it had been at Biscay. That was the discipline's guarantee to him, and the fight's cost.

He was paying the cost.

---

He understood, at the windlass finishing his bread, one other thing. He understood that Hestby would, in some private hour of the night, work out what Sven had done.

Hestby was not a man who accepted the surface story about a bark-incident at twenty-three. Hestby had watched Brun take a sight after eighteen hours on deck; Hestby had sorted out Odegaard's chronometer; Hestby had negotiated Torgerson's tobacco down to half on a principle of fair trade. Hestby was, at twenty-three, a man who took in evidence and arranged it into stories. Given enough time — a week, two weeks — Hestby would arrange the evidence of the fight and would come, by a path Sven could not predict but could be sure of, to the correct answer: that Sven had taken the knife, that Sven had planted the knife under Torgerson's bench, that Sven had provoked the fight.

When Hestby arrived at the correct answer, Hestby would also arrive at the reason.

Sven did not, on the windlass at supper, fear the arrival.

Sven understood — with the flat clarity — that Hestby's arriving at the reason would not be a thing that broke the six feet. Hestby, having arrived at the reason, would respect the reason. Hestby would say nothing. Hestby would keep the six feet because Hestby would understand that the six feet was what Sven had bought with the fight and that the six feet was what Sven needed. Hestby would, in other words, enter into the discipline from the other side.

The discipline would, in entering from the other side, become the discipline of two men and not one.

That, Sven thought, was the answer to the question Sundberg's two-seconds-longer look had been asking.

The answer was: yes, I am the man's problem to solve, and I have solved it.

He finished his bread. He handed his plate to the boy. He went forward to the windlass bitts and put his back against the bitts and looked at the dark water of the creek, and he did not turn his head to where Hestby was, at the far end of the deck, putting his own plate aside and standing up to go to his watch.

He did not turn his head.

He had paid the cost.

He was not going to spend it.
