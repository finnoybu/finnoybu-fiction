The Indian Ocean was the ocean Olav had been warned about.

He had been warned about it by his father in the boat-shed at Finnøy the previous February, in a single sentence his father had offered without looking up from the work of peening a rivet: *the Indian Ocean is the ocean that will tell you whether the sea is the life or the season, and it will tell you at a pace no man can speed up.* He had been warned about it by Sundberg on the fifth day out of Cape Town at a mess-table exchange that had lasted perhaps six words: *this is a long one, Hestby.* He had been warned about it, most recently and most specifically, by Sven at the supper coil on the second day: *the Indian Ocean does not tell a man kindly.*

He had been warned. He had come into it anyway.

Four weeks out of Cape Town and the ocean had not, to the eye, delivered any of the warnings. The weather had held. The southeast trades — the wind that carried a bark east across the bottom of the Indian Ocean on the outbound leg to India — had been steady since the fifth day, blowing fifteen to twenty knots on the starboard quarter, running the *Semiramis* at seven or eight knots hour after hour over a long southeast swell. The sky was mostly clear. The sun came up on a sea that had, for twenty-six days now, looked the same, and it went down on a sea that had, for twenty-six days now, looked the same.

This, Olav understood, was the warning. The ocean was telling him about itself by not telling him anything.

The bark was, on the twenty-seventh day, at roughly thirty-four degrees south latitude and seventy-two degrees east longitude by the captain's noon reckoning. They were a thousand miles from any land in any direction. The nearest real port was Port Louis on Mauritius, which was two and a half days to the north. They were going there for nothing. They were going, across the next four weeks, toward the Bay of Bengal at the top of the ocean, and before they reached the Bay they would touch no land. The ocean had decided to be empty. The bark's work was to cross the emptiness without breaking.

The men were holding up. The work was steady — the daily routine of watches and meals and the small cleaning and mending the bark required of a crew that had been at sea seventy-nine days — and the work filled the time the emptiness might otherwise have hollowed out. Sundberg had been a bosun for long enough to know the signs of a crew beginning to go thin in a passage, and he watched for them; he had not, this week, had to do anything about any of the men.

Olav had also been watching. He had been watching the way a man watches who has decided that he is going to be a mate on some future ship and has begun, without announcing it to himself, to observe his own crew as a mate would. He had not told Sven he was doing it. He had not told Sundberg. He had simply, in the quiet weeks of the Indian Ocean, moved his eye to the crew and begun to log, in the part of himself that logged things, what each of the eighteen men was doing, and how they were doing it, and where the small fatigues were beginning to show.

Torgerson the steward was one of the eighteen.

---

Torgerson had not, since the first morning of the voyage, spoken more than the necessary to any man on board.

He was thirty. He was from Arendal. He had been a steward on Bergen ships for seven years. He ladled coffee at six bells and bread at eight, he kept the captain's cabin-linens, he ran the galley boy to ground when the galley boy ran elsewhere. He was missing his left small finger at the second joint; Olav had noticed that on the first day. He did not, in the eight weeks Olav had been on the *Semiramis,* smile. Olav had assumed, for the first three weeks, that this was a steward's face — that stewards were a brotherhood of men who had decided, some years into the trade, that smiling was a form of labor that did not pay — and he had not thought about it further.

On the twenty-eighth day out of Cape Town, at six bells of the afternoon watch, Torgerson came to Olav at the main fife rail, where Olav was coiling, and he said, in the voice of a man making a business proposal:

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"You gave the Ålesund boy his chronometer at Biscay."

"I did."

"Would you give it to me."

Olav looked at him.

Torgerson was standing at a polite distance — not close enough to share a conversation that was private, not far enough to be formal. His hands were at his sides. His face was the face it had always been: closed, not unfriendly, the face of a steward who had decided at twenty-five that he was not going to waste expression on men who did not require it. He was holding, in his right hand, a small cloth bag.

"You have been the steward on Bergen ships for seven years," Olav said. "Why have you not learned the chronometer before."

"I have not learned it because no man has ever offered. A mate does not teach a steward. A captain does not teach a steward. The steward does the galley and the linens and the cups. He does not go to the chart. I have watched the chart-work for seven years. I have never been shown how to read the thing the chart-work comes out of."

"You watched me teach the boy."

"I watched you teach the boy. You did it standing up. You did it in about four minutes. You did it without asking whether he had been taught or not. I want the four minutes. I want them in the evenings in the galley when the captain is below and the mate is at the rail. I have tobacco." He held out the cloth bag. "Good tobacco. A Bergen friend of mine has a cousin who buys from a man who buys from the Brazilian hands off the bark that calls at the Cape once a year. Four ounces a week. Eight weeks on the outbound, whatever we agree on at Calcutta. A pipe's-worth for each lesson."

Olav considered the proposal.

He did not, himself, smoke. He had smoked twice at Mrs. Kindingstad's at the dinner-table on Sundays when his landlady's husband had pressed a pipe into his hand, and he had not enjoyed either occasion. But tobacco on a bark was not only for smoking. Tobacco was the currency a common seaman used below the level of wages — it was how a man paid another man for a small favor, or bought his way into a card game, or acquired a pair of dry socks from a man whose sea-chest was better stocked than his own. Four ounces a week of good Brazilian tobacco, paid to a common seaman who was also a navigator, was a currency Olav could spend in the forecastle across the next fourteen months in ways that would compound.

He understood, also, that Torgerson's proposal was not really about tobacco.

The proposal was about Torgerson's having watched the chart-work for seven years and having decided, sometime in those seven years, that he was going to learn the chart-work himself on some voyage before he turned forty and not on a later one. The tobacco was the currency Torgerson was using because the tobacco was the currency a steward had. The steward was not a rich man. The tobacco was what he could offer. The proposal was, Olav understood, a serious one.

"Torgerson."

"Yes."

"I will give you the chronometer. I will also give you the noon sight if we get that far. I will not take the tobacco."

"You must take the tobacco."

"I do not smoke."

"The tobacco is not for your smoking. The tobacco is for your trading. You will find trading to do with it in a month. Take the tobacco."

Olav thought about this for a beat.

"Torgerson."

"Yes."

"I will take half. Two ounces a week. The other half you keep. A fair trade is a trade where both men have given up something. If I take the whole, you are the only one giving up. Two and two is the trade I will take."

Torgerson looked at him.

His face did the thing his face had not, in eight weeks, done: it moved. The left corner of the mouth rose by perhaps a quarter of an inch. The rise was not a smile. It was the small weathered acknowledgment of a steward who had, at twenty-eight or twenty-nine, decided that smiling was not labor he would pay, and who was, at thirty, letting the rule lapse for a beat because the situation had earned the lapse.

"Two and two," Torgerson said. "Good."

He put the bag on the fife rail. He turned and went forward.

---

The lessons began that evening.

Olav had asked for the galley at the first bell of the first night-watch, after the steward had served the supper and the captain had gone below. The galley at that hour was empty of anyone but Torgerson, who had banked the fire and was wiping down the steward's bench with a rag soaked in hot water. The lamp hung above the bench. There was room, on the bench, for two men with a slate between them.

Olav came in with the slate. He set it on the bench. Torgerson put down the rag and came and stood on the far side.

"First lesson," Olav said. "Reading. Before we touch the sight-taking. You read a chronometer backwards."

He explained, with the small slate in front of them, the method he had given the boy Odegaard in Biscay. Second first. Then minute. Then hour. The eye held each number before it moved on; the eye did not double back. The eye did not flick. The eye practiced the backwards reading on the cabin clock until the backwards reading was what the eye did without asking.

Torgerson listened without interrupting. He had, Olav noted, the stillness of a man who had been a steward for seven years and had therefore learned to listen at a depth that most men did not, because a steward who did not listen at depth was a steward who made expensive mistakes with the captain's coffee. He listened. He asked, at the end of the explanation, one question. The question was the question Olav had asked Nilson in the first week at Mrs. Kindingstad's.

"What if the eye reads wrong."

"Then you take the sight again in half a minute. A noon sight is not a single sight. A man takes four or five at noon and averages them. The wrong reading corrects itself in the averaging, as long as the wrong reading is not the same kind of wrong as the other readings. That is why you practice the backwards method until the eye does it automatically — because when the eye does it automatically, the eye does not bring its fears to the reading. The fears are what make the readings wrong the same way four times."

Torgerson nodded.

"Good. First lesson is a week of reading the cabin clock. Next Sunday evening we start on the sight."

"Yes."

Olav picked up the slate. He stood at the galley door for a moment.

"Torgerson."

"Yes."

"The small finger."

"A dogfish at Arendal harbor when I was eight. It took the joint before I got my hand out of the water. The knuckle is good. I can still hold a knife. A man can lose a joint of a finger and go on being a steward."

"Yes."

"You were going to ask."

"I was not."

"You were going to ask later."

"Yes. I was."

"Now you have asked."

"Yes."

"Good. Between two men who are going to sit across a slate three evenings a week for the next fourteen months, it is better that the questions that can be asked have been asked."

He went back to the bench.

Olav went out of the galley into the night. The bark was running east on the trade on a steady quarter-wind with the stars overhead at a thickness he would, he thought, never quite get used to. He stood for a moment at the main bitt and looked up at them.

---

He thought, at the bitt, about the things he had been teaching.

He had taught the Ålesund boy to read a chronometer. He had taught the steward to read a chronometer. He would teach him, in the next fourteen months, to take a sight. He had, in the last three months, become the man at the *Semiramis*'s fife rail who taught. He had become this man by accident and by the small pull of the bark's structure — a bark without a third mate tended, in the quiet middle of a long passage, to let its navigator-common-seaman teach whatever the crew wanted to be taught, because the teaching occupied hours that the ocean would otherwise have hollowed — and he had become it also, he thought at the bitt, by the small private decision he had made at the supper coil in Biscay when Sven had asked him whether he would take Brun's mate's berth on the next voyage and he had said *no.*

The no had meant that he was not going to be a mate on a future *Semiramis.* It had also meant, without his having understood it at the time, that he was going to be, on this *Semiramis,* a man who taught. Because if he was not going to take the path of a long career as a mate, he had to give away, in the fourteen months he had left on this voyage, the things he had been given in the four years since his own voyage on the *Diana.* The chronometer. The sight. The feel for where the ship was. The quiet particular calm a navigator had at a chart table.

He could not take all of it home to the boat-shed at Finnøy and store it in the drawer above the stove. He had to spend some of it on the voyage.

Torgerson was one of the places to spend it.

The Ålesund boy was one of the places to spend it.

Sven — Sven was also a place, but a different kind of place, and Olav did not, at the bitt, allow himself to work out what kind — was going to be one of them before the voyage was out.

He understood, at the bitt on the twenty-eighth day out of Cape Town with the trades steady on the starboard quarter and the Southern Cross low on the horizon off the starboard quarter, that the voyage was going to be a voyage of giving away the things he had been given. He had not known this at the Stavanger quay. He had not known it at Cape Town. He knew it now.

The knowing was not sad. It was the small private clarity a man got when he understood a shape of his own life he had not previously understood.

He went below.

---

In the forecastle he lay in his hammock and listened to the bark.

Four hammocks down Sven was asleep. Two hammocks over Odegaard was awake and trying not to be — Olav could hear the shallow careful breath a young man made when he was trying to pretend to be asleep so that a slightly older man he looked up to would not think he was not holding up. Odegaard was holding up. He would sleep in twenty minutes. Olav did not, tonight, intervene. Intervening was not always what teaching was.

He thought of Olava.

The letter from Cape Town was, by his rough figure, at Bergen now, waiting for the Lindøy coaster. It would be on the coaster on Friday. She would have it by next Tuesday. She would read it at the kitchen table in the lamplight.

He thought about the letter he would write at Calcutta.

The letter at Calcutta would say the things the Cape Town letter had said: the ship is well, I am well, the tea is holding me. It would also say some things the Cape Town letter had not said — that the ocean was long, that the steward was learning the chronometer, that the Ålesund boy had his chronometer reading right, that the stars at thirty-four degrees south were a different arrangement of the same stars. The letter at Calcutta would not say what had happened at the royal yard with Sven. The letter at Calcutta would not say what was going to happen to him in the ports ahead that he had not, on the second day of the voyage, been braced for.

She would, reading the letter at Calcutta, know that the letter was the letter that had been written. She would, knowing it, put it in the folder and not ask. The not-asking was the ledger. The ledger was the marriage.

He slept.

The bark ran on east.
