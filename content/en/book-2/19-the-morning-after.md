The bark cleared the Sagar pilot-station on the evening of the second day of the homeward leg.

Banerjee went down into the pilot-brig with a nod to Olav that was the same nod he had given at the end of the first Hooghly descent — the small formal English handshake of a man who had, a second time, piloted the bark without needing to in the sense that mattered. Olav shook it back. He watched the pilot-brig's lantern go down the ebb in the dusk. When the lantern was below the horizon he went forward.

The Bay of Bengal opened out to the east.

The wind was a northeasterly at about ten knots, a quieter trade than the southwest monsoon had been at the outbound, and the bark set her course a little south of east with her royals loose and her topgallants full. The chronometers in the captain's cabin were ticking toward London mean time. The homeward leg, by Brun's schedule, was a hundred and twenty-seven days — the two months across the Indian Ocean, the week around the Cape, the six weeks up the Atlantic, the two weeks through the Channel, the last week up to Bergen — and the bark, at her first evening of the leg, went to her rhythm with the even particular calm a deep-water ship had at the beginning of a return.

The six feet of deck between the windlass and the foot of the foremast held.

It held at the supper. It held at the first dog-watch. It held at the middle watch. It held at the breakfast of the first morning. It held at the four bells of the forenoon when Olav was sent to the main topgallant to inspect a line and Sven was sent to the fore topsail to inspect another line, and the two of them, coming down from their separate climbs at the same two minutes, did not — by a minute calibration on the part of both of them that the deck did not register — reach the deck at the same minute. Olav came down first. Sven came down ninety seconds later.

Both of them understood, at the bottom of their respective shrouds, that the minute-and-a-half had been arranged. Neither of them said anything to the other about it. They went to their watches.

---

Olav had, in the two days of the dry-dock, not written in his small bound book. He had not written because writing at Mrs. Lauritsen's would have been — in a way he had not been able to think through at the time but understood after — a kind of self-narration he had not wanted in the room. He had, on the pilot's morning of the Thursday, made a single-line entry under the Monday's page: *Two nights at Mrs. Lauritsen's, Russell Street. Good house. Back on board.*

He had written nothing else about it.

On the evening of the second day of the leg he opened the book at his sea-chest in the forecastle dark — the rest of the watch below asleep or pretending to be — and he stared at the blank page that came after the *back on board* line.

He had thought about the blank page several times in the two days since Thursday. He had thought about writing on it the thing he had not written at Mrs. Lauritsen's. He had thought about writing down the particulars he had wanted to remember — the soap smell at Sven's collar, the sound of the canvas bag against the chair-leg, the weight of the hand on his face in the small hours, the names Sven had given him in the dark of the second evening — because a navigator was a man who wrote particulars down and because the particulars of the two nights were the particulars he had, in his life, not previously been given to write.

He had thought also about not writing any of it.

He thought about the not-writing at the blank page in the forecastle dark. He thought about what a written page would mean. A written page would mean a page that existed. A page that existed was a page a man could, in theory, be made to produce. A page that existed was a page a wife might, in the thirty years of a marriage, open at the wrong moment. A page that existed was a page the owner of it had to remember where he had put.

He thought: *the only safe place for the two nights is my head.*

He thought: *my head is not a perfectly safe place. My head will not, at sixty, remember with the accuracy my pencil can remember. My head will remember Sven's face in the gray light of the Wednesday morning, and my head will remember the soap smell at his collar, and my head will remember the particular count Sven gave me with his hand on my face in the small hours. My head will forget other things. My head will, in the forgetting, soften the two nights into a set of general impressions that will not be exactly what the two nights were.*

He thought: *that is what I have to accept. The two nights were mine to live. They are not mine to preserve. The preserving is a thing that belongs to a man who was allowed to have lived a thing publicly. I did not live these nights publicly. I cannot preserve them in the way a man preserves a public thing. I have the memory. The memory is imperfect. The imperfection is the cost.*

He closed the book without writing on the blank page.

He put the book back in the chest under the leather folder.

He lay in his hammock.

He listened to the bark.

---

On the third night of the leg he saw Sven at the forward rail at the first dog-watch.

Sven was leaning on the cap-rail with his elbows on it and his hands folded and his eyes on the wake. He was alone. It was the kind of quarter-hour Olav had come to know as Sven's — the quarter-hour between the supper and the first bell — and it was the same quarter-hour Olav had, at Biscay and at Cape Town and at the Indian Ocean before Calcutta, stood at his own part of the rail at a separate place along the bark's length.

On this evening Olav did not stand at his separate place.

He came to the rail beside Sven.

He came to the rail beside him with six feet of cap-rail between them, which was the six feet of the deck translated into the vertical plane of the rail, and he stood with his own hands folded and his own eyes on the wake, and for perhaps three minutes neither of them said anything.

Then Sven, without turning his head:

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"You are closer than the six feet."

"I know."

"Why."

"Because the six feet cannot always be six feet."

Sven considered this.

He did not move his hands from the cap-rail.

"You are telling me it can be five feet at the rail at the first dog-watch on a Sunday evening."

"I am telling you it can be five feet at the rail at the first dog-watch on the nights we do not go around the world asking it to be six."

"And the rest of the time it is six."

"The rest of the time it is six. Yes."

Sven turned his head then.

He looked at Olav. His face in the dusk had the particular small weariness of a man who had, at Mrs. Lauritsen's, used up a piece of what he had been holding, and who had not yet replenished it, and who was — at four days out of Calcutta — still operating on a depleted store of the particular thing a married Bergen man needed to hold the discipline.

"I can do five feet on Sundays at the rail at the first dog-watch," Sven said.

"Then Sundays at the rail at the first dog-watch is the five feet."

"Yes."

"That is the deal."

"That is the deal."

"Good."

Sven turned back to the wake.

They stood for another three minutes without speaking.

Then the bell went for the first dog, and the mate's voice came from the quarterdeck for the watch change, and Sven took his elbows off the cap-rail and went aft to his watch, and Olav took his hands off the cap-rail and stayed at his own place, and the five feet on Sundays at the rail became the small agreed exception to the six feet that was the rest of the voyage's geometry.

---

Odegaard asked him a question at the galley door on the morning of the seventh day.

The boy had come to him, earlier in the week, with a small further chronometer problem — a correction in the application of the equation of time that Nilson had given Olav at Stavanger in the autumn of '79 but that Olav had not yet passed to the boy — and Olav had been at the galley door on the Monday morning to explain it to him. The boy had followed the explanation. The boy was no longer, at four months at sea, the boy he had been at Biscay. He was, by now, a competent reader of the chronometer and was beginning to ask the small intelligent questions a young man who would be a navigator in five years asked.

The question he asked at the galley door that morning was not about the chronometer.

"Hestby."

"Odegaard."

"I want to ask a thing that is not about the work."

"Ask."

The boy hesitated. Then, with the small pink at the ears a sixteen-year-old Ålesund boy had when he was asking a thing he was embarrassed about:

"Were you and Haugen at the same lodging-house at Calcutta."

Olav looked at him for a beat.

"Why do you ask that, Odegaard."

"Because Reuss said at breakfast that you were. He said he walked by a Danish place on Russell Street on his way back on the Wednesday night and saw Haugen at the upper window. He said he had asked Haugen about it on the bark yesterday and Haugen had said he did not know what Reuss was talking about, and then Reuss had said at the table this morning that he was sure Haugen had been there. I am asking you because I do not want to ask Haugen. I do not think I should be asking anyone. I am asking you because if I do not ask anyone, I will ask myself, and I do not trust my own answer at sixteen about anything that is a thing I am asking myself."

Olav understood, at the boy's words, several things at the same time.

He understood that Reuss had seen Sven at the window. He understood that Reuss had made a remark about it at the forecastle breakfast. He understood that whatever Reuss had said had been said in the particular tone men of Reuss's kind used when they were making a remark about another man that they did not wish to be responsible for having made. He understood that the tone had reached Odegaard, and that Odegaard — sixteen, honest, not yet trained out of the asking of questions he did not, at sixteen, have the equipment to not ask — had come to him.

He also understood that the question was, at the galley door, the first test of the architecture that Sven had built at the fight and that they had both held since.

He did not, in thinking through the test, take more than the count of one breath.

"Odegaard."

"Yes."

"I was at a place called Mrs. Lauritsen's on Russell Street. I booked the room on the Monday through the consul. I went up on the Tuesday evening. It is a Danish Christian lodging-house run by an older woman who keeps it for Scandinavian clerks and for the Scandinavian sailors who want a quiet place instead of the Bowbazar. Haugen came in at the Tuesday late after the supper. I did not know he was coming. He had been at Bowbazar and had left. He asked the landlady for a fourth room. He stayed the two nights. He slept in his own room. I slept in mine. We did not know, before he came in the door, that either of us was at the house. Reuss did see him at the window. Haugen was, I suspect, looking at the street because a man in a new lodging-house looks at the street. That is what happened."

The boy looked at him.

"You slept in separate rooms."

"We slept in separate rooms."

"Why did Haugen say he did not know what Reuss was talking about."

"Because Haugen knew that Reuss had made the remark in a tone, and he did not wish to be responsible for a tone he had not made. He denied being at the house because the denying was the shortest way out of a conversation he did not want to have with Reuss at the forecastle breakfast table."

"I understand."

"Odegaard."

"Yes."

"You are going to, in the next eight days, hear a version of this from Reuss again. Reuss is, I think, going to continue to make the remark, because Reuss is a man who has decided that the remark is the remark he is going to make on this voyage. The remark is not, in itself, a bad remark. It is only a remark. You are going to hear it. You are not going to know what to do with it."

"Yes."

"I am going to tell you what to do with it. You are going to let Reuss make the remark. You are not going to agree with it and you are not going to contradict it. You are going to say, when you are asked, the thing I have told you: that you understand Haugen was at Mrs. Lauritsen's, that he had his own room, that you do not know more than that because it is not your business to know more. That is the sentence. You will not add to it. You will not shorten it. You will say it in the flat tone of a sixteen-year-old Ålesund boy who has been asked and is answering. Is that understood."

"Yes."

"Good. Also, Odegaard."

"Yes."

"Thank you for asking me. You were right to ask me and not Haugen. I am not going to mention that you asked me."

"Thank you, Hestby."

"Go eat your breakfast."

The boy went back to the galley line.

Olav stood at the galley door for another minute.

He understood, at the door, that he had just told the boy a version of the two nights that was not the whole truth but was not a lie. He understood that the version he had told was the version the rest of his life was going to depend on. He understood that Sven, on the bark, was telling Reuss and Lie and whoever else the same version, in the same small parts, in the same flat tone, and that the version was — in the small daily maintenance of it — the shape the discipline was going to take from now on.

The discipline, from now on, was not going to be the discipline of keeping. It was going to be the discipline of saying.

He had the saying now.

He would have it for the rest of his life.

---

Later that evening, at his own rail-place — not Sven's, not the Sunday five-feet — Olav thought about what he had said to the boy.

He thought about the word *separate rooms* and about the word *slept.* He thought about the fact that both words had been, in the boy's hearing of them, what the boy had wanted to hear and had therefore been the words that had, at the galley door, resolved the boy's question. He thought about the fact that the words had not been what had happened. He thought about the fact that he had said them anyway.

He thought: *I have told a version of a thing that is not the thing. I have told it to a sixteen-year-old at a galley door on a bark at sea. I will, in the years to come, tell other versions of it to other people at other doors. The versions will, each of them, be shaped by who is at the door and what that person can be told without being harmed. The versions will never be, any of them, the whole thing. The whole thing is for me.*

He thought: *this is what the older men mean when they say a man has a life of his own.*

He thought: *I am, at twenty-three, coming into the life of my own. I did not understand, at the quay in June, what the phrase meant. I understand now. A life of one's own is the life nobody else gets to see the whole of. It is the life the letters home do not describe. It is the life the wife does not ask about. It is the life the boatswain does not file. It is the life the older men have been telling the younger men they would have, and the younger men, hearing the phrase, think it is a gift. It is not a gift. It is the cost of being someone.*

He thought: *I am paying the cost.*

He thought: *I am going to be paying it, in small daily installments, for the rest of my life.*

He thought: *I am not, at the rail on the seventh night of the Indian Ocean leg home, sorry to be paying it.*

He went below.

He slept.

The bark ran on across the Bay of Bengal on the quiet northeasterly, Sven's hammock five down from Olav's in the port bank of the forecastle, the six feet of deck maintained by daylight, the five feet held on Sunday evenings at the rail at the first dog-watch, the homeward leg now in its seventh day and its four months and a fortnight ahead of it, and Olav — at twenty-three, the navigator of record on the bark, the man who had told a sixteen-year-old a version of a thing at a galley door — asleep in his hammock with the two nights at Mrs. Lauritsen's put, for the first time since the Thursday, in the drawer his life was going to keep them in.

The drawer was the same drawer.

It was fuller.

It held.
