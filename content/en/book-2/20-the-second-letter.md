The second letter came through on a Tuesday at the beginning of March.

It had left Calcutta, by the Norwegian consul Møller's careful stamp on the cover, on the first P&O homeward steamer after the *Semiramis* had lifted — the ninth of November — and had gone by Aden and Southampton and Bergen and the coastal mail to Lindøy, and it arrived at the Lindøy jetty on the Tuesday coaster with three other letters for three other Lindøy houses, in the second week of a wet Norwegian March. Olava was at the kitchen table.

She was reviewing, when her mother came in with the letter, the small folded sheet of paper she had taken out of the back of the ledger that morning and had laid flat on the table to read. The sheet was her forecast of the voyage — the six anchor-dates she had calculated at her desk in June — and she was reviewing it because the March letter had been forecast for *sometime in February or the first week of March,* and the first week of March had passed, and she had begun to be the kind of woman who looked at the forecast on the days it had not yet resolved.

Her mother set the letter down on the table beside the forecast without speaking.

Olava put her hand on the letter. She did not open it. She looked first at the cover.

The cover said: *Miss Olava Lindøy, Lindøy by Stavanger, Norway,* in Olav's hand. The upper-left said: *Bark Semiramis, Calcutta, first week November 1881.* The wafer was the small red one.

She took the letter up to her room.

---

She did not, this time, sit at the desk and look at the letter before reading it. She sat, and she broke the wafer with her thumbnail, and she read.

The letter said:

> *Olava,*
>
> *We are at Calcutta for the second time. This letter is being given to the consul Møller this afternoon to post on the next P&O steamer home. We lifted Rangoon on the twenty-eighth of October. The passage from Rangoon was twelve days. We are in dry-dock for three days at a yard up the river while the carpenter refastens a plank at the garboard that worked at the seams on the Bay of Bengal run. The work is a precaution. The ship is sound. The work will be finished by Thursday noon.*
>
> *I saw the Shwedagon. I went on the Thursday morning with a deckhand from the watch, as the captain ordered me to. The captain had said a man does not go to a temple alone in a city he has not been to before. I went. The pagoda is the gold thing the handbook's colored plates show. It is also the thing the handbook does not show, which is the scale. I stood at the eastern gate for perhaps a minute before I crossed into the platform. I took off my cap. I looked. I walked the platform for about an hour. I did not, in the hour, think anything I can report; I only looked. I promised at Cape Town that I would see it for you. I have. I am writing it down as promised.*
>
> *The captain has offered me a mate's berth for the next Semiramis voyage at my sign-off in Bergen in November of next year. I refused it. I wrote to you in the Cape Town letter about the refusal, because at Cape Town it was fresh and the captain had just told me I was a fortunate woman's husband-to-be. I am writing again at Calcutta because I have had a month to think about the refusal and the refusal has not changed its shape. I will come home in November of next year. I will come home and I will marry you in the spring. The next Semiramis voyage will lift in September with a different mate. That is the arrangement.*
>
> *The voyage is going well. The ship is well. I am well. The cough has not come. The tea — at sea, not the Indian tea ashore, which is a different thing — is holding me. I have been eating it. I will continue.*
>
> *Torgerson the steward has finished his first course with me and has begun taking his own noon sights on Thursdays at the galley table. He is a faster student than the Ålesund boy on deck. The boy is also coming along but more slowly because he is sixteen and has other things in his head that Torgerson at thirty no longer has.*
>
> *We had an ape come aboard at the Bassein approaches from a country-boat that sold us coconuts. He was in the second basket. The captain has given him to my keeping. He is named Bassein. He has a small crate on the larboard foredeck behind the windlass. He is intelligent and untroublesome. He eats coconut and ship's biscuit and occasionally lemon rinds. I feed him at six bells of the morning watch. He has taken to knowing my step.*
>
> *Marit's engagement being almost on the paper — I read your last letter four times. Tell her I send my regards and that I will, if the paper has come by the time of my next letter from the Cape, write her a letter of my own.*
>
> *The Calcutta city itself is the size of Christiania and Stockholm put together. I have been to it on the captain's business three times. I have not been to it on my own business once, except on the Monday before the dry-docking when I went to the Asiatic Society's library on Park Street to read the Bengal Tidal Observatory reports, which I needed for the Hooghly descent. I spent an hour at the library. It was cool. It was quiet. It had a smell I have not smelled since Mrs. Kindingstad's kitchen at Christmas of '79. That is the second thing I am writing down for you from this leg.*
>
> *We lift on Thursday noon for the homeward. The captain says a hundred and twenty-seven days to Bergen, across two oceans. I will see you at Lindøy before the end of November, and on the ninety-sixth day of this letter's travel you will have got the Cape letter from the homeward leg — that will be the one with Saint Helena in the date-line — which I am thinking of now and have not yet written.*
>
> *Yours, Olav.*

She read the letter four times, as she had the first.

She did, this time, the small separate reading for the things the letter had not said.

---

The letter, she understood at the third reading, had not said any of the things a woman in her position — Olava Lindøy, twenty-one years old, engaged to a man at sea on a bark that was turning for home after nine months of an eighteen-month voyage — might reasonably have been waiting to hear.

The letter had not said *I miss you,* which was a sentence she had not expected and was not going to have held him to.

The letter had not said *I love you,* which was a sentence their particular arrangement had, at Christmas of '79, dispensed with as a thing that did not need to be written into letters and that would continue to be dispensed with until Olav was home.

The letter had not said *I have done a thing I need to tell you.* This, she registered, was the sentence she had been waiting — not for, because she had not been asked by herself to wait for any sentence in particular, but at the edge of — for several months. The sentence had been the shape of a sentence she had understood, since the November Monday of the engagement on her father's jetty, was a sentence that might at some point come from Olav in a letter from a foreign port.

It had not come in this letter.

She sat with the absence of it for a minute.

She thought, with the careful flat register she used for her interior work on Olav's letters, about the absence.

She thought: *the absence is a fact. It may mean there is no such sentence to be written. It may also mean the sentence exists but has not, at the writing of this letter, been a sentence he has decided to send. The absence by itself does not tell me which. I have agreed not to ask. I am not going to ask.*

She thought: *I am, however, going to note.*

She took the ledger out of the drawer.

She turned to the page she had prepared in June. She crossed out the phrase *Calcutta letter / February first or second week* and she wrote, in the smaller careful final-entry hand:

> *Calcutta letter received 10 Mar 1882. Posted 9 Nov 1881, per the cover. Seventeen weeks in transit; longer than I forecast, but within the P&O's ordinary variation for the India route in winter. Three pages. Hand consistent with the Cape letter. He has seen the Shwedagon for me. He has refused the next Semiramis voyage and has confirmed November homecoming and spring wedding. He is teaching the steward navigation. He has an ape on the foredeck. He has been to the Asiatic Society library and has not been to the Calcutta city on his own business except for the library. He has not been ashore for ordinary shore-leave, by any reading of what the letter says and does not say.*

She stopped the pen.

She did not move to the small indent she kept for private readings.

She considered, for a moment, whether to write in the indent what she had registered at the third reading about the absent sentence.

She decided not to.

She decided not because she thought the absent sentence unworthy of noting — the absent sentence was, she understood, precisely the sort of absence that a thoughtful ledger-keeper would note — but because the noting of it would, for her, formalize an expectation she had decided eighteen months ago not to formalize. She had agreed at Christmas not to ask. The not-asking had been a discipline. The not-asking had, as its corollary, a not-preparing — a deliberate refusal to have built in herself an expectation of the sentence, so that when or if the sentence came, she would receive it without the reception being a reception she had rehearsed.

She had not rehearsed.

She was not, by writing the absent sentence into the ledger's private indent, going to begin rehearsing.

She closed the ledger.

She put the letter in the folder.

---

She went down to the kitchen.

The lamp was lit. Her mother was at the stove. Her father was at the table with the week-old Stavanger newspaper open in front of his good hand. Marit was at the window with her coffee.

"Olava."

"Yes."

"The letter came."

"The letter came. He is at Calcutta for the second time. They were lifting on the Thursday after the writing for the homeward. The ship is sound. He is well. He has seen the gold pagoda at Rangoon, as he promised at Cape Town. He has refused the next *Semiramis* voyage, as he wrote me at Cape Town that he had told the captain he would. He has an ape on the foredeck."

"An ape."

"A small Burmese monkey that came aboard from a coconut-boat. He is keeping it. He has named it Bassein."

Marit at the window made the small particular laugh Marit made when a piece of news was the kind she would, at eighteen, take into her head and turn over for an afternoon. She had, Olava registered, already decided that the monkey was going to be the news of this letter as far as Marit was concerned, and Olava was not going to take the monkey from her.

"An ape," Marit said. "He is keeping an ape."

"He is keeping an ape."

Her father looked up.

"The captain permits this."

"The captain gave the ape into his keeping."

"The captain is either a softer man than he looks or he enjoys an experiment."

"He is not, Papa, a softer man. He has written, in Olav's hand, as the kind of thing Olav would notice. I think the captain wanted the ape kept and did not want to keep it himself and gave it to the man he had decided was the ship's keeper of small responsibilities."

"Then the captain has formed an opinion of Olav."

"Yes. The letter says so elsewhere. The captain has offered him the next *Semiramis* voyage as first mate. Olav has refused."

Her father considered this. He folded his newspaper. He set it to one side. He put both hands — the wooden one and the good one — on the table flat, which was his gesture when a piece of news had moved a thing in his head from one room to another.

"He has refused."

"He has refused."

"Because of the spring wedding."

"Because of the spring wedding."

"That is the refusal of a man who is the man I gave my daughter to last November, Olava."

"I know, Papa."

"Good. We will not have a son-in-law who sails the autumn of his wedding."

"No."

"Good."

He opened the newspaper again. Marit at the window watched him for a beat. Her mother at the stove did not, as usual, say anything. She stirred the porridge. She moved the kettle to the rear of the stove. She brought the three bowls to the table. The routine of the kitchen closed around the letter's news the way the routine of the kitchen always did — without ceremony, without pause, the letter already a piece of the ordinary record of the morning.

---

Later, at her desk upstairs, Olava wrote back.

The letter she wrote was shorter than his, as her letters always were. She wrote:

> *Olav,*
>
> *Your Calcutta letter arrived on the 10th of March. Seventeen weeks, longer than I had forecast, but in the P&O's winter variation. It is in the folder.*
>
> *You have seen the Shwedagon for me. Thank you. I read the paragraph about the eastern gate four times. I will read it a fifth time tomorrow and a sixth time next week, and at some point in the next eighteen months I will have read it often enough that I will begin to see it for myself in the way a woman at Lindøy can see a thing a man wrote to her from a platform in a Burmese city, which is to say dimly and at a distance and with my own embellishments the paragraph did not ask for. I am not going to be ashamed of the embellishments. That is what a wife's reading is.*
>
> *You have refused the next Semiramis voyage. My father heard the news at breakfast. He put both hands on the table. Then he said you were the man he gave his daughter to last November. He opened the newspaper again and said nothing more. That is how he told me what I already knew.*
>
> *The ape. Marit has laid full claim to the ape. If you bring him back to Norway with you he will be at Lindøy by the Wednesday coaster and Marit will have named him twice more and taught him one Norwegian word by Friday. I am telling you this so that if you were intending to bring him back you will know what the country-boat at the Hooghly has begun. I do not know what you are going to do with him at Bergen. I assume you will find him a household. If you do not I will find him one. He should not be released into a Stavanger winter.*
>
> *Marit's engagement is on the paper as of the first Sunday in February. She will have the small wedding at Pentecost next year. She is very serious about the planning and very pleased and has stopped teasing me, at least in the hours she is at home, about the letters. Tell her — when you write the letter of your own you proposed at the Cape — that you know. She will be pleased to be told directly.*
>
> *I had forecast February for this letter. I was wrong by a week. I will adjust the forecast in the ledger; the Calcutta-winter variation is now a known quantity. The Cape letter for the homeward I have forecast for the third week of June. The St Helena letter, if you write one, I have for early August. The next after that is Copenhagen in October and then Bergen itself at your arrival.*
>
> *Six letters ahead of us. I am keeping the count.*
>
> *You are well. The cough has not come. The tea is holding. I read those sentences last, because I wanted them last, because the last paragraph of a letter is what stays with a woman in the hours after she reads it.*
>
> *Yours, Olava.*

She folded the letter. She addressed it to *Mr. Olav Hestby, bark Semiramis of Bergen, c/o the Norwegian consul, Cape Town.* She marked it *Please hold for arrival.* She sealed it with the red wafer.

The letter would go down on the Friday coaster to Stavanger. From Stavanger it would go on the English mail to Southampton. From Southampton to Cape Town would be — by her calculation — about five weeks. It would reach the consul at the Cape by the middle of April. The *Semiramis* was, by her schedule, due at the Cape at the beginning of May. The consul would hand it to Olav when he came ashore.

She put the letter on the tray by the door.

She went to bed.

She did not, before she slept, reopen Olav's letter. She had, she was beginning to understand, a discipline of her own — the discipline a woman ashore built for herself to match the discipline the man afloat had built for himself — and her discipline was that she read the letter four times at its arrival and four times never again. She read it four times so that she had the letter in her; she stopped reading so that the having did not become an obsession she did not want to live in.

She had Olav's letter in her.

She had the absent sentence in her also, even though she had not written it down.

She slept.

In the folder on the shelf above the desk the second letter joined the first; and the ledger with its six forecasts, now five, lay closed in the drawer under the desk; and the house at Lindøy, in its ordinary March quiet, held Olava on the night of the second letter as it had held her on the night of the first, and would hold her through the four letters ahead of her between this Tuesday and Olav's coming home in November.
