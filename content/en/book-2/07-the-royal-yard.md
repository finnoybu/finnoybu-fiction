They were twenty-one days east of the Cape when the main royal sheet chafed through.

It chafed through at six bells of the afternoon watch on a Thursday, in a wind that had not been particularly strong — a fifteen-knot southerly with the bark making eight and a half knots on her larboard tack — and the chafing was the kind of small careless wear that had not been the fault of any particular man and was no one's to explain. The royal sail carried away at the corner. The cry went up from the lookout; the mate turned from the binnacle; Sundberg, who had been at the foredeck, came aft at a half-run. The topgallant was reefed off the starboard side; the royal was a ragged piece of canvas flapping at the yardarm that was going, if it was not brought in, to take the yard with it in the next quarter hour.

Sundberg picked two men out of the watch without looking.

"Haugen. Hestby. Aloft."

They went.

---

Olav climbed because his hands and feet climbed before his mind asked them to. This was the difference between eighteen and twenty-three that he had registered at Cape Town two months before — the body that had decided the climbing was the body's work and did not require permission at each rung. He came up the main shrouds, over the top, up the topmast shrouds to the crosstrees, over, up the topgallant shrouds, over the topgallant crosstrees — a fitting the bark's size required that the *Sigrid* had not had — and up the single royal backstay to the royal yard. Sven was, through the whole climb, five feet below him on the same shroud, moving at the same rate, breathing steadily through his nose.

At the royal yard Olav went out on the footrope to starboard and Sven went out to port.

The ragged sail was at the starboard yardarm. The sheet had carried away at the block and a strip of canvas perhaps two feet wide was flapping, half still bent to the yard at the earing and half torn free. The yard itself was riding the flap, swinging harder than a royal yard of this weight should swing, and Olav understood in the first two seconds at the yard that the job was the job a boatswain would normally have given to three men, that Sundberg had sent only two because three on this yard in this wind was one more than the yard would hold, and that the work, whatever they could make of it, was going to be work done fast.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"Come to the earing."

Olav came along the footrope to the earing. The footrope swung under his boots. The bark heeled. He clamped his belly to the yard and hooked his right arm through the jackstay and brought his left hand to the earing.

Sven was at the earing from the port side, having come along the yard with his own belly on the yard and his own hands on the jackstay, and they were, by the time they had both reached the earing, breathing each on the other man's jaw. A royal yard was not — when two men were at the earing — a place of much space. Their shoulders were touching. Their hips were touching. Sven's right hand, holding the jackstay, was against the inside of Olav's right arm at the bicep where the coat had pulled up. The inside of Sven's wrist was against the inside of Olav's elbow. Their two left hands were at the earing together, Olav's below Sven's by the breadth of a knuckle.

"The earing is foul," Sven said, quietly, into Olav's ear because the wind would not otherwise have carried it. "It has jammed at the cleat. I cannot get it free without my knife. I need you to pull on the flap while I work the cleat. Can you hold the weight."

"Yes."

"Then hold."

Olav took the torn strip of canvas in his left hand. He pulled. The strip came free of the yard by three inches. The yard, released of the weight of the flapping at its far end, steadied. Sven, his left hand free of the earing now because Olav was taking the tension, slid a knife from the sheath at his belt and worked the cleat.

The work took perhaps ninety seconds. In those ninety seconds Olav held the canvas taut with his left hand and held the jackstay with his right hand and kept his belly on the yard, and he did not move — because to move would have dropped the tension and let the sail flap again — and Sven at his shoulder did not move except at the left hand that was working the cleat. They were held, by the mechanics of the work, in the position they had come into at the earing, and the position was close enough that Olav could feel Sven's breath at the side of his face, at the hinge of his jaw where Sven's mouth had spoken into him, and he could feel the warmth of Sven's ribs through two layers of wool against his own ribs, and he could feel the steady unhurried rise and fall of Sven's breath because Sven was a man who, at a royal yard in a fifteen-knot southerly at a fouled earing, did not lose the rhythm of his breath.

Olav registered all of this.

He did not permit the registering to do anything to his own breath. He kept his own breath steady. He kept his hand on the canvas steady. The cleat gave under Sven's knife with a small particular clunk, and the earing came free, and the sail dropped into the bunt, and Sven said, in the same quiet into the same ear:

"Good. Now the gasket. You go down to the fid. I will pass you the earing across the yard."

"Yes."

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"You held that well."

"I held what I had to hold."

"Yes. You did."

Sven moved back along the yard to his side. Olav came down three feet along the footrope to the fid. They worked the sail into the gasket the way the bark's two-man pattern on a royal required — Sven gathering the bunt from his side, Olav gathering from his, then the earings crossed, then the gasket wound, then the final turns made fast at the footrope. The work took seven minutes. By the end of it the sail was furled cleanly enough that a mate inspecting from the deck would not, on a casual look, have seen that the canvas was torn.

They came down.

At the deck Sundberg looked at them. He looked at the yard. He looked at them again.

"Good work," he said.

"Yes," Sven said.

Olav did not say anything. He went forward.

---

At the forecastle water-cask he filled his tin mug and he drank the water down in one long draft, and when he had emptied the mug he stood for a moment with his back against the bulkhead and let his hands shake for the small private count they had earned by the work.

His hands shook not because the work had been dangerous — the work had been, on the scale of the work a common seaman did on a deep-water bark, only moderately dangerous, and he had done more dangerous at Wilmington and at the royal yard of the *Sigrid* in the calm of '76 — but because his body had registered, in the seven minutes at the earing with Sven's shoulder at his shoulder and Sven's breath at his jaw, a thing the body had not been asked to register since the lee of the galley on the *Sigrid*.

The *Sigrid*'s galley had been five years ago.

He had decided, at the third day of his marriage to the engagement in the winter of 1880, that the body was not going to be asked again. He had kept the decision. He had kept it through the school. He had kept it through Easter at Lindøy. He had kept it through the engagement itself. He had kept it through the two months on the *Semiramis* thus far. He had kept it, in fact, without noticing that he was keeping it, because the keeping had gone, in the eighteen months since the engagement, into the body as a habit rather than a decision.

The habit had not been broken at the earing. But the habit had, at the earing, been made aware of itself.

He let his hands shake.

He did not, in the shaking, let them shake for long. He took another mug of water. He drank it. The shaking eased. He stood at the water-cask for another minute with his eyes closed and he thought, with the flat clarity his interior used for the thoughts it did not wish to dwell in:

*That was seamanship.*

*That was the work the bosun sent two men to.*

*That was the work the two men were sent to because they were the two best hands at that yard in that wind, and because the earing had jammed and needed to be freed in under two minutes, and because three men on the yard would have broken the yard.*

*That was seamanship. That is the label. The label is a true label as far as the work.*

He opened his eyes. He went aft to his watch.

---

He did not, for the rest of the watch, seek out Sven.

He did not avoid him either. Sven was at the foredeck; Olav was at the main. They had, at five bells of the first dog-watch, passed each other at the starboard rail on the way to different lines, and Sven had looked at him the way Sven looked at him — the small private dryness, the not-unkindness — and Olav had nodded the nod a man gave another man who had done good work with him at a yardarm, and they had passed.

At supper Sven came and sat on the coil of rope beside him in the usual place. The hand's width of coil between them was the hand's width it had been since the second day.

They ate for a minute without speaking.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"The earing."

"Yes."

"You understood what I asked you to do before I had finished asking."

"I could see what needed to be done."

"I know. I am saying I am glad you are the man on this watch who is aloft with me at a fouled earing. I am not glad about it in any way that the mate or the bosun would be interested in. I am saying it man to man."

"Yes."

"That is all I am saying at supper tonight."

"Yes."

They finished their supper. Sven handed his plate to the boy and went forward to the windlass and stood with his back against the bitts and watched the wake. Olav finished his bread and took his plate to the galley and washed his mug at the deck pump and came back to his coil of rope and sat on it for a minute looking at nothing.

He understood, on the coil of rope with his plate empty in his lap, that Sven had said the thing Sven had said because Sven had registered at the earing what Olav had registered at the earing, and that Sven's saying it at supper in that particular restraint had been Sven's way of letting Olav know that the registering had been mutual and that Sven, who was a married Bergen man with a wife and a boy in Sandviken, was not going to let the registering become a thing either of them had to manage.

The restraint was its own language. The language was a language Olav had learned, in pieces, on the *Sigrid* five years ago. He had not expected to find the language used on a bark in the Indian Ocean by a married Bergen man at twenty-four.

He had found it.

---

That night at the rail before his watch ended Olav stood for some minutes with his hands on the cap and watched the wake.

The sea ran under the bark in the long south-Indian-Ocean roll. The stars came out overhead with the particular clarity a man only got fifteen hundred miles from any land. The mainsail overhead made the sound a mainsail made on a steady beam reach. Somewhere below Olav's feet the chronometers in the captain's cabin ticked toward London mean time.

He thought of Olava.

He thought of her in the kitchen at Lindøy, the letter from Cape Town on the table in front of her, the lamplight on the paper, her eyes going over the short careful sentences he had written her about the mountain and Dahl and the signature. He thought of her finishing the letter and folding it and putting it in the leather folder by her bed where she kept, she had told him at Christmas, everything of his that had a date on it. He thought of her not asking, in her next letter to him, about the thing the Cape Town letter had not said.

He thought: *there is a thing today that the letter I will write at Calcutta will not say.*

He thought: *the letter I will write at Calcutta will say that I held an earing at a royal yard in the Indian Ocean with a Bergen man named Sven Haugen, that the earing was freed, that the sail was saved, that the bark lost no time. The letter will say that I am well and that the tea is holding me. The letter will not say what happened to my hands at the water-cask ten minutes after I came down off the yard.*

He thought: *she will not ask. She agreed at Christmas of '79 not to ask. She will read the letter. She will put it in the folder. The folder will hold what the letter has said and will not hold what the letter has not said. That is the ledger. That is the marriage.*

He thought: *the drawer at the boat-shed holds Haakon's letter and nothing else. The drawer in me holds Haakon and now holds what happened today at the royal yard, and these two things are in the same drawer because the drawer does not make distinctions, and the distinctions are mine to make or not to make.*

He thought: *I do not, tonight, make the distinction.*

He looked at the sea.

The sea did not, by itself, contain an answer to any question he had been asking it. He had known, for some years, that it did not. He had looked at it anyway, the way a man looked at a fire he was not going to warm his hands at — for the size of it, for the way it reminded him that his own size was small, for the small comfort that comes from standing at something larger than oneself and not being required, at the standing, to do anything.

He stayed at the rail until eight bells.

Then he went below.

In his hammock he slept almost at once, the body's sleep of a man who had been aloft at a royal yard in the afternoon and had worked the rest of the watch and had eaten his supper and had stood at the rail for a quarter hour, and his sleep was the deep unsurfacing sleep of a twenty-three-year-old whose body had decided, without his permission, that it was not going to dwell in the registering of the afternoon past what the registering had already taken.

He slept.

The bark ran on east.
