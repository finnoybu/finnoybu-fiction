The *Semiramis* lifted Calcutta on the morning ebb of the first Wednesday in October, and Olav piloted her down the Hooghly at Banerjee's shoulder on a descent that took the full daylight of two days and that was, at the James and Mary shoal and at the Fultah bend, as demanding a piece of pilotage as Olav had ever been asked to attempt.

He did not, at the shoal or at the bend, make the mistake that would have lost the ship. He did not, in fact, make any mistake at all; Banerjee corrected him twice on bearings he had given too high by a quarter-point, and Banerjee let a third correction pass on the grounds that Olav had already begun to correct it himself. At the Sagar pilot-station on the evening of the second day the Bengali pilot put out his hand and shook Olav's in the small formal English way that the pilots of the Hooghly had made their own custom since the East India Company, and he said:

"Mr. Hestby. You will not need me on your next Hooghly. You will pay for a pilot because the law says so. You will not, by then, need one."

"Thank you, Mr. Banerjee."

"I have piloted two hundred Europeans down this river. You are one of four I have shaken hands with at the Sagar station. It is a small number. I keep it small on purpose."

"I will remember the shake."

"You will."

Banerjee went down into the pilot-brig's dinghy.

The bark turned east for Rangoon.

---

They made the mouth of the Rangoon river on the eleventh day.

The weather had held — the tail end of the southwest monsoon was still giving them a reliable quarter-breeze, and the run across the Bay of Bengal had been an easy one at an average of nine knots — and they picked up the Rangoon pilot at the sea-buoy on a Monday morning under a high pale sky. The pilot this time was a Burmese man of about thirty with an English of a slightly different cadence from Banerjee's; his name was Maung Thein; he took the bark up the river with the same quiet practised economy the Hooghly pilot had used, on a river that was different from the Hooghly — shorter, less crowded, more trees on the banks — and by the Tuesday afternoon the *Semiramis* was at her mooring off the timber-yards on the Pazundaung creek, a mile and a half below the city of Rangoon and a mile north of the point at which the Irrawaddy and the Pegu rivers joined to make a broad brown estuary that ran out past Elephant Point to the Andaman Sea.

The crew had four days at Rangoon while the bark's rice went aboard and the indigo-casks were shifted to make room for the Burmese rice that would form the bulk of the homeward cargo.

Olav had one duty in the four days. On the Wednesday morning Brun took him ashore to a British shipping agent in Phayre Street to sign for the rice, as he had signed at Dahl's at Cape Town and at the Bengal Chamber at Calcutta. The signing at Phayre Street took twenty minutes. On the way back to the river Brun stopped at the gate of the British garrison compound, pointed at the gold shape that lifted above the city's roofline to the north, and said:

"Hestby."

"Sir."

"That is the Shwedagon."

"Yes, sir."

"You have Thursday. You will go there. You will take one of the deckhands with you, because a man does not go to a temple alone in a city he has not been to before. You will go early. You will be back on board by the second dog-watch. Those are your orders."

"Yes, sir."

"I will not ask what you thought of it when you come back. I will only ask whether you went. You will say yes. That is all I require."

"Yes, sir."

Brun went down to the dinghy.

---

Olav went to the Shwedagon on the Thursday morning.

He took Sven.

He had not, in asking Sven, stopped to consider the asking. He had come back on board from the Phayre Street signing, and he had stood at the forward rail for the twenty minutes before supper, and at supper he had said to Sven at the coil of rope:

"The captain gave me tomorrow morning ashore. He told me to take a deckhand."

"Which deckhand."

"You."

Sven had eaten another bite of bread before he answered.

"Good."

That was all.

---

At eight o'clock on the Thursday morning they came up the Strand from the bark in a hired country-boat, paid the man a rupee, and walked into the city of Rangoon under a sun that was, at October, hot but not yet the hot of the dry season to come. The streets at that hour had the small busyness of a tropical port first waking — vendors setting out fruit on cloths, clerks walking to offices, Indian gharry-wallahs soliciting fares in Hindi because the Rangoon gharry trade was half-Indian and half-Burmese and the Indian half spoke to Europeans first. They turned down the gharry-wallahs. They walked.

The walk up to the Shwedagon took half an hour.

The pagoda stood on a low hill at the north edge of the city — a green hill that had, until the British annexation thirty years before, been a religious precinct around which the rest of the city had grown — and a man approached it along a long covered walkway of stairs, the stairs flanked by small stalls selling flowers and candles and gold leaf and small devotional pictures to the Buddhist pilgrims who were, on this Thursday morning, going up and coming down in numbers Olav had not seen at any European religious place.

He had read about the Shwedagon in the library at Park Street. He had read about the hti — the golden spire that crowned the stupa — and about the gold plates that covered the stupa below the spire, and about the diamond at the tip. He had read the engineering and the art-history. He had not read anything that had prepared him for the walk up.

The stalls were hung with a kind of small white paper prayer-flag that Olav had not known of. The air along the walkway was, because of the candles, half incense. The people going up the stairs were of every age — grandmothers climbing with a hand on the banister, fathers carrying babies, monks in saffron robes, young men and young women in the Burmese dress of the city. The quality of the going-up was not the quality of a European procession; it was more casual, less coordinated, the quality of a familiar and daily pilgrimage.

At the top the pagoda platform opened.

It was paved in stone. It was enormous. At its centre was the main stupa — the gold bell-shape rising perhaps three hundred feet from the platform to the tip of the hti, the gold catching the morning sun at an intensity Olav had not thought gold could catch — and around the main stupa were dozens of smaller stupas and shrines and prayer-halls, each with its own candles and its own flowers and its own people kneeling at its base.

Olav stopped at the east gate. He took off his cap, because the Burmese practice was that a man took off his cap at the gate, and because a man from Finnøy who had been to the Hestby church with his mother on every Sunday of his childhood did not, at twenty-three, need to be told that there was a gate one did not cross with one's head covered.

Sven took off his cap.

They stood at the gate for perhaps a minute without moving.

Olav did not, in the minute, think anything he could later remember thinking. He only looked.

The gold of the stupa did, to the eye, what the mountain at Cape Town had done — it exceeded any description any handbook had offered — but the exceeding was of a different kind. The mountain had exceeded by the size of its shape; the stupa exceeded by the intensity of its being itself. The gold was not decorative gold. The gold was the whole purpose of the thing. The stupa was, in some way Olav did not at twenty-three have the theology for, *entirely what it was trying to be,* and the trying and the being had not failed.

He thought — briefly, at the gate — of the small wooden Hestby church on Finnøy, with its whitewashed walls and its oil-lamps and the small carved angel his great-grandfather had carved in 1812 that hung above the altar and that was the only carving in the church. He thought of the three hundred feet of gold in front of him. He thought of the small carved angel. He did not, in the thinking, diminish the angel. The angel was what it was; the stupa was what the stupa was; both were the places two peoples had built because the peoples had needed such places. The stupa was larger. The stupa was not, at a scale that mattered, different in kind.

He registered, at the gate, that the thought he was having was the thought of a man who had been raised in a church and had not, until this morning, seen the church's equivalent in another tradition.

He took a step forward onto the platform.

---

They walked the platform for perhaps an hour.

They did not, by unspoken agreement, talk. The platform was not a place for the kind of talk two men did on a coil of rope at supper; the platform was a place for each man to be in his own silence. Olav watched the devotions around him — an old woman lighting eleven candles at a small side shrine and arranging each one with a small particular care; a boy of about ten on his knees at the base of the main stupa with his forehead against the stone; a monk walking slowly around the platform in the direction the pilgrims walked, which was clockwise, with a begging-bowl held in both hands. He did not, at any point, feel that he was intruding. The Burmese pilgrims walked past him and Sven the way Norwegian pilgrims would have walked past two Burmese sailors at the stavekirke at Urnes on a Sunday — with the small courteous un-noticing of a people who had long ago decided that the presence of foreigners at their sacred place was neither a compliment nor an affront but a simple fact, and was not to be made into anything it did not need to be.

Twice, in the hour, Olav caught himself looking at Sven.

He caught himself the first time at the north shrine, where an old man in a white cotton coat was chanting a sutra under his breath. Olav had been watching the old man; he had turned his head; Sven had been standing to his left, looking at the same old man. Sven's face at that moment had the still attention of a man who was, at twenty-four on a temple platform eight thousand miles from Sandviken, entirely present at what he was watching and entirely silent about it.

Olav had registered Sven's face. He had looked back at the old man.

The second time was twenty minutes later at the eastern gate, as they were preparing to leave. Olav had been putting his cap back on. Sven had already put his on. Sven had turned to Olav with the particular small squint a man used against the sun when the sun was at the height it was at ten in the morning in October in Rangoon, and Olav had, in the same moment, been looking at him, and their eyes had met for the breath it took a breath to take, and then Sven had nodded once and started down the stairs, and Olav had followed him.

The second meeting had been, for the count of one breath, a meeting. It had not been the kind of meeting a man could later pretend had not been a meeting. It had been a thing that had happened. Olav had registered it. Sven had registered it. Neither of them had, on the stairs or on the walk back to the river, said anything.

---

At the riverbank, waiting for the country-boat to take them back to the bark, they stood for some minutes in the shade of a banyan tree.

Olav spoke first.

"Sven."

"Yes."

"Thank you for coming."

"You asked. I said good."

"Yes."

"There is a thing I will say once about what we saw, Hestby."

"Say it."

"The gold. A man could stand at that gate for a week and not take it in. We had an hour. I will not, for the rest of my life, see gold on a wall or on a ring or on a woman's neck without thinking of that hour. That is what the Shwedagon did for me. A man who has been the second of two on such a walk has — I think — given me the hour. I am saying I am aware of the giving."

Olav did not, for a moment, answer.

He had not thought of the morning, at the Shwedagon, as a thing he had given to Sven. He had thought of it as a thing he had been ordered to do by the captain and had done in the company of the only man on the bark it would have been possible to do it with. The thought that the company had been a gift — that his choice of Sven over any of the other sixteen common seamen had been a gift — had not been a thought he had formulated at the bark or at the river or on the walk up the stairs.

He formulated it now, at the banyan, in the shade.

He thought: *I chose Sven because I could not, in the moment of Brun's permission, have chosen another man.*

He thought: *the choosing was the giving.*

He thought: *Sven has understood the giving. He has said it in the way a married Bergen man with a wife and boy at home says a thing that cannot be said in any other way.*

He thought: *I do not answer what Sven has said. I register it. I keep it.*

"Sven," he said aloud.

"Yes."

"The country-boat is here."

"Yes."

They got into the boat.

The Burmese boatman took them back to the Pazundaung creek and the *Semiramis* with the tide running out under the hull.

---

That evening at six bells of the first dog-watch Olav stood at the forward rail and looked upriver at the gold shape of the Shwedagon, which was still — even from a mile and a half south — catching the last light of the day. The gold had the particular quality, at sunset, that the pagoda had been painted by its builders to have. The light failed on the river; the pagoda held the light a minute longer than the river did; then the pagoda too went dim, and the city's lamps came up behind it.

He went below.

He did not write about the Shwedagon in his next letter to Olava.

He wrote, in the Calcutta-outbound note the consul Møller would have posted on the first English steamer a week before, that he would see the gold pagoda at Rangoon for her. He had seen it. He would write, in the letter from Calcutta on the homeward leg in December, the sentence he had promised: *I saw the gold pagoda at Rangoon. It was what the handbook said. It was also what the handbook did not say.* That sentence would be the whole of the letter's record.

The other thing — the thing at the eastern gate when he and Sven had met eyes for the breath of one breath — would not go into any letter.

That thing would go where the drawer at Finnøy kept what it kept.

He slept.

The bark lay at her creek mooring under the Burmese stars, the rice going aboard through the night by lantern light, the Shwedagon out of sight to the north but not, in the head of a twenty-three-year-old common seaman from Finnøy, out of mind.
