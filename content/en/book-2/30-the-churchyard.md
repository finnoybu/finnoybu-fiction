On the afternoon of the thirteenth of March, 1883 — the day before the wedding — Olav walked up alone to the Hestby churchyard on the hill at the top of the island.

He had come across from Lindøy on the morning boat with his father. Olava was still at Lindøy, preparing — with Marit and her mother — the small family ceremonies of the day-before, which at a Lindøy wedding of this period were the particular last rites a bride did on her father's island before she crossed, at the last crossing, to the husband's. His own mother was at the Finnøy kitchen managing the three-days' cake. His father was at the boat-shed with his brother, going over a ledger that had gone — in the weeks of wedding-preparation — a little slack. The family at Finnøy on the thirteenth of March did the work the day before a wedding required of it, and Olav — having come across — had been released, at three in the afternoon, to walk.

He had walked for about an hour.

He had walked the lane at the north edge of the island, past the two houses that were his uncle's and his godmother's, and up the small hill behind his uncle's to the field his father had bought in '76, which was the small property Olav would, in the years after his marriage, take over the management of. He had walked back along the ridge above the field with the view of the channel open to the south. He had stopped at the bench his grandfather had set into the stone wall above the ridge in 1864, and had sat there for a quarter hour looking at the water.

At four o'clock he had gone up to the churchyard.

---

The churchyard at Hestby in March was the churchyard it had always been in March — the grass coming up, the last of the winter turned, the small flags at one or two of the newer graves still at half-bent from a recent wind. The church itself was white-painted wood, as it had been since 1812, with the particular small squared bell-tower and the thick low stone wall that marked the church's own ground apart from the surrounding field. The gate was oak. The gate had his great-grandfather's initials — *P.H. 1812* — carved, in the old Hestby style, into the top crossbar.

He opened the gate.

He walked in among the stones.

---

The stones at Hestby held the Hestbye dead.

They held them in the old spelling. Pastor Kleven, with the agreement of the parish council, had kept the old spelling on every stone he had consecrated since he had come to the church in 1848, and his predecessor had kept it for forty years before that, and the spelling on the stones was therefore — across the whole of the churchyard — *Hestbye,* regardless of what the descendants who visited the stones signed in the register.

Olav walked slowly along the paths.

He stopped at his great-grandfather's stone first. *Peder Hestbye, 1748–1834.* The stone was the oldest of the family stones in the churchyard; it had been cut by a Stavanger mason in 1834 and set into the earth by Peder's two sons on a March afternoon the parish had recorded as unusually cold.

He stood at the stone for perhaps half a minute.

He did not, in the half-minute, speak.

He moved on.

His grandfather's stone was the one he had stopped at with his father in October. *Jens Hestbye, 1795–1849.* His grandfather had died five years before Olav's father had been married. He had died, the family story went, on the road from Stavanger to Finnøy in a sudden March blow, having been caught at the exposed part of the coaster-crossing by weather the coaster captain had misjudged. The story had been, when Olav was a child, the story his father had told him three or four times without telling him more than the barest outline.

Olav stood at the stone.

He thought, at the stone, about the story.

He thought: *a Hestbye at fifty-four died in a coaster crossing on a March blow, and his son — my father — grew up without him, and the Hestbye line went on by the small quiet means a line went on. I am twenty-four tomorrow at the altar. I will, if the arithmetic the actuaries use at the Bergen bank is correct, have — if nothing intervenes — another thirty or forty years. I will live, by those figures, to the end of the century and perhaps beyond. My children and my children's children will be the ones who will, if the line continues, put my stone here beside this one.*

He did not, at the stone, allow the thought to go further.

He moved on.

---

He walked the churchyard for perhaps another twenty minutes.

He stopped at three more stones — his grandmother's (*Anna Hestbye, 1801–1872*), his great-uncle's (*Ole Hestbye, 1812–1888,* which had been cut in January at the stonemason's and was waiting, blank of the date of death except for the lower mark *1888* which had been incorrectly filled in by the mason who had not yet been told Ole was still alive; Ole was in the house at the north end of the island, seventy years old, in robust health), and at a smaller stone near the church's east wall that had on it *Sigrid Hestbye, 1836–1839,* which was the stone of a sister of his father's who had died at three in the summer of '39 and whom his father had, in Olav's entire life, mentioned exactly once — at a Christmas in Olav's fifteenth year, when his father had at the supper said *Sigrid would have been forty-eight this year* and had, the moment after saying it, refused to expand.

He stood at Sigrid's stone longer than he had stood at the others.

He thought: *the* Sigrid *I signed on to in June of '76 was named in some small Norwegian convention for the stone I am standing at. A Norwegian brig of the period was often named for a girl of a captain's family who had died, and the brig's name carried the girl's name out into the world the girl had not lived long enough to see. My* Sigrid *carried, I now understand, the ghost of my aunt Sigrid Hestbye of Finnøy who died at three in 1839. I did not, at eighteen in June of '76, know this. I probably will not, in my lifetime, verify it. It may not even be true. But the naming pattern is the pattern, and my brig was named at an Arendal yard in 1863 in the way Norwegian brigs of the generation were named, and somewhere in the line of the naming was the small sister-of-a-captain who had not lived. The* Sigrid *I sailed on at eighteen was named, in its deepest ancestry, for an aunt whose stone I am standing at today.*

He did not know whether this was true.

He decided he would, regardless, keep the thought.

---

At five o'clock the pastor came out of the church.

Kleven had heard him at the gate an hour before and had, in the pastor's quiet way, not come out to interrupt him. The pastor had been finishing the small last arrangements of the wedding-morning sermon in the vestry. He came out now because the four o'clock light was fading and the pastor had decided, after Olav had been in the yard for an hour, that the courtesy of the pastor was to step out briefly and say whatever was due.

"Olav."

"Pastor."

"You have been walking the stones."

"I have."

"Good."

"Pastor."

"Yes."

"The spelling on the stones."

"*Hestbye.*"

"Yes."

"You will sign *Hestby* tomorrow at the altar."

"I will."

"Your children will sign *Hestby.*"

"They will."

"Your children's children will sign *Hestby.*"

"They will."

"The stones will, for another century, say *Hestbye.*"

"Yes."

"That is the parish's bargain with the modern spelling. The registers move with the living. The stones hold to the dead. The two spellings are one name at the different speeds of its own keeping."

"Yes."

"I will not, Olav, write *Hestby* on any stone in this yard for as long as I am the pastor. When you come to me in fifty years with your own stone ordered from the Stavanger mason — and I hope it is fifty years — you will have your son or your daughter come to my successor to have the engraving confirmed. They will argue, perhaps, for the modern. My successor will, I suspect, hold to the old. That is what Hestbye is."

"Yes."

"Go down to your father's house. It is getting cold. Your mother will want you at the table for supper."

"Yes, Pastor."

"Olav."

"Yes."

"I will see you at eleven tomorrow at the altar."

"Yes, Pastor."

Kleven went back into the church.

---

Olav stood at the east wall of the churchyard for another minute.

He looked down the slope toward the village and past the village toward the channel. The channel was gray. The Lindøy coast was a darker gray across it. On the Lindøy side — he could just see, with the eye of a man who had been looking across the channel all his life — was the Lindøy jetty. On the Lindøy jetty, in the fading afternoon, a small figure in a coat he did not, at this distance, have any reason to be able to identify might have been Olava. It might not. He did not know. She might be at the jetty because the day-before rites had taken her, at the afternoon, to walk down to the water; she might also be at the kitchen with her mother; she might also be in her room with Marit, doing the last quiet things a bride did alone.

He decided he would not know which until he saw her at the altar the following morning.

He turned from the wall.

---

He walked down from the churchyard toward the Hestby house.

At the gate — at the *P.H. 1812* — he paused and put his hand on the top crossbar for a moment, as he had once, as a boy of ten, put his hand on the same crossbar after his confirmation in this church at his mother's urging, because his mother had wanted him to feel, at the gate, the particular small weight the name carried at that place.

He felt it now at twenty-four as he had felt it at ten.

He did not, at twenty-four, put it into words. The putting into words was not the kind of thing a man of his house and his church and his hands did at the wedding-eve gate.

He closed the gate behind him.

He walked down the hill.

---

That evening at supper at the kitchen at Finnøy his mother asked him whether he had walked up.

"I walked up."

"You saw the stones."

"I saw the stones."

"Sigrid's."

"Yes, Mother."

She nodded. She did not say more. She set the plate in front of him.

His father, who was at the table with his newspaper and his coffee, said:

"Kleven."

"I spoke to him briefly. He said he will see me at eleven tomorrow at the altar."

"Good."

"Father."

"Yes."

"I will not, tomorrow at the altar, sign *Hestbye.* I will sign *Hestby.*"

"I know."

"It occurred to me at the churchyard that I had been at the signing of the banns in October without having thought about it. The two spellings are — Pastor Kleven called it — the parish's bargain with the modern."

"They have been the parish's bargain since my own signing in '58. I signed *Hestby* then. My father, had he lived to see me marry, would have signed *Hestbye* as a witness. The bargain was already a bargain in his day. It is a bargain that is made at each generation and is made again. You are making it tomorrow."

"Yes."

"Good."

His mother brought the bread.

They ate.

---

That night in the loft at Finnøy — the last night he would sleep in the loft as an unmarried son of the house; from the fifteenth of March he would sleep there as the married son — Olav sat for some time at the small desk under the window with his small bound book open in front of him on the last unused page.

He had, in the five months since the homecoming, written in the book perhaps eight times. Short entries. The kind of sentences a man made when he was not sure whether the thing he was writing was going to matter to him at sixty and did not, at twenty-four, have the means to find out.

He wrote, now, at the last page he had chosen for this purpose on the Christmas morning at Finnøy:

> *13 March 1883, Finnøy loft.*
>
> *I am twenty-four, engaged to Olava Lindøye of Lindøy since November of 1880, at home from the Semiramis since October of last year.*
>
> *Tomorrow at eleven I will sign a register at the altar of the Hestbye church on this island. The register will have both our names in the old spelling in Pastor Kleven's hand and both our signatures in the modern spelling in our own. Pastor Kleven baptized me in this church in 1858 and confirmed me in 1872. He will marry me in it in 1883.*
>
> *I am the Olav who went to sea at eighteen in '76. I am the Olav who came home with the cough in '77. I am the Olav who met Olava at Landa in '79. I am the Olav who signed the certificate at Stavanger in '80. I am the Olav who signed the articles of the Semiramis in June of '81. I am the Olav who kissed Olava on the Stavanger quay the morning we lifted. I am the Olav who sat at a corner table in Nyhavn on an evening in September of '82 and took a card and burned it. I am the Olav who put a second letter in the drawer at the boat-shed on the second Tuesday of November. I am the Olav who sat at the back garden bench with Olava on Christmas Eve and was told by her that she knew. I am, this evening at the small desk in the loft, all of those Olavs at the same time.*
>
> *Tomorrow at the altar I will sign all of them together in the modern Hestby.*
>
> *The drawer at the boat-shed holds the two letters.*
>
> *The drawer will hold them for the rest of my life.*
>
> *The wife I marry tomorrow has agreed not to ask what is in the drawer. The husband I become tomorrow has agreed not to tell. The not-asking and the not-telling are the form of the marriage.*
>
> *The marriage is the form of the life.*

He set the pen down.

He blotted the page.

He looked at what he had written.

He understood, at the small desk in the loft under the window with the writing done, that the book had arrived, in the last paragraph, at the sentence it had been walking toward since he had bought the book at the Asiatic Society stall at Calcutta in October of '81. The book had been — he had understood, at the crate with Bassein in the Bay of Bengal — the small private record of the man he had been becoming on the voyage. The record had been, in pieces, honest. The last paragraph was the honesty he had not, until tonight, been in a position to write.

He had written it.

He closed the book.

He put it into the bottom of the wooden chest he had brought across from Lindøy for the two Finnøy days, under the wedding coat, wrapped in a small piece of linen he had been given by his mother for this purpose in February.

He did not know, at the ceiling, whether he would write in the book again after this page.

He thought he might not.

The book had done its work.

---

He blew out the candle.

He lay on his back in the loft bed.

He listened to the house.

The house was the house he had grown up in. His mother was in the kitchen banking the stove for the night, as she always did at half past ten. His father was at the parlor finishing the Stavanger newspaper, as he always did. The wind on the channel was the small light March wind of a clear evening. The Hestbye church at the top of the island was, at this hour, dark against the stars.

He thought, briefly, of Olava at Lindøy across the channel, who was — he trusted — at this hour doing the equivalent of what he was doing, which was lying in her bed in her room and listening to her own house. He did not, in the thinking, try to imagine her more particularly than that. The more-particularly was, by the economy they had been building since Christmas of '82, not his to imagine. Tomorrow she would walk into the Hestbye church on her father's good arm, and he would see her coming up the aisle in the dress Mrs. Lindøy had been sewing for her since January, and the seeing would be the first time in his life he had seen her in that dress, and the seeing would be the wedding.

He thought of Haakon Berg at Sandviken, who would be — by Haakon's own arrangement, made on the quay at Shields in August of '82 and probably kept since — somewhere in Bergen or at Voss or at Shields on this Thursday evening, and who had not, in the fifteen months and nineteen days since the Semiramis had lifted Stavanger, been at any quay Olav had been at. He thought of Sven Haugen at Sandviken, who had signed off the *Semiramis* at the Bergen pay-off and who was — Torgerson's January letter had said — back with his wife and his boy in the apartment at Sandviken and was between voyages and was, Torgerson understood, preparing for a North Sea crossing in April. He thought of Anders Lindqvist at Stockholm, who had not written a second letter and would not.

He thought of all three at the same time.

He put the thinking into the drawer — the drawer in him, which was the drawer that mirrored the one at the boat-shed but that was larger and that held the things the physical drawer could not — and he closed the drawer.

The drawer held.

---

He slept.

The house went quiet. The wind held. The stars over Finnøy rotated, across the late-winter night, the small cold half-turn they would rotate in the nine hours between Olav's sleeping and the morning bell at the Hestbye church that would, at half past ten, call the parish up for the wedding.

At half past ten the bell would ring.

At eleven Pastor Kleven would open the register.

At eleven-oh-four — by the watch his father would be holding, which he had been winding the same way at the same hour every morning for forty years — Olav and Olava would stand at the altar, and the pastor would read the service in the Norwegian of the parish, and the two would say the small formal phrases a Norwegian couple of the period said at the altar, and the register would be signed by both of them in the modern spelling under the old in the pastor's hand, and Marit and Jens Hestby and Captain Lindøy would sign as witnesses, and the bell would ring again, and the two Hestbys — *Hestby,* the modern spelling at the register; *Hestbye,* the old spelling carried forward in the pastor's line and in the ancestral stones — would walk out of the Hestbye church on the hill at the top of the island as man and wife.

That was tomorrow.

Tonight in the loft Olav slept, the sleep of a twenty-four-year-old Norwegian navigator on the night before the wedding he had been walking toward at various paces across five and a half years, and the house at Finnøy held him in its quiet small ordinary March keeping, and the drawer at the boat-shed with the two letters held the piece of him the wedding was not going to remove and that Olava, on the bench on Christmas Eve, had already told him she knew she was marrying.

The voyage was done.

The book was done.

The marriage began at eleven in the morning.

He slept through to the bell.
