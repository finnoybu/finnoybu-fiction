The barometer had been falling since the second dog-watch on the third day.

Olav had not, in the forecastle at supper, known it was falling; he had noticed only that the air had gone still by the time he came up for his watch at eight bells of the afternoon, and that Sundberg's face had gone still with it. Stillness in the bosun was the way Sundberg's face registered information the rest of the ship had not yet been told. Olav had learned the look in one day. He set his hands to the brace fall he was coiling and did not ask.

By four bells of the first dog-watch the sky had changed. The western horizon had gone the color of an old coin that had been under water. The bark ran on, her course a point south of west, the air around her going warm and thick and the wind light enough that she was making no more than four knots and had to be coaxed at the spokes to hold her heading at all. Captain Brun came up at five bells, looked at the glass in the binnacle, looked at Aarseth, looked at the western sky, and said, without raising his voice above the pitch of a man giving a piece of household information:

"Mr. Aarseth. Strike the royals and the flying jib. Reef the topgallants to the second band. We will have weather in three hours."

"Aye, sir."

The orders went along the watches. Olav was sent aloft to the main topgallant with four other men from his watch. Sven was one of them. They worked the yard in a silence that had, Olav would later think, already been a storm silence before the storm came — the silence of men who have understood what the captain has seen, and who have accepted the work the understanding requires, and who have no breath to spare for anything else.

At the foot of the topgallant shrouds, after the gasket was passed, Sven said without looking at Olav:

"He has called it closer than any captain I have sailed with."

"Yes."

"Three hours."

"Three hours."

"He will be right."

"I know."

They went to the topsail. The topsail took another twenty minutes.

---

The weather came at eight bells.

It came out of the west-southwest the way a hand comes out of a dark room — a slap that had a weight behind it and was followed by other slaps on the same line. The first gust was not the worst; the first gust was the one that told the bark a stronger gust was behind it. By the time Olav was off the topsail yard and on the deck the wind was a voice in the rigging he had not yet heard from the *Semiramis.* The voice was different from the *Sigrid*'s. The *Sigrid* had spoken, in bad weather, in a higher register — a brig's rigging was tighter and shorter and sang above the men. The *Semiramis*'s rigging was longer and its voice came from lower down; it was a voice that a man heard at the level of his chest rather than his ear.

The bark heeled. She went over to starboard by ten degrees, recovered, went over by fifteen. The watch on deck worked the sheets and braces without being told, the commands coming from Aarseth and Sundberg on a pitch that did not have to be shouted because the crew was where the crew was supposed to be.

Olav was at the starboard main topsail brace with a Danish man named Storm. The Danish man's name was a family name and had nothing to do with the weather; he was, Olav had gathered in two days, a quiet man of thirty whose competence showed in the way he took slack out of a line before the line had gone slack. They took slack now. They belayed. They took slack again.

The rain came at half past the hour. It came the way North Sea rain comes in June — warm where it fell on the deck, cold when it touched a man's face, running off the oilskins in small cold trails inside the collar where the oilskin did not quite close. Olav's oilskin was new; it shed for twenty minutes and then gave up at the seams. His wool shirt under it took on the rain and held it.

By one bell of the first night-watch the bark was running with her main topsail single-reefed and her foretopsail double-reefed and the jibs all in and the wind pressing fifty knots or a little above on a quarter sea that was building into something uglier than a quarter sea should. She was making nine knots. She was holding her course. The work on deck was the work of a crew that had now been tested by the weather and had passed its first test, and the men at the braces, Olav noticed, did not look at each other about it; they looked at the work.

Captain Brun was on the quarterdeck. He had been on the quarterdeck since eight bells. He had not gone below. His coat was soaked. His beard was soaked. The binnacle-light lit his face from below and Olav, from the main topsail brace, could see that Brun's eyes had in them, at this hour, the particular attention of a man who had stood on quarterdecks in worse weather than this for forty years and had learned, in the forty years, to give each piece of weather only the attention it deserved, no more and no less. This piece of weather was not yet one of the worst. The captain had not yet given it his worst attention.

The captain, Olav registered, was rationing.

He registered it at the brace. He did not let the registering expand.

---

The weather held through the night.

At eight bells of the morning watch the wind began to back round from southwest through west toward northwest, and the sea, as it will when the wind backs fast, went confused for an hour — cross-seas from the old direction fighting cross-seas from the new — and the bark rolled heavily enough that a man on deck had to hold to something at all times or fall. Olav held to the life-line that had been rigged at first light along the main bitt. He held it with his left hand and did his work with his right. He had done this on the *Sigrid* in the Atlantic in '76. His hand remembered the work the way a hand remembers a prayer that has not been said in five years — uncertainly for a moment, then completely.

By noon the wind had settled onto northwest at about thirty knots and the sky, overhead, had cleared to a gray that had the depth of a sky that has been emptied. The sun was a dim bright thing behind the gray. The mate sent half the watch below to change into dry clothes.

Olav went below. He stripped the wet wool. He put on dry wool. He came back on deck.

The captain was still on the quarterdeck. The captain had not, since eight bells of the previous afternoon, gone below. The steward had brought him coffee twice and bread once; Olav had seen. The captain was drinking a third cup now, and in his right hand — passed up to him by the mate — was a sextant.

Olav stopped at the main fife rail and watched.

He had watched Sars take a sight on the *Diana* in '78, more than once. He had taken sights himself at the navigation school in Stavanger. He had held a sextant the way his body had been taught to hold a sextant — steady with the left hand at the frame, adjusting the index with the right thumb, the eye at the telescope. He knew the motion. He had never watched a captain take a sight after a storm in which the captain had been on deck for eighteen hours without sleep.

Brun took the sight the way he did everything: without waste. He raised the sextant. He brought the sun down to the horizon. He brought the sun to the horizon with the small adjustment at the index. He held the sight for the moment a captain holds it. He swung the sextant on the vertical axis slightly — the rocking motion a navigator uses to find the true altitude by letting the image scribe its arc against the horizon — and read the scale. He lowered the sextant. He walked to the binnacle. He wrote.

Olav, at the main fife rail, registered what he had just watched.

He had watched a man who had not slept in eighteen hours take a noon sight after a storm with the economy of a movement that was the movement a man learns by taking ten thousand such sights in the years before the ten thousand and first. The sight had taken perhaps forty seconds from the first raising of the sextant to the writing at the binnacle. The forty seconds had not, in any second of them, included a movement that had not been required. There had been no second adjustment. There had been no check-and-recheck. The captain had raised the sextant to the sun he had already known the altitude of; the raising was the confirmation, not the finding.

Olav registered, at the fife rail, that a man who could take such a sight after such a night had taught himself, over forty years, to think about where the ship was without having to think about it. The sight was the external record of an interior arithmetic that Brun was carrying in his body the way a man carries the feel of his own house.

Olav thought: *I have seen this before. I have seen it in Sars. I have seen it in my father on the mast of a boat-shed smack. I have not, until this moment, seen it in a man who was also the man whose ship I am going to sail on for the next eighteen months.*

He thought: *Brun is the man.*

He thought: *the man I write the certificate exam for is not the man who signs my mate's ticket. The man who signs my mate's ticket is the man whose body has taught him to find the sun before he raises the sextant.*

He did not, at the fife rail, let the thought expand further than this. He had work to do. He did not need, at twenty-three, to have the rest of the thought.

He went back to his watch.

---

The wind eased through the afternoon.

By the second dog-watch the sea had flattened into a long low northwest roll and the bark was making eight knots under a full set again, the topgallants back on the yards, the royal sails loosed by six bells. The crew was tired in the way a crew is tired after its first deep-water weather — not broken, only tested; the men ate supper with the small silent appetite of men who have worked for sixteen hours and are now discovering that they would like to eat.

Olav sat on his coil of rope with his plate on his knee. Sven came and sat beside him. The hand's width of coil between them was the same hand's width it had been the previous evening. Neither man spoke for some minutes.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"You saw the captain take the sight."

"Yes."

"I saw you watching him."

"Yes."

"A man who watches another man take a sight for that long is a man who can take the sight himself."

"I can take the sight."

"I know. The watch-list said *navigation.* I am telling you I saw you watch."

"Yes."

Sven ate the last of his bread. He wiped his thumb against the edge of his plate and put the thumb in his mouth and looked out at the sea.

"You are going to come off this voyage as Brun's first mate. I have seen him watch you too. He has watched you twice in the last day for longer than was needed. He is deciding whether he was right to sign you for common seaman, and he is deciding he was right. Men like him only watch a man twice."

"I am a common seaman."

"Yes. You are a common seaman whom the captain watches. That is a separate trade. It does not pay better. It pays differently."

Olav took this in. He did not, for a long moment, answer.

"Sven."

"Yes."

"You are telling me I am being watched."

"I am telling you I noticed."

"You are telling me to be careful."

"No. I am telling you to know. Careful is for a man who does not want to be watched. You signed for this. You know what you signed for."

"Yes."

"Then eat."

Olav ate. He ate slowly. He made the plate last.

---

That night in the forecastle the men slept the deep sleep of a crew that has been emptied by its first weather. Olav lay in his hammock for a long while without joining them.

He thought, in the dark, about the way Brun had raised the sextant after eighteen hours on his feet. He thought about the small motion of the thumb on the index. He thought about the way the horizon had been where the captain had already known it would be.

He thought: *in eighteen months on this bark, if the Indian Ocean does not undo me, I am going to have watched him take two thousand sights. I am going to have taken my own. I am going to come home with the sight in my body the way Sars has it in his and my father never got to have it. That is what I have signed for. That is the trade.*

He turned his face to the canvas.

He thought of Olava. He thought of her at the Stavanger quay. He thought: *the wool coat will be the same wool coat when I come back, and the ledger in the drawer by her bed will have eighteen entries in it, and she will read me my own letters aloud across the kitchen table in the lamplight on the Saturday after I am home, because that is the way she will have decided to give them back to me.*

He thought: *the sight is the work. The letters are the marriage. The drawer is the rest.*

He slept.

The bark ran on through the clearing night at eight knots, northwest wind on her starboard quarter, the chronometers in the captain's cabin ticking toward London mean time, the captain himself, at last, asleep in his berth for the first time in twenty hours.
