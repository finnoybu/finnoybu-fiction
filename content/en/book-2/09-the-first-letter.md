Olava heard the Lindøy coaster's whistle at seven o'clock on the morning of the last Tuesday in September.

She was in the kitchen. She had been in the kitchen since half past six, because the morning routine at Lindøy in the autumn had gone back, as it always did after the long midsummer evenings, to the standard of the winter months — porridge at seven, her mother at the stove, her father at the table with the week-old Stavanger newspaper open in front of his good hand, Marit at the window with the coffee-pot still too hot to pour. Olava had been setting the plates. The coaster's whistle came through the open window at seven on the nose, the way it had every Tuesday since the whistle had been fitted to the coaster in '77, and Olava set the last plate down carefully enough that her mother did not look up from the stove to check whether she had set it carefully enough.

The setting-down had been careful because Olava had been waiting, for four Tuesdays, for a letter from Cape Town.

She had calculated, at her desk in the small bedroom above the parlour on the night after Olav had left Stavanger, the outbound route of the *Semiramis,* the likely ports at which the letters would be posted, and the rough schedule by which each letter would reach Norway. She had not worked from a grey handbook — her father had one, but she had not opened it; she had worked from the general shape of the English and European post, which she had been reading as a reader of newspapers for some years — and she had arrived at a rough forecast: Cape Town letter to reach Lindøy in the fourth or fifth week of September; Calcutta letter in December; Calcutta homebound letter in March of next year; Rangoon in June; St Helena in September; Copenhagen in November. She had written the six dates on a small slip of paper she kept in the back of the ledger.

This was the fifth Tuesday.

The coaster's whistle did not always mean a letter. Four of the last five Tuesdays it had not meant a letter. It had meant only that the coaster had come in with the week's supplies, and the Lindøy farm-boy would walk up from the jetty with whatever the coaster had brought, and sometimes the whatever would include letters and sometimes it would not. Olava had, the previous four Tuesdays, not allowed herself to stand at the window watching the farm-boy come up the path. She had, the previous four Tuesdays, stayed in the kitchen with the plates and the porridge and the ordinary work of a morning, and she had only allowed herself to know whether a letter had come when her mother — who, without announcing it, had made the receiving of the mail her own office through this autumn — had brought the letters into the kitchen.

This Tuesday she did the same.

---

Her mother brought the mail into the kitchen at a quarter past seven. There were three items. A Stavanger paper — the same paper her father was reading, only a week newer. A folded note from the minister at Lindøy parish about a meeting of the women's committee. And a letter.

The letter was addressed to *Miss Olava Lindøy, Lindøy by Stavanger, Norway,* in Olav's hand. The upper-left of the cover said, in the same hand, *Bark Semiramis, Cape Town, end July 1881.* The wafer on the back was the small red one Olava had sent him in a packet in May with his new spare shirts, because she had known he would be sealing letters for eighteen months and had not trusted the ship's wafers to hold.

Her mother laid the letter on the table in front of Olava without speaking.

Olava's father, at the newspaper, looked up. He looked at the letter. He looked at Olava. He looked down again.

"Read it at your table," he said, which was the Lindøy household's way of saying *we will not watch you read.*

"Yes," Olava said.

She took the letter. She took it upstairs, to the small writing table at the window of her bedroom, which was the table she did her accounts at and where she had, through the summer, been writing her own letters to Olav that the ship would not, at Cape Town, yet have received. She sat. She broke the wafer with her thumbnail.

She did not read the letter immediately.

She laid it open on the table and she looked at it for a long minute before she read it. She looked at it the way a woman looks at a letter from a man she has been without for a hundred days and will be without for fifteen hundred more — not to take in the content, not yet, but to take in the fact of it: the hand, the weight of the paper, the particular small correctness of the lay of the lines, the way the *O* of *Olava* at the salutation had the small foot Olav's capital O always had, the way the ink was a slightly different ink than the ink he used at home.

She looked.

She understood, looking, that he was writing in a new ink because the old ink-well had run out and he had bought a new one at Cape Town — because of course he had, he had been at sea ten weeks by the time of the writing, the old ink would have been finished at Biscay — and she understood that the *O* with the small foot was the *O* of a man whose hand had settled, in ten weeks at sea, into the particular private hand he was going to use for her letters across the voyage. That hand was a hand he had not, before June, had. It was a hand that belonged to the version of Olav the *Semiramis* was making. She would come to know the hand as the hand of that version, and when he came home in November of next year the hand would go away — the home Olav would revert to the home hand — and the letters she had, across the voyage, would be the only place in the world where the *Semiramis* hand existed.

She had not thought of this before the letter was on her table. She thought of it now.

She read the letter.

---

The letter said:

> *Olava,*
>
> *We are at Cape Town on the seventh day of our eight-day refit. The bark has done well. We will lift on Friday for the long run east across the Indian Ocean to Calcutta.*
>
> *I signed today, for the first time, a commercial document as the navigator of the Semiramis. The captain took me ashore for the signing. The agent was Danish. The ledger was a ledger. The pen was mine. I signed cleanly. The captain walked me back to the ship afterward and told me that the business forgets, and that in forty years I will be the man at the quay telling this to a younger man. I registered what he was telling me. I am writing it here because I want you to know that I registered it.*
>
> *The mountain at Cape Town is not a thing the handbooks describe. It is larger than you have a way of imagining until you have seen it. I looked at it for a minute at the rail on the morning we came in because Sundberg told me that is what a man does on his first Cape, and then I went back to the work. I will look at it again when we lift on Friday. I will look at it for you.*
>
> *The ship is well. I am well. The cough has not come. I am eating the tea as I promised.*
>
> *Yours, Olav.*

She read it four times.

She read the first reading to know what it said. She read the second reading to know how it said it. She read the third reading to know what it had not said. She read the fourth reading to put together, for herself, the shape of the man who had written it, so that she could — when she was ready — write back.

---

The ledger was in the drawer of the writing table.

She took it out. She opened it to the page she had prepared in June, the night after he had left, with *1881* written at the top and with the six forecast letter-dates listed in pencil below it. She had written, next to *Cape Town / fourth or fifth week of September, 1881,* the phrase *expected letter.* She crossed the phrase out now, cleanly, with a single horizontal line, and she wrote above the crossing, in the slightly smaller hand she used for final entries:

> *Cape Town letter received 27 Sept 1881. Posted end of July, per the cover date. Eight weeks in transit from Dahl's agency at Cape Town. Three pages. Hand settled. He has become the navigator on the voyage before the crossing of the Indian Ocean. The captain took him ashore to sign at Dahl's. Captain's small welcome on the walk back registered and recorded in the letter. The mountain is looked at for me. The cough has not come. He is eating the tea.*

She stopped the pen. She considered.

She added, below, the smaller line she kept — on the same page but with a small indent — for the things she noted that were not for the ledger proper but for her own reading of him later:

> *The letter is three pages and contains no word about the crew. He has not named a single man on the ship. He named Dahl — the agent — and Sundberg — the bosun — and the captain. He did not name the mate. He did not name any common seaman. A man who is in the second month of an eighteen-month voyage and has not named a single man he eats with in his first letter home is a man who is either very tired or very careful. He is not very tired. Therefore he is very careful.*
>
> *I have noted the carefulness. I will not, in my reply, ask about the crew.*

She closed the ledger. She put the pencil back in the drawer. She folded the letter and put the letter in the small leather folder she kept on the shelf above the writing table, where she kept — she had told him at Christmas of '79 and he had not, in the year and a half since, asked about it — everything of his that had a date on it. The folder was thicker this autumn than it had been last autumn. It would be thicker still by next autumn. She had, when she had bought the folder from the bookbinder at Stavanger in the spring of '80, chosen a folder with enough capacity for ten years of a man's life. She had thought, at the bookbinder's, that this had been overestimating. She understood now that it had not been.

She sat for a minute at the desk with the folder closed under her hand.

---

She went back down to the kitchen.

The porridge was ready. Her father had set down the newspaper. Marit had poured the coffee. Her mother had set the table.

"Olava."

"Yes."

"The letter came."

"Yes."

"He is well."

"He is well. He is at Cape Town. They were lifting for Calcutta the Friday after he wrote. He signed a consignment as the bark's navigator."

"Good."

Her father did not ask for the letter. He had, since the engagement, established the rule — unannounced, the way the captain of Lindøy established all his rules — that Olav's letters to Olava were Olava's letters, and that he would read only what she offered to read to him, and that she would offer only what she wished to offer. Olava had at first, in the autumn of '80 when the rule established itself, found it unnecessary — she would have been happy to read any letter in full aloud at the breakfast table — and she had come, through the first year of the engagement, to understand that the rule was not about what she would have been happy to do. The rule was about what the captain had decided, at sixty-four, that he was not going to do. He was not going to be the father who read his daughter's engagement letters. He was going to be the father who sat at the breakfast table and heard from her mouth what she chose to say, and then returned to his newspaper, and allowed the privacy of the letter to be the privacy of the letter.

She offered, that morning, three sentences.

"He signed at a Danish agent's called Dahl. The captain took him ashore to do it. He has become the bark's navigator in everything but the wages. He is eating the tea."

"Good."

"Marit."

"Yes."

"He saw a mountain at Cape Town."

Marit, at the window with her coffee, looked up. She was, Olava had recently registered, at the age Olava herself had been at the tag game at Landa. The resemblance was not a thing Marit yet knew about herself; it would come to her in a year or two. The resemblance was also, at eighteen, half deliberate — Marit had been watching Olava since she was fourteen, and Marit did some of what she did because she had decided, without announcing it, that the Olava she knew was the Olava she wanted to be a version of.

"A mountain," Marit said.

"A very large one."

"A mountain in Africa."

"A mountain in Africa."

Marit considered this for a beat.

"Then he looked at Africa," she said.

"He looked at Africa."

"For us."

"For us."

Her father made a small sound that was not laughter but was the sound a man of his generation made when something his daughter said pleased him. He picked the newspaper back up. He turned the page. The conversation went back to the ordinary business of the morning.

---

That evening at her desk Olava wrote back.

The letter she wrote was a shorter letter than his — she had decided, in June, that her letters to him at sea were going to be short, because a man at sea did not need his wife-to-be sending him six pages of a Lindøy autumn when what he needed was the small particular news of the place he was coming home to — and she wrote, in her neat hand:

> *Olav,*
>
> *Your Cape Town letter arrived on the morning of the 27th. Eight weeks in transit, very nearly the figure you had given me on the evening before you left. You may use my forecast from now on as an anchor; I had the 27th written in the ledger a week ago.*
>
> *You signed at Dahl's. I am glad. I am gladder than I will say here, because you have written to me about the signing in the way a man writes to a wife-to-be about a moment he will remember for forty years, and that is not a letter I am going to answer at greater length than the moment deserves. I have put your letter in the folder. The folder has begun to be the folder I told you it was going to be.*
>
> *Look at the mountain when you lift. I will take the looking gladly.*
>
> *Marit is engaged to the schoolmaster's son in all but the paper. The paper will come in the spring, I think. She sends her regards. My father sends his. My mother does not send anything in particular, because she is my mother, but she heated the porridge the morning your letter came at a particular temperature she does not heat it at every Tuesday, and I noted it.*
>
> *The cough not coming is the sentence I read four times. Keep eating the tea.*
>
> *Yours, Olava.*

She folded the letter. She addressed it to *Mr. Olav Hestby, bark Semiramis of Bergen, c/o Messrs. Brun & Co., Calcutta.* She added, below the address, *Please hold for arrival.* She sealed it with the red wafer.

The letter would go down to Stavanger on the Friday coaster. From Stavanger it would go on the English mail-boat to Hull, and from Hull it would go on the India mail to Bombay and from Bombay by country steamer up the coast to Calcutta, and it would be, at the Norwegian consul's office in Calcutta, by the time the *Semiramis* made her arrival.

She put the sealed letter on the small tray by the door of her room where the outgoing mail for the Friday coaster lived.

She went to bed.

She did not, before she slept, go over the incoming letter again. She had read it four times. She had made her entries. The letter was now the folder's. She had, by the careful calibrated discipline of a woman who had decided to be engaged to a man at sea for two years, allowed herself the full receiving of the letter in its morning and had allowed herself the full answering of it in its evening, and she was not going to allow herself the turning-over of it through the night, because the turning-over through the night was the way a woman waiting for a man made the waiting into something it did not need to be.

She had decided, in June, that she was not going to let the waiting become anything it did not need to be.

She kept the decision.

She slept.

In the drawer of the writing table the ledger lay closed over the letter's paragraph, and the new paragraph, and the small indent with the private sentences she had written for herself that were not for the ledger proper; and the folder on the shelf above the desk contained, now, the first letter of the voyage, and the small red wafer that had sealed it, and all the other papers of Olav's that had a date on them; and in the kitchen below her bedroom the lamp was out, and her mother had banked the fire, and her father had gone up to his own room with his newspaper folded under his arm, and the house at Lindøy had gone into the deep quiet that a house in its people went into in the last week of September, in the autumn of the year the man of the engagement was ten thousand miles away looking at a mountain she had asked him to look at for her.
