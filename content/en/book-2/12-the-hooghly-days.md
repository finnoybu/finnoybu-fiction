The bark lay at her Strand mooring for three weeks while the timber came out of her hold and the rice and indigo went in.

The work of discharging and loading a thousand-ton bark in Calcutta in the autumn of 1881 was not work the crew did. It was done by Bengali stevedores under the direction of a Calcutta labor-contractor, in gangs of twenty, with long bamboo poles and small flat lighters that came and went off the bark's larboard side in a rhythm that started at six in the morning and ended at six in the evening and did not, through three weeks, miss a beat. The crew's work, during the discharge, was lighter — watch-keeping, the continual cleaning and mending the bark always needed, the small ship-board tasks a carpenter and a sailmaker and a steward generated on a ship at rest. There was time. There was, for the first time in eighty-seven days, more time than work.

It was a thing Olav had been warned about by Sundberg on the second day at the mooring.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"In port the crew has three risks the sea does not."

"I will listen."

"Drink. Women. Boredom. The drink takes some men out of the crew. The women take some more. Boredom takes the rest, and the men who were going to fight go through with it, and the men who were going to spend all their wages find the men who are going to take those wages, and by the day before we lift I will have a watch of eighteen men with perhaps twelve of them still useful. I have been a bosun at Calcutta four times. The figure is the figure."

"What does the bosun do about this."

"I give them a fourth risk to think about, which is me. The fourth risk is a risk I can calibrate. The other three I cannot. If I let the three run unopposed, I am negligent. If I oppose them with too heavy a hand, the crew comes to hate me and the voyage home is bad. The balance is the balance."

"And I."

"You are a common seaman, Hestby. You are also the navigator. You do not, as a rule, go ashore with the watch. You go ashore with the captain on his business. The captain's business is not the lower streets. The captain's business is the Exchange and the pilots' office and the agents. You will not, on this voyage, fight in any Bowbazar bar. You will not pay the Bowbazar prices. You will also not have the Bowbazar stories to tell the forecastle in the evening. You are not going to be popular in port, Hestby. Your popularity does not matter. Your popularity in Bergen in November of next year is the popularity that matters. Work that out in your head now and save yourself the confusion later."

"Yes."

"Good."

Sundberg went forward.

Olav understood that the bosun had been telling him, in the roundabout Karlskrona way, how to spend the three weeks at Calcutta. The bosun had said it the way Sundberg said most things — by naming the problem and trusting Olav to solve it — and Olav, who had watched Sundberg for ninety days now and had begun to understand Sundberg the way Sundberg understood him, took the advice as it had been given.

He spent his three weeks as the navigator.

---

The chart corrections went onto the ship's chart on the first morning, between six bells of the morning and four bells of the forenoon, as Brun had authorized. Olav worked at the chart-table in the captain's cabin with the colonel's slate beside him and the bark's own chart under his hands, and he inked the corrections in the small neat script of a navigator's second pass — not the pencil of a first draft, but the ink of a corrected original — and he found, at the end of the forenoon, that he had worked three hours without looking up and had not, during the three hours, thought about anything except the James and Mary shoal and the Mayapur bar and the new Welsh wreck at Fultah. It was the first extended stretch of the voyage in which his mind had been entirely at the work and nowhere else. He registered, at the end of the three hours, that this was the particular gift of a proper chart-table — that it absorbed a navigator the way a boat-shed at Finnøy absorbed his father, the way a kitchen absorbed his mother, the way a sickroom absorbed Olava when her brother had been dying.

A gift was a gift. He had not, at twenty-three, had many. He took this one.

---

On the Thursday he went ashore with Brun to the pilots' office and spent the afternoon at the Rangoon charts with Colonel Wighton, and the Rangoon charts were the charts Olav would, in six weeks, pilot down. He wrote a second set of corrections on a second slate. He worked until the punkah-boy's shift ended at five o'clock and the colonel's second clerk had brought him tea, which he drank, and which was the first Indian-style tea he had drunk and which was nothing like the tea he had promised Olava he would eat. He wrote a note of it in his head for the Calcutta letter.

On the Saturday he went with Brun to the Bengal Chamber of Commerce on Clive Street — a different Clive Street from Shields, but with the same colonial logic to its naming — and he watched Brun at the Exchange negotiate the rice and indigo at a pace that told Olav, if he had not known, that the captain had been negotiating rice and indigo at Calcutta every other year for fifteen years. He wrote down the prices in a small ledger Brun had given him on the Thursday. The ledger was for the navigator's use — a record a mate-to-be kept so that on a future voyage he would know the prices of the last one — and Olav wrote in the ledger with the small careful entries he had seen Olava write in hers.

On the Monday he went alone to the Asiatic Society's library on Park Street, which was a five-minute rickshaw ride from the Strand, and he sat in a reading-room under a punkah and read the Bengal Tidal Observatory's monthly report for August and September of '81, which gave the heights of the Hooghly bore at the springs and the neaps and which was, to a navigator about to take a bark down the river on an ebb in three weeks, material he had an interest in. He had asked Brun whether the trip to Park Street was permitted; Brun had said *go,* and had, when Olav had hesitated, laughed once — the first laugh Olav had heard from him in ninety-eight days — and said *I was seeing the same reports in the same room in 1859. Go.*

The library at Park Street was cool and had the thick dusty quiet of a European-built library in a hot city. Olav sat at a long reading-table with the Tidal Observatory reports and with two volumes of the *Asiatic Society Journal* which he did not, in his three hours, read but which he opened and turned pages in because the turning of pages in a volume of the *Asiatic Society Journal* was, at twenty-three in Calcutta, a small private pleasure he had not earned in any formal way but was, at twenty-three in Calcutta, permitted.

He registered, walking out of the library into the four o'clock heat, that he had been at Calcutta eight days and had not, in those eight days, done anything that resembled what Sundberg had called *the three risks.* He had not been to the Bowbazar. He had not been to any bar. He had not been to the streets where the sailors of other ships were, that evening, already at their second gin and at their first decision of what the evening was going to cost them. He had been at the Asiatic Society library reading about the tides.

He thought, on the walk back to the customhouse stairs: *I have become a particular kind of man on this voyage without noticing. The particular kind is the kind my father was at sixty when I was twelve. He did not go to the Stavanger bars when he took me in for the Christmas errands. He went to the chandler's and to the ironmonger's and to the post-house. I had not understood, at twelve, that he was that kind of man because he had chosen to be. I understand, at twenty-three, that he chose. And I have been choosing the same, on this voyage, in a city fifteen thousand miles from his, and I have not yet written to him about it, and I will not write to him about it because he will know from the absence of the writing that I have made the choice. He made the same choice himself, and his father before him. We are a family of men who do not write about the bars they did not go to.*

He thought this. He put his cap back on. He went down to the Strand.

---

On the second Friday at Calcutta he took his first Indian Ocean letter ashore.

He had written the letter over four evenings in the captain's chart-room, in pieces, in the ink that had been his Cape Town ink and was now nearly half gone. The letter was longer than the Cape Town letter. The length was the length a man wrote to his betrothed when he had been at a foreign port for two weeks and had seen, at the port, more than he could carry without setting some of it down.

The letter said:

> *Olava,*
>
> *We are at Calcutta. We made the Strand mooring on the fourth day after the pilot came aboard. The pilot is a Bengali called Banerjee. He took us up at eight knots on the flood past a place called Diamond Harbour where we anchored one night, and past a wreck at Fultah where a Welsh brig went down in June, and past the James and Mary shoal, which is the bottom-shifting place the handbooks at home warn about. I will pilot her down myself in three weeks with Banerjee at my shoulder. The captain has said so. The Colonel at the pilots' office has said so.*
>
> *The captain has also said — on Wednesday, at the customhouse stairs — that he will sign me off at Bergen in November of next year as his first mate. I told him I would not take the next Semiramis voyage. He asked why. I gave him the answer. He said I was giving the answer of a man who was not lying to himself about what he was refusing. He said you were a fortunate woman. He said this once and would not say it again. I am writing it to you because I want you to know that he said it, and because I wanted him to know, in the saying of it, that he had said it to a man who was going to tell his Olava.*
>
> *I have been to the Asiatic Society's library. I have been to the Bengal Chamber of Commerce. I have been to the Bengal Pilots' office three times. I have not been to the lower streets. I have decided, on this voyage, to be the kind of sailor in port that my father was at sixty, which is the kind who goes to the chandler's and not to the bar. I did not decide this; I watched myself deciding it. I am writing this down because I want you to know how the deciding felt, which was like watching oneself become more one's own father than one had thought one was going to be at twenty-three.*
>
> *There is a mountain here called the Shwedagon, but it is not here; it is at Rangoon. We will call there in six weeks on our way back. It is the gold mountain the handbooks draw pictures of in the colored plates. I will see it for you.*
>
> *The ship is well. I am well. The cough has not come. The tea here is not tea. The steward and I are reading each other's chronometers in the galley on Thursday evenings. The steward is a good man; his name is Torgerson; he is from Arendal; he is thirty. I named him because you will think I have not named a crew member yet and I am correcting the record.*
>
> *Yours, Olav.*

He folded the letter. He sealed it with the red wafer.

He wrote the address on the cover in the slightly larger hand he used for covers.

He took the letter ashore with Brun on the Friday. He gave it into the keeping of the Norwegian consul at Dalhousie Square, who had a small office over a tea-broker's warehouse and who took the letter with the businesslike hand of a man who had been receiving Norwegian sailors' letters at Calcutta for twelve years. The consul — a thin man named Møller with spectacles — stamped the cover with the consul's seal, which meant that the post would go on the first P&O steamer out of the Hooghly bound for Aden and thence to Southampton. The letter would reach Bergen, the consul said, by the first week of February.

"Mr. Hestby."

"Yes."

"Will there be a second letter before you lift."

"Not this time. The one is enough."

"Good. A man who writes two letters home from the same port is a man his wife-to-be worries about. A man who writes one is a man she reads. A man who writes none is a man who has forgotten."

"I will not be the third."

"No. I did not think so."

The consul stamped the letter again, unnecessarily, the second stamp a small formal flourish that was the consul's way of marking the moment a man had entrusted the consul with a thing he cared about. Olav took off his cap, put it back on, and went out into Dalhousie Square.

---

That evening, back on the bark, he stood at the main fife rail and watched the city's lamps come up across the water.

He thought, at the rail: *the letter is in the mail. The mail will go Tuesday. The letter will be read at Lindøy in February. Between now and February she will have the Cape Town letter and only the Cape Town letter.*

He thought: *she has the Cape Town letter. She has read it four times. She has put it in the folder. She is going about her autumn at Lindøy as if the Cape Town letter is the whole of what she knows of me, because the Cape Town letter is the whole of what she knows of me until February.*

He thought: *the marriage is the long rope. The long rope is spun out of a letter every four months. The long rope is not a rope a man can test for strength until he is home and has the whole of it in his hand.*

He thought: *I do not, at Calcutta, test it.*

He went below.

In his hammock, with Sven's steady breath five down and the bark lying quiet on the mud, he slept the good sleep of a man who had, at a foreign port in his second month of the foreign part of a voyage, kept the discipline he had set for himself and had found the discipline to be one his body would hold without having to be asked.

He slept.

The bark lay at her mooring. The city glowed beyond her. The Hooghly went on running past her hull at half a knot on the last of the ebb.
