They made Cape Town on the fifty-second day out of Stavanger.

The approach came up under the forenoon watch on a Tuesday in the last week of July. Olav was at the foretopgallant loosing the royal when the cry went up from the lookout at the main truck — *land ho* — and he came down the shrouds in the even unhurried way the watch had learned to come down since the weather in the North Sea, and he crossed to the starboard rail and looked.

The Table Mountain lay on the horizon to the south-southeast like a long piece of dark bread that had been set down on the water by a very large and careless hand. The clouds that gave it its name — the *tablecloth* the old hands called it, a kind of shallow layered lenticular that hugged the upper face — were on it that morning only in patches; the face of the mountain was mostly bare in the July light. Below the mountain, at the foot of its long sloping skirt, was the city.

Olav had read about Cape Town. He had read about it at Mrs. Kindingstad's in the winter of the school, in one of the gray-covered handbooks the library at Stavanger kept for its navigation students. The handbook had described a port of about forty thousand inhabitants, mostly English and Dutch-descended, with a harbor that was adequate for refit and victualling and was known, among the deep-water men, as the port at which a bark on the outbound leg to India took her last certain fresh water before the long passage across the Indian Ocean. The handbook had also described — as the gray handbooks did — the currents at the Cape, the particular fury of the southeaster that came off the mountain in the summer, the usual company agents, the usual forms to be filled, the usual charges. The handbook had not described the mountain.

The mountain was the thing Olav had not been prepared for. He stood at the rail and looked at it and understood, in the small slow way a man understands a new fact about the world, that there were shapes of land in the world that the handbooks did not describe because no gray-covered handbook written in a Stavanger reading-room could describe them. The mountain was one. He would, he thought, see several more of these in the next fourteen months.

Sundberg came to the rail beside him.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"First Cape."

"First Cape."

"There is a thing a man does on his first Cape."

"What thing."

"He looks at the mountain for a minute without saying anything, and then he goes back to the work. You have the minute."

Olav looked at the mountain for another thirty seconds.

"Now the work."

"Now the work," Sundberg said, and he moved off toward the foredeck where the anchor cable was already being cleared.

Olav followed him.

---

They lay in Table Bay for eight days.

Captain Brun had taken, before leaving Bergen, a commission from a Danish coal-agent with an office in Cape Town to top off the *Semiramis*'s coal for the galley and to carry a small consignment of mining iron from Cape Town's inner warehouses up to Natal at the first chance the ship's owners gave him after Calcutta. The consignment was a small piece of business. It added four tons to the bark's deadweight and perhaps two hundred kroner to the voyage's books. But the agent had, on the basis of the consignment, agreed to handle the *Semiramis*'s water-casks at a rate that compensated for the small tonnage, and Brun had agreed, because Brun was a man who had done this calculation a hundred times before and who knew the value of a standing arrangement with a coal-agent at a port that lay, for a Bergen bark, at the halfway mark of every voyage she would ever make to the East.

The refit work was the work Olav had expected.

They scraped the hull at the waterline. They struck the main topgallant yard down to the deck and re-set the iron strop that had given in the North Sea blow and had been mended only provisionally since. They replaced a frayed main sheet. They took aboard sixteen tons of galley coal and thirty-two casks of fresh water from a barge that came out from the Cape Town inner dock on the second day. The work was done in the steady African winter weather of the Cape — cool mornings, pale bright afternoons, the southeaster picking up about three on most days and blowing through the night — and the crew, eight weeks into the voyage and hardened into a unit by the North Sea blow and by the quiet weeks of Biscay and by the Portuguese trade winds that had run them down past Madeira without incident, did the work of a crew that had come into its rhythm.

Olav did his share. He went aloft with Sven and the Stjernerøy man Kinn on the second afternoon to strike the topgallant yard. He stood a rail watch on the third night. He worked the water-cask tackles on the fourth morning. He went over the side on a boatswain's stool on the fifth afternoon to help the carpenter inspect a seam at the garboard; the seam was sound, the voyage across the Bay had not opened it, and the inspection was a formality, but the formality was what a ship at mid-voyage in Table Bay did, and Olav was glad, hanging at the garboard seam with one hand on the mallet and his boots against the hull and the water of Table Bay ten feet below him, to have done the work on the side of a bark.

On the sixth day Captain Brun took him ashore.

---

The captain had gone ashore on each of the five previous days on ship's business. He had taken Aarseth on the second day and the mate of the second watch on the third; on the fourth and fifth days he had gone alone. On the sixth day, after eight bells of the morning watch, he came to the main fife rail and stopped in front of Olav.

"Hestby."

"Sir."

"Put on your coat."

"Sir."

"Mr. Sundberg has my leave to let you off the morning watch. We will be gone two hours. Bring your leather folder."

Olav put on his coat. He took the leather folder from his sea-chest — the folder that had, since the spring, contained his certificate, his postcard from Hamre, and Olava's letter of *I have noted* — and he joined the captain at the gangway. The captain did not, on the gangway, explain where they were going or why. Brun was not, in Olav's now two months' experience of him, a man who explained. They went down the gangway to the waiting lighter and took the lighter into the dock and climbed up the dock-stairs and walked, without speaking, along the quay toward the town.

The town had paved streets under its main lines and dirt under the side ones. The paved streets had shops on them with English signs and Dutch signs and one Portuguese sign and one Malay one. The side streets had houses and small warehouses and a butcher and a carpenter and the particular smells a warm-weather town produced in the late morning of a dry winter. The people on the streets were, as the handbook had said, mostly English and Dutch-descended and of the kind Olav had read about. There were also people he had not read about — Malay women in bright cottons carrying baskets, black African men in European jackets, a Chinese clerk with a ledger under his arm coming out of a trading house. The mix was different from the mix at any port Olav had been to. Wilmington, five years ago, had had its own mix — black workers at the pitch barrels, white sailors in the lower streets, the quiet separate business of them — but the mix at Wilmington had been a mix arranged around a particular cruelty, and the mix at Cape Town was arranged around something else Olav did not, at the first walk, fully understand. He would think about it later. He thought about it a little now.

The captain led him to a stone building with *Dahl & Bothma, Commission Agents* lettered in gold and black on the window. Inside were three desks, a coffee-service, and a short fat Danish man with enormous sideburns who stood up from the middle desk when Brun came in and said, in Danish:

"Captain."

"Dahl. This is Mr. Hestby. He carries the chart-work for this voyage. I brought him so that he could sign for the Natal consignment as the ship's navigator. You had asked."

"I had. Hestby."

"Mr. Dahl."

The captain did not, in the introduction, smile. Dahl did. Dahl gestured to a leather chair at the side of his desk and Olav sat, and Dahl opened a ledger and turned it toward Olav and pointed with a thick finger to a line in the ledger that had Olav's name on it, written in a clerk's hand in the upper column, with three blank lines below for a signature.

Olav took the leather folder from under his arm and opened it and took out the certificate and laid the certificate on the desk beside the ledger, because he had learned in the last eight weeks — at the signing of the articles at Stavanger, at the presenting of the certificate in Brun's cabin, at the three separate occasions since when someone had needed to see his papers — that a man showed the certificate before he wrote his name. Dahl looked at the certificate. Dahl nodded. Olav took his own pen from his coat pocket, dipped it in the ink-well Dahl had pushed across the desk, and signed the three lines in the careful hand he had learned to sign at Mrs. Kindingstad's:

*Olav Hestby, Navigator, bark Semiramis of Bergen.*

Dahl read the signature. He nodded again. He turned the ledger back to himself and blotted the ink.

"Thank you, Mr. Hestby. The consignment will be loaded the day after tomorrow. We appreciate your signature."

"Yes."

Brun stood. Olav stood. They left.

Outside, on the street, the captain did not, for perhaps half a block, speak. Then, at a crossing, he stopped.

"Hestby."

"Sir."

"That is the first time you have signed your name on a commercial document as a navigator."

"Yes, sir."

"You signed it cleanly. You did not, in signing, hesitate. You did not, after signing, look at the signature to check whether it was yours. You handed the certificate before you were asked. These are the small things a shipping agent notes, and they are the small things that determine whether a company that uses that agent twice a year continues to give its business to the bark the agent recommends. You have been the navigator on this voyage for one signature today. You will be the navigator on this voyage for the rest of it. We will be back aboard in thirty minutes. Walk with me."

They walked. They did not speak again until they reached the quay. At the quay Brun stopped and looked back once at the town and the mountain, and he said, in the small neutral voice a man used for a closing remark:

"I went ashore at my first Cape thirty-seven years ago this summer. I signed the consignment papers for the master of my brig as a second mate. The agent was a Dutchman. The agent is dead now. The brig is broken up in a scrapyard in Rotterdam. The master is buried at Swansea. The son of the Dutchman runs the office two streets up from Dahl's; he will not remember me if I call on him tomorrow. This is the business. It forgets. You will sign a hundred consignments in your career, Hestby, and in forty years you will be the old man at the quay explaining to a younger navigator that the business forgets. That is the business."

He started down the dock stairs.

Olav followed him.

He did not, on the way down, allow himself to be moved by the captain's small speech. He had understood, in the minute the speech had lasted, that the captain had meant it as a kind of welcome — a welcome to a trade Olav had now been told he belonged to. The welcome had been given in the captain's register, which was the register of a man who did not give anything else in any register. Olav had taken it. He did not need to respond to it. He would not have been expected to.

---

That evening, at the rail in Table Bay with the southeaster coming down the mountain and the bark tugging at her cable, Olav stood alone for some minutes and looked at the town and the mountain and he thought about Wilmington.

He had not, for five years, thought about Wilmington except as the thing in his life that had happened to him in the autumn of his eighteenth year. He had thought about it as an event — the desertion, the boarding house, the Swedish galley-cook's boat, the names he and Thomas had given when they signed for the first coaster out — but he had not thought about it as a port. The Wilmington of his memory was a river and a pitch-wharf and a rigging he had been made to tar without a stool. The Cape Town of this afternoon was a stone office with gold lettering and a short fat Danish agent who had not cared whether Olav was nervous or not because Olav had not been nervous.

He thought: *I was eighteen at Wilmington. I was nervous about everything. I was afraid of being seen by the wrong man at the wrong moment. I was afraid of the captain who was afraid of me. I was afraid of Haakon seeing that I was going to leave. I was afraid of Thomas changing his mind. I was afraid of myself.*

He thought: *I was twenty-three at Cape Town today. I was not afraid of Dahl. I was not afraid of Brun. I was not afraid of the ledger. I was not afraid of the signature being mine. The signature was mine. The hand that wrote it was the hand of a man.*

He thought: *this is the difference between eighteen and twenty-three. It is not the difference of five years. It is the difference of a cough, and a year at Mrs. Kindingstad's, and a certificate, and an engagement, and a wool coat at a jetty, and a drawer with a letter in it that I did not open the evening I came down to Stavanger. The difference is the sum of all the things I have done and not done since the Wilmington river.*

He thought: *I have not told Olava about Wilmington.*

He thought: *I have not told her because she did not ask. I have not told her because her father, on the jetty at Finnøy on the November Monday two and a half years ago, told me not to. I have not told her because telling her would be taking a thing from her that she had agreed to let me keep. I am not, tonight at Cape Town, going to tell her. I am, in this journal I am keeping for her, going to write her a letter about the mountain and Dahl and the signature. The letter will be true. It will not be the whole truth. It will be the truth I have decided she is going to have.*

He went below.

At the small desk in the narrow space the captain had, after a week at sea, permitted him for chart-work when he was not on watch, he wrote the first letter of the outbound leg:

> *Olava,*
>
> *We are at Cape Town on the seventh day of our eight-day refit. The bark has done well. We will lift on Friday for the long run east across the Indian Ocean to Calcutta.*
>
> *I signed today, for the first time, a commercial document as the navigator of the Semiramis. The captain took me ashore for the signing. The agent was Danish. The ledger was a ledger. The pen was mine. I signed cleanly. The captain walked me back to the ship afterward and told me that the business forgets, and that in forty years I will be the man at the quay telling this to a younger man. I registered what he was telling me. I am writing it here because I want you to know that I registered it.*
>
> *The mountain at Cape Town is not a thing the handbooks describe. It is larger than you have a way of imagining until you have seen it. I looked at it for a minute at the rail on the morning we came in because Sundberg told me that is what a man does on his first Cape, and then I went back to the work. I will look at it again when we lift on Friday. I will look at it for you.*
>
> *The ship is well. I am well. The cough has not come. I am eating the tea as I promised.*
>
> *Yours, Olav.*

He folded the letter. He sealed it with the small red wafer he had brought from Finnøy. He addressed it to *Miss Olava Lindøy, Lindøy by Stavanger, Norway,* and he put it in the envelope Brun kept for the ship's outbound mail.

The letter would go ashore to Dahl in the morning. Dahl would put it on the next English mail to Southampton. From Southampton it would reach Bergen in six weeks. From Bergen it would reach Lindøy on the autumn coaster.

It would reach her, by his rough figure, sometime in the last week of September.

He thought of her reading it at the kitchen table at Lindøy by the lamplight.

He did not, in the thinking, let the thinking go further.

He closed his writing case.

He went up to take the middle watch.
