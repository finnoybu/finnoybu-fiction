The *Semiramis* made Copenhagen on the seventh of September.

Brun had, at the Channel, decided to put her in for orders rather than run direct for Bergen. The decision was a common one on the homeward leg of a bark whose owners expected the next voyage's instructions to be waiting for her master in the hand of a Scandinavian agent at the mid-point port. Copenhagen was the usual mid-point. The bark took her pilot at Elsinore; came up the Sound in a light north-westerly; anchored in the outer roads on the evening of the sixth; warped in to a customhouse mooring on the morning of the seventh.

Brun went ashore at two bells of the forenoon.

He had said, at the morning muster, that the bark would lie at Copenhagen for two days while he collected the orders and conducted the company's business at the agents' office on Stormgade. He had said the crew would have the usual two nights ashore at their own expense. He had said that Olav, this time, was to come ashore with him on the first morning and assist with the chart-work at the bark's Copenhagen agent, as he had at Cape Town and Calcutta.

On the second evening the crew would have its shore-leave.

On the third morning they would lift for Bergen.

---

The first morning went at Stormgade.

The Copenhagen agent was a Dane of about fifty named Petersen. He received the bark's captain and the bark's navigator in an office on the third floor of a stone building opposite the Royal Theater. The office was brown and cool and had a long wall of chart-drawers and the particular small smell a Scandinavian commercial agent's office had in the September of 1882 — tobacco, paper, a faint clean smell of the cleaning woman who had, the night before, wiped down the table. Petersen took their coats. He brought coffee. He laid out the papers. He spoke, in the correct Dano-Norwegian of a commercial agent, at a pace that allowed Olav to follow while Brun was reading the clauses.

The orders were the orders Brun had expected: next *Semiramis* voyage to lift Bergen on the fifteenth of September 1883, twelve months from the end of this voyage, East India again, Bergen to Calcutta to Bergen, eighteen months. The company had — Petersen read aloud — pencilled in a first mate's berth for *O. Hestby,* on the condition that Captain Brun confirmed Hestby's acceptance at the Bergen pay-off.

Brun looked up from the clauses.

"Hestby."

"Sir."

"Do you wish Mr. Petersen to strike your name from the clause now, or do you wish to do it at Bergen in four weeks."

"Sir, I would like it struck now. I told you at Calcutta I would not take the next voyage. I do not want the clause in your company's books carrying a name I have already declined."

"Very good. Petersen."

"Captain."

"Strike the name. My next mate will be signed at Bergen. I will send the company the name by the first coaster after the pay-off."

"Understood."

Petersen drew a single line through *O. Hestby* in the clause. He initialled the strike. He turned the page.

They finished the papers in an hour. Brun paid Petersen's fee. They went out into Stormgade at half past eleven.

On the walk back to the customhouse quay Brun said, at a crossing:

"Hestby."

"Sir."

"You understand that the striking of your name is the moment your mate's berth on the next *Semiramis* is gone."

"Yes, sir."

"I will, between here and Bergen, not raise it again. You will not be offered the berth a second time. A company that has had a name struck once does not offer the berth again to the same name within a year. That is the company's rule."

"I understand."

"I wanted you to understand it this morning, at the striking, and not three days from now at the pay-off when Petersen's line would be a line you could not unstrike."

"Yes, sir."

"Good."

Brun did not, in the walk back to the quay, say anything else.

---

Olav was at his rail-place on the forenoon watch that afternoon.

He stood at the cap-rail and looked at the city across the water. Copenhagen from the bark's mooring was a long low line of the kind of old European city a handbook described with the word *handsome* — red tile roofs, the particular green-roofed steeples of the Danish tradition, the smell of a Baltic harbor in early autumn — and the day was a mild bright day of the sort the Sound had in September when the summer had not yet gone and the northwesterly had not yet come. The light on the buildings was a late-afternoon light. Olav watched a pair of small Danish ferries move along the quay and thought about the name Petersen had struck.

He did not, in the thinking, regret the striking.

He thought instead about how it had felt, at the walk back, to hear Brun tell him the striking was final. The finality had been, at the crossing at which the captain had said it, a piece of the geography he had for a year been walking toward. He had not, at the crossing, had any response except the three words *I understand, sir.* He had not wanted any other response. The finality had arrived, and the finality had been accepted, and the matter had gone off the table.

What was on the table now was the two days at Copenhagen.

---

The crew took its shore-leave on the evening of the second day.

Sven went with Odegaard and Torgerson and two others to a Danish sailor-place near the Nyhavn where — Torgerson told Olav over the bench at the galley on the morning of the second day — a man could get a plate of real food and a bed for four kroner. Torgerson was, Olav understood, arranging to keep Sven within the forecastle's ordinary shore-leave envelope, as Torgerson had been arranging since the May conversation at the chart-table when the steward had given Olav the Arendal advertisement. Torgerson had, without Olav having asked him, taken on a small shepherding role Sven had not requested and that Sven — Olav registered — had accepted.

Olav did not go with them.

He had arranged, through Petersen at the morning, a single-night room at a small Scandinavian lodging house on Vesterbrogade — not a Danish-sailor place, not a Bowbazar, a quiet house kept by a Norwegian-born widow — and he had booked it for the Wednesday night only. On the Thursday night he would return to the bark. He had explained to Brun at the quarterdeck on the Tuesday evening that he wished to have the one evening ashore to walk the city and eat his supper in a Scandinavian house, and Brun had given him the leave with the minimal motion of his head that was Brun's form of approval for the particular kind of request a man of Olav's construction made.

On the Wednesday evening he went ashore alone.

---

He walked.

He had, in his pocket, a small folded map of Copenhagen Petersen had given him at the morning. He had, in his head, a list of four places the handbook at Stavanger had recommended a Norwegian sailor ashore in Copenhagen see: the Round Tower, the Børs, the Marmorkirken, and the Kongens Have. He walked to the Round Tower first. He went up the spiral ramp to the platform at the top. He stood at the parapet and looked across the city at the harbor. He could see, from the tower, the bark at the customhouse quay — a small black shape with her masts raking a little in the September dusk — and he looked at her for perhaps a minute before he came down.

He walked down Købmagergade to the Strøget. He walked the Strøget to Kongens Nytorv. He crossed the square to the Charlottenborg and turned down to the Nyhavn, where the ordinary evening business of a Copenhagen port-quarter was underway — sailors drinking at the open tables of the tavern fronts, Danish women walking home from their shops, a small boy with a hoop, a fiddler with a cap at his feet playing a Danish tune Olav did not know.

He walked the Nyhavn at the south side, which was the quieter side, opposite the ships moored along the north quay. He was looking for a particular small restaurant his landlady had mentioned at his tea-time check-in — a Norwegian-run place at the corner of Nyhavn and Skt Annæ Plads that served fish in a Bergen style — and he was walking the quay at the particular slow pace a Norwegian sailor of twenty-three with a single evening ashore walked when he had no appointment and knew the place was open until nine.

He was perhaps thirty yards from the corner when a man came out of a doorway on his side of the quay and stopped.

The man stopped because he had, a moment before, been going to walk in Olav's direction, and he had seen Olav.

Olav had seen the man at the same moment.

The man, Olav registered in the count of one breath, was about twenty-eight. He was tall. He was dressed in the good everyday clothes of a Scandinavian clerk of a certain kind — a dark coat, a pale waistcoat, a narrow tie — and had under his arm a folded evening paper. He had the small neat fair mustache of a Stockholm clerk, which was not a Copenhagen mustache; and he had, Olav registered at the second breath, the small held attention of a man who had, at the moment of looking up from a doorway, recognized something in another man's face.

The man did not, for the count of one breath, speak.

He said then, in a careful Swedish that the Scandinavian ear heard as Swedish and not as Danish:

"Pardon me."

"Yes."

"You are Norwegian."

"Yes."

"You are a sailor."

"Yes. Off a bark at the customhouse."

"Forgive me. I am asking because I am at a lodging at this corner and I was going to my evening supper, and I saw your face, and I thought — I thought you were a man I knew at a clerks' meeting at the Stockholm Exchange two years ago. You are not he. I apologize."

The man raised his cap slightly.

He was going to walk past. Olav registered — in the half-second in which the raising of the cap was happening — that the man was going to walk past, that the encounter was going to end, that the polite small Scandinavian apology had concluded the matter in the way it should be concluded. The man had mistaken him for a clerk from Stockholm. The man was a Swedish clerk in Copenhagen on some small business. The encounter was a small civil exchange on a quay that would, in thirty seconds, be as if it had not happened.

Olav understood, at the half-second, that the man had not mistaken him for anyone.

He understood it from the man's face. The man had, at the word *sailor,* seen a thing in Olav that had confirmed, not corrected, whatever he had seen at first. The second look at Olav's face had been the look of a man who had understood, on seeing the sailor's coat and the sailor's hands, that he was not looking at the clerk from the Stockholm Exchange — the clerk from the Stockholm Exchange had not had a sailor's hands — and had understood, further, that the original recognition had not been of the clerk at all but had been of something in the cast of Olav's face, and that the something was the thing the second look had confirmed.

The man had, in the half-second, raised his cap to give Olav the option of not pursuing it.

Olav took the option, in a way the man was not expecting.

"You are going to your supper."

"I am, yes."

"I am also going to a supper. A place at this corner I was told about. A Norwegian place."

"The Bergen fish-place. I know it. I eat there once a week. I am going there tonight."

"Then we are going to the same door."

"We are."

The man did not, for the count of a breath, move.

Then he said:

"My name is Anders Lindqvist."

"Olav Hestby."

"Hestby."

"From Finnøy."

"Finnøy is in the Stavanger channel."

"Yes."

"Then you are far from home."

"I am at the end of a voyage to Calcutta. I am at home in four weeks."

"Ah."

Lindqvist was looking at him. The look was neither forward nor retreating. It was a look Olav had not, in his life, been looked at by a man in a street by, but he had understood, at the half-second when the cap had been raised, that he might.

"Mr. Lindqvist."

"Yes."

"Shall we have our supper at the same table or at separate ones."

"That is a question to ask at the door."

"I am asking it at the quay."

"Then at the same table. Unless at the door you decide otherwise. Most men decide at the door."

"I will not, at the door, decide otherwise."

"Then the same table."

"Yes."

---

They walked the last thirty yards to the Bergen fish-place without speaking.

They sat at a corner table near the window. Lindqvist ordered, in Danish with the Stockholm accent the Copenhagen waiter picked up at once, a plate of boiled cod with butter and a glass of Pilsner. Olav ordered the same. They spoke, for the first half hour, of the things two Scandinavians at a corner table in Copenhagen in September 1882 would ordinarily have spoken of. Olav's voyage. Lindqvist's business, which was insurance — he was, he explained, with a small Stockholm firm that wrote policies for Baltic steamer traffic, and he was in Copenhagen for a fortnight on the negotiation of a set of rates with a Danish counterpart office. The weather. Politics at a distance. A new sort of rope the Copenhagen chandler had begun carrying.

It was ordinary conversation.

It was also, on the under-layer neither of them named, not ordinary.

Olav understood — as the conversation moved through the second half hour — that what they were doing at the corner table was the thing that happened when two men of a particular construction met in a particular city at a particular kind of table and decided, each on his own, to allow the table to continue. They were conducting the ordinary conversation on the surface. They were, under the conversation, acknowledging to each other — in small details of phrasing, in the angle at which each held his eye, in the particular short laughs neither of them gave at the other's remarks — that the under-layer existed.

Neither of them crossed the under-layer into the surface.

It was not the way these things were done, Olav understood, in a Copenhagen Nyhavn restaurant in 1882. These things were done, when they were done, by the two men agreeing with their eyes over two suppers that both men understood both men, and by the two men agreeing — without a word to name it — either to proceed to a shared lodging at the end of the supper, or to part at the door and remember each other for the rest of their lives as the man at the corner table in Copenhagen in September.

Olav had decided, at the ordering of the cod, which of the two he was going to choose.

Lindqvist, he understood at the second half hour, had understood his choice from the moment the cod had been ordered.

---

At the end of the supper Lindqvist paid for his own plate. Olav paid for his. They walked out into the Nyhavn together.

The quay was dark now. The lamps were lit. A small Danish fiddler at the south end was, at half past eight, still playing his tune. Lindqvist and Olav walked the quay slowly in the direction of the Charlottenborg.

At the corner Lindqvist stopped.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"I am going to my lodging now."

"I am going to mine."

"They are, I suspect, not the same lodging."

"They are not."

"I did not think they would be."

"No."

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"Will you take an address."

Olav considered for a beat.

He considered what the taking of an address would mean, and what the not-taking of an address would mean, and what the particular construction of Lindqvist as a Stockholm clerk of twenty-eight with a Baltic-insurance firm and an annual trip to Copenhagen for a fortnight of rate negotiations implied about the address and about the rate at which letters might, from a Lindøy farmhouse or a Hestby boat-shed, be directed to it.

He decided.

"Yes. I will take it."

"Here."

Lindqvist took from his inside pocket a small card — the ordinary calling card of a Scandinavian commercial clerk, printed at a Stockholm press — and handed it to Olav. The card said *Anders Lindqvist, Stockholm Marine Insurance Co., Skeppsbron 18, Stockholm.*

Olav put the card inside his coat.

"Mr. Lindqvist."

"Yes."

"I am going to write to you once, in a month or so, from Finnøy. The letter will be short. It will say that the *Semiramis* made Bergen and that I am home. After that letter I am not going to write again, and I am asking you not to write to me. The Finnøy post is a small post. My fiancée is on Lindøy. Her sister handles much of the Lindøy correspondence. A Stockholm letter to a Finnøy farm is a letter the post-house remarks on."

"I understand."

"You are accepting the condition."

"I am accepting the condition, Hestby. I have not asked you for more than the evening. The card is so that the evening has a return address when it reaches you on a winter morning and you have something to write in answer, if you wish. One return answer is what I am asking for. If the answer does not come, I will not send anything after. If the answer comes, I will file it and I will not send anything after. I understand the construction."

"Good."

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"I will not, at the mention of your name in any room in Stockholm, look up. I will not keep your card. I am going to burn it tonight at my lodging so that it is not in my possession. You will have the address in your head, if you have looked at it carefully. If you have not looked, you have only the card, and after the letter you will burn the card, and we will each keep the evening in our own interior."

"Yes."

"Good."

Lindqvist did not shake his hand.

He took off his cap instead, briefly. It was the smallest and most correct of the gestures a Scandinavian man of his generation made to another Scandinavian man at a Copenhagen quay corner in September. He put the cap back on. He turned and went up Skt Annæ Plads.

Olav stood at the corner for perhaps a minute.

He looked at the card in his hand once.

He committed the address to his memory — *Anders Lindqvist, Stockholm Marine Insurance Co., Skeppsbron 18, Stockholm* — by reading it three times.

Then he walked back to his own lodging on Vesterbrogade.

In his room at the Norwegian-born widow's he took the card and, at the small stove the room had for the autumn nights, he lit the edge of it from the candle and he watched the card burn until it was ash. He brushed the ash into the stove. He closed the stove-door.

He washed at the basin.

He lay on the bed.

---

He did not sleep for some time.

He thought, at the dark ceiling, about the evening.

He thought about the cod. He thought about Lindqvist's Stockholm accent in the Dano-Norwegian. He thought about the particular small short laugh Lindqvist had given at a remark Olav had made about the Royal Theater. He thought about the raising of the cap at the corner — the first raising, not the parting one — and about how the first raising had been the question and how Olav's answer had been the sentence *Shall we have our supper at the same table or at separate ones.*

He thought: *I said that sentence.*

He thought: *I said it without consulting myself. I said it because the man at the quay was the man at the quay and I was the man at the quay, and the sentence had arrived in my mouth before the part of me that manages sentences had a chance to ask whether the sentence was one I was going to say.*

He thought: *I did not say it to do anything about the under-layer. I said it to acknowledge that the under-layer existed, and to say it to the only other man in the world who would understand it as such, and to say it in the only Scandinavian city of a certain size in which two such men could meet on a quay corner in September and sit at a corner table and conduct the ordinary conversation with the under-layer between them and not be, for the conducting, threatened.*

He thought: *Copenhagen was the city.*

He thought: *the quay was the quay.*

He thought: *Lindqvist was the man.*

He thought: *and the end of an eighteen-month voyage at a mid-point port with four weeks to home was the moment.*

He thought: *if I had met Lindqvist at Cape Town on the outbound last July I would not have sat at the table. I would have said, at the corner, that I had another engagement, and I would have gone to the Bergen fish-place alone, and the evening would have been a small polite Scandinavian evening of not-having. At Copenhagen on the homeward I sat. The four weeks were not, I now understand, incidental to the sitting. The four weeks were the condition. The four weeks are what made the sitting possible. If the voyage had been a longer voyage — if there had been six months more ahead of me — I would not have sat. I have sat because I am at the end.*

He thought: *I have, in the sitting, not done anything except sit. I have not touched the man. I have not been touched by him. I have agreed an address and have burned the card. I have promised the one letter. I have declined further correspondence. The sitting will, at the post-Bergen accounting of my drawer, be a small entry that says Copenhagen, corner table, two hours, one letter agreed, card burned.*

He thought: *that is all.*

He thought: *I do not, at the end of the evening at twenty-three, regret the sitting.*

He thought: *I also understand, in the register I have been using for the voyage, that the sitting was the last of the voyage's tests. I passed it. Or I failed it. I do not know which is the correct verb. I declined to go with Lindqvist to a shared lodging, and I took his address. Both of those are things a man of my construction can do. Together they are a thing my construction has to hold.*

He thought: *the holding is the work.*

He thought: *I will, at the Finnøy boat-shed drawer, add the evening to the drawer, and the drawer will hold it with the rest, and the rest now includes Haakon and the Sigrid in '76 and the lee of the galley and Mrs. Lauritsen's on Russell Street and the evening at the corner at Nyhavn.*

He thought: *the drawer is getting full.*

He thought: *it is not, however, too full. It has room.*

He slept.

The widow's clock in the hall downstairs rang eleven. He was, by then, not awake to hear it.
