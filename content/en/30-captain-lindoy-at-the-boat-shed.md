The shed held him most evenings of the late autumn of 1880.

He had begun going out to it after supper in the summer following his daughter's Christmas conversation with the Hestby boy — the conversation he had not been in the kitchen for, by the agreement his daughter had made with him the night before over the small lamp at her mother's kitchen — and he had not, across the summer and into the autumn, given up the habit. The shed was his. The bench was his. The wooden hand, on its small wooden stand where he had set it at supper and would pick it up again at midnight, was his. The shed was, in the phrase his father had given him when he was twelve and the boat-shed at Halsnøy had become his father's evening refuge after a voyage, *a shape a man carried into a room that was his own shape.*

He was at the shed at seven o'clock on a Tuesday evening in the last week of October.

The lamp on the bench was the small brass one his wife had given him in '62. The lamp had been a lamp of his wife's grandmother's at Halsnøy. He had taken it to the shed in '76, the year after Asbjørn, because his wife had said — in the small flat voice she used for things she did not wish to be argued with — *take it, the shed is yours.* He had taken it. He had put it on the bench. It had been on the bench since.

On the bench beside the lamp were the items he had been working at for the past two weeks: a small leather wallet of Russian leather, about six inches by four, that he had bought at a Bergen chandler in '68 and had used, across the twelve years since, for the papers he wished to keep at the house rather than on a ship. The wallet had been in the drawer of the writing-desk in the parlor for most of those twelve years. He had taken it out in the second week of October and had begun the work of preparing it for the piece of paper it was going to carry to Finnøy on the last Monday of November.

The paper was the engagement paper.

He was polishing the wallet.

---

The right hand did the polishing. The left hand — the wooden hand — sat beside the wallet on the bench, oriented toward the lamp, in the position that let him, if he wished, reach for it. He did not, in the work of polishing, need the left hand. The work was a one-handed work; polishing leather with a soft cloth and a small tin of the beeswax compound the chandler at Stavanger made up for him twice a year was a work that a man with one hand could do more slowly but no worse than a man with two.

He polished slowly.

He had, at sixty-two, the patience of a man who had had his impatience taken out of him a long time ago.

The wooden hand on the bench had been his left hand since the fourteenth of March, 1857. He had been thirty-nine. He had been bosun on the *Eva,* a Stavanger-registered brig working the Brest-Le Havre cognac run, and the rope had taken the hand on the ninth day out of Brest on a night of weather that had not been, by the standards of the men who had been at the *Eva* longer than he had, an especially hard night. The rope had been one of the lower topsail buntlines. It had jammed at the yardarm. He had gone up to clear it. He had cleared it. On the way down, on the weather side of the foremast, a different line — the weather sheet of the same sail, which the mate at the deck had been, at that moment, trying to reset without calling him off the rigging — had run out under load and had taken the hand at the wrist. The hand had gone with the line. He had come down on the lee side with his right hand and his teeth, because he had, on the way down, decided that he was not going to fall off a mast at thirty-nine having taken a hand for the ship, and a man who was not going to fall off a mast found a way down using whatever was left.

The French surgeon at Brest had finished the work the rope had begun. The surgeon had been a navy man of perhaps sixty, retired to the port as a civilian surgeon for the merchant trade, with the small gruff impatient kindness of a man who had been cutting off sailors' hands for forty years. He had done the cutting at the Brest infirmary in the morning. He had told Lindøy, in the thick English of a French sailor, *you will have a hand. The hand will be wood. You will learn the wood. Good day, monsieur.* He had left.

Lindøy had learned the wood.

He had learned it across the twenty-three years since.

---

He picked up the wooden hand.

He set it on his knee.

He did not, in the setting, pause the polishing; his right hand kept at the wallet. The wooden hand on his knee was a habit. He did it in the shed on the evenings when the memory of Brest came up, because the hand on the knee — the weight of it, the oak of it, the small brass pin at the wrist that held the strap — was, he had learned over the twenty-three years, a thing that turned the memory of Brest from the kind of memory that ate a man into the kind of memory a man held.

Tonight the memory was not of Brest. Tonight the memory was of Asbjørn.

He had, across the five and a half years since Asbjørn, not allowed the memory to live in the house. The house was his wife's. His wife had — across the year after the death and the four and a half years since — done the work of shaping the house into a house the family could live in without the son's absence filling the rooms. His wife's work had been strong work. He had not, in any week of the five and a half years, registered the absence at the kitchen table or in the parlor or at the foot of the stair. The house had closed around the loss. That was what houses built to hold women's work did.

The shed was different.

The shed had been, across the twenty-three years since Brest, the place he had carried the things the house had not been asked to hold. Brest had been in the shed since '57. Brest had been in the shed on the nights of the six years when Brest had been, in his interior, the loudest thing a man could carry without going into the kitchen at suppertime and pulling down a bottle. Brest was still in the shed. Brest would be in the shed when he died.

Asbjørn had, since June, begun to be in the shed too.

Asbjørn had died in February of '75. The cough had come on him in the October of the year before, at sixteen, in the first autumn term at the Stavanger gymnasium his mother had sent him to in the hope that he would be, at seventeen, ready for the navigation school and, at nineteen, ready for a berth on a coaster that took him to the Atlantic in the shape his father had wanted Asbjørn to be in when the Atlantic took him. The cough had deepened through the winter. The Stavanger doctor — Dr. Due, who would, in the years after, be the doctor the family went to — had said in the December, with the small flat clarity Due used in the consulting-room, *it is the tuberculosis. He has a year, perhaps two.* Asbjørn had had six more weeks. He had come home from the Stavanger gymnasium on the train at the end of the December term and had never, in the five weeks between the train and the coffin, been well enough to leave the back bedroom. He had died in the back bedroom on a Thursday morning in early February with his mother at the bedside and his father at the foot of the bed and his sister Olava — who had been fourteen, who had already been the woman she had become — at the small chair beside the bed holding the cup of water Asbjørn had not been able to drink.

Lindøy had, across the five and a half years since the Thursday morning, held himself against the memory in the way a man holds himself against a wind on a deck: by bracing. The bracing had, in the early years, taken most of his strength. By '78 he had begun to need less of his strength for the bracing. By '80 he had begun to need, on certain evenings, the shed.

The shed was where the son was.

---

He set the wooden hand back on the bench.

He picked up the wallet again.

He turned it in his right hand. The leather had, under two weeks of beeswax, become softer. It would not, by the last Monday of November, be fully broken in — two weeks was not enough — but it would be a wallet a man had worked for. The engagement paper, when his wife had folded it three times at the kitchen table on the Sunday before he sailed and had given it to him to put in the wallet, would go into a wallet that had been readied for it. That was the small private ceremony he was making in the shed across the late autumn. His wife did not know he was making it. He did not intend to tell her. She would know, at the Sunday kitchen, by the look of the wallet.

The boy.

He had, across the fifteen months since the Hestby boy had first landed at the Lindøy jetty with his father's borrowed boat under him, formed an opinion of the boy. The opinion had begun at the first visit as a cautious opinion — a boy of twenty who had been at sea for five years and had come back with a cough and a silent-ness around his past was a boy whose depths a Lindøy captain was inclined to sound before committing a daughter to them — and had moved, across the five subsequent visits and the small handful of conversations he had had with the boy himself at the Lindøy shed and bench, into an opinion he was now willing to register.

The boy was the right boy.

He was right because he was, at twenty-one, the kind of man Lindøy had not been at twenty-one. The boy had been, in the five years at sea, turned into a man whose face did not announce his interior. The face had been turned. Lindøy had recognized the kind of turning. Lindøy had had his own face turned on the *Eva* across the Brest year, and had been — at thirty-nine at the French infirmary — a man whose face did not announce his interior for the forty-five years afterward. The boy had started early. Lindøy approved of the starting-early. A man whose face did its own work at twenty-one was a man whose face would do the work the marriage required at thirty-five, at fifty, at seventy.

He had approved at the third visit. He had not said he had approved. He had told his wife in the parlor after the third visit, in the small flat voice he used for things that were not to be further discussed, *the boy will do.* His wife had said, *I know.* He had said nothing further. His wife had understood what he had not said — that the approving had been the approving of a captain who had, in three visits, satisfied himself that the boy was the boy Olava had chosen — and she had not required him to say it twice.

The one piece of the boy that was not yet closed in Lindøy's ledger was Wilmington.

He had known since August. He had known by the small particular silence the boy had around his eighteenth and nineteenth years — the years from the summer of '76 through the autumn of '77, the eleven months the boy had, in none of his conversations at Lindøy, accounted for at the kind of specific detail a sailor who had been through those ports would naturally produce. Lindøy had, at the third visit in the August, put the question indirectly and had watched the boy's answer. The answer had been clean — a list of ports, a list of ships, a handful of small specific details. The answer had been too clean. A boy who had been at sea the way the boy had been at sea at eighteen did not produce a clean answer about eleven months. A boy who produced a clean answer had been trained to produce it.

The training had been, Lindøy had understood with the small flat clarity of a man who had known three other sailors who had walked off ships under borrowed names, the widow-house training.

He had not asked the boy about it.

He had instead, the following week, gone to Stavanger for a consular hour that he told his wife was about a harbor-board matter and was not. At the Stavanger consulate — a small two-room office on the upper floor of a building on Kirkegata — the consul, a man Lindøy had known for twenty years, had listened to the question without asking why the question was being asked. The consul had pulled the Norwegian articles for the eleven months in question. The articles had not had the boy's name on them. The consul, understanding the answer the articles had given, had asked Lindøy what he wished to do. Lindøy had told him.

The consul had, across the next four weeks, written three letters on Lindøy's behalf to the merchant rolls at Liverpool, and had received back the three confirmations Lindøy had needed. The boy's eleven months were now, in the Norwegian articles as Lindøy had arranged them, a coastwise year out of Liverpool under the boy's own name. The marriage would not be questioned. Lindøy had paid the consul's fee — a small Stavanger sum, the kind of sum a captain paid for a consular favor — and had gone home on the evening ferry and had not told his wife, and did not intend to.

He would tell the boy.

He would tell the boy at the Finnøy jetty on the afternoon of the signing, at the moment of leaving, in the small closing minute a captain had with a future son-in-law when the paperwork was in the coat pocket and the boat was at the tiller. The telling would be brief. The boy would understand. The boy was the kind of boy who understood the kind of thing Lindøy was going to tell him.

---

He set the wallet down.

He sat for some time without moving. The lamp on the bench was steady. The wooden hand on the bench was still. His right hand — the one that had done the polishing — lay on his knee where he had rested it.

He thought, for the first time across the two weeks at the shed, about Olava.

He had not, across the two weeks, permitted himself to think about Olava at the shed. The shed was for the wallet and for Brest and for Asbjørn and for the evaluation of the boy. Olava was not for the shed. Olava was for the kitchen. She had been for the kitchen since she had been five years old and had come to the kitchen table with her small slate to ask him to draw her the shape of a coastwise sloop that he had been telling her about at the supper. She was twenty now. She was in the kitchen tonight, under the same lamp she had been under for fifteen years, helping her mother with whatever her mother was at. She would be, on the last Monday of November, the subject of a paper he would be carrying in the wallet he was polishing.

He permitted himself, for one minute, to think about her at the shed.

He did not think about her as a child. He thought about her as the twenty-year-old woman he would be giving, at some month after the signing, to the Hestby boy in the small front parlor at Finnøy. The woman was — had been, since perhaps the fifteenth year, and had become visibly since the sixteenth — the kind of woman who did not require the protecting the captain was, in his interior, ready to do. She had, at sixteen, already made herself into the kind of woman who protected her own self. She had, at nineteen, on the Christmas morning the captain had agreed not to be in the kitchen for, made the agreement with the boy herself. She had made the agreement on her own terms. The captain had, in the parlor across the hall, heard the small tone of her voice through the wall — he had not been able to hear the words — and had known, without hearing the words, that she was making the agreement the way she had decided to make it.

He had been glad. He had been glad in the way a father is glad when a daughter has become, past his protecting, a woman who would not need the protecting.

He had also, at the same time, registered the small sharp registering a man registers when he understands that the protecting he had been, in his interior, preparing himself to do across the daughter's first twenty years had not, in the end, been required in the shape he had prepared it for. He was not resentful of the not-requiring. He was glad of it. But the readiness — the shape a father makes in himself to protect a daughter — did not, by the daughter's not-requiring, unmake itself. It remained. It had, since the Christmas morning, remained in a shape that had no place to go.

The shed, Lindøy understood tonight, had become the place the readiness went.

He picked up the wallet again.

He held it for a moment without polishing. Then he set it down.

He picked up the wooden hand. He put it on the leather strap he had been wearing across the wrist for twenty-three years. He fastened the small brass buckle with his teeth, the way he fastened it every morning and had not, in twenty-three years, let anyone else fasten for him.

He stood.

He walked to the shed door.

He opened it.

The late October evening was cold. The wind was easterly. The shed faced the channel, and the channel at this hour was a narrow dark line of water between the Lindøy shore and the Finnøy shore, with the Finnøy lighthouse visible at four miles of distance and, behind the lighthouse, the low dark shape of Finnøy island with a single small light at the Hestby-gaard kitchen window.

The kitchen window with the single small light was the Hestby boy's mother's kitchen.

It was the window his daughter would, at some evening in some year the future had not yet named, be visible at from this shed door.

He stood at the door for some minutes.

He did not cross himself. He was not, by the nature of his Norwegian Lutheranism, a man who crossed himself. But he permitted himself, at the door, the small inward gesture a man permits himself when he has stood at a door and has looked across a channel at the house his daughter is going to be visible at and has understood that the looking is a piece of the preparation he had not known was part of the preparation.

He closed the door.

He went back to the bench.

He finished the polishing.

At midnight he took the wooden hand off the wrist and set it back on the bench in the position it had been in when he had sat down. He put out the lamp. He went across the yard to the house. He went up the stair. He got into the bed beside his wife, who had been asleep for three hours and who did not wake when he got in.

He slept.
