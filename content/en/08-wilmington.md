The brig made Wilmington on the thirtieth day out of Stavanger. The North Carolina coast came up out of the haze on a Sunday morning in the form of low sandbar islands that did not look, Olav thought, the way a coast was supposed to look. The Norwegian coast rose; the Portuguese coast climbed; the African coast smelled. The Carolina coast simply *appeared,* flat and pale and uninterested, a long line of yellow sand that gave no signal as to whether a ship would be welcomed or warned off.

A pilot came aboard at the bar. He was a thin man in a straw hat, with skin the color of tobacco and the slow careful English of the American coast. He took the brig over the bar and up the river, past two American men-of-war at anchor, past a town that Olav could not pronounce, past a long pine-bordered shore on which black workers were stacking pitch barrels in the heat. The river was the color of cooled coffee. The trees on the banks were a kind of tree Olav had never seen, with hanging grey beards on them like old men under sail. The sky was very pale and very wide and very *foreign.*

He stood at the rail and he looked. He did not speak.

Beside him, Haakon was also at the rail. They had not spoken alone since the night of the royal-yard. They were not alone now — half the crew was at the rail — but Olav could feel Haakon's silence three feet to his left as a particular quality of air, the way a man feels another man's presence in a dark room.

"Wilmington," Haakon said, after a long while.

"Yes."

"You will not see the city for a day or two. We will load at the lower wharf. The town is two miles up."

"Yes."

"The town is —" Haakon paused, considering. "It is a town for sailors. There are houses. There is whiskey. There are women. There is the law, which is kind to white sailors and is not kind to any other kind. The four are not, in practice, offered to you in that order. You will be offered three of them in the first hour ashore. The fourth finds you on its own time. Stay clear of the lower streets after dark. Stay with one of the older men if you go ashore at all. Do not go alone."

"I will not."

"Good."

Haakon did not move from the rail.

He did not say what he had perhaps come to the rail to say.

Olav did not ask him.

They stood there, side by side, and watched the Carolina shore pass.

---

The work began at the lower wharf on Monday morning and went on for two weeks.

The captain had decided that, while the previous cargo of salt was discharged into lighters, the rigging of the brig would be tarred. This was a fair decision in itself — the rigging was due — but the captain had decided also that the tarring would be done by Olav, alone, with no boatswain's stool, and that it would be done daily until it was done, including the parts of the rigging that men did not normally tar without a stool because it would kill them.

Olav did the work. He did it because he had decided in the salt-hold that he was going to live, and he had understood, at the salt-hold and at the truck and at the royal-yard, that he was going to live by enduring whatever the captain put on him until he could leave. Tarring the rigging without a stool was a thing he could endure. It would take all of his hands' skin off him and it would make his shoulders into things that did not belong to him, but it would not, by itself, kill him. He did the work.

He did it singing. This was a refinement he came to on the second morning. The captain, at the quarter, was watching. The crew on deck was working at the cargo. The carpenter had set up the deck stages. Olav, fifty feet up the foremast shrouds with a pail of tar lashed to his belt and the brush in his right hand and the rigging held in his left, started singing one of the opsangs his uncles had sung on Finnøy. He sang it not loudly. He sang it well. He had a tolerable voice, and he sang it the way it was meant to be sung — slow, with a small drop on the last beat of the line, the way a man sings when he means to be heard a long way.

The captain did not look up. The captain did not look up the next morning either, when Olav started a different song. By the third day the carpenter, on the deck below, was humming the chorus.

The mate, Solvaag, on the third afternoon, walked under the foremast and spat into the scuppers and shouted up:

"Quit singing, boy!"

Olav kept singing.

Solvaag picked up a marlinspike from the deck and threw it at the foremast. It struck the wood three feet below where Olav was working. The marlinspike fell and stuck point-down in the deck.

Olav kept singing.

Solvaag stood looking up at him for a long minute. Then he walked away. He did not, Olav noticed, go to the captain.

That was, Olav thought, the first piece of information he had had in some time that was unambiguously good news.

---

The man Olav came to know, in the second week at Wilmington, was a deckhand named Thomas Engelstad.

Thomas was twenty-three. He was from Stjernerøy, an island twenty miles from Finnøy, close enough that Olav had been to it twice as a boy. He was tall and thin with the kind of yellow-brown hair that does not grey, only fades, and a mouth that fell easily into a wry quiet smile that he kept mostly to himself. He was on the watch opposite Olav's. They had not spoken at sea. They began to speak in port, because Thomas was the man assigned to mix the tar that Olav was using, and so Thomas was the man Olav came down to twice a day.

"Hestby."

"Engelstad."

"You are singing them mad."

"I know."

"It is a good plan."

"It was not a plan. It became one."

"That is the best kind."

Thomas dipped Olav's tar-pail into the barrel and refilled it. He worked with an easy precision. He looked up at Olav while he worked.

"You are going to leave," he said.

It was not a question.

Olav did not answer.

"It is on your face," Thomas said. "And it is on his. You should not let it be on your face. You should let him see it on your face only when you are gone."

Olav took the pail from him. The pail was heavy with new tar; the rope handle creaked in his hand.

"Thomas."

"Yes."

"You have known me for three days."

"Yes."

"Why do you say this to me."

Thomas wiped his hands on a rag. He did not look at Olav while he wiped them. When he was finished he folded the rag and tucked it into his belt.

"Because I am going to leave too," he said. "And I have been waiting for someone to leave with."

Olav looked at him.

"How long."

"Since Lisbon."

"Lisbon."

"I saw what he did to Nils. I did not move either, Hestby. I was on the deck. I was three steps from him. I did not move. After Nils, I decided. I was waiting for the second man."

"Why the second man."

"Because one man can desert. Two men can desert. One man caught is a fool. Two men caught is a conspiracy. Two men is harder to deny. Two men is harder to flog. Two men is one man for the captain to make an example of and one man for the consul to send home. The second man is the one who saves the first."

Olav thought about this.

"You are the second," he said.

"I am the second," Thomas said. "You are the first. You decided in Lisbon too."

"I decided in the salt-hold."

"Then you are still the first."

He smiled, briefly — that wry quiet smile that was not, Olav was learning, a kind smile so much as a survival.

"Take your tar up," he said. "Sing him another song. We will talk on Sunday."

---

On Sunday, in the dog-watch, they walked together to the lee of the deckhouse and sat on a coil of rope and they made the plan.

It was a simple plan because complicated plans, Thomas said, were the kind that hanged you. The brig was due to lie at the wharf for another four days. There was a Swedish galley-cook at a small steamer berthed at the wharf below them, a man Thomas knew from a previous voyage, who could be persuaded to lend them a small boat for an hour at midnight in return for a sum of money that Thomas, between the two of them, had. They would slip ashore. They would walk to a boarding house Thomas knew of in the lower town, run by a Norwegian widow whose business was sailors who had decided to stop being sailors for a while. From the boarding house they would, in a week, find a different berth on a different ship — under different names — bound for any port that was not this one.

"Different names," Olav said.

"Different names."

"Even at home, eventually?"

"Eventually we will use our own names at home. But not on a ship. Not for a year. The captain will write a letter to the Norwegian consul. The consul will look for our names on every set of articles in every American port. We do not give them our names."

"What name will I use."

Thomas considered him.

"What was your mother's father's name?"

"Vaageland."

"Vaageland."

"Yes."

"Then for the next year, on a ship, you are Olav Vaageland."

Olav thought about this. The new name felt strange in his mouth. He spoke it once, silently. Then again. *Olav Vaageland.* Vaageland was a farm on the south side of Finnøy, where his mother had been born. The name was as good as any. The name had nothing to do with the church. The name was, he realized with a quiet small surprise, *unrooted* — and so for the next year it would do.

He did not tell Thomas about Hestby. He did not say *the church on Finnøy.* He did not say *the name I was baptized with.* He did not say *I have been carrying a stone around with me.* He said only:

"Vaageland. Good."

"Good."

They sat in the lee for some time without speaking. The river was very dark. A single light burned on the steamer below them. From up in the town, very faintly, came the sound of a fiddle.

"Thomas."

"Yes."

"What about Haakon."

Thomas did not answer for a moment. He looked at his hands.

"Haakon Berg is a sailor who has decided not to leave," he said. "We are sailors who have decided to leave. We do not tell Haakon."

Olav understood, much later, that Thomas had been wrong about Haakon — that Haakon had not so much *decided* not to leave as understood, under a weight Thomas could not see, that he could not. But on Sunday in the dog-watch with the river at his back, Olav accepted Thomas's framing, because Thomas's framing was the framing under which leaving was bearable.

"He will know. After."

"He will know after. He will not know before. We do not put it on him. If we put it on him he will have to choose between telling the captain and lying to the captain, and either choice will hurt him. We are kinder if we leave him with no choice."

Olav thought about this for a long time.

The river was very dark. The fiddle from the town was still going — a slow turn, a phrase he thought he half-recognized. He thought about Haakon in the forecastle. He thought about Haakon's hammock three down from his. He thought about the small white scar at Haakon's temple, which he had seen only once, in the binnacle-light of the squall night, and which he had been carrying inside him for two months without knowing he was carrying it. He thought about a man waking at six bells of the morning watch and counting hammocks.

He has thought about all of these things, with very small variations, every week of his life since.

He thought, with no idea where the thought came from, that if he did not say goodbye to Haakon he would hear that fiddle in his head for the rest of his life. He did not, even thinking it, allow himself to know whether the thought was true.

"Yes," he said at last. "We are kinder."

He said it aloud to make himself believe it.

He was not, on Sunday in the dog-watch in the lee of the deckhouse of a brig at the lower wharf of Wilmington, sure that he believed it.

He told himself he believed it.

He put the part that did not believe it in the drawer.

The drawer, by now, was almost the size of the deckhouse.
