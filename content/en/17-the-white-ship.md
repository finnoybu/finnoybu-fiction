Captain Sars of the *Diana* was sixty-three years old and looked perhaps fifty. He had the kind of face that had been a handsome face once and had stayed handsome by the unfair simple method of not changing very much. His hair was still mostly its original blond. His beard was trimmed close to the jaw. His coat — when Olav saw him on deck for the first time, that afternoon-colored Vardøy night, three hours after the *Diana* had tied up at the next wharf — was the same clean white as the ship.

Olav had walked the hundred yards along the wharf with his sea-chest and the cough and the small wet spot on his sleeve. He had presented himself at the *Diana*'s gangway. The mate, a young man perhaps not much older than Olav, had told him to wait, and had gone aft to the captain. The mate had come back. The captain would see him in the saloon.

The saloon was on the after-deck, behind the wheelhouse, and was reached by a narrow companionway with white-painted bulkheads on either side and a brass handrail. Olav, descending it with his coat over his arm, had the brief unfair sensation that he was walking into the wrong life — into a life that had not been arranged for a man with a cough and a small wet spot on his sleeve. He came out of the companionway into a small cabin with two windows and a green cloth on the table and a Bible on a shelf above the table, and behind the table, in a chair, was Captain Sars.

"Sit down, lad."

Olav sat.

Captain Sars did not, as Captain Gjermund had done, smile at him. He did not, as Brennevin had done, look at his pocket-watch first. He looked at Olav's face for a moment, the way a man might look at a chart he had been handed by a stranger, and then he leaned forward across the table and said, in a low voice that was not unkind:

"You are not well, son."

"No, sir."

"How long."

"A year, sir. It has been bad these last weeks. The salt at Brennevin's wharf has not helped."

"Brennevin is on the *Allegro.*"

"Yes, sir."

"I know Brennevin."

The captain considered him for what Olav thought was a long time and was perhaps twenty seconds. Olav, in the considering, had the chance to look around the saloon. The walls were white. The cushions on the seat under the windows were a faded green wool. There was a small framed lithograph of a steamer over the door — not the *Diana,* a different steamer, one Olav did not know — and under the lithograph the words *Captain Halvar Sars, master and part-owner, Bergen, 1868.* The cabin smelled of soap and brass-polish and, very faintly, of the same coffee the *Sigrid*'s captain had been so particular about. Olav understood, sitting in this cabin with this man across from him, that he was for the first time in three years inside a piece of the maritime world that did not, as a matter of policy, intend him harm.

"I am bound for Bergen," Captain Sars said. "I lift in three days. I have no berth open. I have a stockfish cargo coming aboard tomorrow that I am loading short-handed. If you will help us load — your back is what it is and I will not work it harder than the cough allows — I will give you a passage to Bergen for your help and your food, and a dollar a day on top of it for your trouble. From Bergen you can find a coaster to Stavanger. Will that do."

"Yes, sir."

"You will sleep in the spare berth in the mate's cabin. It is small. It is clean. Mr. Holm" — he nodded at the mate, who had stayed in the saloon doorway — "will not mind. The food is the food we eat. It is good food. There is fresh fruit aboard from Bergen. You will eat what is put in front of you and you will not refuse the eggs. Is this understood."

"Yes, sir."

"Mr. Holm."

"Sir."

"Take the lad's chest below. Find him a shirt that is not damp. The sleeve of his shirt has — yes, I see, lad — see to it that he has a clean one."

Mr. Holm took Olav's chest. Olav, before he stood up, did the thing he had not, in any of the consulting-rooms or doctors' offices or captain's cabins of the last year, done. He let his face do what his face wanted to do. It was not weeping. It was a small loosening of the muscles around his eyes and his jaw, the kind of small loosening that happens to the face of a man who has not been spoken to kindly by a stranger in a long time.

Captain Sars, across the table, saw the loosening and pretended he had not seen it. He looked at the lithograph of the other steamer. He waited until Olav had stood up.

"Welcome aboard the *Diana,* lad."

"Thank you, sir."

Olav followed the mate out of the saloon, up the companionway, and down to the mate's cabin under the wheelhouse. The mate gave him a clean shirt. Olav put it on.

When he came back on deck the air was the same gray Vardøy air it had been an hour before, but something inside him was different, and he stood for a moment at the rail of the *Diana* and looked across the wharf at the *Allegro,* and at Brennevin standing at the *Allegro*'s rail in his watch-fob, and at the other Russian boat just tying up to take more salt.

Brennevin was looking at him.

Olav looked back.

Then he turned, and went forward to where the mate was uncoiling a line for him to stow.

---

The loading of the stockfish took three days. It was harder work than the salt-bushels had been, because stockfish is awkward — long thin boards of dried cod, hard as oak, that came down to the wharf in stacks lashed with hemp and had to be man-handled into the *Diana*'s holds — but the work had a different quality on the *Diana.* The mate worked alongside Olav. The bosun worked alongside Olav. At noon the steward — a small round man named Ekeland — brought tin mugs of cocoa and slices of bread spread thickly with butter, and at five in the afternoon there was a half-hour rest and a full mug of tea, and at seven the work stopped because Captain Sars believed that men who worked past seven did not work as well the next morning.

Olav ate everything Ekeland put in front of him. He ate eggs. He ate the white meat of fish. He ate butter on bread until he could not look at butter on bread. He coughed less. By the third day he noticed, with a small private surprise, that he could climb the companionway without stopping at the half-step.

The *Diana* lifted on the afternoon of the fourth day. They cleared the sound with a fair wind off the starboard quarter, and the engineer (a Scotsman who had no Norwegian) took the engine to half-ahead and held it there, and the steamer settled into her cruise like a woman settling into a chair she had been standing in front of.

---

The voyage south was twelve days.

It was, Olav understood without being able to say so at the time, the voyage during which his life turned. He could not, when he came to think on it later, name the day on which the cough went away. He could not name the night on which he first slept through to the morning bell without waking. He could not name the meal at which he first ate his portion and asked for more. These things happened in the order they had to happen, and he did not notice them happening, and one morning off Trondheim he was in the rigging with the bosun mending a stay and he looked down at his hand on the marline and saw that his hand was no longer the hand of the boy who had stepped onto Vardøy's wharf at three in the morning of an afternoon-colored night. The hand was thicker. The skin at the corners was no longer cracked. He stared at the hand for some time. He showed it to the bosun. The bosun, who had been a sailor for forty years and had seen this happen before, did not say anything. The bosun nodded once, the way a man might nod at a piece of weather he had been waiting for.

Captain Sars saw Olav perhaps once a day, at the morning inspection. He always asked the same question. *How are you, lad?* And Olav, after the first few days, found himself answering not the form of the question but its substance. *Better today, sir. Slept four hours in a row, sir. Ate a second portion at dinner, sir.* And the captain, who did not, in those moments, say much, would nod once and say *good* and move on.

On the seventh evening of the voyage, in the off-watch, Olav was at the rail looking at the coast as it slid past. Captain Sars came up beside him. He did not, at first, speak. He put his hands on the rail beside Olav's. They were the hands of an old man — older than the face — with the thinning at the back and the small brown spots that came with sixty Norwegian winters. The rings on the fingers were a wedding ring and a small signet on the smallest finger.

"You are mending, lad."

"Yes, sir."

"Do you know why."

"Sir?"

"I will tell you why. It is because I have stopped at fishing villages on this coast for forty years and have buried perhaps a dozen young men who had what you had. They had it because they ate badly and they slept in damp holds and they were worked, by men who did not care whether they lived, until they did not. Half of them might have lived if they had been put on a clean ship for two months at the right time and fed eggs and made to sleep eight hours. I cannot save them retrospectively. I can save the next one. You are the next one this season. There will be another. I am tired, lad, of being the only captain on this coast who keeps eggs."

Olav did not answer. He did not know what to say to a sentence like that.

The captain was looking out at the coast. He did not look at Olav.

"You will go home from Bergen. You will spend a winter at home. You will eat what your mother feeds you. In the spring you will think about going to the Stavanger navigation school. I do not, lad, know the rest of your story, and I am not asking. I am telling you the next three things to do because I have learned that young men with this cough are sometimes too tired to think the next thing for themselves. The next thing is rest. The thing after that is school. The thing after the school is a first mate's berth on a ship whose captain you choose. Do you understand."

"Yes, sir."

"Good."

He patted the rail once with his old hand and walked away.

Olav stood at the rail for some time.
