They cleared the Tagus on the next morning's tide and stood out into the Atlantic. The wind was northerly and steady. The captain had decided, evidently, that they were now to take a partial cargo across the ocean — to Wilmington, in the Carolinas — for a return load of pitch-pine and naval stores. This was a longer voyage than the one Oliver had signed for. Oliver had signed for *Lisbon and back.* The captain had not asked the crew about the change of orders. He did not, the carpenter said, do that.

Oliver did not protest. He had decided two nights before that he was not going to make this voyage — that he would, in some port, in some way, leave it — and so the captain's reroute did not alter his plans, only his timeline. Wilmington was as good a place to desert as any. Better, perhaps. He had heard there were boarding houses in Wilmington that did not ask too many questions.

He went forward and worked the brig down the Iberian coast. He did not look at Haakon. He did not look at the captain. He looked at the rope and the deck and the sail, and he kept his hands moving, and he did not let his face do anything his face did not need to do.

---

On the third day out from Lisbon they were caught by a calm.

The wind died at noon and did not come back. The brig sat on a glassy ocean under a brass-colored sky and rocked very slightly on the swell. The crew, at first, was glad of the rest. By the afternoon they were bored. By the evening they were uncomfortable; in a calm a ship's smells come up through her decks, and a brig with five days of unwashed coal-blackened crew and a half-stowed salt cargo is not a ship one wishes to be becalmed on for long.

The captain came up on deck at eight bells of the second dog-watch. His coat was new-pressed; his beard was wet from washing. The smile was on him. Oliver, who had not addressed him since the salt-hold, kept his eyes on his rope.

"Mr. Hestby."

"Sir."

"There is no wind. There will be no wind tonight. I should like you to do me a service."

"Sir."

"The Norwegian flag at the foretop has come loose at its halyard. I would like it set right before the wind picks up tomorrow. We may be sighted by a pilot before the morning, and a brig flying her flag wrong is a brig that does not get her pilot."

"Yes, sir."

"You will go aloft and re-bend the flag-halyard at the truck."

The truck. Not the foretop. The truck — the very top of the topmast, above the topgallant yard, above the royal yard, above everything that men climbed in the regular course of work. Oliver had been to the foretop. He had not been higher. The truck was perhaps ninety feet above the deck.

"Yes, sir."

"Take what you need. Be quick."

The captain smiled and turned away.

Oliver went to the rope-locker. He took a small line for the halyard and a marlinspike for the splice. He put them in the canvas pouch on his belt. He went to the foot of the foremast. The shrouds were waiting for him, dark against the brass sky.

Haakon was on the deck. He was at the windlass with the carpenter, working on something Oliver could not see. He looked up when Oliver passed him. He looked at Oliver's face. He looked at the captain's back across the deck. He looked back at Oliver.

He did not say anything.

Oliver climbed.

---

He went up the main shrouds because it was easier for the next stage, then the futtocks, then the foretop platform, then the topmast shrouds. The topmast shrouds were thinner than the lowers and they swayed in the calm with the small motion of the swell. He climbed past the topmast crosstrees and up the topgallant rigging, which was thinner still, and then up the royal rigging, which was a single line on each side and which a man climbed by hooking his arm through it and walking himself up against it like a child climbing a frayed rope in a barn.

He reached the truck.

The truck of a topmast is a wooden disk perhaps ten inches across. It has a small hole in it for the flag-halyard, and that is its purpose: to be the highest point on a ship and to fly something. It is not made to be sat on. A man at the truck wraps his legs around the topmast below him and holds on with whatever he has — knees, elbows, the inside of his arms, his teeth if he must.

Oliver wrapped his legs. He held on with his elbows. He took the marlinspike out of his pouch and reached up to the truck to take the old flag-halyard out of the small hole.

He could not get it. The wood of the truck around the hole was soft. He pushed the marlinspike against it to free the halyard. The marlinspike sank into the wood the way a spoon goes into porridge.

He pulled the marlinspike out and looked at it.

There was wet wood on the iron.

He looked at the truck. He pushed the marlinspike against the wood again. Again the iron sank in. He pushed it in further. He pushed it the full length of the spike, six inches of iron, and the iron disappeared into the head of the topmast as into mud.

He kept pushing. The marlinspike went in until his hand was against the wood.

The wood of the topmast had rotted through.

Oliver hung at the truck of the foremast of the brig in a calm in the Atlantic and understood, with a clarity that came at him from the back of his head and walked all the way down his spine, that the topmast was hollow. He understood that the topmast was held to the lower mast only by the iron caps and by the standing rigging. He understood that if he were to lean his weight against the truck — if he were to grip it and pull, as a man on a yardarm might grip and pull to brace himself — the topmast would come down. He understood that he would come down with it. He understood, finally and exactly, that he would die.

He did not move.

He stayed where he was for perhaps a full minute. He breathed slowly. He let the cold thing pass through him the way it had passed through him in the salt-hold. He let his hands listen to what they were holding. He found that the standing rigging — the wire shrouds and stays that held the topmast in place — was still tight. The topmast was rotten in its head, but it was lashed by wire to the lower mast, and the wire was sound. He could descend by the wire. He could not, must not, must under no circumstances, lean his weight against the wood at any point above the wire's hold.

He took his weight off the truck. He shifted himself down to the topgallant crosstrees. From the crosstrees he reached the topmast shrouds. From the topmast shrouds he reached the foretop. From the foretop he reached the lower shrouds. From the lower shrouds he reached the deck.

The descent took him perhaps four minutes. He could not, afterward, remember any of it.

---

He stood on the deck. His legs were not, he discovered, working. He had to lean against the foremast.

The captain was on the quarterdeck. The captain was looking at him.

Oliver crossed the deck — he did not remember crossing it — and stopped at the foot of the quarterdeck ladder.

"Sir."

"Mr. Hestby. The flag is still loose."

"Sir, the topmast is rotten."

"The topmast is what."

"The topmast is rotten through, sir. The wood at the truck has gone. I can sink a marlinspike in to the hand. The standing rigging is holding the topmast on the lower mast."

The captain looked at him for some time. The smile had not moved. It would not move; Oliver understood this now, that the smile was the captain's face and that the captain's face was the smile, and that whatever passed behind it would not show.

"Carpenter," the captain said, without turning his head.

"Cap'n."

"Look at the foretopmast. Now."

The carpenter went up the foremast. Oliver did not watch him. Oliver was watching the deck. Two minutes later the carpenter came down. He came down the same way Oliver had come down: wire by wire, very carefully, his eyes on his hands.

"It is rotten, sir," the carpenter said. "He is right."

The captain nodded once, slowly.

"Take it down," he said. "Have a new spar shaped from the spare. Put it in tomorrow."

"Aye, sir."

The captain looked at Oliver again.

"Mr. Hestby."

"Sir."

"You have done me a service. The foretopmast might have come down on the next blow and taken a man and a yard with it." The smile, briefly, did something almost like animation. "You have a good eye."

"Yes, sir."

"You may go below."

Oliver did not move. He could not, he found, decide whether to thank the captain or to spit on him. His mouth had not yet chosen. He stood at the foot of the quarterdeck ladder until he understood that standing there was conspicuous, and then he turned and walked aft along the deck and into the forecastle.

---

Haakon was at the foot of the foremast as Oliver came down the ladder. He had a tin mug in his hand. He held it out without speaking. Oliver took it. It was coffee. There was no rum in it. Oliver was, he found, glad of that; he did not want to feel anything just now that he could not feel without help.

"Drink," Haakon said.

Oliver drank. The coffee was hot. His hands shook around the mug.

Haakon watched the shake. He did not comment on it. After a moment he reached out and took the mug back, held it himself while Oliver got his hands steady, and gave it back. Oliver drank the rest.

"How high," Haakon said.

"The truck."

"The truck."

"Yes."

"How rotten."

"To the hand." Oliver held up his hand to show the depth. He was surprised that his hand could still do this; he had not been sure his hand still belonged to him. "The whole head."

Haakon let his breath out, slowly, through his nose. He looked up at the foremast. The foretopmast looked, from the deck, exactly as it had looked an hour before — a sound, slim, varnished pole standing against a brass sky.

"The standing rigging," Haakon said.

"Held."

"Then you came down."

"Then I came down."

Haakon nodded. He did not look at Oliver.

"You are alive," he said.

"Yes."

"Good."

He took the empty mug. He turned to go aft.

He stopped after one step.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"If a captain ever tells you to go to the truck on a brig that has been at sea long enough to rot a topmast — and you cannot refuse — you check the truck from the topgallant crosstrees first. With the marlinspike. Before you put your weight on it."

"Yes."

"I should have told you that before you went up."

"You did not know he would send me."

"I knew what he is. That was enough."

He looked at Oliver, finally. The pale noon-blue eyes were very still.

"I am sorry," he said.

Then he went aft.

Oliver stood at the foot of the foremast with the empty space the mug had left in his hand, and he thought:

*That is the second man on this ship who has tried to kill me. Or the first man twice. Or the first man only, and the second time he meant something else.*

He thought: *I do not care which it was. I am going to live.*

And then, because the body insists on its small honesties even when the mind has gone elsewhere:

*Haakon said* sorry *to me.*

He had not heard the word from another man's mouth, said to him, since his mother had said it once when he was nine. He thought about this for a moment. Then he put it in the drawer.

The drawer was getting full.

He went below.
