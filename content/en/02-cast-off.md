The brig lay at the quay like a promise already half broken, and Olav Hestby was eighteen years old and still young enough to believe a ship could keep a man's word for him.

He had come down before the sun, his sea-chest on his shoulder and his father walking a pace behind him like a man who had done this once already and had not been convinced then either. The Stavanger harbor was the color of iron. Above the roofs the gulls were up but not yet talking; the water made no sound except where the brig's hull shifted against the fenders, a low reluctant knocking like a question being repeated.

"Mind your mouth," his father said, not for the first time. "The captain has a softness in his face, but that is not the same as a softness in him."

"I know."

"You do not."

Olav set the sea-chest down on the granite and looked up at the masts. They were higher than he remembered. He had seen this brig a dozen times, tied here or standing off in the roads, and yet now, with his name signed to her articles, she looked taller, narrower, more a thing of her own than he had thought a ship could be. The foreyard was already crossed. The standing rigging had been newly tarred; he could smell it over the weed and the salt, a hot dark honeyed smell that would sit in his clothes for the rest of the summer.

"Olav," his father said.

"Yes."

"Do the thing you agreed to do. Do not be clever. You are not clever enough yet."

Olav nodded. His father — Jens — laid a hand on the back of his neck, briefly, the way a man might set a mark on an animal he was releasing, and then stepped back.

"Your mother sends her bread."

"Tell her thank you."

"Tell her yourself, when you come back."

Olav lifted the chest again. It was heavier than it had been at the house. He thought it might have grown heavier every step between the door and the water.

---

"Hestby! Move!"

The mate was on the deck, a short red-bearded man whose name was Solvaag and who had a habit, Olav would learn, of speaking only in imperatives, as if verbs with no subject were the only verbs a ship required. Olav hoisted the chest onto the rail and swung over it, dropped onto the deck, and straightened. The sound of his boots on the planking was the first real sound he had made that morning, and it surprised him.

The crew was aboard. He counted them with his eyes before he meant to: the mate, another man he took to be the second mate, the carpenter by his belt, a boy younger than himself at the galley door, two men he did not know coiling rope on the fo'c'sle, a man with his back turned at the windlass, and — on the quarterdeck — the captain.

Captain Gjermund had the face of a Lutheran pastor and the eyes of a man who had survived a thing he did not speak of. He was smiling. He had been smiling since Olav's father had brought Olav to his house three weeks before, when the articles were signed and the wages agreed and the captain's wife had served coffee in a cup so thin you could see the brown through it.

"Our youngest," the captain said. "Come up."

Olav came up. The captain set a hand on his shoulder and turned him so that Olav faced the water, and the mountains behind the water, and the small bright wound of the sun just cresting the eastern hills.

"That is what you leave," the captain said. "Look at it well. A man who does not look at what he leaves will not know what he is carrying."

Olav looked. He had lived within sight of those hills since the day he was born. He thought he should feel something enormous. What he felt, chiefly, was cold.

"Good lad," the captain said, and took his hand away. "Solvaag, put him at the rope."

---

They unmoored at a quarter past six. Olav was at the warping line with the man he had seen at the windlass, who turned out to be the boatswain, and who turned out to be twenty-five years old and to have come from a farm at Hogganvik and to be named Haakon Berg.

The brig was named *Sigrid*. Olav had signed her articles three weeks before in the captain's parlor, and he had been saying her name in his head, in the small careful way a man tries on a thing that does not yet belong to him, ever since. The first time he said it aloud after they cast off — *Sigrid,* under his breath at the warping line, no one to hear it — he understood that he was saying her name now the way a man says the name of a place he is going to live.

Olav did not catch all of this at once. He caught it in pieces, over the next hour, in the way a man catches the shape of a coast through fog — first a darkness, then the line of it, then at last the headlands. What he caught first, at the rope, was the set of the other man's hands.

Haakon Berg had the hands of a farmer who had become a sailor late, which is a particular kind of hand: wide across the palm, thick in the wrist, with the knuckles slightly swollen from a winter of milking or a summer of haying or both. His left thumb was missing the nail, not recently — the flesh over the bone had grown thick and smooth as if to make up for what had been lost. When he hauled, he set his weight low and took the strain into his shoulders before his arms, the way a man lifts a stone out of a field.

Olav's hands, by contrast, looked new.

"Hold the turn," Haakon said. "Don't fight it. Let me bring it."

Olav held. The rope came in. His shoulders burned, not unpleasantly. He understood, for perhaps the first time in his life, that there was a kind of labor that could be shared between two men without a word passing between them, and that the sharing was itself a language.

"You are Hestby?" Haakon said.

"Olav."

"Olav Hestby."

"Yes."

"From Finnøy."

"Yes."

Haakon glanced at him, once. His eyes were the pale blue of the sky at noon, which is not a blue one can say anything poetic about; it is only that it is a color almost too common to notice unless one notices it for a particular reason.

"I am Haakon," he said. "Hogganvik. Pleased."

Olav thought of several things to say and said none of them. He nodded. They went back to the rope.

---

The brig cast off on the slack of the tide. The mate gave the orders; the captain stood at the quarter and watched; the crew moved through the sequence of the departure with the quiet competence of men who had done it a hundred times before and would do it a hundred times more. The opsang started somewhere aft — the boy at the galley door had a voice clean as a bell — and the rest picked it up by ones and twos, a working-song in a minor key that Olav had heard his uncles sing on Finnøy when he was too small to pull the bellows at the forge. It was not a sad song, but it was not cheerful. It was a song for men who had to leave, set to a rhythm that made leaving possible.

They warped out of the inner harbor. The brig's head came round. The sails dropped — topsails first, then courses, then jibs — and took the light wind, and for a long moment the ship hung there as if weighing whether she would go. Then she went.

Olav stood at the windward rail and watched Stavanger thin out. The steeple of the domkirke cleared the warehouse roofs. The green came up behind the town. Beyond the green, very far off, the low blue shape that was Finnøy, with the hills he had known from his cradle. His father was somewhere on the quay. He had not looked back to see him there. He did not look now.

He thought: *I am going.*

He thought: *I am gone.*

The two thoughts were different, and he did not know which was the truth.

---

At eight bells they went to breakfast. The steward, a large man from Flekkefjord named Tolvsen, ladled gruel into tin bowls and passed around bread so hard Olav had to soak his piece in the gruel to get his teeth through it. Nobody complained. Olav understood, without being told, that complaints were a thing one earned the right to make, and that he had not earned anything yet.

He sat on the deck between the mainmast and the forward hatch and ate with his bowl on his knee. Haakon came and sat beside him. Not close. A hand's breadth of plank between them. He ate with the economical steadiness of a man who has eaten worse. When he was finished he ran a finger around the inside of the bowl, put the finger in his mouth, and looked out at the water.

"Ever been out on a brig?" he said.

"No."

"She'll behave different from a smack. Longer in the stride. Harder to coax off the wind in a seaway. You'll want to be awake for it."

"I will."

"Will you."

"Yes."

Haakon smiled. It was a brief smile, and it was not condescending. It was the smile of a man who remembered being told the same thing and remembered how little the telling had done.

"Stay close to the mate on watch for the first week," he said. "Not to him, I mean. Near him. Watch what he does before you do it. If he tells you to go aloft, come find me first. I will go with you the first time."

"Why?"

"So you do not die."

Olav laughed, because he thought it was a joke. Haakon did not laugh. After a moment Olav stopped laughing too.

"Is it that bad," Olav said.

"It is as bad as you make it. But the first time is the time you make it."

He stood up, collected Olav's empty bowl without asking for it, and went aft with both bowls balanced in one hand. Olav watched him go — not, he told himself, for any reason he could name, but because there was nothing else to watch just then and he had to look somewhere.

The brig rolled once, gently, like a woman turning in her sleep, and settled into the line of her course.

---

By noon the land was a blue smudge behind them. Olav went aloft for the first time that afternoon, not because he was ordered to but because Haakon, passing him at the foot of the foremast, paused and tipped his chin upward and said *come.* Olav went.

He followed Haakon into the ratlines. He had climbed ratlines before — the mate on the coast-smack had made him climb them until the grip was rote — but these were higher, and the ship was bigger, and the angle of heel was more pronounced than on a small boat. He felt his hands go cold and then hot. He kept climbing. At the foretop Haakon stopped, swung out onto the platform, and held out a hand. Olav took it. The hand closed around his forearm and pulled him up, the bare thumb pressing into the inside of his wrist where the pulse ran.

"There," Haakon said, when Olav was up. "Look."

Olav looked. Below them the deck was a narrow stripe of planking with men the size of his thumbnail moving on it. Around them the sea was enormous. The horizon was further away than Olav had understood a horizon could be. A bird — a fulmar, he thought — passed at his eye level, perfectly indifferent to him, and went on into the whiteness.

"That is what you are paid for," Haakon said.

"The view?"

"The habit of it. Most men never see this. You will see it so often you will forget to look. So look now."

Olav looked. He looked for a long time. When he remembered to breathe, the air was cold in his teeth.

Haakon was still holding his forearm. Olav realized it only when Haakon let go, and the cold rushed into the place where the hand had been, and Olav understood that he had been warm there without noticing, and that the not-noticing had been the thing.

"Down now," Haakon said. "Before the mate finds out."

They went down. Haakon first, so that if Olav fell, he would fall onto Haakon and not onto the deck. Olav did not fall. He did not say anything about the forearm, or the hand, or the cold of the air when the hand was gone.

At the deck Haakon clapped him once, lightly, on the shoulder, and went aft without looking back.

Olav stayed at the foot of the shrouds for a long minute, rubbing the inside of his wrist with his thumb, trying to think about the voyage, about the captain's eyes, about the rotten bread and the short bed he would sleep in and the two years of his life he had just, at a stroke, given to a man named Gjermund whose softness was not the thing his father had warned him about.

He thought about none of these things for long.

He thought, instead, that he had not known, until this morning, that a man's thumb could leave a shape on him.

He stopped thinking it because he did not know what to do with the thought.

The brig ran south-southwest with a steady breeze on her quarter. The coast of Norway went on being blue for another half hour and then was not there anymore.

---

That night he slept badly. The hammock was rigged too tight; every time he tried to turn, the canvas trapped him. The forecastle was full of the smell of unwashed men, and the sound of the brig at sea — creaking, shifting, a slow animal groan from the stringers — was new to him in a way the day sounds had not been new. He lay on his back with his hands folded over his stomach and listened to the watch change on the deck above him. Boots. Voices. The mate's bark. Then silence, until the next half-hour bell.

Haakon's hammock was three down from his. Olav had noted this without meaning to.

He thought about his mother. He thought about the bread she had sent that he had not yet eaten. He thought about the way his father's hand had felt on the back of his neck, and the way Haakon's had felt on his forearm, and tried to tell himself that they were the same kind of touch, and failed, and stopped trying.

The ship heeled. His hammock swung. Somewhere in the forecastle a man snored, caught himself, and was silent again.

He closed his eyes. He did not sleep. He waited.

At the next bell the watch changed; he heard Haakon get up. The weight of a man sliding out of his hammock, the soft thud of feet on the decking, the grunt of a body stretching. Olav kept his eyes closed. Haakon passed through the forecastle toward the hatch.

For a moment — Olav felt it, did not imagine it — the man paused at the foot of Olav's hammock and stood there. Then a hand reached into the dark and found Olav's wrist where it had fallen over the edge of the canvas. The hand closed around it for the count of a breath. Then it was gone.

The hatch opened. The hatch shut.

Olav opened his eyes in the dark. He could see nothing. He could hear the ship, and the sea, and his own breathing, which had quickened without his permission.

He held his wrist with his other hand. He held it for a long time.
