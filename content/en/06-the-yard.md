The new foretopmast went up the next morning. The carpenter shaped a fresh spar from the spare in the deck-house, lashed it to the lower mast with the standing rigging, and by noon the brig had her full rig back. The foretop flag, when Mathis the galley boy went up to bend it on a fresh halyard, snapped out cleanly in the southwesterly that had at last picked up. The captain came on deck and watched it snap. He smiled.

The wind came in. By the second dog-watch the brig was making nine knots and a dirty grey was building in the southwest behind them.

"We will have weather by morning," the carpenter said. He was in the deck-house with his tools, dust still on his hands from the spar.

"How dirty," Olav said.

"Not very. A summer blow, perhaps. The captain will not shorten sail."

"Why not?"

"Because he is making up a day he lost in Lisbon and because he is pleased with himself for catching the wind, and because he thinks the brig is his and not the sea's."

The carpenter bent over a chisel he was sharpening. He did not look at Olav. He had not looked at Olav since the salt-hold; nobody had. The carpenter's not-looking was a particular kind of not-looking that Olav had begun to recognize on the brig as a form of communication. It said: *I see what is happening to you. I cannot help you. I will not pretend I do not see.*

"He will send someone aloft tonight," the carpenter said, to his chisel.

"Will he."

"Yes. There will be a sail he will say wants tending. He will pick a man."

"Which man."

The carpenter ran his thumb along the chisel-edge.

"Whichever man told him in Lisbon how he should stow salt."

Olav thought about this.

"The captain has a long memory," he said.

"Captains have long memories," the carpenter said. "Especially the ones whose hands are not as good as they think."

He set the chisel down. He turned, finally, and looked at Olav. His face was not unkind; it was not a face capable of unkindness. It was old in the way faces become old when a man has spent forty years inside other men's tempers and has learned to keep his own.

"Mr. Hestby."

"Sir."

"I am sixty-one years old. I have outlasted six captains. I am telling you a thing now, and you will not say to anyone that I said it. Do you understand?"

"Yes."

"There is no captain on earth who can kill a sailor with a sail in fair weather. The sail and the wind are not weapons. The man on the yard is a weapon only against himself, and only if he panics. Do you panic, Mr. Hestby?"

"No, sir."

"Have you been tested?"

"At the truck."

The carpenter nodded once.

"Then you have been tested. Good. Tonight, when you go aloft — and you will go aloft — you will think of one thing only. You will think: *I am holding on with my arms. The wind is on my back. The yard is a yard, and it is mine to ride.* You will not think about the captain. The captain will be on the deck. The captain cannot reach you on a yard. Do you understand?"

"Yes."

"Repeat it."

"I am holding on with my arms. The wind is on my back. The yard is a yard, and it is mine to ride."

"Good."

The carpenter turned back to his chisel. He did not say anything else. After a moment Olav understood that the conversation was over, and he went out on deck.

The grey was darker in the southwest now. The brig was carrying everything she had aloft. The captain was at the quarter, smiling. The first stars were out, very small, on the eastern horizon.

---

The order came at four bells of the first watch.

"Mr. Hestby, aloft. The royal-yard footrope is twisted on the starboard side. Get up there and clear it."

"Yes, sir."

The royal yard was the highest yard on a brig — above the topgallant, just below the truck where the rotten wood had been. It was not a yard one cleared a footrope on alone in the dark in a building wind. It was a yard that wanted two men in daylight and one man only in necessity. Olav took the order as it had been intended and went forward.

Haakon was at the foot of the foremast. He had come on watch at four bells; he had heard the order. He looked at Olav, and Olav looked at him.

"I will go," Haakon said.

"He sent me."

"I will go with you."

"He sent me."

"Then I will be on deck. Below the yard. If you fall I will catch you."

Olav looked at him.

"You cannot catch a man who falls from the royal yard."

"No. But I will be there."

"That will not help."

"It is not for you," Haakon said. "It is for me."

Olav did not have an answer for this. He started up the shrouds. Haakon stayed at the foot of the foremast.

---

Going aloft in a building wind on a brig running under full canvas is a thing that men spend years learning and that no one, ever, finds easy. Olav climbed slowly. The wind was beam-on and rising. The shrouds drummed under his hands. The foretopmast — the new spar, freshly stepped — flexed a little as he climbed past the topgallant crosstrees. He thought, in passing: *the carpenter's spar is sound, but it has not been at sea before. We do not know what it will do.* He kept climbing.

He reached the royal yard.

The footrope was, as ordered, twisted. He could see it from the crosstrees: two turns of the rope had crossed back on themselves where they passed through the bracket on the yardarm. It was a five-minute job in calm weather. It was a job, in this weather, of a kind no one would ever assign to a man he wished alive.

He worked his way out onto the yardarm. He clamped his thighs to the yard and his arms over the top of it. The wind, which had been rising against his shoulders all the climb, was now full on his side; the brig beneath him was heeling perhaps fifteen degrees, which meant that the yardarm was no longer quite horizontal but was tilted out over the sea, and the sea was a hundred and ten feet below him, dark and creased.

He worked the twist out of the footrope. It took longer than five minutes. He had to use one hand at a time, with the other arm hooked through the yard, and the work needed two hands to be efficient. He did it with one. The rope was cold and slick from the spray that had been climbing the rigging since the wind picked up. His fingers, which had been Stavanger fingers two months ago, were now sea-fingers — tougher, slower, dumber, and more reliable. They worked.

The carpenter's words came back to him as he worked, not the way a prayer comes back but the way a rhythm does, in time with the rocking of the brig: *I am holding on with my arms. The wind is on my back. The yard is a yard, and it is mine to ride.*

He held on. The wind was on his back. The yard was a yard.

He cleared the twist.

He worked his way back along the yard to the topmast. He gripped the topmast with both arms. He was, briefly, very glad of the topmast — of the dumb solidity of it under his arms, of the fact that he was no longer over the sea but over the deck — and then he started down.

He came down faster than he had gone up. The wind pressed him into the rigging, which was a help. At the topmast crosstrees he paused for a moment. He looked down.

Haakon was at the foot of the foremast. He had not moved. He was looking up.

Olav came down to him.

When his feet touched the deck Haakon turned. He set his hand, very briefly, at the small of Olav's back where the wool was wet from the spray. He did not press. He did not speak. He took his hand away. He walked aft. Olav followed him with his eyes for a moment, then looked away. He went to the lee of the mast and bent his head down to his knees and stood there, just so, for the count of perhaps ten breaths.

When he straightened up the captain was at the quarter. The smile was on him. He did not, this time, say *good lad.* He did not, this time, say anything at all. He looked at Olav. He took out his pipe and tapped it against the rail. The wind took the spent ash and rolled it forward along the deck and over the side.

That was, Olav understood, the end of the conversation. The captain had asked his question — *will the boy fall* — and the boy had not fallen, and so the captain would think of something else.

Olav went forward.

---

Below, in the forecastle, he could not sleep. The wind was working in the rigging above him; the brig was making, by the feel of her, twelve knots; the bunk-canvas was shaking against the beam where his shoulder rested. His hands were burning. He had not noticed, on the yard, that he had stripped the skin off two of his fingers; he noticed it now. He had no rag for them. He put them in his mouth. The taste of his own blood was, somehow, comforting. It was the taste of *something that belongs to me.*

He listened for Haakon's hammock. He could not, at first, locate it; the forecastle was a noise of small creaks and the wind. Then he heard it. Three down, as always. Haakon was moving — a small turn, a settling.

Then Haakon stopped moving.

Then Haakon did not move for so long that Olav understood he had not gone to sleep but had only made himself still, the way a man does who is listening.

Olav, in his hammock, made himself still also. He did not know what they were listening to. He knew only that they were listening together.

He was very tired. He let his eyes close.

Just before he went under he thought: *if the captain sends me to the yard again tomorrow, I will go. If he sends me three times more, I will go. He will not break me by sending me to the yard. He will have to find another way.*

He thought: *Haakon is awake.*

He thought: *we are awake together.*

He did not, this time, put it in the drawer.

He fell asleep.
