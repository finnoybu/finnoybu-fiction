Olav made it to the next summer the way men in his country had made it to the next summer for a thousand years before him: by getting the firewood in, by mending what was broken, by eating his mother's food, and by waiting.

The autumn was an autumn. The winter was a winter. The spring was the kind of spring his father had always called *the spring that does not believe in itself,* grey until April and then sudden. He helped his father at the boats. He read the Stavanger newspaper when it came. He wrote to the Stavanger navigation school in March, enclosing the letter Captain Sars had given him at the Bergen wharf, and the school wrote back in May to say he had been accepted for the autumn course, beginning the first week of October. The letter was a piece of paper of a kind he had never received before. He showed it to his father at the kitchen table. His father read it twice and put it on the shelf above the kitchen window and did not, that night, say anything about it.

The cough did not come back.

He grew. He had not, when he had come home in August, finished growing; the cough and the year on the brig and the year of rooming-houses had stalled the body. In the winter and the spring the body resumed. He gained two inches. He gained twenty pounds, then ten more, then five. By the time the summer came he was, at last, the height his father had been at thirty, and the breadth.

He turned twenty in March.

His father, on that day, did not mark it. His mother brought a small honey-cake to the table at supper. She did not put a candle on it because they were not, his family, a family that put candles on cakes. They ate the cake. His father said *twenty,* once, the way a man says *twenty* when he is verifying that a number he has been carrying for some years is the correct number. That was the whole of the celebration.

He felt the year. He had not felt the previous year, because the previous year had been the cough and the recovering and there had been no room to feel a number alongside those. This year there was room. He felt twenty.

He felt, also, restless.

---

The Landa gathering came at midsummer, as it had every year of Olav's life, on the first Saturday after the longest day. Landa is a low broad meadow on the north side of Finnøy, sloping down to the channel, and on the meadow on the first Saturday after the longest day the families of Finnøy and Stjernerøy and Lindøy and Mosterøy and the smaller islands brought their boats around and tied them to the small jetty and unloaded their hampers and their fiddles and their children, and the meadow was, for one afternoon and one evening and one short night, the place where the islands of the channel were a single village.

Olav had not, in five years, been to Landa. He had been at sea the first summer. He had been in America the second. He had been at Vardøy the third. He had been at home the fourth, but in the cough, and his mother had not let him go.

This was the fifth summer. He went.

He went in his father's small boat, with his sister Aagot at the tiller and his mother in the bow with the hamper. The wind was light from the south-east. The crossing took an hour. He had not been on a small boat in this channel — in any boat in this channel that he was sailing rather than working on — since the year before he had signed the *Sigrid*'s articles. The smallness of the boat was a thing he had forgotten. He sat in the middle of the boat with his hands on his knees and let the boat be small around him. Aagot, at the tiller, watched him watch the channel.

"You are looking at it," she said.

"Yes."

"You did not look at it on the way in from Tau in August."

"No."

"You are different than you were in August."

"Yes."

Aagot was eight years younger than him. She was twelve. She had been four when he had sailed the first time. She was, in the small boat with her hand on the tiller, a person Olav had not, until this morning, properly met. She had his mother's eyes and his father's mouth and a freckle at the side of her nose that had not been there when he had left.

"What is the school like."

"I have not been yet."

"What will it be like."

"I think it will be the way the doctor's office in Stavanger was. Men talking about a thing they have spent their lives knowing about, to other men who do not yet know about it. I will take notes. I will take exams. In a year I will know what they know."

"Will you go to sea after?"

"Yes."

"For how long."

"I do not know. Some men go for a year. Some go for ten."

"Ten?"

"Yes."

Aagot considered this. She did not say anything for some time. The boat ran easily before the wind. The Landa shore was perhaps half a mile ahead.

"Father has counted the summers," she said. "He counts them at the kitchen window when he thinks I am not in the kitchen."

"I know."

"You know."

"He told me, once, that he had counted them."

"Then he loves you."

"Yes."

"I love you," Aagot said, in the tone of a girl who is trying out the sentence for the first time aloud, and who is not sure whether she is being too forward or too accurate, "but I am too young yet to count the summers. I will start counting when I am older."

Olav looked at her. She was looking at the tiller.

"Aagot."

"Yes."

"Thank you for telling me."

"You are welcome."

He did not know what to say after that, so he said nothing. The boat ran the last half mile to Landa in a quiet that was the kind of quiet a man and a girl share who have just agreed, without saying so, that they are going to know each other for the rest of one of their lives.

---

The meadow at Landa was as he remembered it. The hampers were spread on cloths along the seaward edge. The fiddles were tuning under the one large alder at the north end. The children were already running, most of them barefoot, in the long grass; the older boys were at the water's edge with a ball. The smell of the meadow was the smell he had carried in a drawer in his head across an ocean — wet grass, old smoke from a fish-fire down the shore, salt off the channel, the particular green of Finnøy summer that did not exist in any other green of his life.

He helped his mother with the hamper. He carried it to the cloth she had picked out near the alder. He stood, for a moment, with his hands in his pockets, looking at the meadow, registering that he was at Landa for the first time in five years and that he was twenty years old and that the channel was full of small boats and that the fiddles were tuning and that the body that was standing on the meadow was the body of a man Captain Sars had given back to him in exchange for a stockfish loading and a dollar a day.

He did not, looking at the meadow, think of Haakon. He had been training himself, all spring, not to think of Haakon at gatherings; the not-thinking was a thing the body had begun to do without his having to ask. The drawer was where it had been. The drawer was, at midsummer at Landa, closed.

He thought, instead, that he was, perhaps, going to enjoy the afternoon. The thought was not a large thought. It was only that he had not, in some years, allowed himself the thought.

He said it to himself once. *I am, perhaps, going to enjoy the afternoon.*

Then, because saying it had been more than he had meant to say, he set off across the meadow to find his sister, who had run ahead and was already somewhere among the older girls at the alder, learning whatever the older girls were teaching her about the boys at the water's edge with the ball.
