---
title: "Haakon's Watch"

---

Haakon turned out for the midwatch the way a man turns out for the work he has been doing since before he had a beard: without thinking about it, without complaint, with one hand on the beam above his hammock and the other already finding his boots in the dark.

The forecastle was the warm dark it always was at four bells of the first watch, all sleep-breath and wool. He swung down without a sound. He had learned, on his second voyage, that a boatswain who woke a forecastle made a forecastle that did not love him, and a forecastle that did not love him made a long voyage. His feet found the planking. He stood for a moment with his weight on the balls of them and listened. The brig was running on a freshening wind; he could feel it in the standing rigging through the soles. Three days out of Stavanger and she was already in her stride.

Three down from him the boy from Finnøy was breathing the slow breath of a hand who had not yet learned what a brig sounded like at night, which is to say he was breathing it shallow and fast. Haakon did not look at the hammock. He pulled his coat off the peg, took his cap, and went up the forward hatch.

---

On deck the wind hit him in the chest. Northwesterly, freshening, the smell of the Dutch coast on it — pinewood and a thinness of fog from the polders. He took the binnacle for a moment to check the course before relieving the mate of the deck. South-southwest. The brig was making seven, perhaps a touch over. He looked aft along the deck. The mate of the watch — Solvaag — was at the weather rail with his back to him, doing the thing Solvaag did at the changing of the watch, which was pretending he had not heard the boatswain come up.

"Mr. Solvaag."

"Berg."

"I have her."

Solvaag grunted and went forward without looking at him. Haakon was used to the not-looking. Solvaag was a man who, in twenty years, would either be a captain or a dead man, and he had chosen the path that made the second more likely than the first. Haakon had served under three of him already. There was nothing to be done with a Solvaag except to stand the watch and let him go.

He took the wheel from the man who had been holding it and sent that man forward to coil. Then he stood at the wheel with the brig under his hands and the night ahead of him and did the thing he did, every night he had the wheel, which was to take stock. The wind. The sail. The set of the swell against the bow. The way the brig answered when he eased her up a half-point. The way she came back. The way she rode.

She rode well tonight.

A quarter hour passed. The man of the watch he had sent forward returned with a tin mug of coffee — Tolvsen the steward had it ready at the change, as Tolvsen always did — and Haakon took the mug with his left hand and kept his right on the spokes and drank. The coffee was hot enough to be a comfort and weak enough to be honest, which was, on a brig three days out, the best a man could hope for.

He thought, while he drank, of the boy.

He thought of him without permitting the thought to turn into anything. Eighteen years old. Finnøy. The hands at the warping line on the morning of the cast-off — wide hands, but new ones; you could see the smallness of the calluses. The face on the foretop when he had been pulled up — a face that had not yet learned how to keep itself still under what it was seeing. The thumb on the inside of the wrist when Haakon had taken his arm. He had not, for some months, taken a man's arm and felt the pulse jump under his thumb. He had felt it tonight. He had taken his hand away and not made anything of the taking-away. The boy had not known what to do with the cold where the hand had been. Haakon had known. Haakon had known for ten years.

He let the thought finish. He drank his coffee. He set the mug on the binnacle ledge.

He would put the thought, when he was off watch, in the place he put such thoughts. He had a place. The place was older than the boy was.

---

At three bells the boy came up.

He came up by the forescuttle, in the wool coat his mother had buttoned for him, with his cap pulled to his eyebrows and his hands already cold. Haakon watched him cross the deck. The boy did not yet know how to walk on a heeling deck without leaning into the heel; he was correcting late. He would learn it in a week. They all did.

"Mr. Hestby."

"Haakon."

"Boatswain, to you. Until four bells."

The boy nodded the nod of a young man who has been corrected and means to be corrected only once. Haakon noted the nod. It was a good nod. Some boys took the correction badly; this one took it as instruction. The difference between those two kinds of boys was the difference between a man who could be trained and a man who could not.

He sent him forward to the lashings on the boat, which were fine and did not need tightening but which gave the boy a small task he could complete under his own eye. The boy went. Haakon watched him go. The boy did not look back. Good. A boy who looked back when sent on a small errand was a boy who wanted to be watched. This boy did not yet want to be watched. He did not yet, Haakon thought, know what it meant to want to be watched.

When the boy came back Haakon put him on the wheel.

Haakon set the boy's hands on the spokes beside his own. There was no proper way to teach a man to steer except to put his hands where yours had been and let him feel the brig through them. Haakon's right hand was an inch from the boy's left; he could have moved his own hand half an inch and the backs of their fingers would have been touching. He did not move his hand. The brig rode. The boy held her on her course, slow at first and then with the small certain knowing of a hand that had had a tiller before.

"Good," Haakon said, after a while. "You have the feel."

"I had it from a boat at home."

"That is where most men get it."

He stepped back. He let the boy steer alone. He watched. The boy was good. He bore off when the brig wanted to come up; he brought her back without overcorrecting; he let her find her own line and only nudged her when she strayed. After twenty minutes Haakon understood, with the small flat clarity in which he understood most things, that the boy was going to be a sailor.

He understood, also, that he was going to spend the next three months not letting himself look at the boy's hands on the spokes.

He looked at the sea instead. The squall-line was building in the southwest. He had felt it an hour ago in the falling of the temperature; he could see it now as a darker line low against the cloud. It would be on them in thirty minutes. He let the boy steer.

---

The squall came at three bells. He called the watch up. They took in the royals, two men to the yard, one on the bunt — clean work, done in three minutes flat. The boy was on the starboard yardarm. He moved well. He did not, at the height, hesitate; he did not, at the bunt, fumble. Haakon watched from the deck. He noted that he was watching for the boy specifically, and he noted that he had been watching for him since the boy came up the ratlines.

The squall hit. It hit hard for ten minutes — wind that staggered her, rain that came at the angle a man holding a hose at his hip would aim it — and then it was past, the way summer squalls in the North Sea always are, not a weather but a sentence.

Halfway through the ten minutes a lee-sheet at the foretopsail had let go, and the iron-shod block at its end had come through the air at the pinrail in the pendulum a half-ton of iron and wet manila made of itself at the end of a blown gust. Mathis, at the pin, had caught the fall on a turn of line and dead-bonged it against the iron before the second pass. It did not, in the event, find anyone. But Haakon, from the deck, had watched the boy's body at the pin. The boy had taken a step back — out, which was outward — and he had been two feet clear when Mathis caught the block. Two feet was not much. Two feet on another deck at another pin, at a rail the boy had not marked, would have been nothing.

Haakon noted it.

He would, in the lee of the galley when the blanket was out, tell him.

The boy was on the deck when Haakon came down off the foremast. Soaked through. His teeth were doing what teeth do when a man has been working hard in the cold and the working has stopped. Haakon looked at him for the count of a breath. He thought, as he would have thought of any of the watch: *that boy is going to be sick by morning if he stands in this rain through the rest of the watch.*

He thought, without permitting himself to think it any more than the first thought: *I am going to do for him what I would do for any of them.*

"Forward," he said. "Lee of the galley. I'll come."

The boy went.

Haakon went to the steward's locker. He took a wool blanket — one of the brown crew blankets, dry from where Tolvsen kept them folded above the stove. He took a tin mug. He measured a thumb of rum into it from the cask the captain did not know about, and he took the coffee pot from the deck-house stove and filled the mug to the top. He carried the mug and the blanket forward.

The lee of the galley was the warmest place on the deck at that hour. It was not warm. It was less cold than the rest. The boy was there, against the bulkhead, with his teeth still doing what teeth do.

Haakon handed him the mug. "Drink."

The boy drank. Haakon watched him drink. The rum hit first; he saw it in the boy's shoulders, the small loosening of them. Then the coffee. The teeth stopped.

He sat down on the deck with his back against the galley wall, beside the boy. He laid the blanket across both their knees.

"Shoulder to mine," he said, as if he were explaining a piece of rigging. "Your wool is wet. Mine is not. We will share the dry of me until the watch is out."

The boy put his shoulder to Haakon's.

It was not, Haakon told himself, anything. He had done it before. He had done it for Mathis the galley boy on the second night out of Stavanger when Mathis had been sent up in a fog and come down shaking. He had done it once for Solvaag, of all people, on a North Atlantic crossing in '74 when Solvaag had taken a green sea over the bow and was too proud to go below. It was what a boatswain did. It was a small piece of seamanship.

He told himself this and then, because he had been honest with himself for a long time about the small lies he told himself, he amended it: *it is a small piece of seamanship. It is not* only *a small piece of seamanship.*

He held the amendment in his head for the count of three breaths. Then he put the amendment in the place where he put such things.

He turned his face slightly away from the boy and looked at the dark water past the lee bulwark.

---

They talked, after a while. Haakon began it with a piece of seamanship, because seamanship was the way he knew how to begin.

"Boy."

"Boatswain."

"The block on the foretopsail sheet. Halfway through."

"Yes, boatswain."

"Your step was back."

The boy did not, for a moment, answer. He had not, until Haakon had named the block, been thinking about the block. He was thinking about it now.

"It was two feet."

"It was two feet because Mathis caught the fall. It would have been not-two-feet at another pin on another deck."

"Yes, boatswain."

"A thing swung from a pivot — a block, a bucket, a marlinspike, a line's end — has its force at the end of the arc. The inside of the arc is empty of force. A man's step against a swinging thing is the step in, not the step out. You step toward the pivot. You pass under the end of the arc before the end of the arc passes through you. You understand me."

"I think so."

"You will understand when you need it. The ears do not keep it. The body does. Hear it once. The body will keep it."

"Yes, boatswain."

"Good. Remember the pin tonight. Next squall you are inside."

"Yes, boatswain."

Haakon let the lesson sit. He let the boy turn it over in the dark under the blanket, which was the way a piece of seamanship settled into a man young enough to keep it. He looked at the dark water. He did not, while the lesson was settling, say anything further.

Then the boy asked about Hogganvik. Haakon answered. The farm. The cliff. The fifty head of sheep. The mother who had painted the house red twenty years ago and not since. The two younger brothers whose existence had made him a sailor. The boy asked whether he had wanted to be a sailor; he answered as he always answered, which was with the truth as he had come to understand it at twenty-five. *I wanted to be out of Hogganvik.* The boy had not pressed.

The boy said *I am glad you are.*

Haakon noted, in the place inside him that did the noting, that this was the first thing the boy had said that was not a thing the watch required him to say.

"Why," he asked, because it was the asking a man did when he wished, and did not wish, to know.

The boy did not have an answer ready. Haakon could feel the boy's mouth working through the not-having-an-answer. He let the not-having stand. He had himself, at eighteen, said things he had not had answers ready for, said to older men whose answers would have been the wrong answers anyway.

"Because you told me when to come down," the boy said at last.

"Good reason," Haakon said.

"Yes."

"That is seamanship."

"Yes."

"That is all it is."

He said it because it was the sentence he had been building toward for the whole watch — the sentence that would put what was happening on the deck back into the drawer he kept it in, the drawer that had a label on it and the label said *seamanship* and the label was a true label as far as the work went and a false label as far as what he was, but he had decided, ten years ago, that the false labels were the labels he would live by, because the world had not yet given him true ones.

"That is all it is," he said again.

The boy said *yes.*

He did not look at Haakon. Haakon did not look at the boy. They looked at the dark water.

---

At the watch-change bell — four bells, by which time Haakon was due to relieve the wheel and the boy was due to coil — the bell rang and Haakon, because he was a man who had decided ten years ago that he would live by false labels and because he had been honest with himself for a long time about the cost of that decision, did the thing he had not, at twenty-five, done in five years.

Under the blanket, where the dark and the wool together hid the doing of it, his hand found the boy's hand.

He did not take it. He did not press it. He laid the side of his palm against the back of the boy's, the way a man lays his hand against a piece of warm wood to know it is warm. The boy's hand did not move. The boy's hand did not pull away. After perhaps three breaths — Haakon counted them; they were the slow breaths of a man who has decided not to do the thing he is most afraid of — Haakon withdrew his hand and stood up and said, quietly, *the wheel,* and walked aft to take the wheel from the man who had been holding it.

He did not look back at the boy.

He held the wheel for the rest of the watch with both hands on the spokes and his eyes on the binnacle and his attention on the brig, and he did not let himself think about what he had done, because to think about it would have been to hold it, and to hold it would have been to know that he could not put it down.

At eight bells the mate of the next watch relieved him. He went forward. The boy was no longer at the lee of the galley. He had gone below.

Haakon went down the forward hatch.

---

In the forecastle, in the dark, he stood for a moment by his hammock. He could hear the boy three down from him. The boy was awake. Haakon could hear it in the shallowness of the breath. He stood in the dark with his hand on the lashing of his own hammock, listening to a boy three hammocks down who was awake, who was not pretending to be asleep, who was waiting for some sound that Haakon could choose to make or not to make.

He did not make the sound.

He climbed into his hammock. He folded his arms across his chest. He looked up into the dark above the beams.

He thought, in the order in which he thought the things:

That he had laid his hand on the back of the boy's hand under the blanket at watch-change, in the dark, where no one could see, and had counted three breaths, and had withdrawn the hand, and had walked away.

That the boy had not pulled his hand away.

That this was the first time in five years he had done such a thing, and the first time in his life he had done it and not been certain afterward that the not-doing-more had been the right not-doing.

That he was not going to do anything else this voyage.

That he had told himself this same sentence at twenty, at twenty-one, at twenty-three, on three different ships about three different boys, and that on two of the three he had kept the sentence and on one of the three he had not, and that the one of the three on whom he had not was a man named Erik whom he had, six months later in a port in Lübeck, left at the end of the voyage by walking off the ship without a word — closing the door, he had told himself even then, from his own side — and the not-knowing of what had happened to Erik after was a thing Haakon had carried for four years and would, he now understood, carry for the rest of his life.

That this boy from Finnøy was not Erik. That this boy did not yet know what he was, and that Haakon, who did know, owed him the not-doing.

That seamanship was the label he had chosen. That seamanship was a true label as far as the work. That the work was not the whole of what was happening. That the false part of the label was what allowed him to live.

He thought all of this and then he stopped thinking, because the thinking was an indulgence he did not, at twenty-five, with three months still to run on this voyage and a boy three hammocks down who was awake, allow himself.

The drawer was where it had always been.

He put what had happened tonight in it.

The drawer was old. The drawer had room. The drawer was, by now, almost full — he had been adding to it since he was fifteen, since the boy at the parish school whose name he could no longer say without the small unraveling — but it was not yet full. It would, perhaps, never be full. The drawer was the size of a man's life, and a man's life was a thing that went on holding what it held.

He closed his eyes.

He did not sleep at first. He listened to the boy three down breathing and did not let himself match the breath. After some time the boy's breath slowed and deepened and went into the long quiet of a young man finally sleeping, and Haakon, in his own hammock, allowed himself the small bitter mercy of breathing along with the sleep he had not been able to make happen for himself.

He slept, eventually.

When the next bell rang he was up before it had finished, and he was on deck before any of the other men of his watch, and he had his coat buttoned and his cap on and the day's first orders on his tongue, and no one — not Solvaag, not Tolvsen, not the mate of the morning watch, not the boy from Finnøy who came up at six bells with his hair flat from the hammock and his eyes carefully not finding Haakon's — would have known, looking at him, that a thing had passed in the night that was not, for him, going to pass.

That was, he thought, the work.

That was what he had become good at.

That was the part of seamanship that had no name in any of the books.
