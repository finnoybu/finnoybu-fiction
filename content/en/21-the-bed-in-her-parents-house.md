He went to Lindøy for the Christmas, on a fair winter wind, in the third week of December.

The cold of the channel that morning was the cold of a thing that had been getting colder for two months and was now, at the solstice, deciding what it was. The boat his father had lent him — the same boat, smaller than he remembered each time he stepped into it — held the cold the way a stone holds it, not as a sensation but as a fact. He sat at the tiller with his coat buttoned to the chin and his hat down to his eyebrows, and he watched the Lindøy shore come up over the bow, and he understood, watching it come, that this was the visit at which the thing they had been arranging by letter on Wednesdays and Sundays was going to be arranged in some other way.

He had been at the school eleven weeks. The autumn term ended on the eighteenth and the spring term began on the eighth of January, which gave him three weeks at home — the first week at Finnøy, the second at Lindøy, the third at Finnøy again. He had written to Olava in November to propose the arrangement. She had answered the same week to accept it. Her father, in a small note enclosed with her answer, had written *the back room above the kitchen is at your disposal.* That was the captain's permission rendered into the smallest sentence the captain had been able to render it into.

Olav landed at Lindøy on the morning of the twenty-third. The wind was northerly and cold. The Lindøy jetty had a thin skin of ice on the boards. The captain met him at the jetty as before, in a heavier coat, with the same wooden hand. They walked up to the house in silence; the cold did not encourage talk.

The house was the house, with a fire going in the parlor stove and the smell of the bread Olava's mother had baked that morning. The mother greeted him from the kitchen without coming to the door, the way a woman greets a young man whose visits have begun to be the kind of visits a household assimilates rather than an event a household marks. Marit was at the schoolmaster's again. Olava came in from the back garden where she had been getting the last of the late kale, with her cheeks red from the cold and her hair coming out of its braid, and she said *you came on a fair wind* in the same tone she had said it in August. He said *yes* in the same tone he had said it then. They did not, in the kitchen with her mother three feet away at the stove, do anything else.

---

The Christmas Eve service was at the Lindøy church at five o'clock. The whole household went — the captain in his good coat, the mother in her dark wool dress, Marit in a new dress her mother had made her, Olava in the dress she had worn at supper the night Olav had come the first time. The dog stayed at the house. The road to the church was ice-packed in the ruts; Olava put her hand on Olav's arm at the worst patch and did not, after the patch was passed, take it off until they reached the churchyard wall. Olav understood, walking past the wall toward the door, that the hand on his arm was the first time her hand had been on him in any extended way and that the captain, walking ahead with the mother, knew it was happening and had decided that the road's icy ruts were enough cover for it and that he would not, today, intervene.

At the door of the church Olava took her hand from his arm. She did not look at him as she did. They went in.

The church was the same it had always been. The minister gave the Christmas sermon. The hymns were the ones the hymns always were. Olav stood and sat with the family. He looked, briefly, at the row of headstones visible through the south window; he could not, from the pew, see the second-row stone Olava sat at for an hour each month, but he knew it was there. He thought, very briefly, of his own brother's stone behind the Hestby church, which he had not, in twenty-one years, sat at. He understood that he had a year, perhaps, before he was going to have to.

After the service they walked home the way they had walked to the church. Olava did not take his arm at the icy patch this time. She did not need to.

---

Christmas morning Olav woke at his usual time. The room was cold. He could see his breath above the bed. He lay in the bed for a quarter hour, listening to the house begin to move around him — the captain in the kitchen, the mother at the stove, Marit on the stair. He thought he might get up and go down. He did not, in the cold of the room, find the resolve.

There was a knock at the door.

"Yes?"

The door opened. Olava was at the door with a tray. On the tray were a bowl of porridge with cream, a thick slice of brown bread with butter and brown cheese, a cup of hot coffee with milk, and a small dish of preserved cloudberries her mother had jarred in August.

"Father said. Mother said. I bring it."

She came into the room. She crossed to the bed. She set the tray on the small table at the bedside. She did not, in the placing, look at his face.

He sat up. He pulled the coverlet up. He took the tray onto his knees.

She did not leave.

She stood at the bedside for a moment, with her hands clasped at her waist. Then she sat down, very deliberately, on the edge of the bed at the foot, the way a person sits on the edge of a bed she intends to make a small statement at and then leave. She was perhaps three feet from him.

"Eat."

He ate. The porridge was hot. The cream was thick. The bread was thick. The cheese was the brown cheese he had been eating in this house since August; it was the best brown cheese he had eaten in any house. He ate and he did not speak. She sat and she did not speak. After perhaps two minutes he had eaten half the porridge, and then he set down the spoon and looked at her.

"Olava."

"Yes."

"You have a thing to say."

"Yes."

"Say it."

She looked at her hands in her lap. She looked at the cloudberries. She looked at him.

"I have decided, Olav Hestby, that I will marry you, when you ask. I am telling you now because I would rather not have you wonder, in the time between now and your asking, what the answer is going to be. The answer is yes. I am telling you the answer before the question because my mother has told me, all my life, that a girl who waits for a man to ask her a question may wait longer than she means to wait, and I do not want to spend the spring waiting. I am twenty next spring. You are twenty-one. You will be at school for another six months and at sea after that. The years are the years. We will not get more of them by waiting on a question that we both already know the answer to."

She did not, in saying any of this, look away from his face.

"I am not, by saying this, asking you to ask me today. You are at school and you have no money and you have a year still, perhaps two, before the asking is the responsible thing to do. I am only telling you that the answer, when the asking comes, is the answer. I am also telling you because I think you have been carrying the not-asking in your head as a thing that might cost you my answer, and I would like you to put down the carrying and use the head you were carrying it in for your studies."

Olav looked at her.

"Olava."

"Yes."

"Your father knows you are here."

"Yes."

"Your father knows what you are saying."

"He does not know the words. He knows the substance. He approved the breakfast. He understood what was going to happen at the breakfast. He understood it because I told him last night."

"You told him last night."

"I told him last night because I do not, at twenty next spring, intend to live in a house where the saying of a thing must wait on the men in the house for permission to be said. I am not against the men in the house. I love my father. I love my brother who is dead. I will love you when I marry you. But I have decided that the saying is mine. I told my father so. He listened. He said *I will not be in the kitchen at the time you take up the tray.* That was the agreement we came to."

She lifted her chin. The chin-lift was, Olav understood, the first piece of physical defiance she had performed in his sight. She had performed it to a man who had agreed to it in advance, and the agreement did not, in the doing, reduce the force of the lift.

"So," she said.

"So."

"I have said it."

"Yes."

"You may eat the rest of the porridge."

He ate the rest of the porridge.

She watched him eat it. She did not, while he ate it, take her eyes from his face. When he had finished the porridge and started on the bread she said:

"You are going to ask me a thing."

"Yes."

"Ask it."

"How long have you decided."

"Since the first time I gave you the disk at Landa."

"Six months."

"Six months."

"You decided in six minutes."

"I decided in two. The other six months were so that I could be sure I had been right at the two minutes."

He set down the bread.

"Olava."

"Yes."

"I have not been honest with you about every part of myself."

She did not, hearing this, react in the way he had thought she might react. She did not look surprised. She did not look hurt. She did not, particularly, look. Her face stayed where it had been.

"I know," she said.

"You know."

"Olav. You wrote me twenty letters between July and December. There is a part of you that none of the twenty letters touched. I have been writing to a man who is not telling me about a piece of his life that is, I think, large. I do not know what the piece is. I know there is a piece. I knew this in October when the seventh letter came and I read it and I understood, putting it down, that I could not have told my mother what was at the bottom of the letter even though I had read every word. I am not asking you, today, what the piece is. I am telling you that I know there is a piece, and that I am marrying you in the knowledge that there is a piece, and that I do not require the piece to be told to me before the asking. I require, only, that the piece not become a piece you carry alone forever. If, at some point in the next forty years, you find that the piece has become too heavy to carry alone any more, I will be in the room with you. Until then I will not ask. Is that an arrangement we can make."

Olav did not, for some time, speak.

"Yes," he said.

"Then we have made it."

"Yes."

She stood up from the edge of the bed. She did not, in standing, lose any of the formality with which she had sat. She straightened the coverlet at the foot of the bed where her sitting had wrinkled it. Her hand brushed the line of his foot through the wool. Olav held very still. She did not, in the straightening, comment on the touching, and she did not take her hand away faster than the work required. Then she looked at the tray.

"You are not eating the cloudberries."

"I am full."

"Eat them when you are not full. Do not, at the end of the day, give them back to my mother. She made them in August because the August was a good cloudberry August and she believes that cloudberries should be eaten by the people they were jarred for. Promise me you will eat them."

"I promise."

"Good."

She went to the door. She stopped at the door. She turned.

"Olav."

"Yes."

"I do not know what the piece is. I have made my peace with not knowing. But I will say this once, today, only because I think you need to hear it from me and not from your own head. Whatever it is, I have already, in deciding to marry you, decided to live with it. Do not, in the years you carry it, make the carrying harder than it has to be by also carrying the question of whether the carrying is forgivable. It is. It is forgiven. The forgiving is the small first thing I am bringing to the marriage. There will be other things later. Today, for now, that one."

She went out. She closed the door behind her.

Olav sat in the bed with the tray on his knees and the cloudberries beside the bread and the cup of coffee gone cold in the cup. He did not, for some time, move. He looked at the small dish of cloudberries — orange-gold, with the small black seeds visible in the preserve — and he understood, holding the spoon over them, that he had just been married, in the back room of the Lindøy house at Christmas in the year of his twenty-first year, by a girl of nineteen who had decided to marry him and had communicated the decision and had received his consent and had, before leaving the room, given him absolution for a thing he had not yet confessed and had then asked him to eat his mother's cloudberries.

He ate the cloudberries.

They were the best cloudberries he had eaten in any year.

When he had finished he set the empty dish on the tray, set the tray on the small table at the bedside, lay back down on the bed, and looked at the ceiling. He listened to the house. The captain was in the parlor. The mother was at the stove. Marit was on the stair, going somewhere. Olava was in the kitchen with her mother, doing whatever it is daughters do in kitchens at half past nine on Christmas morning.

After a time he got up and dressed and went down to the kitchen.
