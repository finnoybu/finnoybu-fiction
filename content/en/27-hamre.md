The exam was scheduled for the first week of June. The school had been preparing for it since March. Prof. Nilson had told the class, in March, that the exam was the only thing that mattered — that everything they had done since October was preparation for the three days of June, and that the three days would be administered by a committee of three captains from the Navy and the merchant marine, and that the captains would be looking not at what the students had memorized but at what the students could do.

The exam was three days long.

Day one was theory: arithmetic, plane trigonometry, the basic tables. A written paper of four hours in the morning and an oral interview of half an hour in the afternoon. Olav, who had been doing the arithmetic by hand for three years on the brig and the *Diana* and the *Allegro,* was through the morning paper in two and a half hours and used the remaining hour and a half to check his answers. The afternoon oral was given by a small gray-bearded captain named Storm who looked like he had been disappointed by every student he had ever interviewed and who, when Olav answered the third question correctly, looked at him for a long moment and said *Good* in a tone that suggested he had not, that day, expected to use the word.

Day two was charts: dead-reckoning, current adjustment, the use of the lead and the log. A practical exercise in the morning at the chart table and an oral interview of half an hour in the afternoon. Olav did the practical exercise on a chart of the approaches to Hamburg through the Elbe — the chart was, by some accident of selection, the chart at the mouth of which the *Sigrid* had been driven onto the sandbar in '64, and Olav, who had stood at the chart room shelf one afternoon in November and put his thumbnail to a different part of the same waters, did not let his face register that he knew the chart from another life. He plotted the course the captain had told him to plot. The course was correct.

Day three was navigation under simulated weather: a problem set the candidate's ability to apply theory to a hypothetical voyage, and an oral defense of the candidate's solution. This was the day the school called *the chart of bad weather,* and the students of the previous year had told the students of Olav's year that this was the day on which the half of them who would not pass would not pass.

---

Olav was at the third desk in the second row. Hamre was three desks behind him and one to the left. They had not, on the previous two days, spoken; the exam discipline did not permit it. Olav had not, since the start of the exam week, looked at Hamre's face. He had not needed to. Hamre's silence behind him, across two days, had been audible in the way the silences of men managing things were audible, and Olav had registered the silence without turning around.

The day-three problem was to plot the course of a fictional bark from Bordeaux to Cardiff, with three days of southwesterly weather off the Bay of Biscay, a forced reduction of canvas at midnight of the second day, a chronometer error of fifteen seconds discovered on the morning of the third day, and a landfall at Lundy in light fog. The students had four hours.

Olav started at the chart. He worked the dead-reckoning. He computed the chronometer correction. He plotted the landfall. He was through the problem in three hours and used the fourth to write up the defense paragraphs in his small careful hand. At the end of the four hours the captains called *Pens down,* and the students walked from the chart room to the corridor outside for the half-hour break before the oral defense.

In the corridor Hamre was at the wall, with his hand on the wall, and his face was the white of a man who had just spent four hours at a problem he had not solved.

Olav walked over to him.

"Hamre."

"Hestby."

"How far."

"I have the chronometer correction wrong. I have known it for an hour. I have not been able to find where I put the wrong sign. I have been going through the work for an hour. I will not, in the half-hour, find it."

Olav looked at him.

"Show me."

"Hestby — they are about to —"

"Show me, Hamre. The defense will be in twenty minutes. Show me the page now."

Hamre led him into the empty chart room. He spread his work on the table. Olav looked at it for perhaps thirty seconds.

The error was at the chronometer correction. Hamre had reversed the sign on the longitude adjustment — east instead of west, which had carried through every subsequent calculation and had put his landfall thirty miles further into the Channel than the problem required. The error was a single minus sign at line eight of his chronometer page. The error was two pencil-strokes to fix. The error meant that Hamre's entire course was wrong, that his entire defense paragraphs were wrong, and that the captains in the next room would, in twenty minutes, fail him on the basis of a single minus sign.

"Here," Olav said.

He pointed at line eight. He did not, in pointing, take the pencil from Hamre. He let Hamre see the line. Hamre looked at the line. Hamre did not, for a moment, see what Olav was pointing at.

"There," Olav said. "Sign."

Hamre looked at the sign. His face did, for the first time in eight months, register something. The registering was not relief. The registering was the small particular horror of a man who has spent four hours not seeing a thing that is now obvious to him.

"It is a minus."

"Yes."

"It should be —"

"Plus."

"Plus."

"Yes."

"Hestby —"

"You have eighteen minutes. Take the page with you. Do the rest of the work in your head. Do not redo the paper. The captains will look at the chart and the calculation; they will not look at the paragraphs. Argue from the corrected sign in your defense. Tell them you know your written calculation is wrong because you reversed the sign and you can show them where, and tell them what the corrected work would show. The captains will accept this. They have, between them, marked perhaps four thousand of these papers in their lives. They have seen a sign reversal before. They will not fail you for it if you can talk about it."

"You are sure of this."

"I am not sure of this, Hamre. I am telling you what I would do if I were you."

Hamre did not, for a long moment, move.

"All right," he said.

"Go."

Hamre went.

Olav, in the empty chart room with Hamre's pages spread on the table, stood for a moment with his hand on the table edge. He had not thought about whether helping Hamre was permitted by the exam rules. He had not, in the moment of looking at the page, thought about anything except the line with the minus sign. He thought about it now, in the empty chart room, briefly. He decided he did not care whether it was permitted. He went back to the corridor and waited his turn for the defense.

---

The results were posted at four o'clock the same afternoon, on a slip of paper pinned to the corridor wall outside the chart room. Olav had passed. So had Hamre. Two of the thirty had not.

Hamre found Olav outside the school at the bottom of the steps.

"Hestby."

"Hamre."

"I owe you."

"You do not."

"I do. I will not — I do not know how to — Hestby. I owe you a thing I cannot, in the form of money or in the form of any other return I can think of, repay. I will not, in our lifetimes, find a way to repay it. I am telling you, also, because I am leaving for Bergen tomorrow morning to apprentice at a shipping firm and I do not, after tomorrow morning, expect to see you again. I wanted you to know that I knew."

Olav looked at him.

"Hamre."

"Yes."

"You have not been at ease in the school all year."

"No."

"What has it been."

Hamre did not, for a moment, answer.

"It has been a thing that is not yours to know," he said. "Do not ask me at the bottom of the school steps. I am asking you to extend the courtesy one more day."

"All right."

"Thank you, Hestby."

"Hamre."

"Yes."

"Do me one favor."

"Yes."

"When you reach Bergen, find a small gray-bearded man named Sars who is the master of a steamer called the *Diana.* Tell him Hestby of Finnøy sends his regards. Tell him I sat my certificate this June."

"I will."

"Goodbye, Hamre."

"Goodbye, Hestby."

Hamre turned. He walked up Bredalmendingen. Olav watched him for some time. Hamre did not look back.
