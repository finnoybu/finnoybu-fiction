The first letter came in late January.

It came from a Bergen shipping firm called Houlder & Sønn. Houlder had been one of the first firms Olav had written to in September; they had answered then with the polite denial all the firms had answered with. They had, evidently, kept his letter. The letter that arrived on the fourth Tuesday of January said that Houlder & Sønn had a berth open on a coaster of theirs, the *Aase Marie,* at second mate, for the spring run between Bergen and Hamburg. The voyage was six months. The wage was not generous, but the wage was a wage. The captain was a man named Brønlund who had been one of Houlder's masters for fifteen years and who had been informed of Olav's qualifications and was willing to take a young first-timer in the second mate's berth.

The letter ended: *We will require your decision by the second Monday of February. If you accept, you will report to Bergen on the first Monday of March.*

Olav read the letter twice. He took it to the kitchen and put it on the table.

His father read it. His father set it down.

"You will accept."

"I have not yet decided."

"What is to decide. Houlder has put his hand out. You are a navigator without a berth. You take the hand."

"Yes."

"You write Monday next. You go to Bergen first of March. You are at sea by the spring tide. You see Olava in the autumn when you come back."

"Yes."

His father did not, in the saying of any of this, look pleased. He looked, rather, like a man who has already made the calculation Olav was being slow to make. The calculation was the obvious one. The calculation was that this was the offer; this was the only offer; this would be the offer Olav would accept.

Olav took the letter back to the loft and sat with it for an hour.

He did not, in the hour, write the acceptance letter.

---

The second letter came on the next morning's mail.

It came from Stavanger, from the office of a shipping agent named Lønningen — not the clerk Olav had lodged with at Mrs. Kindingstad's, but Lønningen's older brother, who was the agent for an East India bark called the *Semiramis* of Bergen. The *Semiramis* was preparing for a voyage to Calcutta with a cargo of Norwegian timber, returning with rice and indigo. The voyage was eighteen months. The captain was a man named Brun, a master mariner of forty years, who was looking for a strong, healthy, certified man for an able seaman's berth — not an officer's berth — at common seaman's wages, with the understanding that the voyage would put a young navigator into deep-sea conditions a coaster would not, and that the next voyage of the *Semiramis* (in 1883) would have an officer's berth available for a man who had distinguished himself in the able seaman's role on the present voyage.

The letter ended: *Captain Brun would prefer to interview the candidate at Stavanger. He sails in the second week of June. We require your decision by the third Monday of February.*

Olav read the second letter three times.

He did not, this time, take it to the kitchen.

He took it to the loft. He sat at the small table at the end of the bed. He laid the two letters side by side.

The Houlder offer was the offer his father wanted him to take and the offer Olava would, if she had been told, want him to take. Six months. Bergen-Hamburg. Home in autumn. A second mate's berth that would pay enough for him to see her three times before the autumn. The certificate would be earning. The marriage would be possible by the next year.

The Brun offer was the demotion. Eighteen months. India. Common seaman's wages. Home, if he survived it, at the end of 1882 or the beginning of 1883. The certificate would not be earning. The marriage would have to wait two years. And the *Semiramis* would put him into the kind of voyage Sars had described on the rail of the *Diana,* in the speech about the next three things, that Olav had been carrying the shape of in his head since that evening three summers before.

He sat with the two letters for the rest of the afternoon.

He understood that the Houlder offer was the safe one, and that the safe one was not the one Sars would have told him to take, and that he had already, by the second hour at the table in the loft, decided to take the Brun offer, and that he would now have to spend the next ten days finding the right way to tell his father and the right way to tell Olava and the wrong way (he understood this with no joy) to tell himself that the deciding had not been the easier choice.

---

He wrote to Olava on the Wednesday.

The letter was short. *I have had two offers. I have decided which to take. The offer I am taking will keep me away for eighteen months. I will sail the second week of June. I am asking your permission. If you say no, I will take the other offer and we will be married in the spring.*

He did not, in the writing, mention the Brun captain's name or the *Semiramis* or Calcutta. He did not, in the writing, defend the choice. He asked the question.

He took the letter to the post.

---

Olava's answer came on the following Monday.

The answer was as short as the question:

> *Olav.*
>
> *Take the offer. I am not asking why you have chosen the longer one. I am noting that you have. I am noting that you wrote me to ask before you wrote your father. I am noting that you did not, in asking, pretend I had no standing in your decision.*
>
> *Eighteen months is a long time. We have been engaged six months. Eighteen months is three of those. We will, at the end of it, have been engaged for two years, and you will have done the voyage that Sars told you about on the* Diana, *and you will come home and we will be married. You will not, on the voyage, write me less than once a month. You will not, on the voyage, eat the things you do not need to eat to stay strong. You will not, in any port, be alone after midnight.*
>
> *That is the entirety of my conditions.*
>
> *The other thing I am noting, Olav — and I am not asking you to acknowledge the noting — is that the voyage you have chosen is the voyage that takes you to ports where you have been before, in a manner of speaking, in the years before I knew you. I have agreed at Christmas not to ask. I am not asking now. I am only saying I have noted, and that the noting is a thing I will keep alongside my other notings.*
>
> *Yours, Olava.*

Olav read the letter four times.

He read the second-to-last paragraph six times.

He understood, on the sixth reading, that Olava had inferred, from his choosing of the longer voyage and from her own knowing-without-being-told instinct, that the East India voyage was a voyage that would take him through ports of the world that the carrying piece of him already knew. She did not, in the letter, name the ports. She did not name what she suspected. She named only the noting. The noting was the ledger entry.

He folded the letter carefully. He put it in the leather folder beside the certificate and the postcard.

The leather folder was, by the end of January, a folder of three papers.

---

He wrote to Houlder & Sønn the same evening, declining their offer with his thanks.

He wrote to Lønningen of Stavanger the same evening, accepting the *Semiramis* berth and proposing to come to Stavanger for the interview with Captain Brun the following week.

He wrote to his father (who was, that evening, at Mosterøy with his uncle on a small carpentry job) with the news.

He took the three letters to the post in the morning.

He did not, walking back, feel particularly proud.

He felt, instead, that he had begun the marriage.
