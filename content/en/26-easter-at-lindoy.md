He went to Lindøy for the Easter, on the Maundy Thursday of April 1880, in his father's small boat with the wind out of the east and the channel still cold enough that the spray on the gunwales went to crystal before it ran.

The school had broken for ten days. He had spent the first two of the ten at Finnøy, helping his father with the boats and answering his mother's questions about Mrs. Kindingstad's kale (the kale, by April, having become a piece of correspondence between the two mothers that was carried on at three removes, with Olav and Olava as the postmen). On the Wednesday evening his father had said *go,* and his mother had given him a small parcel of bread and dried cod for the crossing and the Lindøys' household, and on the Thursday morning he had cast off at first light.

The crossing took two and a half hours. The water was the color of slate. The Lindøy hills came up green for the first time since November, and the smoke from the Lindøy chimney was thin against the green; the household was burning peat, which meant Captain Lindøy had decided the season had turned far enough that the wood could be saved for the cold nights still to come.

Olava was at the jetty when he tied up.

She had not been at the jetty for any of his arrivals after the August one; her mother had taken to meeting him at the door, and Olava had taken to coming in from the back garden with her hands in dirt. The standing-at-the-jetty was a thing she had decided, this Easter, to resume. He understood, climbing up onto the boards, that the resumption was the first piece of news of the visit.

"Olav."

"Olava."

"You came on a fair wind."

"Yes."

She did not, this time, say anything else. She took the line from him and made it fast at the iron ring with hands that knew the work better than his did. She straightened. She looked at him for the count of perhaps three breaths, the way she had looked at him across the meadow at Landa nearly two years before, with the small head-tilt that meant she was, for her own reasons, taking inventory.

"Father is at the boats," she said. "Mother is at the kitchen. Marit is at the schoolmaster's. They have, between them, agreed that I am to walk you up to the house alone. You will please not, when we reach the house, give my mother any indication that the walking-up alone was prearranged. She has decided that this is a thing she is bestowing on us, and I would prefer she keep the decision."

"Yes."

"Good."

She turned and started up the path from the jetty.

He picked up his sea-chest and followed her.

---

The walk from the Lindøy jetty to the house was a quarter mile of low stone wall and small pasture and a single bend at the dry-stone byre. He had walked it five times before — twice in the August of '79, three times since — and he knew its small turnings the way a man learns the few hundred yards of road between any two places he is going to walk between for the rest of his life.

She walked ahead of him for the first half of the path. He carried the chest. She did not look back. At the bend at the byre she stopped, and waited for him to come up alongside her, and she put her hand briefly on his sleeve at the bicep, the way a woman puts her hand on her husband's sleeve in church.

She kept the hand there for the count of five breaths. He counted.

Then she took her hand away. She started walking again. She did not, in the taking-away, say anything.

He understood, walking the second half of the path beside her, that this was the news of the visit; or rather, that this was the form the news of the visit was going to take. The hand on his sleeve had not been there at Christmas. It had not been there at the November signing, when she had held his face at the jetty and let him go. It had not been there at any prior visit. It was here now. It was going to be here again, perhaps, in different forms, over the course of the ten days. He was, Olav thought, going to spend the next ten days noting where her hand went, and counting the breaths it stayed, and not saying anything about the noting and the counting; and she was going to know that he was doing all of this; and she was going to know, also, that he had decided to do it without being asked.

This, he understood as the house came into view above the small pasture, was the form of the engagement that had not yet been announced.

---

Captain Lindøy was at the boat-shed, as Olava had said. He was working a piece of birch on a small bench-clamp, with his good hand on the rasp and his wooden hand braced against the bench to hold the work steady. The wooden hand had been polished recently; the leather around the wrist was darker than the rest of it, where Olava's mother had been oiling the leather to keep it from cracking in the spring damp.

The captain looked up when Olav came into the shed.

"Hestby."

"Sir."

"The wind held you a fair crossing."

"Yes, sir."

"Set your chest in the back room. My wife has put a kettle on. Olava will, in three quarters of an hour, be looking for a man to walk to the high pasture with her. The path is dry. You may have the boots in the porch if yours are not." He set the rasp down. "She has been talking about the high pasture since the first of March. The talking is, I think, a thing she has been doing on the assumption that you would be at the high pasture by the second week of April. I am telling you so that you do not, in your own slowness, surprise her."

"Yes, sir."

"Good."

The captain went back to the rasp. He did not look up again.

Olav set his chest in the back room. The room was the one he had slept in twice before. The window faced the channel. From the window, in the April light, he could see Finnøy across the water, with his own house's chimney smoke thin against the eastern hills.

He went down to the kitchen. The mother was at the stove. The kettle was singing.

"Olav."

"Ma'am."

"You are thinner than at Christmas."

"I have been at the school."

"I was not asking. I was telling. Sit. There is bread."

He sat. There was bread. There was butter. There was the brown cheese he had been eating in this house since August. The mother poured coffee into a cup that was not the cup she had used at any prior visit; this one was thinner, with a small green vine painted at the rim, the kind of cup a woman puts on a table for a visitor she has decided is no longer a visitor.

He drank the coffee. He ate the bread.

The mother did not, while he ate, ask him anything. She moved around the kitchen with the small competent quiet of a woman who had been moving around the same kitchen for thirty-five years, and she let him eat. After perhaps ten minutes she said, without looking at him:

"My daughter has been talking about the high pasture."

"The captain mentioned."

"My daughter does not, in the ordinary case, talk about a thing for six weeks unless she means the talking to be heard. You have heard. Go up the path with her after the kettle has cooled. Take the bread in your pocket; she will not have eaten."

"Yes, ma'am."

"Olav."

"Yes, ma'am."

"I am not, this Easter, going to ask after the school. The school is the school. The school will give you the certificate or it will not. The thing I am going to ask after, by the end of the ten days, is what you intend to do with the certificate when you have it. I am telling you the question now so that you will, over the ten days, prepare an answer. The answer is mine to receive, not my husband's. He will not ask. I will."

"Yes, ma'am."

She turned then, and looked at him across the kitchen for the first time since he had sat. Her eyes were the gray-channel eyes of her daughter, older. She looked at him the way she had looked at him at the August supper, the first visit; she looked at him longer this time.

"Good," she said. "You will do."

She went back to the stove.

---

The path to the high pasture went up behind the house, through the small pasture, past the spring at the cleft in the rock, and up the long slope of grass to the rise where the channel and Finnøy and Mosterøy and the whole near sea opened out at once.

They walked it together at a quarter to noon. Olava had on the wool coat she had worn in November and a kerchief over her hair against the wind. He had the bread in his pocket. They did not speak for the first part of the climb; the path was steep enough that talking would have cost them breath.

At the spring she stopped. She unhooked a small tin cup that hung on a hawthorn branch beside the water — the cup had been there, she said, since her mother was a girl — and she drank, and she filled the cup again and handed it to him. He drank. The water was the cold of water that had come out of stone in March and remembered it.

She took the cup back. She hung it on the branch.

She put her hand in his.

She did this without ceremony. She did this without asking. She did this, he understood at the moment of her doing it, because she had decided sometime between November and April that the next time they were alone on a path her hand would be in his hand, and she had decided that she would do it without speaking.

Her hand was small in his. It was warmer than the spring water. It had a small callus at the base of the thumb from the kitchen knives. He did not, in the moment of taking it, do anything except close his fingers around it.

They walked up the rest of the path with their hands joined.

At the top of the rise the sea was the color the sea was in April, which is to say the color of nothing he could name without lying. The gulls were up. Mosterøy was a long blue shape on the southern horizon. Finnøy was small behind them. The wind was from the east.

She did not let go of his hand when they reached the top. He did not let go either. They stood for some time at the top with their hands together and their faces turned into the wind, and they did not speak. Then she said, very quietly, the way she had said *come up to the house* at the November jetty:

"I have not been alone with you since November."

"No."

"I will not be alone with you again, on a path or anywhere else, between now and the wedding."

"No."

"Father will not allow it."

"No."

"Mother will not allow it."

"No."

"This is the only walk we will take alone before we are married."

"Yes."

She did not, in saying any of this, look at him. She looked at the sea.

After perhaps a minute she said:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"Will you do something for me."

"Yes."

"You will not, at the end of the walk, ask me for anything I cannot give without being married to you. You will walk me back down with my hand in yours, and you will let go at the spring, and you will not — between the spring and the back door — touch me again. You will trust me to know what I am asking. You will not ask me why."

"Yes."

"Thank you, Olav."

"Yes."

They stood at the top of the rise for a quarter of an hour more. He did not, in the quarter of an hour, ask her why. He did, in the quarter of an hour, understand without being told that what she was asking him to do was the work of a marriage that had not yet started; and that she was asking him, in the small flat way she asked for things, to do the work now so that the marriage when it started would have, on its first day, the foundation of his having already done it.

He did the work.

They walked down the path together with her hand in his. At the spring he let go of her hand, and she hung the tin cup back on the hawthorn branch, and they walked the last hundred yards to the back door of the house side by side and not touching.

Her mother looked up from the kitchen window as they came around the byre. She did not, when she saw they were not touching, smile. She did not, when she saw they were not touching, look pleased. She looked, briefly, at her daughter's face, and at Olav's, and she went back to whatever she had been doing at the table.

That was the inspection. It had taken her perhaps four seconds. She had passed the answer she had wanted.

---

The remaining nine days of the visit were the days a household runs when a young man is staying in it who is engaged to the daughter of the house but not yet married to her.

He helped Captain Lindøy at the boat-shed for the mornings of the first three days. They worked a small skiff that had taken winter damage to the keel; the captain instructed him in the dressing of the keel-piece with the wooden hand braced against the work; Olav did the cutting with the rasp; the captain checked his work with the good hand and pronounced it acceptable, by which the captain meant good. They did not, in the boat-shed, speak of Olava. They spoke of the boats and of a Bergen captain the captain had known thirty years before and of the price of pitch in Stavanger.

He sat with the family for supper for nine evenings. The mother asked him, on the seventh evening, what he intended to do with the certificate when he had it. He gave her the answer he had been preparing for six days. The answer was that he intended to take the first berth he was offered in a ship of any size, on any honest run, that would put a wage in his pocket and a navigator's name on his papers; that he would take the berth even if it were a long one; that he would, on the long voyage if there was one, write Olava once a month at the least; that the marriage was the marriage, and the berth was the work that paid for the marriage, and he was the man who would do whichever work the two of them required of him in whichever order they required it. He did not, in the answer, name a specific ship. He named the plan. The plan was the answer the mother had asked for.

She listened. She nodded once. She said *good.*

Then she said, *eat your fish.*

He saw Olava every day. He did not, in the nine days, take her hand again. She did not, in the nine days, take his. They sat across from each other at supper. They walked from the house to the church on Easter Sunday with the rest of the family, and she did not, on the icy patch on the road, take his arm. They sat in the kitchen with her mother in the evenings, and they did not, in any of the evenings, brush against each other in the moving around the kitchen for the kettle.

The not-touching, he understood by the third evening, was the form for the next two years. She had told him about it on the rise at the high pasture; she had not, in the telling, named the next two years; she had named only the walks-and-anywhere-else. He had understood the rest. The rest was the silent half of what she had said.

He understood, also, by the seventh evening, that the not-touching was not a withholding. It was a promise — the promise that, when the touching came, it would come in a country where the touching belonged. The country was the marriage, and the marriage was being built between them, this Easter, by the work of standing in the same room without reaching across it.

This was not the kind of romance the men in his school books would have recognized as romance. He understood that too. He understood, sitting at the kitchen table on the eighth evening with Olava across from him peeling an apple for her father and not looking at him, that the romance he was inside was the romance of two Norwegian people of their class and their century arranging themselves into the shape they were going to be in for the rest of their lives, and that the arranging was a thing done in absences and small gestures and the four seconds her mother had spent inspecting their faces at the back door, and that the arranging was as much a romance as anything the men in his school books had been able to name.

---

He left on the morning of the second Sunday after Easter.

The captain came down to the jetty with him to cast off. The mother stood in the doorway of the house with a hand at her throat. Olava came down the path from the house wearing the wool coat and the kerchief, and she walked the last twenty feet to the jetty alone, and she stood at the head of the jetty as he stepped down into the boat.

She did not, this time, hold his face.

She raised her right hand to her chin, briefly, the gesture a woman in church makes when she has finished a small private prayer; she let the hand fall; she looked at him for the count of three breaths the way she had looked at him at the August jetty, and the November jetty, and at the top of the rise above the high pasture. Then she said:

"Sail well."

"I will."

"I will see you in June, after the certificate."

"Yes."

"Olav."

"Yes."

"Do not, at the school, work yourself into the cough. The cough is a thing I will not, in the months between now and the certificate, have you bring back to me."

"Yes."

"Good. Go."

He went.

He cast off and pushed out from the jetty with the long oar and stepped the mast and let the small lugsail take the wind, and he turned the bow into the channel and ran for Finnøy with the wind on his quarter. The Lindøy hills receded behind him. Olava was at the head of the jetty for as long as he could see her. She did not, while he could see her, raise her hand. She stood with her hands clasped in front of her at the waist, the way a woman stands when she means a man to know she is standing there.

When the boat had cleared the small headland at the south end of Lindøy, and the jetty was no longer visible, he eased the sheet a fraction and looked back once more — not at the place where the jetty had been but at the rise above the high pasture, which was visible from the channel, a small clean line of green against the April sky.

He thought: *I have been on that rise with her hand in mine for one quarter of an hour.*

He thought: *I will not be on that rise with her hand in mine again for two years.*

He thought: *I will be on that rise with her hand in mine for the rest of our lives after.*

He held the three thoughts in the order he thought them. He did not put any of them in any drawer. They were not, he understood, drawer-thoughts. They were the kind of thoughts a man held in his open hand and looked at, and let the wind take or not take, and lived with whichever way it went.

The boat ran for Finnøy on a fair wind out of the east.
