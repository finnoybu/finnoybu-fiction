title: "The Cough Returns"

The cough came back in February.

It came back the way the cough had come back at Vardøy: on the second day of a week of cold weather, in the early morning, in a cough that he had not, the first morning, recognized as the cough. He had thought it was a winter cold. He had thought he had eaten something that did not agree with him. He had had two such coughs already that winter and had recovered from them in three days each, as a healthy young man does.

This cough did not recover in three days.

By the end of the first week the cough had a small wet thickness at the bottom of it that was the wet thickness he had known on the *Allegro.* He recognized it, on the morning of the eighth day, with his sleeve at his mouth, in the small uncertain light of the room at Mrs. Kindingstad's. He sat for a long time on the edge of the bed with his sleeve at his mouth before he allowed himself to take the sleeve away and look at it.

The sleeve had a small wet spot on it.

He did not, in the small uncertain light, look at the spot for long.

He folded the sleeve under, so that the spot was not visible from the outside of the cuff. He stood up. He dressed. He went down to breakfast.

---

The school was the school. He attended his lectures. He took notes. He answered Prof. Nilson's questions when called on. He did the chart-work. His handwriting did not, that week, change. His face did not, that week, change. He had spent five months on the *Sigrid* learning how not to let his face do anything his face did not need to do, and the lesson had stayed; the lesson would, he understood now, stay forever.

He did not write to Olava that Wednesday. The Wednesday letter had been due. He did not write it. He wrote it on Saturday instead, two days late, and he did not, in the letter, mention the cough. He wrote about the haversine and about a small piece of weather over the Stavanger harbor and about a book of charts Prof. Nilson had recommended. He did not write about the cough because writing about it would be to say it, and saying it would be to give it the dignity of a name, and he was not, in the second week, prepared to give it the dignity.

By the end of the second week the cough had loosened. The wet spot on the sleeve had diminished. He thought, perhaps, that he had been wrong about it. He thought it might have been a winter cold after all.

By the end of the third week the cough was almost gone.

He was, by then, more frightened than he had been on any night in the boarding house at Wilmington, on any night at Vardøy, on any night on any of the ships of any of his voyages. He was frightened in the way the body is frightened when a thing it has come to trust is shown, briefly, to be untrustworthy. He had trusted, since the loft at Finnøy in the August he came home cured, that the cough was off; that his body was a body Sars had given back to him; that the years ahead were years he could plan for. The three weeks of February had told him that the cough was off until the cough was on again, and that it could come on at any time, and that the not-coming-on for two years had not been a guarantee. It had been a remission.

He understood this in his head with the small clarity Dr. Due had used in the consulting-room. He understood it, also, with the body — the body that knew now that it had a passenger who could rejoin the ride at any station and could ride for any length of time.

---

In the third week, on a Thursday evening, he went to a small tavern at the bottom of Bredalmendingen called the Wagtail. He did not, ordinarily, go to taverns. He went, that evening, because the room at Mrs. Kindingstad's was a room he could not, after three weeks of February, sit in alone for another evening without doing something he would regret. The tavern was full of merchant-marine men in port for the winter. Olav took a small table by the window. He ordered a beer he did not particularly want. He sat with the beer and watched the room.

At the next table over were three sailors of perhaps thirty, in heavy coats, with the look of men who had been at the Wagtail since five o'clock. They were not loud. They were drunk in the particular subdued way Norwegian sailors get drunk, which is to say that the drink slowed them rather than accelerated them. They were talking about ports. The middle one — a man with a black beard and a missing front tooth — was telling a story about Hamburg. Olav, half listening, sipped his beer.

The man with the black beard finished the Hamburg story. The man on his left — older, grey-bearded, with a long scar at the side of his face — said *I was in Hamburg in '78, on the* Sigrid *out of Stavanger.*

Olav set down his beer.

He did not, in the setting down, do it loudly. He did it the way a man sets down a glass when he wants no one to notice that he is now listening.

"The *Sigrid?*" said the middle one. "That brig that came home with no captain."

"That was *Sigrid.*"

"What happened to him."

"Gjermund? He was at Hamburg with us in '78. He was master then. He went mad on the way home. He drove the brig onto a sandbar at the mouth of the Elbe and stood at the wheel and laughed. The mate took the wheel from him. They got her off the bar with the next tide. The mate brought her into Stavanger. The owners put Gjermund in the asylum at Eg in '79. He died there last year."

"He died."

"He died."

"Of what."

"Of being himself, I suppose. The asylum did not say. He had a smile on him at the end, they said. The man at the asylum told me. It was a smile, the asylum-man said, that he did not change for anything. It was on him when he died. The asylum-man said it was the strangest smile he had ever seen on a dying man's face. He had seen many."

The three of them were silent for a moment. Then the middle one said, *Bad business,* and the grey-bearded one said, *Bad business indeed,* and they moved on to a different ship and a different port. Olav, at the table by the window, did not move for some time.

He did not finish the beer. He paid for it. He went out into the cold of Bredalmendingen. He walked to Mrs. Kindingstad's. He climbed the stairs to the room at the back. He sat on the edge of the bed. He took off his coat. He sat with his coat on his lap.

The cough had been gone, almost, for a week. It had not come back that day. It did not come back that night. It did not, that he could recall later, come back at all that spring.

But that night, in the cold of the room, he understood that the captain who had tried to kill him three times on the brig had been, in the end, a man who could not sustain his own face into the asylum at Eg, and that the smile he had carried on the *Sigrid* — the smile that Olav had spent five months reading, and three years afterward had still been able, in his head, to recall — had been the same smile he had died with. The smile had outlasted the captain. The smile had been the captain. The captain had been a man whose smile had eaten him.

Olav sat on the bed for an hour with this understanding. He did not, in the hour, think of Haakon at first. He thought of the captain. He thought of a man who had been a piece of weather across the part of his life in which the drawer had been built, and of how the man's smile had ended in a Norwegian asylum without the man having ever, perhaps, taken it off. He thought of the man at the asylum who had seen the smile and had tried, after, to describe it to a sailor in a tavern in Stavanger.

He thought, after some time, of Haakon. He could not, having thought of the captain, not think of Haakon. The drawer was open, briefly, in the cold of the room at Mrs. Kindingstad's, and he let it be open for the count of perhaps four minutes. He thought of the boatswain from Hogganvik who had stood at the foot of the foremast on the night Olav had come down with the cut becket in his belt and had said *I am sorry.* He thought of the brig coming into Stavanger after the captain had been taken off her, with Haakon, presumably, on her, presumably alive, presumably gone home to Hogganvik for a few months, presumably back at sea now on some other ship. He did not know. He did not know any of it. He had never asked.

He thought, also — and this was the new thing — that there was now a woman at Lindøy who knew there was a drawer. She had told him so over cloudberries on Christmas morning. She had said she would be in the room with him when the carrying got too heavy. He was not summoning her tonight. The contents were not, tonight, that heavy. But the carrying had stopped being the kind of carrying that no one knew about. Someone else now knew. The drawer was, after Christmas, a different kind of object. He was beginning, in the cold of the room at Mrs. Kindingstad's, to learn what kind.

He did not, in the four minutes, ask. The drawer closed, as quietly as it had opened, when the cold of the room reasserted itself and he understood that he was sitting on the edge of the bed in his coat with no way of going on with the evening except by going to bed.

He went to bed.

---

He wrote to Olava the next morning. He wrote the letter he had not, in three weeks, been able to write. He did not tell her about the cough. He did not tell her about the captain. He told her, instead, that he had been thinking of the cloudberries she had jarred for him at Christmas, and that he hoped her family was well, and that he was looking forward, with some impatience, to the end of the term and to seeing her in Lindøy at Easter.

He sealed the letter. He addressed it the way she had told him to, at the jetty in August: *Olava Lindøy, the captain's daughter, Lindøy, by Stavanger.* He took it down Bredalmendingen to the post-house and gave it to the postmaster and watched the postmaster put it in the Tuesday sack.

It would reach her on Tuesday evening. She would read it that night by the kitchen lamp. She would know, when she had read it, what was not in it. She had told him as much, on Christmas morning, in the back room above her mother's kitchen, with a dish of cloudberries on the tray between them.

He walked back to Mrs. Kindingstad's by a different street.
