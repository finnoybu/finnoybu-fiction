The first letter of the autumn reached her on the second Sunday of August.

It was the third letter since Olav had sailed home from Lindøy in the small boat his father had lent him at the end of June. The two earlier letters had been July letters — the first a short note the week after his return reporting that the certificate had gone on the shelf above his father's kitchen window and that the shelf now had two papers on it; the second a Wednesday letter at the end of the month about the haymaking on Mosterøy and the uncle's opinion of a mare he had bought at auction.

The August letter was longer than the July letters. It had four pages. She read it at the kitchen table after the Sunday supper, with the lamp on the shelf above the stove and her mother across the table with her knitting. Her father was in the parlor. Marit was at the schoolmaster's for the evening. The dog was at the door.

The letter said, in its four pages, the things Olav said in letters now: the small domestic arithmetic of a man at home without a berth. He had written to three Stavanger firms. He had written to a man in Bergen Sars had recommended. He expected replies in two to three weeks. He had been at the boats with his father. He had read a book Prof. Nilson had given him at the end of the term. He was considering, if no berth presented itself by November, a winter at the boats with his uncle at Mosterøy for the small wage. The uncle had offered.

She read the letter twice.

She set it on the table beside her mother's knitting.

Her mother looked up from the knitting.

"Is he well."

"Yes."

"Will he come in August."

"He has not said."

"Then he will not."

"No."

Her mother nodded and went back to the knitting. The exchange had been the exchange the two of them had developed across the past ten months for Sundays at the kitchen table after a letter. It was a short exchange. It contained what her mother wished to know and what Olava wished to say, and it did not, by either woman's intention, contain anything else.

Olava folded the letter in three. She put it in the small inside pocket of her apron. She stood up from the table. She cleared the supper things to the basin, the way she had cleared them at the end of every supper at Lindøy since she was eleven.

Her father came through from the parlor at the end of the clearing. He took his hat from the hook by the kitchen door. He said, to her mother:

"I will be at the shed for an hour."

"In the dark?"

"There is a lamp in the shed."

"In the dark in August?"

"There is a lamp."

He went out. The door shut behind him. Her mother looked at the closed door for a moment, then looked at Olava.

"Your father," she said, "when Asbjørn was a boy, did the same."

"What same."

"The going to the shed after supper. On Sundays particularly."

"Did he."

"Yes."

Her mother did not, after saying this, continue. She picked up the knitting again. She worked at it for a minute. Olava watched her mother from the basin. Her mother did not look up. Her mother was, Olava understood, conveying a thing her mother had decided to convey tonight without further elaboration. The thing was: the father's return, since the summer, to the shed-after-supper habit of his son-raising years was the shape the father's grief was taking at sixty-two, five and a half years after Asbjørn's death and one and a half years before the daughter's wedding. The mother was not commenting. The mother was only placing the fact on the kitchen table beside the knitting so that Olava, at twenty, would register it.

Olava registered it.

She did not say anything.

She finished clearing the basin. She went up to her room.

---

She wrote her Sunday letter at the small desk at the foot of her bed in the square of afternoon light the window gave for an hour between three and four o'clock.

She had, since the autumn of the year before, written the Sunday letter at this hour. She had established the hour because the hour had been the hour when no one else in the house required her. Her mother was at her own stitching at the kitchen. Marit was at the schoolmaster's, where she went most Sunday afternoons since the engagement had been arranged in June. Her father was at the shed. The dog was at the kitchen. The house was, between three and four on a Sunday, hers.

She wrote on a single sheet of heavy paper. The paper had come to the household from the Stavanger stationer for as long as Olava could remember; Olava had, since the autumn of the year before, kept her own supply in the small drawer of the desk.

She wrote slowly. She did not draft first. She had found, across the ten months, that drafting produced a lesser letter than writing in a single sitting. The Sunday letter was a sitting-letter. She allowed it to be what she wrote in the hour. She did not go back to correct any sentence; she did not, across the ten months, remember a sentence she had wanted to take back; she had arrived at the register by November of the year before and the register had held.

The register was: weather, family, household-work she had done that week, a small observation about the school or the parish, and — in the last paragraph — one sentence of a different kind. The last sentence was not domestic. The last sentence was the part of the letter she had, at the beginning, imagined she would not know how to write and had, by the third letter, begun writing without effort. The last sentence was an observation about him. Not about his letters; about him. About something she had registered across the week about the man the Sunday letter was going to. It was, she understood, the small part of the letter that was doing what the letter was for.

The observation on a Sunday in the middle of September was:

> *You will, I think, not hear from the Stavanger firms before the end of the month. The replies, when they come, will be polite. They will not be what you are hoping for. The next berth will come from a direction you are not at this moment writing toward.*

She sealed the letter.

She addressed it to *Olav Hestby, Hestby-gaard, Finnøy, by Stavanger.*

She put it in the kitchen tray for the Thursday post.

---

On the first Sunday of October she went, after the midday dinner, to the back garden bench.

The bench had been put there by her father in the spring of '78. It was a simple bench of white-painted pine, set against the south wall of the small garden so that a person sitting on it had the sun on her face in the forenoon and the shade of the house on her face in the afternoon. She had been, in the two and a half years the bench had stood, the person who sat on the bench most often. Her father sat on it sometimes in the early summer when the spring work was done. Her mother did not sit on the bench. Marit sat on it twice that Olava remembered.

Olava sat on it for an hour or two each week, in the weather that allowed.

She sat on it this Sunday because she had decided, at the Sunday service in the morning, that she would do the small piece of private accounting she had been putting off since June.

She took from the apron-pocket the small square of folded paper she had put there that morning. She unfolded it on her knee. The paper had, in her small pencilled hand, a list of four dates:

> *November 1880 — the signing.*
>
> *Spring 1881 — Easter. Olav at Lindøy for a week.*
>
> *June 1881 — the berth. The voyage.*
>
> *— The return.*

The fourth line was without a date.

She looked at the list.

The signing, at Finnøy, in November, was the engagement the two households had been arranging since August by their mothers' fortnightly correspondence and their fathers' less frequent letters. The date had been set in the mother-letter of the second week of August. Her father would sail to Finnøy on the last Monday of November. He would bring the leather wallet and the small set of papers he had been drafting in his bedroom across the two months since the date was set. He would come back by the evening tide with the papers signed. That was the signing.

The Easter at Lindøy would be, Olava understood, the first week Olav and she would have as engaged persons. They had, in the year of the pre-engagement, had three visits at Lindøy and the one Christmas visit at which the arrangement had been made. The Easter would be the first visit at which the arrangement was written down. She did not think the arrangement would feel different. She thought she would find, on the Easter morning when she met him at the jetty, that the written-down arrangement was a formality laid over an arrangement that had already, in its unwritten form, been the floor of their walking for ten months.

The berth and the voyage she had been turning over for some weeks. The berth would, as she had written to him on Sunday, not come from the Stavanger firms. She did not know from where it would come. She suspected Bergen — someone in the small network Sars had been putting together for Olav across the summer, whose name Sars had not yet, in his July letter, been prepared to give. The voyage would be, she thought, not a coastal voyage. A new navigator whose certificate was ten months old and whose captain from the *Sigrid* had been at Eg asylum was not a man Norwegian shipping firms were going to send on a coastal run close to home. They would send him out. A long voyage, she thought. The Horn, perhaps. The east, perhaps. She did not know which. She knew the shape of the absence. The shape of the absence was the shape a voyage of a year and a half or two years has, and she did not, today at the bench, require a number more precise than that.

The fourth line she had written without a date because the date was the date she could not forecast. The return would be what the return was. Men came back from long voyages on dates their wives did not set. The return was the piece she had committed herself at Christmas to carrying in its not-knowing.

She looked at the list for a long time.

She put a pencilled parenthesis around the fourth line. Then she took the pencil and wrote, under the fourth line, a fifth:

> *The notebook.*

She wrote the two words and looked at them.

She had been, across the summer, deciding whether she would keep a notebook for the engagement year and the absence.

A notebook kept across an absence was not a thing most Norwegian island wives of her mother's generation had kept. Her mother had not kept one during her father's voyages. Her father had been a captain who sailed home on predictable rotations of three and six months. Her mother had kept household accounts in a small ledger and written her fortnightly letters to the women of her correspondence-network and done nothing further.

Olava had been, at the bench across the summer, thinking that her marriage would require something more.

She did not, at twenty in the October of 1880, fully know why. She knew only that the arrangement she had made at Christmas — the carrying of the not-asking, the agreement that there was a piece she would not ask after — required of her, in the years ahead, a register. The register was not for Olav. The register was for her. It was the thing she would, across the absence, keep by way of keeping the arrangement. It was the thing she would, at the end of a long run of not-asking, be able to look at and know whether the agreement was still the agreement.

She would, she decided on the bench, buy the notebook.

She would buy it in Stavanger in November, after the signing, on a morning when her father had business at the consulate and she would walk out alone to the bookbinder at the end of Kongsgata. She would buy a small cheap octavo notebook. She would bring it home on the boat that afternoon. She would write the first entry in it at the kitchen table after supper on the night her father had put the leather wallet in the drawer of his desk.

She folded the paper. She put it back in the apron-pocket.

She sat on the bench for another half hour.

---

Marit came down the path from the high pasture at five.

She came through the garden gate with her cheeks red from the walk and her hair escaped from its braid and the small kerchief she wore at her throat askew. She was seventeen in the autumn of 1880. She had been, since the end of June, engaged to the schoolmaster's son — an engagement that had been arranged by a kitchen conversation between her mother and the schoolmaster's wife and ratified by a short letter between the fathers, and which Marit had accepted with an enthusiasm that had surprised no one at Lindøy because Marit had been, since she was fifteen, choosing Eirik at every small daily opportunity. The engagement had not been signed yet. The signing would be the week after Olava's own, in the first week of December; the schoolmaster was waiting for the engagement at Lindøy to be first, on the ordering-instinct of a man who knew the captain and knew that the captain's daughter's engagement should take precedence.

Marit sat down on the bench beside Olava without asking.

"You are thinking."

"I am."

"About the letter."

"In part."

"In what other part."

"About the notebook I am going to buy in Stavanger."

"What notebook."

"The one I will keep during Olav's voyage."

Marit looked at her.

"You are going to keep a notebook."

"Yes."

"Like father."

"No. Father keeps a ledger of cargo and wages. This will be — different."

"What kind of different."

"The kind I will know when I am writing in it."

Marit considered this. She did not press. Marit had not pressed at Olava across the year of the pre-engagement; Marit had registered, by the small domestic intuition of a sister three years younger, that the engagement her sister was making had a shape she had not seen in the engagements the other Lindøy girls had been making, and she had — without naming it — decided that the shape was her sister's shape to name when her sister wished to. Marit's own engagement with Eirik was a more ordinary shape. Marit was marrying a man she had been choosing since fifteen and who had been choosing her for the same time. The engagement was the formal naming of a thing two households had expected since '78. There was no piece. There was no not-asking. There was Eirik.

Marit looked at her knees.

"Olava."

"Yes."

"Will you be — are you — "

"I am what."

Marit looked up.

"Sorry. I do not know how to ask it. I am asking: are you making the engagement for the reason a person makes an engagement, or for some other reason. I am not saying you are not. I am asking because the engagement does not, when I watch it, look like Eirik and me. It does not look like the girls here on the island. It does not look like the girls at Sola. It looks like a thing I do not, having never seen it, know how to look at. I am asking because I am your sister and I wish to know whether the thing is a thing I should be glad for or a thing I should worry about."

Olava looked at her.

"Marit."

"Yes."

"You should be glad."

"I should."

"Yes."

"Because."

"Because the thing is the thing I want. It is not the thing you and Eirik have. It is the thing Olav and I have. It is a different thing. Both things are real things. Mine is the one I want."

Marit nodded slowly.

"All right."

"Yes."

"I was worried."

"You should not be worried. You should be glad."

"I am glad."

"Good."

Marit sat for another minute. Then she said:

"He will bring you cloudberries at Easter, I think."

Olava did not answer. She looked at the hedge. The hedge had the last of the summer's leaves on it.

Marit got up.

"I will be in at the kitchen. Mother was saying she wanted help with the apples."

"Yes."

"Come in when you are cold."

"Yes."

Marit went in.

Olava sat for another five minutes. Then she got up and went in.

---

Through the kitchen window while she was peeling apples at the basin Olava watched her father at the boat-shed across the small yard.

He had gone out after the midday dinner, as he did most Sunday afternoons since the summer, and he had been in the shed for three hours. The shed door was open a hand's breadth to the southwesterly afternoon light. She could see his outline — the good shoulder, the wooden hand on the bench — moving at some small work she could not identify at the distance. He was, from the window, a small moving figure against the dark of the shed interior.

Her mother at the kitchen table was writing a letter.

The letter was the Sunday letter to Mrs. Hestby.

The Sunday letter to Mrs. Hestby had begun in the second week of June, when Olav had come back from Stavanger with the certificate and the two mothers had understood, across the channel, that the arrangement-of-the-engagement had passed from the mothers' general discussion of when to the mothers' particular arrangement of how. The how involved the date of the signing, the location of the Easter visit, the tentative date of the wedding (to be set when Olav had a berth), the small matter of whose parish church — Finnøy or Lindøy — the ceremony would be at. The how required the fortnightly exchange of a careful letter. The exchange had been, across the sixteen weeks since June, the small cross-channel spine of the mother-decision on each side.

Olava's mother wrote the letter at the kitchen table on Sundays between five and six.

She used a single sheet. She used the pen she had been using at the kitchen letter-desk for some years. She did not draft. She wrote in a small steady hand that had not changed, Olava thought, since Olava had first seen her mother write a letter at age eight.

Olava could not, from the basin, read the letter.

She could see the shape of it. The shape of the letter this Sunday was three paragraphs. The first paragraph would be, Olava knew, the small weather-and-garden opening two island wives of the mother-network generation always used. The second paragraph would be a piece of news — today the news would be that the date of the signing had held, that her father had drawn up the first draft of the papers, that he would begin the finishing of them in the last week of the month. The third paragraph would be the single-sentence proposal on the Easter plan: Mrs. Hestby to host the week, or Mrs. Lindøy, or a division.

Her mother sealed the letter. She addressed it — *Mrs Jens Hestby, Hestby-gaard, Finnøy, by Stavanger* — in the same small steady hand. She placed it in the kitchen tray for the Thursday post.

She looked up from the table.

"Olava."

"Yes."

"Will you take this with your letter to the post on Thursday."

"Yes."

"Thank you."

Her mother stood. She went to the shelf for the small bottle of vinegar the apples wanted.

---

That evening at bedtime Olava lay in the bed at the back of the house and listened to the house settling.

Marit was in the bed on the other side of the small partition. She was already asleep. Her mother was at the small prayer-reading she did at her own bedside for ten minutes before lying down. Her father was still at the shed. Olava heard him come in at ten; she heard the shed door shut across the yard; she heard the house door open and shut; she heard his steps on the stair; she heard the door of her parents' bedroom close.

She listened to the house's arithmetic.

In two months the signing. In six months the Easter. In eight months the berth. The return when the return came.

The shape of the year ahead was, by the small private accounting at the bench that afternoon, a shape she had sufficiently mapped to be able to stop forecasting and begin enduring.

She would, in November, buy the notebook.

She would, in December, begin writing in it.

She would keep it across the engagement and across the voyage. She would put entries in it for every week Olav was at sea. She would put entries in it for every month she was herself at home. She would keep the small private indents she had been keeping in her head for the year of the pre-engagement in the small cheap octavo pages where no one but her would see them.

The arrangement would hold.

She turned onto her side.

She slept.
