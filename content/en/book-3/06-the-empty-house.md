The first letter from the *President Daae* came on the Monday following the Wednesday the bark had lifted Bergen.

Olava had been at the kitchen window when the postman came up the road. She had not, on the Sunday or the Monday morning, allowed herself to wait at the window — the not-allowing was the small discipline she had been practicing since the wedding for the moments at which a Lindøy daughter who had become a Finnøy wife had to teach a Finnøy household what kind of woman she was — but she had, at half past nine on the Monday morning, registered the postman's small bell at the road and had gone to the kitchen window to look. The postman had a single envelope. He was bringing it up the path.

She went to the door.

She took the envelope. She thanked the postman. She closed the door.

She did not, with the envelope in her hand, stop at the kitchen. She went up the stairs to the small bedroom under the gable that was her room — the room where the writing-desk lived, where the leather folder that held all of Olav's letters lived, where the ledger that had survived the East India voyage and was now opening on its second voyage lived. She closed the door. She sat at the desk.

She read the letter.

The letter was short. It said Olav had reported on the Tuesday afternoon, the captain had been clear at the first interview, the bark would lift on the morning tide of the Wednesday, the mate (a Bergen man named Tellefsen) was decent, and Olav was well. It was four sentences in his Cape Town hand — the navigator-at-sea hand she had identified two autumns ago as the version-of-him the voyage made.

She read it three times.

She did not, this voyage, read it four. She had decided, in the week before he had left, that the four-readings discipline would be reduced to three. The reasoning was small but specific: the *President Daae* voyage was eighteen months and the eighteen months held perhaps six letters; six letters at four readings each was twenty-four readings; six letters at three readings was eighteen. Eighteen, by her arithmetic, was the figure that would let the letters be the letters and would not let the reading become the obsession she did not want to live in for eighteen months.

She read it three times.

She put it in the folder.

She opened the new ledger she had bought at the Stavanger bookbinder in October — a slimmer book than the first, with a darker leather cover, in which she had written across the top of the first page *Voyage of the President Daae, lift Bergen 17 October 1883, return projected April 1885, eighteen months* — and she made the first entry.

> *17 October Tuesday letter received Finnøy 22 October Monday by the morning post. Five days from Bergen by the Friday and Sunday coasters. Hand the same as the Semiramis homeward Cape hand. Captain Thoresen described as 'clear at the first interview.' Mate Tellefsen described as 'decent.' Bark lifted morning tide of the Wednesday. Olav well.*

She blotted the page.

She closed the ledger.

She went down to the kitchen.

---

Mrs. Hestby was at the stove. She did not look up.

"Letter."

"Letter."

"Bergen?"

"Bergen. He reported Tuesday afternoon. Lift Wednesday morning. Captain clear at the first interview. The mate is decent. He is well."

"Good."

"They will be in mid-Atlantic by now."

"Yes."

"He will write again at Brooklyn in a month or so. The mail from Brooklyn is two weeks each way."

"Yes."

Mrs. Hestby did not, at the stove, ask further. She did not ask whether the captain had been kind. She did not ask whether Olav was eating. She did not ask any of the questions a less calibrated mother of his generation might have asked of a daughter-in-law she had been alone with in a kitchen for five days now. She asked only what the letter had said about the bark and the man on it, and Olava had told her what the letter had said, and the kitchen returned to its ordinary morning rhythm.

This, Olava had registered by the second day of being alone with Mrs. Hestby, was the household economy. Mrs. Hestby did not press. Mrs. Hestby waited for what was offered and did not require what was not. It was not, by Olava's reading, the same as Mrs. Lindøy's economy at home, which was a little more inquisitive within the same broader Norwegian island restraint. Mrs. Hestby's economy was, Olava had begun to understand, the economy of a woman who had decided early in her marriage that she would teach herself the smallest possible questioning consistent with running a household, and had kept the discipline for thirty-five years.

It was a discipline Olava recognized.

It was, in fact, the discipline she herself had been practicing — by a different route, at a different age — and Mrs. Hestby's having practiced it for thirty-five years gave Olava, at twenty-two, a clear forward-look at what the practice looked like at sixty. The forward-look was useful. The forward-look made Mrs. Hestby a kind of teacher Olava had not, in her own mother, had access to — because Mrs. Lindøy was a different kind of woman, no less competent but more inclined to fill a silence than Mrs. Hestby was, and Olava had always known, since she was twelve, that her own discipline of not-filling-the-silence was the small piece of her that had not come from her mother.

It had come, she now understood at the kitchen at Finnøy in the autumn of '83, from an aunt of her father's — Aunt Ingrid, dead since '76 — whom Olava had known only at three childhood visits but whose register she remembered.

Mrs. Hestby was, in some way Olava could not have articulated to anyone at all, the version of Aunt Ingrid that Olava had been waiting for since she was twelve.

She did not, at the kitchen, say so.

She set the table for the noon dinner.

---

The autumn went past in its small careful pace.

Olava and Mrs. Hestby — and Jens, who was at the boat-shed for most of the daylight hours and at the parlour with the Stavanger newspaper in the evenings — settled into the household economy of two women and one older man at a Finnøy farmhouse in the eighteen-month absence of the household's married son.

The mornings were the kitchen. They did the bread on the Tuesdays and the Fridays, the washing on the Mondays, the small mending on whatever afternoons had a needle in them. They walked together to the church on Sundays — the half-mile up the path — and sat in the front pew on the bridegroom's side, which had become the family's pew at Mrs. Hestby's marriage in '58. Pastor Kleven gave the small careful sermons of his old age. Mrs. Hestby's small contralto and Olava's small alto sang the hymns at the second-half register the older Finnøy women had been singing them in for sixty years.

The afternoons varied. Olava spent perhaps two afternoons a week at the writing-desk, doing her letters — to Marit at Lindøy, to Olav's mother's sister at Mosterøy, to a school friend at Stavanger she had been corresponding with since her sixteenth year, and to her own mother across the channel. Mrs. Hestby spent her afternoons at the small repair-work the kitchen always had — a torn apron, a small linen tear, the autumn putting-up of jam from the late berries her sister-in-law brought from Mosterøy — and at her one weekly indulgence, which was the small Lutheran women's-society meeting at Pastor Kleven's wife's parlour on Wednesday afternoons.

The evenings were the kitchen and the parlour. Jens at the parlour with the newspaper; the two women at the kitchen with the small mending or the tea or the conversation the household had been carrying forward across the day. Mrs. Hestby spoke of her own mother (who had died in '64) in the small careful sentences she had learned to use when the mother was not at hand to be addressed; Olava spoke of Marit and Eirik and the small Lindøy news Mrs. Lindøy's letters brought across once a fortnight. They talked, often, about Olav.

Olava had not, before the marriage, understood that Mrs. Hestby would be the one woman in her life with whom she could discuss Olav at the level of detail the marriage required.

She had not understood it because Mrs. Hestby was Olav's mother, and Olava had assumed — by the small Norwegian convention she had grown up under — that a mother-in-law was the woman a wife discussed her husband with last, not first. The assumption had been wrong. The assumption had been wrong because Mrs. Hestby was the woman who had the longest record of Olav-observation on the planet, and because Mrs. Hestby had decided — from a date Olava could not pin down but suspected was the second day after the wedding — that her thirty-five-year record of observing her son was, from that date forward, available to her daughter-in-law without conditions. This was the gift Mrs. Hestby had given Olava without mentioning it, and Olava had begun to use the gift without mentioning it either.

The conversations were not, in their content, the conversations a Norwegian wife of her generation expected to have with her mother-in-law. They were sharper than that, and more particular, and more ranging.

Mrs. Hestby, on the second Wednesday of November, said:

"Olava."

"Yes."

"I am going to tell you a thing about Olav at fifteen."

"Tell."

"At fifteen he came home from the parish school one afternoon with his mouth set in a particular way I had never seen before. He did not speak at supper. He did not speak the next morning. He went out to the boat-shed for the day. He came back at supper and his mouth was the same mouth. I asked him at the kitchen door — when his father was at the parlour and we could speak — what had happened. He said *nothing.* I said *Olav, what happened.* He said *I am not going to tell you.* I said *good. Not telling is also an answer.* He nodded. He went up to the loft. I have not, in the ten years since, asked him what happened that day. He has not, in the ten years, told me. The thing has not, by his face since, troubled him. But I have remembered the kitchen door at fifteen as the day my son became a man with his own piece, and I am telling you now because — when you came up the path with the wool coat in March — I recognized in your face the same kind of small composed knowing-without-asking that had been in my own face at the kitchen door at his fifteenth year. You and I are, I think, both the kind of woman a man like Olav can keep his piece beside without losing himself. I have not, in eighteen months of correspondence with you, said this. I am saying it now in the kitchen at Finnøy because we are alone and because it is a thing I want you to know we share."

Olava did not, for some seconds, answer.

Then she said, in her small composed voice:

"Thank you, Mother."

"You are welcome."

"I have known, from the autumn of '79, that he had a piece. I did not know, at the autumn of '79, that the piece had a name. I know now, by the small careful arithmetic I have been doing since the Christmas Eve of last year, that the piece has a name — perhaps two names, perhaps three. I have not asked him. I will not ask him. The not-asking is the form."

"Yes. I know that is the form."

"You knew."

"I knew at the bench at Lindøy on Christmas Eve last year. I was at the kitchen window that evening when you came up from the back garden after the conversation. I saw your face. I had seen the face on my own face once, in 1858, in November of the year I was engaged to Jens. I knew what the face was."

"Mother."

"Yes."

"What was your November of '58."

Mrs. Hestby did not, for a moment, answer.

She set the cup of tea she was holding down on the table. She looked at the lamp.

"I will tell you," she said. "Once. In the kitchen at Finnøy in November of '83. I will not tell you again. The telling is the only part of the carrying I am willing to spend."

"Tell."

"Jens, in the autumn of '57 when he was twenty-four, took a coastal voyage out of Stavanger that lasted a month. He came back from the voyage in the second week of October. He was different from the man who had left. I had been engaged to him since the previous Christmas. I was nineteen. I asked him, at the second week of being home, what had happened. He said *nothing has happened.* I said *Jens, what happened.* He said *I am not going to tell you.* I said *good. Not telling is also an answer.* He nodded. We were married, on the schedule we had set, in May of '58. We have been married for thirty-five years. He has not, in the thirty-five years, told me. I have not asked him again. The not-asking is the form. The form has held. The marriage has been a good marriage. That is what I know about your November of '58, Olava."

She picked up her tea-cup. She drank from it.

She did not, at the table, say more.

Olava sat with her own cup in her hands for a long minute.

She did not, in the minute, allow herself to ask any of the small questions the speech invited. She had been told what she had been told, and the telling had been the only part Mrs. Hestby was willing to spend, and the spending of any further inquiry would have been to take from her mother-in-law a piece of carrying that was not Olava's to take.

She said:

"Thank you."

"Yes."

"I will not, Mother, ever speak of this again to anyone."

"I know. I would not have told you if I had not known."

"Yes."

The kitchen at Finnøy in November of '83 returned to its ordinary small evening quiet. Jens was, by the next sound from the parlour, about to come in to ask whether the kettle was still warm. The two women at the table had, in the count of perhaps four minutes, exchanged the largest single piece of intimate knowledge that would pass between them in the marriage's first year.

They did not, after the four minutes, speak of it again that evening. Or in the eighteen months. Or in the rest of Mrs. Hestby's life.

But Olava, walking up the stairs at half past ten to her own room and taking out the ledger to make the November entry for the absent letter that would not come from Brooklyn for another three weeks, knew that she had been given, on the second Wednesday of November of '83, the piece of the household's history she had not known she had been waiting for. The piece was the piece that confirmed for her — at the level of one specific Finnøy household across two specific marriages — that the not-asking was the form, that the form held, that the form produced good marriages.

She wrote nothing in the ledger about the conversation.

She wrote, in the small private indent she kept for the things that were for her own reading only:

> *November the eighth. The piece I have been carrying alone since 1879 is, I now know, a piece that has been carried in this house since 1857. I am not the first wife of this family to carry it. I am the second.*

She closed the ledger.

She went to bed.

The Atlantic was running under the *President Daae* somewhere west of Iceland. Olav, by her arithmetic, was at the morning watch. The wind, by her watch on the bedroom mantelpiece, would be a westerly. The bark would be, at this hour, perhaps twelve hundred miles from Brooklyn.

She thought of him at the watch.

She did not, in the thinking, feel particularly alone. The alone-feeling that she had braced for, in the week before he had left, had not arrived. The kitchen had Mrs. Hestby in it. The household had Jens in it. The conversation she had needed to have with Olav about the piece was a conversation she had, by the November Wednesday, had with Olav's mother instead, and the conversation had given her what the marriage's first year had been waiting to be given.

She slept.

The lamp at her bedside went out at eleven by the small clock she had brought from Lindøy.

The house at Finnøy held its three occupants in its ordinary autumn quiet, and the autumn went past, and the first letter from Brooklyn would come — the Brooklyn consul's office had the bark down for arrival in the second week of December — by the middle of January.

She would, at the middle of January, read the letter three times.

She would, between now and then, do the work of two women in a kitchen for the eighteen months of the *President Daae*'s voyage, with the November conversation as the small private foundation that would, she now understood, hold the eighteen months in place.
