The Atlantic crossing on the *President Daae* was fifty-one days.

By the standards of an October-November westbound petroleum bark with a moderately fair-wind voyage and one bad storm in the third week, fifty-one days was, Tellefsen said at the small officer's mess on the morning of the fifty-second, neither fast nor slow. Brun on the *Semiramis* outbound to the Cape had taken roughly the same number of days for a comparable Atlantic leg three years before; the *Daae*'s sister ship, the *Bergen-Atlanterhav,* had taken forty-five on her best westbound and sixty-one on her worst. Fifty-one was the middle of the bark's seven-year average. Thoresen would, by Tellefsen's reading, neither praise the crossing nor complain about it. The fifty-one days had been the fifty-one days.

Olav had been the first mate for fifty-one days.

The fifty-one days had taught him, in pieces, what the trade required of a first mate that the trade had not required of a navigator-common-seaman.

The first thing was the small constant administrative work of the deck. The mate of a deep-water bark was the man who kept the small running record of what the bark had done across each watch — what sail had been set or struck, what corrections had been made to the helm, what small repairs the bosun had ordered, what cargo-shifts had occurred (none, on a petroleum voyage; the barrels stayed where they had been stowed), what supplies had been consumed at the steward's table. The record was the record the captain consulted at noon and that the company offices would consult at the pay-off. The recording of it was a small daily piece of the trade Olav had not, as a common seaman, been responsible for. He learned it. Tellefsen showed him the columns the *Daae*'s log was kept in, the abbreviations Thoresen preferred, the small specific notations the company at Bergen liked to see at the inspection. Olav added the captain's notations to his hand. By the second week he was keeping the log without correction.

The second thing was the small constant social work of the deck. The mate was the man between the captain and the crew. The captain spoke to the mate; the mate spoke to the crew; the crew spoke to the mate; the mate spoke to the captain. The flow went through him. The flow had a register. The register required him to be — at all hours of the watch — the calibrated middle voice of the bark, neither the captain's authority nor the crew's complaint. The voice took practice. Olav, who had at twenty-three been the small careful navigator-on-a-mate's-track on the *Semiramis,* had now, at twenty-five, to be the actual middle voice of a bark whose captain's middle voice was not Brun's. Thoresen's middle voice was sharper than Brun's; the crew was used to a sharper middle voice from the mate position; Olav had to find a middle voice that was sharper than his own without being Tellefsen's. By the third week he had, by trial and error and by the small daily observation of how the crew responded to each of his trial-and-error attempts, found something that worked.

The third thing was the captain.

---

Thoresen was, Olav understood by the second week, the captain who had told him at the first interview that he would be unpleasant and had then proceeded — with a kind of small consistent integrity Olav had not expected — to be exactly that and no more.

The captain shouted at the first mate. The captain had warned that he would. He did. He shouted at Olav, in the second week, when a topsail brace had been left improperly belayed by one of the new common seamen and Olav had not noticed it before the captain had noticed it. The shouting was loud. It went on for perhaps two minutes. The captain's vocabulary was the vocabulary of a Bergen man of his generation who had been at sea for forty years and who had, by the trade, accumulated the small specific catalogue of insults a Norwegian captain used at moments of professional displeasure. The catalogue was not pleasant. It was not, Olav noted at the deck while the shouting was going on, also creative; the captain had a stock of perhaps twenty phrases and he cycled through them in roughly the same order each time. The shouting ended. Olav said *Yes, sir* twice, at the moments at which the captain's pause required it, and then went forward and quietly ordered the brace re-belayed. The crew did the re-belaying without comment. The captain went below.

Tellefsen, that evening at the officer's mess, said:

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"He shouted at you twice in two minutes."

"Yes."

"That is the standard for a first incident. He will, the next time, shout for three. The time after, four. He calibrates upward across the voyage, by his own internal scale, until the seventh or eighth incident at about month four, at which point he plateaus at four-to-five minutes for the rest of the voyage. The plateau is the level. You will hear the plateau perhaps twice a week for the remaining fourteen months."

"Twice a week."

"Twice a week. By the second voyage you sail with him — if you sail with him a second voyage — the plateau will start at the third week and the calibration will be unnecessary. He will know what you can take and you will know what he gives."

"That is the trade."

"That is the trade."

Olav went to his cabin. He wrote in the small bound book:

> *Second-week shouting incident. Two minutes. Tellefsen says the calibration is upward to a plateau of four-to-five at month four. I will, by month four, have heard perhaps twenty-five of these. The body will know how to take them by then.*

He blotted the page. He closed the book.

He understood, at the cabin, that the shouting was not — as he had on the first day half-feared it might be — a thing Thoresen used to break the mate. It was a thing Thoresen used to keep the mate calibrated to the captain's standard. The standard was high. The standard was, by his father's '74 story, also the standard that on a different occasion had broken a man at the foremast for a tin of preserved peaches. But the standard for the first mate was different from the standard for a common seaman who had stolen from the steward. The first mate was being calibrated, not punished. The calibration could be borne. The calibration was, Olav had to admit, working — by the third week his eye for what Thoresen would shout at had sharpened, and by the fourth week the brace incidents had stopped because Olav was catching them at the small initial moment before they became incidents. The shouting was, in the captain's economy, the small daily piece of training he provided his first mate by being the captain he was.

It was not Brun's economy. It was a Thoresen economy. Olav was, at the fourth week, registering that the two could both produce competent first mates and that the costs of each — the slow patient teaching from Brun, the fast loud teaching from Thoresen — were costs the man being trained paid in different currencies.

He paid Thoresen's currency.

---

The third-week storm was the bad one.

It came up off the Grand Banks on a Sunday afternoon — the weather had been turning since the Saturday morning, the barometer had been dropping all that day, Thoresen had ordered the topgallants in at four bells of the Saturday afternoon and the upper topsails reefed at midnight — and by Sunday's eight bells of the morning the wind had built to a seven and was still rising. By two bells of the afternoon it was an eight. By four bells it was a nine. By the end of the watch it was a full North Atlantic gale, with a sea running fifteen feet on the port quarter and the bark down to a single double-reefed main topsail and the foretopmast staysail.

Olav had the deck for the dog-watches. Thoresen took it back at the first night-watch. Olav went below to a half-hour's rest and then came back up at the middle watch. The bark held together. The crew worked. The bosun — a man of fifty-three named Holm who had been at the *Daae* for ten years and was the strongest single piece of the bark's continuity — was at every line that needed him before he was called. The petroleum barrels, which had been Tellefsen's quiet anxiety since the storm had started building (a barrel coming loose in a hold full of barrels was the kind of accident that turned a deep-water bark over in a North Atlantic gale), held in their stowage. The carpenter went down twice during the worst of it to inspect the lashings. The lashings held.

The storm peaked at two bells of the morning watch on the Monday. The wind dropped from a nine to a seven by six bells. By eight bells of the forenoon the bark was making four knots under reefed lower topsails on a sea still running twelve feet but with the wind eased to a steady five.

They had ridden it.

Thoresen, at noon on the Monday, took his sight at the binnacle as if the storm had not happened. He gave the position to Olav. Olav wrote it in the log. The sight put them roughly seven hundred miles from the Brooklyn approach.

The captain, at the binnacle, did not say anything to Olav about how Olav had handled the deck during the dog-watches.

Tellefsen, at the mess that evening, said:

"He did not shout."

"He did not shout."

"That is the captain's praise. The not-shouting after a storm is the captain's praise. He will, on the day we make Brooklyn, write a single line in his notebook about your storm-handling. The line will be brief and it will be favorable. Brun would have told you. Thoresen does not tell. He writes."

"I understand."

"Good. Eat your supper. The remainder of the crossing is — by my reading — eleven days. Brooklyn by the second week of December."

Olav ate his supper.

In his cabin he wrote:

> *Third-week storm. Grand Banks. Full gale through the Sunday night and the Monday morning. Bark held. Crew held. Bosun Holm was where he needed to be at every line. I had the dog-watches; Thoresen had the night and the morning. He did not, at the noon sight, mention the dog-watches. By Tellefsen's reading the not-mentioning is the captain's praise. The notebook will hold the assessment.*

He blotted the page.

He thought, briefly, of Brun on the morning after the North Sea storm in '81 — the noon-sight at the binnacle after eighteen hours on his feet, the small economy of motion. The two captains had, in the eighteen hours after a bad storm, done two different things at the noon-sight. Brun had done the sight as a demonstration of what a master mariner's body knew. Thoresen had done it as a piece of the bark's ordinary work that the storm had not interrupted. The difference was the difference between the two captains' relationship with the trade. Brun loved the trade and showed it in his hands. Thoresen worked the trade and showed it in the absence of any hands at all.

Olav understood, at the cabin, that he would learn more from Thoresen — at twenty-five, at the first-mate's berth, on a petroleum bark on the North Atlantic — than he had perhaps learned from Brun in the eighteen months on the *Semiramis.* The understanding was not a betrayal of Brun. The understanding was that Brun had taught him the things a young navigator needed; Thoresen would teach him the things a soon-to-be captain needed. The two were the trade. He had been lucky in both.

He blew out the lamp.

He slept.

---

The bark made the Brooklyn approach on the morning of the eighth of December.

The pilot came aboard at the Sandy Hook bar — a thin red-bearded American with a Brooklyn-Norwegian accent that Olav understood at once as the speech of a Bergen man who had emigrated young and had been on the Hook pilots' boat for twenty years. He took the bark up through the Narrows. The day was cold and grey; the harbor was full; New York rose on the port hand and Brooklyn on the starboard, and the sheer size of the city Olav had read about at the Asiatic Society library at Calcutta was a thing that — like the Cape Town mountain in July of '81 — exceeded the description.

The bark warped in to the petroleum wharf at Red Hook on the late tide.

Olav stood at the rail as the lines went over. He looked at the city.

Brooklyn was, from the petroleum wharf, a long low industrial waterfront with the wooden warehouses and the brick storage buildings and the small smoke-stacks of a working harbor in the year the petroleum trade had come to define what the harbor did. Beyond the waterfront the city rose in roofs and church-spires and the high commercial buildings of the new American century. The smell on the wind was the smell of petroleum — sharper than the *Daae*'s own internal smell because the Brooklyn refineries on either side of the wharf were burning off their gases in the small constant flares that the wharf men had told Tellefsen, on a previous voyage, would either kill them all in their sleep one night or run forever.

He registered the city.

He thought, at the rail, of Olava at the kitchen at Finnøy at this hour. He thought of the letter he would write at the consul's office at Bergen Avenue tomorrow. He thought of the months ahead.

Thoresen came past him on the way to the gangway.

"Hestby."

"Captain."

"Three weeks at Brooklyn. Discharge starts tomorrow. You and Tellefsen will alternate the deck. I will be ashore most days at the company office and the agents'. You will see me at the supper table."

"Yes, sir."

"You will write home from Bergen Avenue tomorrow. The consul keeps the mail-bag. The company runner takes it to the docks at five."

"Yes, sir."

"Hestby."

"Sir."

"You handled the storm."

He went down the gangway.

That was, Olav understood at the rail, the spoken praise the captain had not given on the Monday morning. He had now given it. It had been four words. It would, by Tellefsen's earlier reading, have to last Olav until the next storm.

Olav went to his cabin.

He wrote two sentences in the small bound book.

> *Brooklyn, 8 Dec 1883. Storm-handling acknowledged in four words at the gangway.*

He blotted the page.

He went up to find Tellefsen and to begin the small administrative work of a first mate at his first foreign port.
