The first six months of Olav and Olava's marriage were the small slow months a Norwegian island couple of their generation lived through at the beginning of a marriage.

They settled at Finnøy. The arrangement Mrs. Lindøy had been negotiating with Olav's mother since the previous autumn had given them the smaller of the two upstairs bedrooms at Olav's father's house — the room Olav had slept in until he was sixteen, before he had moved to the loft — and his mother had, in February, repainted the walls and put in the small writing-desk Olava had asked for under the window. The room was small. It had its window onto the garden. It would do for a year, at minimum, while Olav and his father worked out the small piece of land at the south side of the property where, in the autumn, Olav and his uncle would begin building the cottage that would, by the spring of '85 or thereabouts, become the household's own house.

The cottage-plan had been agreed at Easter — at a Sunday afternoon at the table — by Olav and his father and his uncle, with Olava and his mother and the captain (over from Lindøy for the holiday) at the table for the conversation. The captain had contributed half the timber from a small woodlot he held on Mosterøy. Olav's uncle had contributed the labor of the spring-fall season at his own carpenter's wage halved. Olav had contributed the savings from the *Semiramis* pay-off — a thousand and forty-two kroner in cash — and the small share of the coastal smack at Lindøy that the captain had transferred to him on the wedding day's afternoon, which Olav and his father had agreed they would not work themselves but would lease back to the Lindøy crew at the standard share-rate. The cottage would not be a large cottage. It would have three rooms, a kitchen, a small loft for the children that would, at first, be a storeroom. The plan was the plan.

Olav worked, that spring, with his father at the boats.

His father had taken on, in the autumn while Olav had still been at Lindøy, two small repair jobs and one new build — the new build a small four-oared boat for a Mosterøy fisherman who had been waiting two years for Jens to have the time. Olav joined the work in the second week of April. He worked, that first morning, the way a son who had been at sea for fifteen months worked when he came back to a boat-shed: slowly, with deliberation, allowing his hands to remember what they had not done since June of '81. By the end of the first week his hands had remembered. By the end of the third week his father had stopped, at the supper, mentioning that Olav had taken the morning's work in his stride. By the end of the second month Jens had started giving Olav the planning work on the new build, which was the work — Olav had understood by the end of the first week — Jens had been waiting eighteen months to give him.

The Mosterøy boat was finished in the third week of June.

Olav launched her with his uncle at the small jetty at the south end of Finnøy on the morning of the twenty-sixth. The fisherman — an old man of about seventy named Aanestad whom Olav had known since his confirmation — paid the balance of the price in coins counted out at the boat-shed table. He took the boat across to Mosterøy on the afternoon's tide, with Olav at the tiller for the run across and his fourteen-year-old grandson at the bow. The boy had been sent across that morning specifically to learn the boat's particulars from Olav. Olav showed him. The boy listened. By the time they reached the Mosterøy jetty the boy had the boat's particulars in his head and Olav had — without having quite registered the moment of the becoming — become a Finnøy boatbuilder of the kind his father was, which is to say a man who built boats for old men and taught the particulars to fourteen-year-old boys at the launching.

He came home to Finnøy that evening on Aanestad's son-in-law's smack. Olava was at the jetty.

She did not, since the wedding, hold his face at the jetty. She had moved, at the wedding, from the hand-on-face-at-the-jetty register to a different daily register: a small touch on the forearm, the kind of brief contact a Norwegian wife of her generation gave a husband at the moment he came back to the household from a day's work elsewhere on the island. She gave him the touch on the twenty-sixth. She walked him up the path.

"How was the launch."

"It went well. The boy will be a sailor."

"Aanestad paid."

"Aanestad paid."

"Your father will be pleased."

"He will be pleased."

"There is bread."

"Good."

This was the small marriage-conversation of their summer. It was, Olav understood by the second week, the conversation a marriage made of itself by repetition: the small arrival, the small noting, the small offering of the kitchen's news. It was not what either of them had imagined a marriage being made of when they had been engaged. It was, by the third week, what they had decided it was — the small daily work of living in two halves of the same household — and the not-being-what-they-had-imagined was, for both of them, an acceptable substitution for the imagining.

---

Olava, that spring and summer, was at the small business of setting up the household.

She had come to Finnøy with two trunks of things she had been packing at Lindøy through the engagement. She had her clothes, her small linens for the household, the leather folder that held all of Olav's letters from the voyage, the ledger she had kept across the eighteen months, and a small wooden chest of items her grandmother had given her at Christmas that were the small inheritance a Lindøy daughter brought to the household at her marriage — a brass candlestick of '40s vintage, two pewter spoons her grandfather had bought at Stavanger in 1854, a small embroidered linen cloth her grandmother had embroidered as a girl in 1841. The chest sat in the small room she and Olav now slept in. She had unpacked the trunks in the first week. The chest she unpacked slowly, over the spring, item by item.

She put the candlestick on the bedroom mantelpiece. She put the spoons in the kitchen drawer where the rest of the household's spoons were, with the small explicit permission of Olav's mother, who understood the gesture and accepted it. She put the embroidered cloth on the bedroom dresser. She put the leather folder and the ledger in the lower drawer of the small writing-desk under the window, which became, by quiet agreement, her drawer.

She did not, in the spring or the summer, put anything of hers into the boat-shed.

This had not been discussed. It had been understood. The boat-shed was Olav's father's and Olav's; the kitchen and the upstairs were Mrs. Hestby's and Olava's. The dividing line — Olav had registered, at the second week — was the line a Norwegian island household of his generation had drawn between the men's spaces and the women's, and the line had been drawn by the women across centuries of small careful negotiation that no one in the household needed to comment on. Olava's drawer was at the desk. Olav's drawer — the one he had told her about at the bench — was at the workbench. The two drawers were thirty feet apart and had agreed, by the marriage's small unstated arrangement, not to know each other.

---

By the end of August the summer's small pattern had become the household's pattern.

The marriage at six months was the marriage neither of them had imagined and that both of them were, by the August evenings at the kitchen table with the door open onto the garden, willing to say to each other was a marriage. The cottage's foundation had been broken by Olav's uncle in the second week of August — the four corner-stones laid, the small ceremony with the captain over from Lindøy for the laying, the cellar dug by the end of the month. The Mosterøy fisherman's son who had been at the launch in June had come to the boat-shed twice with his grandfather and had, by his second visit, asked Jens whether Jens would teach him to caulk. Jens had said *come on the Saturday after harvest.* The boy was coming.

Olav, at the boat-shed on the last Saturday of August, looked across at his father at the workbench and understood that the summer had been the summer of his apprenticeship to the man his father was, which was a different apprenticeship from the one he had served at sixteen and had a different kind of usefulness in him. He was, on the last Saturday of August at twenty-five, a boatbuilder of the parish.

He did not, in the understanding, say so.

He went on with the work.

He thought, without permitting the thought to expand, that the next thing was going to be the letter. The letter would come, by his rough September calendar, sometime in the first or second week of the month.

He had not told Olava he was expecting it.

He suspected she was, by her own arithmetic, expecting it too.

Neither of them mentioned it through the rest of August.

The summer ended on the small quiet last day of the month at the kitchen table, with the lamp lit at six and the supper of cold mutton and bread and his mother's pickled cabbage, and the four of them — Jens, his mother, Olava, Olav — at the table doing the ordinary supper of an island household at the close of an ordinary good summer.

The autumn was a week away.

The letter would come with it.
