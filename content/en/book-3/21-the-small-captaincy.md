In the autumn of 1888 Olav took his captain's ticket at Stavanger.

The small examination the Stavanger marine office administered to candidates for the coastal-captain's ticket had been, by the small specific arithmetic Olav had been keeping, the small professional step he had been walking toward since the *Daae* pay-off in April of '85. He had, across the two and a half years since the homecoming, done the small specific accumulation of coastal-mate hours the ticket required. The *Solveig* runs he had been making with his uncle counted. The small specific chart-work he had been doing in the small off-hours at the cottage counted. The Thoresen notebook entry — which had, at the *Daae* pay-off, been filed at the company office at Bergen and was on the public side of the marine office's records — counted. The small specific Brun recommendation from the *Semiramis* pay-off of '82 also counted.

The Stavanger marine office looked at the four pieces of documentation.

They said the ticket would be issued.

The examination was the small formality — a two-hour oral at the marine-office's small board-room with three examiners, on coastal navigation, on the small specific Norwegian coastal regulations, on the small specific responsibilities of a coastal captain — and Olav passed it at the pace a man with his specific documentation was expected to pass it. The ticket was signed on the second Monday of October, 1888. It was the small paper folded into the small leather folder at the cottage that evening, alongside the Stavanger certificate of 1880 and the Asiatic Society card and the small Cape Town Alm card and the small papers of the *Semiramis* and *Daae* voyages.

The folder, by the small arithmetic of the cottage's writing-desk drawer, was now seven papers thick.

---

He did not, in the autumn of '88, take a captain's berth.

He was not ready to. The *Solveig* runs with his uncle were the right work for the small household's years when Anna was two and half, when the second child — a son, named Jens after Olav's father, born in the spring of '88 — was six months old. The small coastal smack was the shape of a married man's trade at twenty-nine with a wife of twenty-eight and two children under three. He had, at thirty, no specific intention of shifting out of the shape.

The ticket was, by his small specific accounting of it, the small career paper that permitted the shift when the shift became the right shift. The shift would, by his expectation, come in some year between '92 and '95. By '95 he would be thirty-six, Anna would be nine, Jens would be seven, Olava would be thirty-five. The small cottage household would be, by that year, settled enough that the small captain's absence at a coastal run of the *Stavanger–Bergen* type would be bearable. The *Solveig*, by that year, would have been run for ten years and would be approaching the refit point at which his uncle — then sixty-six or sixty-seven — would want to step back from the owner-captain role.

The ticket would, in '95 or thereabouts, be exercised.

Now, at '88, it was simply the paper in the folder.

---

Olava, at the small parlour-corner on the evening of the Monday Olav had brought the ticket home, said:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"The ticket."

"The ticket."

"You will not, for some years, use it."

"I will not, for some years, use it. I have, on the way back from Stavanger today, decided the year I will use it. The year is '95 or thereabouts. The reasoning is the small domestic arithmetic."

"'95."

"'95."

"That gives us seven years of your being at the *Solveig* with your uncle."

"Seven years."

"I am — by the small domestic arithmetic my own side of the household does — in agreement with the '95 reasoning. I had been doing the same arithmetic since the birth of Jens in the spring. We have reached the same figure."

"Then the '95 figure."

"The '95 figure."

"Good."

She did not, at the parlour-corner, say more.

She picked up her small sewing — she had been, at the first autumn evenings, sewing a small winter-coat for Anna out of wool Mrs Lindøy had sent across from Lindøy the previous week — and she continued.

Olav, at the small writing-desk, opened the leather folder and added the small captain's ticket to the seven other papers.

The folder closed at eight papers.

The seven years between '88 and '95 went at the small specific pace a Norwegian island household of their period's seven central years went at.

Anna, from two and a half at the ticket to nine at the *Stavanger–Bergen* berth, grew. She walked to the parish school by six, as Olav had. She learned to read, in Olava's small parish-school letter-mornings, by five and a half — an early reader, Olava noted at the small private indent of the ledger she had been keeping for the household's children. She had her grandfather's dark hair still. She had her mother's eyes. She had, by the small specific family observation across the years, her father's quiet at the supper table — the small specific not-speaking-until-she-had-something-to-say that Olav had been practising since he was Anna's age.

Jens — Jens the younger, named for Olav's father — was from six months to seven and a half across the same years. He was a different child. He was louder. He laughed more. He had Olava's fair hair — the same small specific fair Mrs Lindøy had been born with, which Olava had grown out of by eight — and he had, by four, the small specific pointer's eye for the channel from the kitchen window that the men of the family had since Peder Hestbye's day in the 1790s. Jens the younger was, by the small specific observation of the grandfathers, going to be a sailor.

A third child came in the spring of '91 — a girl, Ingrid, named for Olava's Lindøy aunt who had died in '76 (the aunt from whose register Olava had inherited her small-not-filling-the-silence discipline, per the small November '83 conversation at Mrs Hestby's kitchen). Ingrid was the small household's third small child. Olava had, at the delivery, had a harder labor than she had with either of the two before — the birth was ten hours — and Mrs Pettersen at the bedside had, at the end of the ten hours, said quietly to Olava that the third child would, by Mrs Pettersen's experience, be the last for this mother if the mother was to remain well. Olava had received the small opinion with the small composed nod of a woman who had heard what she had already suspected. The household agreed — Olav and Olava between them, at the small bedside the next morning — that Ingrid would be the third and last. Olav took the small specific precaution Mrs Hestby had explained to him at the parlour of Jens's house on the small evening after Ingrid's birth, in the small specific way a Norwegian married man of his generation took such precautions. The precaution, once explained, was a kept precaution.

The three children — Anna, Jens the younger, Ingrid — filled the small cottage.

The small cottage, by '93, was too small.

In the spring of '93 Olav and his uncle and his father began the small two-year work of building the larger house. The larger house — by Olav's and Olava's plan, worked out at the small parlour-corner in the '92-'93 winter — would be built on the same small share of land the cottage sat on, at the north end, with the cottage remaining as the small secondary house for the maintenance of the household's small overflow-purposes. The larger house would have four bedrooms. It would have a small proper kitchen. It would have a small writing-room — a room Olava had explicitly added to the plan at her own request, which was the small specific addition of a Norwegian island wife of her generation who had, across the ten years of keeping the household, decided that her small small-parish-school-teaching would continue into her middle years and would, by her request, have its own room at the new house.

The larger house was finished in the spring of '95.

The household moved in on the second Saturday of May.

On the second Monday of May of '95 — three days after the move into the larger house — Olav went to Stavanger on the morning coaster.

He had been, across the winter of '94-'95, in small specific correspondence with a Stavanger shipping firm — Houlder & Sønn, the firm that had in '81 offered him the second-mate berth on the *Aase Marie* Bergen-Hamburg run he had declined in favor of the *Semiramis.* Houlder & Sønn were now under the second generation of the family; the senior son who had signed Olav's declined-offer letter in '81 had died in '89, and the younger son — Helge Houlder — was running the firm. Helge Houlder had written Olav in the autumn of '94 to ask whether Olav was at a point in his career where a Stavanger–Bergen captaincy might be of interest.

The berth was on a bark called the *Helga* of Stavanger — a small nine-hundred-ton bark, slightly smaller than the *President Daae,* that ran a regular Stavanger–Bergen cargo route with timber and coal and small manufactured goods, with twelve trips a year, each trip six to eight days. The captain of the *Helga* for the previous seven years had retired in the autumn of '94. Houlder had been running her with an acting captain for the winter's six trips. He wanted a permanent captain from the spring of '95 onward.

He had, he said in the letter, been given Olav's name by the small Stavanger shipping community's quiet records. Captain Brun of the *Semiramis,* now seventy and retired at his Bergen house, had — Houlder wrote — recommended Olav when Houlder had written him in September.

The berth's wages were three hundred kroner a month.

The small Stavanger-Bergen round ran six days at sea per round, twelve rounds a year — seventy-two days a year at sea, with the balance at Stavanger and at home at Finnøy. The captain was at home on the small specific Norwegian-island schedule of a *coastwise*-captain of the Norwegian fleet: about twenty-two days at home between each six-day run, with the small family routine of a married captain on a regular route.

Olav came home from Stavanger on the Tuesday with the small berth offer in his coat pocket.

He sat with Olava at the small writing-room at the new house that evening.

They read the offer together.

Olava said, at the end of the second reading:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"This is the '95 berth."

"This is the '95 berth."

"Six days at sea. Twenty-two at home."

"Six at sea. Twenty-two at home."

"Twelve rounds a year."

"Twelve rounds."

"Wages."

"Three hundred kroner. Plus the small captain's bonus of the owner-share arrangement, which at the *Helga*'s historical round is about seven hundred a year."

"Three-six a year."

"Three-six."

"Olav."

"Yes."

"I am — by my arithmetic — in favor."

"Yes."

"The year is '95. The year we planned is '95. The berth's shape is the shape we planned. The wages are the wages the household needs for the larger house's remaining small finishing and for the children's education across the coming ten years. The six-at-sea schedule is the small schedule a married captain of your kind should have."

"Yes."

"I would be, on the evening you come home from each six-day round, at the small jetty."

"Yes."

"The jetty will be a small specific routine. Twelve times a year. The small wool coat, by then perhaps replaced by the next wool coat, will be at the jetty at the small evenings the small specific bark comes up the channel. That is the small shape of our marriage's middle fifteen years, if the *Helga* is the berth you take."

"That is the shape."

"I am in favor."

"Good."

He took the small offer letter. He wrote, at the small writing-room desk, the small careful captain's-acceptance letter to Helge Houlder. He signed it *Captain Olav Hestby.*

It was the first time he had signed his name with the captain's title.

He blotted the page.

He looked at the signature.

He passed the letter to Olava.

She looked at it.

She said:

"Captain Olav Hestby."

"Captain Olav Hestby."

"The signature is good."

"Thank you."

"You will sign it this way at every captain's document across the next fifteen years."

"Yes."

"Good."

She gave him back the letter. He folded it. He sealed it. He put it in the small outgoing tray the larger house's writing-room now had.

The letter would go down on the Friday coaster.

The first *Helga* round would lift Stavanger on the second Wednesday of June, by Houlder's small planning.

---

That evening at the bedside — in the larger house's main bedroom, which was different from the cottage's bedroom but had the same small married-bed-frame the household had been moving across its three addresses since '83 — Olav lay beside Olava in the small quiet.

She said, after the lamp had been turned down:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"The signature at the letter."

"Yes."

"Captain Olav Hestby."

"Yes."

"The name closes the loop that Brun opened at the customhouse stairs at Calcutta in '82."

"It does."

"Thirteen years between Brun's speech and the signature."

"Thirteen years."

"Brun is seventy. He will, by the small Bergen paper my father brings across from Stavanger on the Wednesdays, see the small notice of Houlder's new *Helga* captain in the next week's shipping news. The small notice will name you. Brun will see it. Brun will, by the small notebook he kept on the *Semiramis* bookshelf — which was, by your '82 telling, the kind of notebook he kept on — read it as the closing of the loop he opened in October of '81 at the first interview."

"Yes."

"The loop has closed."

"The loop has closed."

"Olav."

"Yes."

"I am — in my small private indent — proud."

"Olava."

"Yes."

"I am, in my small private register, also proud. Not of the signature. Of the thirteen years between the signature and the speech. Of the family that kept the household at Finnøy across those years. Of the two of us at the small parlour-corner for the '95 arithmetic. Of the three children in the bedrooms down the hall."

"Olav."

"Yes."

"Put the register away for the night. Sleep."

"I will."

"Goodnight, captain."

"Goodnight, Olava."

They slept.

The house at Finnøy on the second Monday of May of '95 held its five occupants — Olav, Olava, Anna (nine), Jens the younger (seven), Ingrid (four) — in the small specific quiet a Norwegian island household held on the night the husband had signed his first captain's document as a captain.

The small Stavanger-Bergen round would lift at the second Wednesday of June.

The new shape of the trade had, at the signature, begun.

The loop had closed.

The loop's next one — the small captain's years, '95 to '10 by Olav's rough projection — was ahead.
