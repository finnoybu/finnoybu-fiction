The long letter from Brooklyn arrived at Lindøy on the second Tuesday of February, 1885.

It came up the path from the postman in the same way every other Brooklyn letter had come up the path through the eighteen months of the *President Daae*'s voyage. It was on the morning post; the postman handed it to Mrs. Hestby at the door because Olava had walked over to Lindøy the previous afternoon for the small midweek visit she had been making to her mother every other week through the winter, and Mrs. Hestby had, on the small careful Hestby-Lindøy domestic protocol, accepted the letter on her daughter-in-law's behalf with the small understanding that the letter would travel across the channel by the next coaster to Lindøy and would reach Olava before the kitchen at Lindøy had served its noon dinner.

The packet was heavier than the previous letters had been. Mrs. Hestby registered the weight in her hand at the door. She did not, by her standing discipline of not-pressing, open it. She wrapped it in a small clean square of linen, put it in the small leather wallet Olav had used for his school papers as a boy, and gave the wallet to Aanestad's grandson — the boy who had taken the boat from Olav at the launch in June of '83 and had since become, at fifteen, the small reliable Finnøy-Mosterøy-Lindøy boatman the household used for small parcels — and she sent the boy across the channel on the eleven o'clock smack.

The boy reached the Lindøy jetty at twenty past noon.

Olava was at the jetty.

She had, by the small Lindøy intuition the household kept for Tuesday mail-arrivals, walked down to the jetty fifteen minutes earlier than the smack's expected arrival because Mrs. Hestby's dispatch of a small parcel by the morning smack from Finnøy was, in the household's vocabulary of small signals, the dispatch the household used when a Brooklyn letter had arrived at Finnøy and Olava was at Lindøy.

The boy gave her the wallet.

She gave him a small five-øre coin from her pocket. He thanked her. He went back to the smack.

She walked up the path to the house with the wallet under her arm.

---

She did not, on the walk up, open the wallet.

She had decided, by the small standing discipline of her own, that the long letters of the voyage — and the wallet's weight told her at the jetty that this was a long letter — were to be opened at the writing-desk in her old Lindøy bedroom (which Mrs. Lindøy had kept for her exactly as it had been at the engagement, with the writing-desk under the window and the small leather folder on the shelf), not at the kitchen door or in the parlour.

She came up the path. She greeted her mother at the door. She told Mrs. Lindøy that the wallet contained the Brooklyn letter and that she would be at the writing-desk for the next hour and that she would come down to the kitchen at the small bell Mrs. Lindøy rang at one-thirty for the noon-dinner. Mrs. Lindøy nodded. She went back to her stove.

Olava went up the stairs.

In the old bedroom she sat at the desk. She unwrapped the linen. She broke the wafer with her thumbnail.

The packet unfolded into six sheets.

She did not, for some seconds, do anything.

She had not, in the eighteen months of the voyage, received a single letter from Olav longer than three sheets. Most had been one sheet. The Cape Town letter of the first voyage had been two. The Bowery letter — though she had not known it had been a Bowery letter; the small careful version Olav had sent had described only that he had been at Manhattan on company business and had been delayed and had come back to the bark — had been one and a half. Six sheets was, by the small particular vocabulary of his letters, the kind of letter she had not been receiving and had not, by the small calibration she had been keeping, been expecting.

She read the letter.

She read it once at the careful slow pace she used for the first reading.

She read it twice at the slightly faster pace she used for the second.

She read it the third time at the slowest pace she had ever used for any of his letters.

The third reading took perhaps forty minutes.

She did not, at the third reading, look up from the desk.

When she had finished it she set the six sheets flat on the desk in their original order. She put her hands in her lap. She looked at the window.

She did not, for a long time, do anything.

---

The small bell rang at one-thirty for the noon-dinner.

She did not, at the bell, immediately stand. She sat for another minute. Then she stood. She went to the small wash-stand in the corner of the bedroom and she washed her face — not because she had been crying, because she had not, but because the long reading had given her a small flushed feeling at the temples that the cold water at the wash-stand would clear — and she went down to the kitchen.

Mrs. Lindøy did not, at the table, ask about the wallet.

The captain — who had come in from the boat-shed — was at the table with his good hand on his coffee. Marit was at the school in Mosterøy on a Tuesday. Eirik was at his father's office at Stavanger; he and Marit kept a small Mosterøy household now, and Marit was teaching half-days at the Mosterøy primary school where she had begun in the autumn of '84.

Olava sat at the table. She ate the small boiled fish and the bread and the potatoes Mrs. Lindøy had made.

She did not, during the meal, mention the letter.

The captain did not ask. Mrs. Lindøy did not ask. The household had, by the long careful Norwegian practice of the parents of married daughters, decided that a Tuesday Brooklyn letter that had taken Olava forty minutes to read at the writing-desk was a letter the kitchen would not press her on. She would, in her own time, tell them what she would tell them.

After the meal Olava helped Mrs. Lindøy with the dishes.

After the dishes she said, at the kitchen sink, drying her hands on the cloth:

"Mother."

"Yes."

"I am going to take the three o'clock smack back to Finnøy."

"Today?"

"Today. The letter is the kind of letter that requires me to be at my own desk to answer it. The desk at Finnøy is where the ledger is. I do not — for this letter — want to make the entry at the Lindøy desk."

"You would have, ordinarily, stayed through Friday."

"I will come back Friday. I will be at Lindøy for the Sunday."

"All right. Take Aanestad's grandson on the smack with you. He is at the harbor; he was going across this afternoon anyway. Tell him I will pay his fare."

"Yes."

"Olava."

"Yes."

"Is Olav well."

"Olav is well. The letter is not a letter about a thing that has gone wrong. It is a letter about a thing he has decided to tell me. He is well. The crossing is going well. The bark is at Brooklyn and lifts in the third week of January for the homeward."

"Then you will go."

"I will go."

She kissed her mother on the cheek — a small kiss, the kind she gave at the small ordinary departures of the household — and she went up to the bedroom to pack the small overnight bag she had brought across on the Monday.

She wrapped the six sheets in the linen. She put them in the wallet. She put the wallet in the bag.

She went down to the harbor.

---

By half past five she was at the kitchen at Finnøy.

Mrs. Hestby was at the stove. Jens was at the parlour with the Stavanger newspaper. The kitchen was warm — the small evening warmth of the Hestby household at half past five on a February evening — and Mrs. Hestby looked up at her as she came in.

"You came back."

"I came back."

"The letter required it."

"The letter required it."

"Have your supper, then go up."

"I will."

She had her supper at the small table. She did not, at the supper, mention the letter to Mrs. Hestby in any particulars. She said only that the letter was longer than the others had been and that she wanted to take some hours at the writing-desk that evening to make the ledger entry.

Mrs. Hestby nodded. She did not ask further.

After the supper Olava went up to her own room. She lit the lamp at the writing-desk. She opened the small drawer where the new ledger lived — the one she had bought at the Stavanger bookbinder in October of '83, the one she had been keeping for the *Daae* voyage — and she opened it to the page she had prepared for January '85.

She wrote, at the page's top, in her ordinary ledger-hand:

> *Brooklyn letter received 10 February 1885 by the morning post at Finnøy, brought across to Lindøy by Aanestad's grandson on the eleven o'clock smack. Six sheets. Posted 7 January Brooklyn per the consul's stamp. Five weeks in transit, by the Cunard's Saturday. Hand consistent with the Cape homeward hand of the Semiramis voyage of '82 — a hand he uses when he is writing to me from a desk where he has decided, before sitting down, to write the kind of letter he is going to write.*

She stopped the pen.

She considered.

She wrote, below, in the small slightly different hand she used for the entries that were the ledger's body rather than its index:

> *The letter is six paragraphs across six sheets. Each paragraph is a description of a man who has been at the bark with him for the eighteen months. The captain (Thoresen — described in a paragraph that is the most extended description of any captain he has given me in any letter in his life). The mate (Tellefsen — generous, second-rank). The bosun (Holm — judgement). The steward (Hov — devotion, with a story about a Cornish whiskey-bottle that he had not, in any earlier letter, told me). And himself — a paragraph on himself which is the paragraph the letter was written for.*
>
> *The paragraph on himself names — for the first time in any letter or conversation between us — that he has, by the eighteen months and by the assessments of three captains and a bosun and a notebook on a bookshelf, become the kind of first mate who will be a captain before he is forty. He says the captaincy is the trade he has walked into. He says the trade has done it to him. He says he will not, in our marriage, have the option of not being a captain. He says the man who comes home in April will be a different man from the man who left in October of '83.*
>
> *He says — in the letter's last paragraph — that the new outline is larger than the outline he had agreed at the engagement to bring home. He says he is giving me the new outline because he is not, by the marriage's discipline, going to be able to give me the larger pieces at the kitchen table. He says the form of the marriage holds. He says the not-asking holds. He says the captaincy is the new piece. He gives it to me in the letter.*
>
> *He has, by my reading, given me what he can.*

She stopped the pen.

She considered for a long time.

She wrote, below, in the small private indent she kept for the things that were for her own reading only:

> *The letter has done a thing the previous letters have not done. The previous letters have honored the engagement's discipline by limiting their content to what could be said in the small ordinary first-mate's voice. This letter has expanded the discipline by extending it to a different voice. The different voice has not broken the discipline; it has — in fact — re-articulated the discipline at a larger scale. The marriage's not-asking is now, by the long letter, the not-asking about a larger thing. He has not given me the names. He has not given me the contents of the drawer. He has given me only the captaincy. The captaincy is a thing the marriage can hold openly. The captaincy is the piece he is offering as the only piece he is in a position to offer at this stage of the eighteen months.*
>
> *I am to receive the captaincy and to extend the discipline to its larger scale. I am to be the wife of the man whose career has the new shape. I am to be that wife in addition to being the wife of the man with the drawer in the boat-shed and the two letters and the third letter that may yet come and the small private interior life I have agreed for some years not to ask about. The captaincy is the addition. I am to add it to what I have already been.*
>
> *I will. I am the wife who has been adding the new shapes since November 1880. I will add this one. I will add it tonight by writing the reply, and I will add it in the spring at the kitchen table when he comes home, and I will add it across the years at the small daily kitchen table at the cottage when he is at home between berths, and I will add it across the long letters from foreign ports at the berths.*
>
> *The letter has been a gift. It has also been the smallest possible request the letter could have made of me. The smallest possible request is the request the marriage's discipline allows him to make. He has made it. I will give him the answer.*

She blotted the page.

She closed the ledger.

She put the ledger back in the drawer.

She went to the small folder where Olav's letters from the *Daae* lived. She opened the folder. She added the six sheets to the back of the folder, in the small careful order they had arrived in. The folder was, by this addition, the thickest it had been in the three years since she had bought it at the Stavanger bookbinder in '80.

She closed the folder.

She put it back on the shelf.

---

She wrote the reply across the next two evenings.

She did not, this time, write a short reply. Her usual replies to his letters had been short by the small calibration she had set herself in '81 — six lines or so, the small wife's-news, the notation of the receiving — and the small calibration had been the right calibration for the small careful first mate's voice his letters had been written in. The long letter from Brooklyn had been written in a different voice, and the reply, by the small symmetry the marriage's correspondence had been operating on for three years, had to be in a voice that matched.

The reply she wrote was three sheets.

She wrote, on the first sheet, the small ordinary acknowledgment of the receiving — the date, the smack, the wallet, the third reading at forty minutes. She wrote that the letter was, in her own ledger's notation, *the longest single thing you have ever written to me in your life.* She wrote that she had given it the ledger entry it deserved.

She wrote, on the second sheet, the answer to the captaincy. She said that she had — across the three years of the engagement and the one and a half of the marriage — been adding the new shapes the small careful arrival of new evidence had asked her to add, and that the captaincy was the next shape, and that she was adding it. She said the addition was not a thing she was doing reluctantly; she said she had, in some part of herself she had not previously been able to articulate, been waiting since the November-Monday signing of '80 for the captaincy to arrive as the piece of him she had not yet been able to add. She said she had, by the long letter, now added it.

She wrote, on the third sheet, the note about how the captaincy would change the household. She said that the cottage Olav's uncle had built across the spring of '84 and finished in the autumn would be, by the small careful arithmetic she had been doing across the winter, the household's main residence rather than its small honeymoon-house — that she had, since the Christmas letter of '84 in which Olav had described the cottage's roof going on, been quietly preparing for the cottage to be the household of a captain at intervals at home rather than a household of a husband always at home. She said she had moved her writing-desk in her head from the small upstairs bedroom at Mrs. Hestby's to the small parlour-corner at the cottage where the desk would, by the spring's planning, sit. She said she had moved the writing-desk in her head because she had understood, since the autumn, that the cottage was going to be the household the captaincy required, and the writing-desk was the household's architectural anchor.

She wrote, in the closing paragraph:

> *I am, by my own reading of your letter, the wife of a captain-to-be. I have known since the engagement that you were on the captain's path. I have known it the way a woman ashore knows what she has agreed to before she has the words for what she has agreed to. I have, since the engagement, been the wife of a captain-to-be without knowing the phrase for what I was. The phrase has, by the long letter, arrived. I will, from the receiving of the long letter forward, use the phrase for myself. I will not use it aloud at the Finnøy kitchen or at the Lindøy kitchen or at the cottage's parlour-corner; the phrase is not the kind of phrase the kitchens are going to use. I will use it for myself.*
>
> *Come home, captain-to-be. The cottage will be ready. The kitchen will be warm. The desk will be at the parlour-corner. The two of us will be the two of us we are going to be for the rest of the marriage's years.*
>
> *Yours, Olava.*

She blotted the page.

She folded the three sheets into a single packet. She sealed it with the red wafer. She addressed it to *Mr. Olav Hestby, c/o the Norwegian Consul, Brooklyn, New York* — the *President Daae* would still be at the wharf for the third week of January when the letter was posted, and Wexelsen would hold the letter at the consul's office for Olav's collection at the morning's mail.

She did not write *bark* on the cover. She wrote *Mr. Olav Hestby.*

The omission — the omission of *first mate, bark President Daae* — was the omission a wife made when she was writing to her husband at the moment in his career at which the bark was about to stop being his title and the husbandship was about to become it. The omission was, by her ledger's small private indent, the small private signal she had decided to send him at the moment of the long letter's reply.

She put the letter in the small tray by the door.

It would go down to Stavanger on the Friday coaster and on the Cunard from Liverpool and would be at Wexelsen's office in Brooklyn by the second week of March, two weeks before the *Daae* lifted for the homeward.

---

She turned the lamp down.

She lay in the bed in her small upstairs room.

She did not, at the bed, allow herself to think further about the long letter. She had thought about it through the third reading and through the ledger entry and through the small private indent and through the three-sheet reply and through the small omission on the cover. The thinking had been enough. The further thinking would have been, by her own discipline, the kind of thinking that turned a letter received and answered into a letter that became its own obsession across the weeks following.

She did not, this voyage, want the obsession.

She had been the wife of a captain-to-be by her own reading of the long letter for some hours.

She would, in eight weeks, be the wife of a captain-to-be at the kitchen table at Finnøy when he came home.

She slept.

The lamp at her bedside burned out at half past eleven.

The house at Finnøy held its three occupants — Mrs. Hestby in the back room, Jens in the side bed, Olava in the small upstairs room with the leather folder of letters on the shelf and the new ledger in the drawer and the small leather wallet of the long letter at the writing-desk where it would, in the morning, be filed into the folder with the other six-sheet packets of the *Daae* voyage.

The voyage was, by her ledger's small careful arithmetic, eight weeks from its end.

The cottage was, by Mr. Olav's uncle's last letter, ready by the second week of May.

The captain-to-be was, by her own private reading, the man she had married.

She slept.
