In the first week of September a letter came from the company at Bergen.

Olav had been expecting it. He had not, in any letter to anyone, said he had been expecting it. But Brun, on the landing at the company office in October of '82, had told him that the company would not offer him the *Semiramis* berth a second time within twelve months, and the twelve-month window had now expired, and Olav had assumed — correctly — that some other berth would, in some form, present itself in the autumn.

The berth presented itself in a letter from a clerk at *Det Bergenske Damskibsselskab* named Hofmoe whom Olav had not previously corresponded with.

The letter was on company stationery. It said:

> *Mr. Hestby —*
>
> *On the recommendation of Captain B. Brun and on the authority of Mr. Johansen, the Bergen office is in a position to offer you the berth of First Mate on the bark* President Daae *of Bergen, lifting the third week of October, 1883, for an Atlantic petroleum voyage to Brooklyn, New York, returning the spring of 1885, under the command of Captain Gerhard Thoresen. The voyage is approximately eighteen months. Wages and articles per the enclosed schedule. Mr. Johansen would appreciate your reply by the second Monday of September.*
>
> *Yours faithfully, T. Hofmoe.*

The schedule attached was the standard Bergen first-mate's articles. The wages were 220 kroner a month, which was three times the common-seaman rate Olav had been paid on the *Semiramis* and which would, across eighteen months, contribute about four thousand kroner toward the cottage. The voyage was the kind Brun had described to him at the chart-table at Calcutta in the autumn of '81 — a deep-water voyage with the kind of cargo and the kind of port that took a man into the trade he was going to have for the next twenty years.

The captain was a name Olav did not recognize.

He took the letter to the kitchen at supper and laid it on the table.

He explained the offer. He explained the wages. He explained the eighteen months. He explained that he had not asked for it — the offer had been arranged by Brun and Johansen on the basis of his sign-off the previous October — and that the second Monday of September was eight days off.

His father read the letter twice. He set it down.

"Thoresen," Jens said.

"You know the name."

"I know the name. I was at Stavanger in '74 when his bark — the *Bergljot,* a different *Bergljot* than the small one Aanestad's brother bought — came in from a New York run with one of the crew lashed to the foremast. The crewman had been at the foremast for the last week of the run because Thoresen had decided that was the appropriate punishment for what the crewman had done, which was steal a tin of preserved peaches from the steward's locker. The crewman survived. He went ashore at Stavanger and did not, by my information, ever sign for another deep-water voyage. He is, I believe, at a coastal smack out of Egersund now."

He put the letter back on the table.

He did not, at the table, say more.

Olava, who had been listening at the stove, came to the table. She picked up the letter. She read it. She set it down.

"Olav," she said.

"Yes."

"This is the next-voyage letter."

"It is."

"You have not asked for it."

"No."

"Brun has arranged it."

"Brun has arranged it."

"You will not be asked again, if you decline."

"I will not be asked again from this company. The other Bergen companies — Houlder, the Stavanger ones — would be the next-after-this. They are not the same company. They do not know me."

"And the captain Thoresen."

"The captain is the captain my father has just told us about."

She looked at Jens. Jens did not look at her. He had said the thing he had to say about Thoresen; the rest of the conversation was, by his judgment, the marriage's.

She looked at Olav.

She said, after a moment:

"Olav. The cottage will not be built by next spring without that wage."

"It will not."

"And I will not, for a year and a half, sleep beside you."

"You will not."

"I have, since the wedding, been preparing for this letter without telling you I was preparing for it. I think you have been preparing for it without telling me you were preparing for it. The letter has come at the day in September that the prepared thing arrives in the mail. We are not, this evening, surprised by the letter. We are only at the table with it."

"Yes."

"What do you want, Olav."

"I want," he said, "to take it. I want to take it because the wage is the wage that builds the cottage and the cottage is the household I want to give us. I want to take it because Brun has gone to the trouble of arranging it and the going-to-the-trouble is a debt I want to honor. I want to take it because Thoresen — as my father has just told us — is the kind of captain a man learns the ugliest part of the trade from, and I want to learn the ugliest part now while I am twenty-five and not at thirty when it will go harder. Those are the wants. The not-wants are the bed beside you and the year-and-a-half. The wants outnumber the not-wants in the arithmetic. They do not, in the body, outnumber them."

She looked at him for some seconds.

She turned to Mrs. Hestby, who was at the stove with her back to the table and was doing — Olav understood — the thing his mother always did at conversations of this kind, which was making small unrelated motions at the stove that allowed her to be present without intervening.

"Mother."

"Yes, Olava."

"You will look at me a moment."

Olav's mother turned.

She looked at her daughter-in-law.

She did not, at the look, say anything.

Olava said:

"You and I will, in October, become each other's only company in this house except for Jens. The arrangement will be eighteen months. I am asking you, before we say yes to the letter at the table, whether you can live with me in this kitchen for eighteen months."

Olav's mother did not, for a moment, answer.

Then she said, in her small flat register:

"Olava."

"Yes."

"I have lived in this kitchen with one woman for twenty-five years. The one woman has been myself. I have not, in twenty-five years, had another woman in this kitchen at the meals on a daily basis, because Jens's mother was at Stavanger by the year I came in and his sister had married out the year before. I have, at this kitchen, been alone with a man's habits and a man's silences and a man's coat at the door. I am forty-five years old. I have, through the spring at this kitchen with you, learned that there is — for a woman of forty-five who has been alone in a kitchen for twenty-five years — a thing that can be had with another woman in the kitchen that I had not previously been able to imagine. I would, at forty-five, like the eighteen months. I will not pretend I would not. The eighteen months would be the eighteen months I had not, until this March, known I wanted."

Olava did not, at the speech, immediately answer.

She put her hand on Mrs. Hestby's hand. Mrs. Hestby let her.

She turned back to Olav.

"Take the letter," she said. "Sign the articles. Build the cottage with the wage. I will be here. Your mother and I will be here. The eighteen months will be the eighteen months."

"Yes."

"That is the deciding."

"That is the deciding."

She went back to the stove. Mrs. Hestby went back to the stove. Olav and his father were at the table with the letter between them.

His father said:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"You will write to Bergen on Monday."

"I will write."

"Good."

He picked up the letter. He folded it. He passed it back to Olav. He went out to the porch with his pipe.

Olav sat at the table for another minute with the letter in his hand.

He did not, in the minute, allow himself to feel anything specific about the having-decided. The deciding had been the deciding. The feeling, like the feeling at most of the small turning-points of his life, would come later — perhaps at the *President Daae* gangway in October, perhaps in mid-Atlantic in November, perhaps at the Brooklyn wharf in January — and he did not, at the kitchen table on the first Tuesday of September of 1883, ask the feeling to arrive on schedule.

He folded the letter into his coat.

He went out to the boat-shed for the evening's work.

---

He wrote the acceptance letter to Hofmoe on the Monday morning.

It was a short letter, in the formal Norwegian he had practiced at Mrs. Kindingstad's for letters to Bergen offices: he accepted the berth, he confirmed the Bergen reporting date of the third Monday of October, he thanked the company for the recommendation, he requested that his regards be passed to Captain Brun. He sealed the letter with the small red wafer Olava had brought into the marriage with her in March. He took it to the post-house at the harbor on the morning's small errand.

The postmaster — a man of forty named Samuelsen who had been at the post-house since '78 and had been the man who had handled Haakon's letter from Shields in May of '81 — took the letter without comment.

Olav walked back up to the boat-shed.

His father was at the workbench. He looked up.

"Posted."

"Posted."

"Then it is done."

"It is done."

His father went back to the work he had been doing, which was the small precise hand-planing of a strake for a new repair job that had come in the previous week. Olav joined him at the bench. They did not, that morning, speak again.

---

Hofmoe's reply came on the next-Tuesday's coaster.

It said:

> *Mr. Hestby —*
>
> *Receipt acknowledged. Your articles are filed. Captain Thoresen has been informed of the appointment and will receive you at the bark* President Daae *at the company quay on the third Monday of October. The bark lifts on the morning tide of the Wednesday following. The voyage will be approximately eighteen months. Pay-off is anticipated at Bergen in the second week of April, 1885, weather permitting.*
>
> *Yours, T. Hofmoe.*

The dates were the dates. April 1885 was the date Olav and Olava had not, since the kitchen table on the Tuesday a fortnight before, said aloud. They had said *eighteen months.* They had said *spring.* They had not said *April 1885,* because *April 1885* was a date the head could land on a calendar in a way it could not land on the imprecise *spring.*

April 1885 was eighteen months from the day on the calendar.

Olav read the letter at the kitchen table at Tuesday breakfast. He read it once aloud to his father and to Olava and to his mother. He did not read the date a second time. He passed the letter to his father, who read it in his own hand, and to Olava, who read it in hers, and his mother — who did not read company letters as a matter of household policy — was content to be told the date by Olava at the stove.

That evening at the back garden bench Olava said:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"April of 1885."

"April of 1885."

"That is when you come back."

"That is when I come back."

"It is twenty months from now."

"It is."

"I have been adjusting the figure since the letter on Tuesday last week from eighteen to twenty without telling you. I had hoped Hofmoe would put a date on it that was earlier than the one in my head. He did not. The date in my head and Hofmoe's date are the same date."

"Yes."

"That is hard."

"It is hard."

"I am not, at the bench tonight, going to revisit the deciding. The deciding was last week. The deciding stands. I am only saying the date out loud once, between you and me, because I want you to know that I have been carrying it."

"Yes."

"That is the saying. I will not say it again."

"Thank you, Olava."

"Yes."

She took his hand.

They sat at the bench for some minutes without speaking.

Then she said, in a different register:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"There is a thing I want to ask you about that is not the date."

"Ask."

"It is about your father's Thoresen story."

"Yes."

"The man at the foremast at the *Bergljot*. Lashed for the last week. For a tin of preserved peaches."

"Yes."

"You did not, at the table, push back against the story. You took it. You said the captain was the kind of man you wanted to learn the ugliest part of the trade from. I have been thinking, since Tuesday, about what it means to want to learn the ugliest part. Whether wanting to learn it is the same as being willing to be the man at the foremast for a week."

Olav considered the question.

"It is not the same."

"How is it not the same."

"The man at the foremast was the man Thoresen chose. The man at the foremast did not ask to be chosen. I am, at twenty-five, not the man Thoresen will choose, because the man Thoresen chooses is the man who has done a thing the captain decides to make an example of, and I am not the kind of man who steals a tin of peaches. I am also the first mate, not a common seaman; the first mate is not the captain's example. The first mate is the man the captain shouts at when the captain wants to keep the crew in line by shouting at the first man down from himself rather than at one of the crew."

"Then you will be the man Thoresen shouts at."

"I will be the man Thoresen shouts at."

"For eighteen months."

"For eighteen months."

"You can stand it."

"I can stand it. I stood Captain Gjermund at eighteen, who was a worse man than Thoresen by my father's account. Standing Thoresen at twenty-five is a different thing from standing Gjermund at eighteen. I have, since the *Sigrid,* been the kind of man who stands captains. That is the trade."

"Yes."

She did not, at the bench, say more.

She put her head on his shoulder briefly. Then she stood up and went up to the house, because the lamp had been lit in the kitchen behind them and her mother — who had come over for the week from Lindøy to help with the small autumn arrangements before Olav left — would be wanting Olava at the table.

He sat at the bench another minute.

He thought about Thoresen. He thought about the *Bergljot* in '74. He thought about the man at the foremast.

He did not, in the thinking, allow himself to feel particularly worried about it. He had known, since the kitchen table the previous Tuesday, that the trade he was entering was a different trade from the trade with Brun. Brun had been the captain a man could hope for; Thoresen would be the captain a man, sooner or later, had to learn from. The arithmetic was the arithmetic.

He went up to the kitchen for supper.

The last days of September went past in their small clear pre-departure way. Olava packed his sea-chest for him in the second week of October — the same chest his father had packed for him in June of '81, with the same wool shirts and the same two pairs of boots and the same leather folder with the certificate and the cards and the small bound book that had closed in March. Mrs. Lindøy came over from Lindøy with a small package of preserves and dried mutton. Marit and Eirik came over from the schoolmaster's house to say goodbye. The captain, on the last Saturday before Olav left, came over and took the small Lindøy share-paper out of his coat and showed Olav once more the figures and reminded him, in the captain's small careful Lindøy voice, that the share would pay its small dividend each November and that the dividend would go into the cottage account and that Olava and Mrs. Hestby would have, each November of the eighteen months, the small particular small sum the captain had calculated.

The captain went home to Lindøy on the Sunday afternoon's coaster.

On the Monday morning Olav and his father went down to the harbor.

The morning coaster took them to Stavanger. The Stavanger morning coaster took them, with a transfer at Stavanger quay, to Bergen. The Bergen coaster was overnight; they slept on the deck in the small first-class shelter a coaster like that provided for paying passengers.

They made Bergen at noon on the Tuesday.

The bark *President Daae* of Bergen was at the company quay.

She was a black bark of about nine hundred tons, slightly smaller than the *Semiramis* and slightly older — built at Hardanger in '69, by the small brass plate on her transom — and she had the look of a bark that had been to the Atlantic petroleum trade for the last six years and would, by the look of her decks and her rigging, go to it for another ten before her owners broke her up.

Captain Thoresen was at the rail.

He was a man of about fifty-five, heavily built, with a black beard and the small sharp eye of a captain who had been at sea since fifteen and had decided, somewhere in the years between, that the trade was a trade he was going to win at by force.

Olav came up the gangway. His father came up behind him with the chest.

Thoresen looked at Olav.

He did not, at the look, smile.

"Hestby."

"Captain."

"You are early."

"Yes, sir."

"Good. The mate is at the company office. He will be back at three. Stow your chest. Report to me at four bells of the afternoon watch. We lift at six tomorrow morning."

"Yes, sir."

He turned away.

Jens, who had come up the gangway behind Olav, set the chest at his son's feet. He did not, at the rail, say anything to Thoresen. He looked at Olav for a moment. He put out his hand. Olav took it.

"Olav."

"Father."

"Eighteen months."

"Eighteen months."

"Come home."

"Yes."

That was it.

Jens went back down the gangway. He went up the quay. He turned at the warehouse corner and raised his hand once. Olav raised his.

Then his father was gone.

Olav took the chest on his shoulder.

He went forward to find his berth.

The voyage had begun.
