The Stavanger coaster came in to the quay at half past noon on the second Saturday of April.

Olav was at the starboard rail as she warped in. His sea-chest was at his feet. The leather folder was under his arm. The small bound book of the third voyage had closed on the Tuesday evening at the Bergen rooming-house. The Friday night at the rooming-house had been the small last formal night of the voyage; he had taken the morning coaster south and had slept in the coaster's small first-class shelter through the crossing down Byfjorden and out along the coast, and had woken at five in the morning to the grey light of a Stavanger April approach.

He was, at twenty-five and seven months, a free man.

The word was not a word he had, in the Friday night's small bed at Øvregaten, been able to use for himself yet. It was the word the pay-off had produced; the word his father had produced, in his own way, at the Bergen quay in October of '82 when he had said *Come home;* the word Johansen had produced at the Wednesday pay-off by the phrase *your name has been put forward as the first mate* that had been named-and-declined in the same breath. The word was the small legal-commercial fact the voyage had produced. It was also, at the starboard rail of the Stavanger coaster on the Saturday morning, a thing his body had not yet entirely caught up to.

His body still held, at the rail, the small careful first mate's watchfulness of eighteen months.

He had decided, at the coaster's cast-off at Bergen the previous morning, that he would not, on the three-day run south, make any attempt to let the watchfulness go. It would go, by his reading of Brun in '82 and of Tellefsen at the officer's mess in March of '85, at the pace the body needed. It would go, perhaps, in pieces across the first summer at the cottage. It would, by the small careful accounting of older sailors who had retired from the trade at forty-five and sixty, not entirely go for months, possibly years.

He watched the quay approach.

The Stavanger quay in the April light was the same quay it had been in June of '81 at the *Semiramis* lift. The same warehouses. The same cranes. The same small group of people at the foot of the third wharf, the wharf the coaster would tie up at. At the group, he registered — because it was, despite the eighteen months, the first thing his eye had been looking for — a small figure in a wool coat.

She was standing at the foot of the wharf.

Marit was a step behind her.

His father and his mother, he registered in the second second, were a few steps beyond them — at the small shelter of the wharf-office's awning — and the captain was beside them, on his good arm.

Olava had come.

---

She had come to Stavanger for the homecoming.

She had not come to Bergen in October of '83 for the departure because the Christmas of '79 agreement had assigned the departure to the parents at Stavanger. She had not come to Bergen in April of '85 for the pay-off because the pay-off was the bark's and the company's and was not the marriage's. She had come, she had decided — at some point in the eight weeks between receiving the long letter and Olav's arrival — to Stavanger for the coaster's arrival because the coaster-arrival was the marriage's, and the marriage's homecoming belonged to her.

She had not told him, in any of her March letters, that she was coming.

He had not — until the small figure in the wool coat became a distinctly readable Olava at about fifty yards — allowed himself to expect her.

He had, he registered at the rail, been doing the small calibrated expectation-management of a married deep-water man at the homecoming of a long voyage, by which the man kept the possibility of his wife being at the quay and the possibility of his wife not being at the quay in the small balanced state that would let him take either without being unmade.

The small balance broke at fifty yards.

He allowed it to break.

---

The coaster tied up at the wharf at twenty to one.

The gangway went over.

Olav took the leather folder under his arm and the sea-chest on his shoulder and he came down the gangway.

He did not, on the gangway, do the small careful not-looking-back he had done at the *Semiramis* lift in June of '81. The not-looking-back had been the departure's discipline. The arrival required a different discipline, which was the looking-forward, and the looking-forward was not a thing the body had to enforce at the homecoming; the body did it without asking.

He came down to the stone of the wharf.

He set the chest down.

Olava was at the foot of the gangway.

She did not, this time, wait for him to cross. She crossed to him. She put both her hands on his face on the cheekbones — the way she had at Stavanger on the June morning in '81, which was the morning the first kiss of their lives had been — and she held his face.

She did not count the breaths aloud. She had not counted since the Christmas of '82 bench.

She kissed him.

The kiss was not the first kiss — the first had been on the quay in June of '81 — and it was not the second — the second had been at the midnight bench on Christmas Eve of '82. It was the third kiss of their lives. It was the first kiss of their marriage on a public quay. It was the kiss of a woman who had, across the seventeen weeks since the long letter had reached her in February, decided that the coaster's arrival at Stavanger on the second Saturday of April would be the first kiss she would give Olav in front of his parents and her father at the Stavanger wharf, and that the deciding would be the gift she would give him at the homecoming.

She held the kiss for the count of perhaps four breaths.

She pulled back.

She did not put her forehead against his. That gesture had been the June of '81 one. The April of '85 gesture was different. She put her hand — one hand — at the back of his neck, where his mother had put her hand at his twelve-year-old confirmation, where his father had put his hand at the Stavanger quay in June of '81 at the departure. She held it there for the count of perhaps six breaths.

"Olav."

"Olava."

"Home."

"Home."

That was the full of the homecoming at the quay.

She took her hand away.

She stepped back. She let him cross to his parents.

---

His father was at the awning.

Jens Hestby was, at fifty-nine, slightly greyer at the temples than he had been in October of '82. His coat was the same coat he had been wearing at the Bergen pay-off thirty months before, and the small careful sweet hay-smell of the airing was, at the distance of the awning, still detectable. He did not, at the coming-up, embrace Olav. The Hestby household did not, at homecomings, embrace at the quays. It let the quay be the quay.

He put out his hand.

Olav took it.

"Olav."

"Father."

"You are home."

"I am home."

"Good."

His mother did not put out her hand. She put both her hands briefly on the sides of his face — not at the cheekbones, as Olava had; lower, at the jaw, the same place she had put them at the Bergen rooming-house in October of '82 on the arrival of the *Semiramis* — and held them there for perhaps two breaths. Then she let go. She did not, at the quay, say anything; she had decided, by the small standing discipline of her register at public moments, that her sentence would be at the kitchen door at Finnøy on the afternoon, and that the awning at Stavanger at twenty to one on a Saturday was not the place for it.

Captain Lindøy, across the small group, put out his good hand.

"Hestby."

"Captain."

"Welcome home."

"Thank you, Captain."

"Marit is at the neighbour's with Eirik. They send their regards. They will come over to Finnøy on the Wednesday coaster for the small supper."

"I will be glad of them."

"Good."

That was the formal arrival.

---

The arrangement for the rest of the day had been settled — Olav understood from the small briefing his mother gave him at the awning before they moved off — by the two mothers' correspondence across the previous fortnight. The afternoon would be the coaster across to Tau and the Lindøy smack from Tau to Finnøy with his parents. Olava would stay at the rooming-house at Stavanger for the Saturday evening and would come across to Finnøy by the Monday coaster from Stavanger through Lindøy. The Saturday and Sunday at Finnøy would be Olav's with his parents in the household. The Monday afternoon Olava would arrive at the house, and the household would, by the transition Mrs Hestby had been quietly designing for some weeks, hand itself from the parents-alone-with-the-returning-son state into the married-household-with-parents state over the course of the Monday supper.

The cottage would be ready for occupancy by the second week of May — four weeks away. The household would, in the interim, be at the parents' house.

This was, Olav registered at the awning, the small careful choreography of a Norwegian island household at the homecoming of its married son from a long voyage. It was Mrs Hestby's choreography. It was Mrs Lindøy's correspondence. It had been in his mother's small careful letter to him at the Cape in September of '84 in a version he had read at the time as the small ordinary household-news and understood now as the careful beginning of the choreography's design.

He was, at the awning, grateful for it.

He did not, at the awning, say so.

---

He stood for a minute at the edge of the small group.

He did not, in the minute, do anything specific. He looked at the wharf. He looked at the coaster. He looked at his father and mother at the awning, at Olava a few steps away, at the captain beside them.

He registered — with the small flat clarity his interior used for the small structural moments — that the homecoming at the Stavanger quay on the second Saturday of April of '85 was the homecoming the eighteen months of the *President Daae* had been walking toward, and that the walking-toward had, at the moment of the kiss at the foot of the gangway, ended.

He had, across the eighteen months, been walking toward Olava at a public quay.

She had, across the same eighteen months, been walking toward him at the same.

The walk had closed at the wharf.

He picked up the sea-chest.

---

They took the one o'clock coaster from the Stavanger quay to Tau.

The crossing was an hour and a quarter. Olav sat at the small bench on the coaster's deck with his father on his right and his mother on his left. Olava and the captain were at the small forward cabin for the crossing — the captain preferred the cabin at this season, because his wooden hand did not hold well against the rail in the small afternoon wind — and Olav did not, on the hour and a quarter, see her.

His father said, at about the third quarter of the crossing:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"Tellefsen is the new master of the *Daae.*"

"Yes."

"I heard from Aanestad who heard from a Mosterøy man who had it from the Bergen papers. Thoresen retired on the Wednesday of the pay-off."

"Yes."

"Then the trade has gone on without you."

"It has gone on."

"Good."

He did not, at the bench, say more.

His mother, on his other side, said nothing across the entire hour and a quarter.

She held his arm at the crook with her small gloved hand.

She had decided, by the small economy of her register, that the holding was the sentence she was saying at the crossing. She held it for the full hour and a quarter. She did not let go until the coaster warped in at the Tau jetty.

---

The Lindøy smack from Tau took them the last twenty minutes across to Finnøy.

The captain had gone on to Lindøy separately on his own boat. Olava had stayed at Stavanger per the arrangement. The three of them — Olav and his parents — were on the smack with the Mosterøy boatman Aanestad had sent across for the afternoon.

They reached the Finnøy jetty at a quarter past three.

His mother, at the jetty, said the one sentence she had been saving since the awning at Stavanger:

"Olav."

"Yes, mother."

"You are home."

"I am home."

"Good."

She went up the path to the house.

His father stayed at the jetty with him for a minute to supervise the sea-chest's transfer from the smack to the small barrow Aanestad's grandson had at the boards.

Then the two of them walked up the path to the house.

---

That evening at the supper his mother had laid the table with the small particular Saturday-supper of the household — a piece of pickled herring, boiled potatoes, the small flatbread she had been making on the Wednesdays for the last month, a small honey-cake she had been saving for the homecoming Saturday.

They ate.

They did not, at the supper, speak much. His father told the small news of the parish — the small news that had accumulated across the three months since Olav's last letter home, which had been a short one from the Brooklyn wharf in late February of '85. His mother, at the stove across the small room, added the one or two small supplements his father forgot.

Olav ate.

He did not, at the table, tell the long version of the eighteen months. The long version had been the February-at-the-wharf letter, and Olava had the letter at Lindøy, and the long version would, by the small economy the marriage had been practicing since the engagement, be transmitted to his parents at a later moment at a later kitchen table in the proportions the two women of the household decided it should be transmitted in.

He told, instead, the small particular news of the Stavanger coaster. The Mosterøy boatman. The small Bergen rooming-house. The Tuesday pay-off at Johansen's office. The landlady at Øvregaten, who had recognized him from October of '82 and had asked after Olava.

His parents took the small news.

The supper ended at half past seven.

His father went to the parlour with the Stavanger newspaper. His mother cleared the plates. Olav, at the kitchen, dried.

---

At the drying, his mother said at the sink — without looking up, in the small careful Mrs Hestby voice she used for the sentences she had been saving:

"Olav."

"Yes, mother."

"She is coming on the Monday."

"Yes."

"The cottage is ready in four weeks."

"Yes."

"I have, across the eighteen months, kept her company. She has kept mine. We will, on the Monday, transition the household into the married arrangement across the Monday supper. I want you to know the transition is the transition I want. It is not the transition I would have wanted in October of '82 when we had had only two weeks with her; it is the transition I want in April of '85 after eighteen months of being her only companion in this kitchen. You are getting her back. I am, in the same transition, giving her back. The giving-back is the small grief that eighteen months has taught me is possible to carry."

She did not, at the sentence, look up.

Olav did not, at the drying, immediately answer.

He thought for a moment about the right thing to say. The right thing to say was not the thanking; thanking would have been the easy answer and his mother had not, at the sink, given him the easy opening. The right thing to say was the acknowledgment that the giving-back would not, in his reading, be the end of her kitchen company with Olava. The cottage was a mile up the path. The Lindøy smack crossed twice a week. The two women would, across the rest of his mother's life, keep the company the eighteen months had built between them.

He said:

"Mother."

"Yes."

"The cottage is at the other end of the lane. The kitchen here will not lose her. The cottage is the household's married arrangement. It is not the end of your kitchen company. I do not, at the sink, know how to say this except to say it."

She set down the cloth she had been wiping with.

She looked at him — once, briefly — and then back at the dish in her hand.

"Thank you, Olav."

"You are welcome."

"That was what I needed to hear."

"Good."

She went back to the dishes. He went back to the drying. The kitchen at Finnøy at eight o'clock on the Saturday evening of the second Saturday of April of '85 held its two people in its small ordinary domestic quiet, with the larger quiet of the coming Monday — and the coming four weeks, and the coming settled years — at the edges of the kitchen but not yet in it.

---

That night in the small upstairs bedroom — not the loft (the loft had been the wedding-night room; the bedroom had become, at Olava's small private redesignation in the summer of '83, the married-bedroom-when-not-at-the-cottage) — Olav lay on his back in the small narrow married bed alone.

He did not, at first, sleep.

He thought about the quay. He thought about the kiss. He thought about the hand at the back of his neck for six breaths. He thought about his mother at the sink and the ten seconds of silence between her sentence and his reply.

He thought, at the ceiling:

*The voyage is done. The homecoming is done. The marriage is, from the Monday supper forward, the marriage of a man who is at home and intends to stay at home. The next thirty years are the next thirty years. I will not, in those thirty years, do what the trade would have had me do if I had taken Tellefsen's offer. I will do the other thing, which is the thing my father has done for thirty-five years, which is the thing my grandfather did before him, which is the thing the cottage was built for.*

He thought:

*I am home.*

He said it aloud, in the small private voice he used for the sentences he wanted to hear himself say.

"I am home."

The room did not answer.

He slept.

In the small kitchen below him, his mother turned down the lamp at ten o'clock and went up to the back bedroom where his father had already been for an hour. The house at Finnøy at ten on the Saturday evening of the second Saturday of April of '85 held its three occupants in the small ordinary quiet of a Norwegian island household on the first Saturday of its married son's return from a long voyage, and the house held steadily, and the spring of '85 began.
