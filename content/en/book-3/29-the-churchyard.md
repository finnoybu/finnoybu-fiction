On the afternoon of the third Tuesday of September of 1938 Olav walked up to the Hestby church on the hill for the last specific visit he would make alone.

He was eighty years and six months. The small thin walking-stick his uncle's son had carved for him ten years before was now the second leg he used for the uphill portion of any walk. The path up to the church was the same path he had walked five thousand times across the eighty years. He walked it slowly. The small autumn light at the path was the late-afternoon light of a third-Tuesday-of-September at the parish — the half-gold half-grey Norwegian light his eye had catalogued across eighty autumns.

He had not, across the six years since the birth of his great-grandson Olav the third in the spring of '34, done much traveling. He had been at the cottage's workbench in the mornings for as long as the mornings had let him; since the winter of '36 he had not been at the workbench with any regularity, because his hands had, at seventy-eight, begun to fail at the precision the workbench's work required. He did the reading at the parlour-corner in the afternoons. He went to the church on the Sundays with Ingrid. He went to the churchyard alone on the Tuesdays when he had — at the small accumulated register of his widower's calendar — the visit that the month required.

This Tuesday was the last of them.

He had decided it at the kitchen in the morning. He had not, at the decision, told Ingrid. He had — in the way he had learned to keep the last decisions to himself across the widower-years — decided in the small quiet of his own interior and had walked up without announcement.

---

He came to the small *P.H. 1812* gate at a quarter past three.

He stood at the gate for a moment.

He registered, at the gate, that the carved initials of his great-grandfather's 1812 carving had, across the hundred and twenty-six years since, worn down to the half-legible depth a Norwegian island carved date had at the hundred-and-twenty-year mark. The *P.* was still clear. The *H.* was still clear. The *1812* was half-legible. The dot between the letters was gone.

The gate itself — the wood — had been replaced, by the small parish work, in 1919. The replacement wood had been carved with the same initials and date by a Stavanger mason Pastor Nordenhaug had commissioned. The 1919 carving had, by the Norwegian-island-weather of the nineteen years since, faded about half as much as the 1812 original had at the same interval.

Both were the gate.

He opened the gate.

He walked into the churchyard.

---

The small family-plot had, across the ten years since his 1928 visit, added one stone.

The small Stavanger mason had, in the winter of '34, installed the eleventh stone for the reason Olav had not expected and had, on the Wednesday in late November of '34 when Ingrid had come down from the parish school with the small news, registered with the small flat clarity the register kept:

Ingrid had died of a influenza on a Friday evening in November of '34, at the age of forty-three, after a four-day illness the Stavanger doctor had not been able to intervene usefully on. She had been the forty-three-year-old unmarried daughter-at-home who the 1918 influenza-and-its-small-successor-strains had, across the decades since, continued to take at the intervals of a parish's years.

She was buried, on the Saturday, at the south edge of the family-plot beside Olava.

Her stone — the *Ingrid Hestbye, 1891–1934* — had been the eleventh stone in the family-plot.

The twelfth space — the *Olav Hestbye, 1858–[date]* space the mason had been pre-paid for in '25 — was, as of the September 1938 visit, still the twelfth space and still empty grass.

Olav was, at eighty and a half, the remaining Hestbye of his generation.

He was also, at eighty and a half, the remaining keeper of the small cottage drawer.

---

He stood at the family-plot.

He read the twelve stones in the order he had always read them — oldest to newest, Peder 1748 to Ingrid 1934.

He stopped at Ingrid's stone.

He said, as he had said at each of the twelve visits he had made since her burial in '34, the one sentence:

"Ingrid."

He moved to Olava's stone.

He said:

"Olava."

He stood at the two stones for some minutes without moving.

Then he did the thing he had decided at the kitchen in the morning.

He said — aloud, in the small still September afternoon of the empty churchyard, at a volume the ambient of a Norwegian island churchyard on a Tuesday in September did not often produce — the four names he had not said aloud in his life except at the small isolated moments of the drawer-archive.

"Haakon."

"Anders."

"Sven."

And, after a small pause — because the fourth name was the name he had, at eighteen, put into the back of his head as the first name and had carried across sixty-two years as the first-name of the sequence — he said:

"Thomas."

The four names were the four names.

Thomas was Thomas Engelstad, who had been Olav's desertion-partner at Wilmington in the autumn of 1876, who had given Olav the desertion-name *Olav Vaageland* at the Sunday dog-watch at the brig's deck, who had stayed on at Mrs Hovland's lodging-house through the winter when Olav had shipped out on the *Anna Maria* on the Tuesday of the third week of October, and who had, on the Friday morning of the third week of March 1877 at a lodging-house Olav had by then been five months absent from, gone out at dawn for the small milk-errand and had not come back. Olav had never — in the sixty-one years since — found out what had happened to Thomas Engelstad. The small Norwegian-American paper networks of the 1880s and '90s had not produced any report of him. Olav had — at twenty-three, at thirty, at fifty, at seventy — periodically asked the inquiries at the Stavanger shipping community and had never, in the sixty-one years, found a single trace of what had happened to Thomas after the Friday morning of the third week of March.

Thomas had been the fourth name.

Thomas had not been in the drawer because Thomas had not written a letter. Thomas had simply disappeared. The disappearance had been, across the sixty-two years, the un-closed piece of Olav's life — the piece that had not produced a letter and had not produced a death-notice and had not produced any specific small ending that could be put into the drawer.

Olav said Thomas's name at the graveside of his wife and his daughter on the afternoon of the third Tuesday of September of 1938 because the name was the last name the architecture of his life had been holding, and because the architecture — at eighty and a half — was going to end, by the simple small arithmetic of his own mortality, within the window of the next few years, and because the naming at the graveside in the silence of the churchyard was the closing of the architecture from his own side.

The four names were the four names.

He said them each once.

He did not say them again.

---

He stood at the family-plot for another five minutes after the saying.

He thought, at the plot, in the small flat register:

*I am, at eighty and a half, the last Hestbye of my generation. I am the keeper of the cottage drawer. The drawer has six items. The six items are all papers; four of them name three of the four names I have just said at the graveside. The fourth name — Thomas — is in the back of my head only. The back of my head does not have an archive-form I can pass on. When I die, Thomas will, by the simple small arithmetic of no-one-living-knowing-him, be unknown to the family and to the parish and to anyone who might otherwise have any record of the name. The piece of Thomas will die with me.*

*The other three — Haakon, Anders, Sven — will, in the small cottage drawer, continue to exist as names on papers for whatever remainder the papers have. The papers will, by my children's and grandchildren's handling of the cottage after I die, at some point be found. The finders will read them. They will work out, or they will not, the context. The context may, across the years, be lost. The names may become, across the future, the unattributed historical fragments a family finds and keeps without knowing. Or they may become, by a reader of the right kind at the right time, the recoverable piece of the great-grandfather's interior. I do not know which.*

*I cannot determine which.*

*I am — at eighty and a half at the graveside — releasing the determining. The drawer will do what the drawer does. The papers will do what the papers do. The line of the family will make of the papers what the line makes of them.*

*That is the limit of my agency.*

*The four names, from this afternoon forward, are no longer my private carrying. They are the historical names on papers that are in a drawer that will outlive me. The carrying has ended. The archive begins.*

He stood another minute.

He turned from the plot.

He walked out of the churchyard.

---

On the path down — at the halfway point where the path crossed the small stream, the same spot he had stopped at in the September of '28 — he paused.

He leaned on the small thin walking-stick for a moment.

He looked at the small stream.

The stream was the stream it had been across his eighty years at the island. It came out of the spring at the top of the hill above the church and ran down across the path and down to the small Lindøy-facing shore. It had, across the eighty years, neither changed course nor slowed. It was, at the September Tuesday of 1938, running at the autumn-flow of the rains of the previous week.

He said, at the stream — quietly, to the small self-voice he had been using at the small final moments of his life:

"I have, at this afternoon's visit, said the four names at the graveside of my wife and my daughter. The four names are now out of my head and into the open air of the parish. The parish air has received them. The carrying is done."

He did not, at the stream, cry.

He walked down the rest of the path.

He came back to the larger house at half past four.

---

Ingrid had been dead for almost four years. Anna was fifty-two; she was at Stavanger with Henrik Berg (now sixty-one and near retirement from the Stavanger primary school). Jens the younger was fifty; he was at Bergen with Ragnhild and their two children (Olav the second at twenty, now at the Bergen commercial school, and a small second child — a girl, Hanne, born in 1921, now at seventeen).

The larger house was, at the September afternoon of 1938, occupied by Olav and a housekeeper — a woman named Mrs Bjornestad who had been with Ingrid's kitchen since '30 and who, after Ingrid's death in '34, had stayed on with Olav at the small daily-housekeeper arrangement the family had paid for since.

Mrs Bjornestad was at the kitchen when Olav came in.

"Captain."

"Mrs Bjornestad."

"Tea."

"Tea."

He sat at the kitchen.

She brought the small mug.

He drank the small afternoon tea.

She did not, at the kitchen, ask about the visit. She had been at the household for eight years and had, by the observation of a Norwegian island housekeeper of her generation, learned to read the signs of her employer's afternoons and to keep the questions for the moments the employer invited them.

She did not invite them.

He drank the tea.

He thanked her.

He went to the small parlour-corner for the evening's reading.

---

That evening at the small writing-desk of the larger house — which had been Olava's writing-desk since '95 and had, since Olava's death in '24, been unused most evenings — Olav did one small thing.

He took out a small piece of paper.

He wrote, in his own hand:

> *Tuesday 20 September 1938. I have this afternoon been to the Hestbye churchyard for a visit of the family-plot. I have, at the graveside of my wife Olava (died 4 November 1924) and my daughter Ingrid (died 16 November 1934), said aloud in the empty churchyard the four names of the four men I have been carrying across my life as the unnamed pieces of my interior. The four names are Haakon Berg of Hogganvik and Sandviken (died at sea 2 March 1911, commemorated on sheet in cottage drawer); Anders Lindqvist of Stockholm (died at sea 29 October 1897, commemorated on sheet in cottage drawer); Sven Haugen of Sandviken (alive at sixty-four as of Jens the younger's last letter from Bergen of 16 September 1938; still at a small Bergen coaster); and Thomas Engelstad of Stjernerøy, my desertion partner of the brig Sigrid at Wilmington in October 1876, who disappeared from Mrs Hovland's lodging-house at Wilmington on the third Friday of March 1877, five months after I had left it and whose fate I have never, in the sixty-one years since, been able to discover.*
>
> *I have, by the saying aloud this afternoon, ended the carrying that has been mine across the sixty-two years of my adult life. The architecture that permitted the carrying — the form of marriage Olava and I built between 1879 and 1924 — ended at her death in '24. The architecture that permitted the not-naming — the discipline my own interior kept across the decades — has ended at this afternoon's saying.*
>
> *The six papers in the cottage drawer will continue to hold the archive of what the saying made public. The archive will outlive me. The archive's future interpretation is not my responsibility.*
>
> *This small sheet is the last sheet I will write on the subject.*
>
> *Olav Hestby, Captain (retired), bark Helga of Stavanger, of Finnøy by Stavanger, eighty years and six months old at the writing.*

He blotted the page.

He folded it once.

He did not take it to the cottage that evening. He had decided, at the parlour-corner, that the small sheet would stay at the writing-desk drawer of the larger house until the moment his family found it after his death. The cottage drawer had its six items. This sheet was the sheet that belonged at the larger house — the sheet that would be found first, when the family came to settle the estate, in the first drawer they would open. The sheet would, by its own contents, direct them to the cottage drawer.

The small archive would, at that moment, transmit.

He put the sheet in the small top drawer of the writing-desk.

He closed the drawer.

He blew out the lamp.

He went up to bed at half past ten.

The larger house at Finnøy on the evening of the third Tuesday of September of 1938 held its two occupants — Olav at the small upstairs bedroom, Mrs Bjornestad at the small ground-floor housekeeper's room — in the small ordinary autumn quiet of a Norwegian island parish-household at the close of its eighty-year-old occupant's last structural afternoon.

The small architecture had ended at the graveside.

The small archive waited.

The captain slept.
