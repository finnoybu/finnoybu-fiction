Olav Hestby died at the larger house at Finnøy on the afternoon of the seventh of April, 1942.

He had been at the final illness for three weeks. A heart-weakness that the Stavanger doctor had been watching since the winter of '40 had, at the turn of the March-to-April of 1942, become the weakness that the Stavanger doctor said at the small end of the Saturday of the fourth of April would be the final one.

He had — across the three weeks — been at the bedroom with Anna and Jens the younger taking turns at the bedside. Mrs Bjornestad ran the kitchen. The small Stavanger doctor came across twice a week.

He died at two o'clock in the afternoon of the seventh. He was eighty-four years and one month. The seventh of April of 1942 was, as it had been since 1940, a Norway under German occupation. The funeral arrangements a Norwegian island parish made in the second year of the occupation were the small constrained arrangements the occupation permitted. Pastor Nordenhaug — still alive at sixty-four, still at the Hestby parish — handled the burial at the Saturday with the modified service the occupation had not prevented the small parish from keeping.

The stone was placed by the Stavanger mason in the autumn of 1942, by the pre-paid arrangement of 1925. It read, in the old-spelling Pastor Kleven had initiated in 1852 and the mason had continued:

*Olav Hestbye, 1858–1942.*

The stone was beside Olava's.

The family-plot, from the autumn of 1942 forward, had twelve stones in the small Hestbye old-spelling.

---

The small archive of the larger house was settled across the two months after the funeral.

Anna came across with Henrik Berg in the third week of April for the estate-closing work a Norwegian island household of their kind required at the death of its last resident. Jens the younger came from Bergen for the fourth week of April and stayed a week.

On the Tuesday of the third week Anna opened the small top drawer of the writing-desk at the parlour-corner.

She found, as the first item at the top of the small pile of her father's final papers, the small folded sheet he had written on the evening of the twentieth of September of 1938 at the writing-desk.

She read it.

She did not, at the reading, immediately speak.

She took the sheet across to the small parlour-corner where Jens the younger was sitting with the small Stavanger paper.

"Jens."

"Yes."

"Read this."

He read it.

He handed it back.

"Anna."

"Yes."

"Father's archive."

"Father's archive."

"At the cottage."

"At the cottage."

"You have the cottage's key."

"I have the cottage's key."

They walked down the lane to the cottage together.

---

At the cottage they went to the small boat-shed annex.

They knelt at the small workbench.

They opened the small drawer.

The six items were where they had been — Haakon's sealed letter of 1881; Anders's letter of 1883; the 1897 Bergen clipping; the 1897 Anders sheet; the 1911 Haakon sheet; the 1916 addendum.

Anna and Jens the younger looked at the six items for some seconds.

Anna, with the discipline her mother had trained into her across the years, did not immediately pick them up.

She said:

"Jens."

"Yes."

"Father's letter at the writing-desk says these are his archive. The archive of four men. The letter names them. We now have the archive."

"Yes."

"Jens."

"Yes."

"What do we do with them."

Jens the younger considered.

He was fifty-four. He had, across his own working life at the Bergen shipping offices, developed the discipline a clerk of his generation used with historical materials. He had, at the small Bergen museum and the small Bergen archives, handled more than a few private family papers of the kind that families at the deaths of their last keepers had to decide what to do with.

He said:

"Anna. We leave them. We put the drawer back. We do not take the papers out. We do not read them further than what we can see from the covers. We close the drawer. We lock the cottage. We keep the cottage as father kept it."

"For how long."

"For our lifetimes. At the death of the last of our generation — at whichever of you or me or the small cousins goes last — the next generation will decide. The archive will sit in the drawer at the workbench at the cottage for another thirty or forty years. The papers will, by the conditions of a sealed drawer at a Norwegian island boat-shed, keep. When the next generation decides what to do with them, they will decide. I do not think father intended for us to decide."

"Jens."

"Yes."

"That feels right."

"It feels right because it is the discipline the marriage ran on for forty-four years and that father continued to run alone for the last eighteen. The discipline was the keeping. We are, at the Tuesday afternoon of the third week of April of 1942, continuing the keeping."

"Yes."

She closed the drawer.

They locked the cottage behind them.

They walked back up the lane.

---

That afternoon at the larger house Anna took the small sheet from the writing-desk.

She did not, at the parlour-corner, put it back in the writing-desk drawer. She put it, instead, in her own small travel-bag — the leather bag she had packed at Stavanger for the two-week stay at Finnøy across the estate-closing work.

She took the sheet back to Stavanger with her at the end of the estate-closing.

At Stavanger she put the sheet in the family-archive box Henrik Berg kept at the small desk in their study — the box that held the family papers the Berg-Hestby household had accumulated across the marriage years. Anna's generation's family archive now had, at its addition of the 1938 sheet, the direction-note to the cottage drawer's archive that Anna and Jens the younger had both decided would sit untouched at the cottage until a later generation decided what to do with it.

The direction-note was the direction-note.

The cottage was the cottage.

The small six-item drawer held.

---

The family at the Hestby-Berg-Schmidt and the Hestby-Bergen lines continued.

Anna's children produced, across the forties and fifties, the third generation of the Anna Hestbye-Berg line. Jens the younger's children — Olav the second and Hanne — produced the Hestby-Bergen line. The small Schmidt line of Marit's children produced their own continuation at Mosterøy.

At the parish of Hestby the keepings continued.

On the first Sunday of June of 1947, at the small Hestby church on the hill, Pastor Nordenhaug — now sixty-nine and at his last year of the parish before his own retirement to Stavanger — baptized Olav Hestby the fourth, the small first son of Olav Hestby the second (who had married in '45) and his wife Signe of Bergen.

Olav the fourth was, by the generational arithmetic, Olav the original's great-grandson.

Pastor Nordenhaug entered the baptism in the parish register at the afternoon after the Sunday service. He wrote the name in the old spelling: *Olav Hestbye, son of Olav Hestbye (II) and Signe Hestbye nee Eriksen, baptized 1 June 1947.*

The parents signed at the modern spelling on the opposite page: *Olav Hestby.*

The two-spellings bargain of 1852 — Pastor Kleven's original institution, Pastor Øverbye's continuation, Pastor Nordenhaug's continuation — was, in the small afternoon register of the Sunday in June of 1947, being kept at the third generation past the captain.

---

Later that afternoon at the small parsonage, Pastor Nordenhaug — who had been the pastor of the parish for twenty-seven years and had baptized, married, and buried most of the members of the Hestbye family across two generations — closed the small parish register.

He put it in the small locked cabinet at the vestry.

He walked out of the parsonage to the small churchyard gate.

He opened the small *P.H. 1812* gate.

He walked into the churchyard.

He went to the small Hestbye family-plot.

He stood at the twelve stones.

He said, in the small quiet voice a Norwegian Lutheran parish pastor used at the graveside of a family he had been the pastor of:

"Peder, 1748 to 1834."

"Anna, 1760 to 1842."

"Jens, 1795 to 1849."

"Sigrid, 1836 to 1839."

"Ole, 1812 to 1892."

"Anna, 1801 to 1872."

"Jens, 1826 to 1908."

"Anna, 1823 to 1914."

"Olava, 1860 to 1924."

"Ingrid, 1891 to 1934."

"Olav, 1858 to 1942."

He did not say more.

He registered — in his own pastor's interior — that the Hestbye line of Olav the captain's generation was complete at the plot. The small third generation — Olav the fourth, baptized this Sunday morning — was, by the afternoon of the first of June of 1947, the new name the parish had just entered in the register.

The old spelling continued.

The new spelling continued.

The two-spellings bargain was, at the Sunday afternoon, in its ninety-fifth year of the parish keeping. It would continue — Pastor Nordenhaug was sure of it — past his own retirement in the autumn, past the successor's tenure, past the century of its keeping and probably longer. The small parish had, across the century, made the bargain its identity. The bargain would not, by Nordenhaug's careful reading of the Norwegian Lutheran parish culture of his time, end at any decision by any pastor.

It would, one day, end at a historical decision of the Norwegian state, in some year he would not see.

Until then it held.

---

At the stones Pastor Nordenhaug put his hand on Olav Hestbye's.

He said, quietly:

"Captain."

He did not say more.

He had, across his twenty-seven years at the parish, performed the ceremony he had just performed at the baptism for approximately one hundred and fifty baptisms. He had, at each of them, kept the two spellings. He had, across the years, come to the graveside of Olav Hestbye on the afternoons after the baptisms of the Hestbye-line children more than once. He had, at each of those afternoons, said *Captain* once at the stone.

The word was the word he used at the stone because the captain had been, at the parish's twentieth-century memory, the captain. The *Captain Olav Hestbye* was the name the parish had, across the decades since 1942, come to hold in its small collective register of the men of the Hestbye line.

The captain had been the captain.

The captain had been a Hestbye at the stone and a Hestby at the signature.

The captain had baptized a son and a daughter and another daughter in this church in the eighties and nineties, and had seen the son baptize a son in the Bergen church in '21, and had seen the daughter's daughter baptize her first child in the Stavanger church in '34, and had, across his eighty-four years, watched the line continue at the two-spellings arrangement the parish had made the lifetime of its maintenance.

The line continued.

The small first of June of 1947 had, at the baptism of Olav Hestbye the fourth, added the new name to the register.

The register was at the small vestry cabinet.

The baptism had been done.

The name — the Hestbye/Hestby of the family and of the parish and of the ninety-five-year-old two-spellings bargain — held.

---

Pastor Nordenhaug took his hand from the captain's stone.

He walked out of the churchyard.

He closed the small *P.H. 1812* gate behind him.

He walked down the path to the parsonage.

The small evening came.

The parish held.

In the small cottage down the lane — which had been untouched since Olav's death in 1942, which the family at Finnøy had been keeping at the upkeep-rotation Anna and Jens the younger had designed in the April of '42 — the small workbench held the small drawer with its six items. The drawer held. The small six papers waited.

The small first of June of 1947 had been the baptism day.

The small new Olav Hestbye lay, at his mother's arms at his grandmother Signe's small house at Bergen, four hours old to the name.

The name closed around the life.
