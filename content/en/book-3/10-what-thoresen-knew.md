Thoresen had not, in nine years, met Hestby's father.

He had heard the name once. The Stavanger man at the inquiry the company had run after the *Bergljot* incident in '74 — the small private inquiry the company had not formally constituted but had nonetheless conducted in a back room of the office at Bergen — had been a Hestby, Thoresen had registered at the time, by the way the inquiry chairman had named the witness in the third paragraph of the small typed report. Jens Hestby, mariner, presently of Finnøy. The witness had been at the Stavanger quay when the *Bergljot* had come in. He had given the inquiry his account of what he had seen at the foremast. The account had matched the accounts of the four other Stavanger men who had been at the quay that day. The inquiry had, on the basis of the five accounts, declared the captain's actions *outside the bounds of permissible discipline as understood by this company* and had issued the small private warning that had been the official act of the inquiry.

Thoresen had read the report at his cabin a fortnight after his arrival back at Bergen.

He had, on reading the report, sat at his cabin's desk for some hours.

He had decided, in the hours, three things.

The first thing he had decided was that he was not going to leave the trade. The trade was the only thing he knew how to do. He was at the time forty-seven years old, the master of a sound bark, with a wife at Bergen and three children. He had been at sea since he was fourteen. He could not, at forty-seven, become a chandler or a clerk; there was no other life he had the equipment for. He would, by his decision in the cabin, continue.

The second thing he had decided was that he was not going to be a captain who again did what he had done at the *Bergljot.* The doing-what-he-had-done had been the small specific failure of his own discipline, at sea, at a moment in mid-Atlantic in his forty-seventh year, when a seaman who had stolen a tin of preserved peaches had become — in his eye at that moment — the symbol of every small undermining of the captain's authority that the captain had been failing to address across thirty-three years of being at sea. The seaman had, at the moment of Thoresen's failure, taken the consequences of that failure. The seaman had survived. The seaman had gone ashore at Stavanger and had not, by the inquiry's information, gone to sea again. Thoresen had decided, in the cabin, that he would not, in the remainder of his career, let his own discipline fail in that way again. He would, instead, calibrate his discipline so precisely and so consistently that the small accumulated undermining would not reach the threshold at which the failure happened. He would shout. He would, perhaps, on rare occasions, strike. He would not, in the remainder of his career, lash a man to a foremast for a tin of peaches.

He had kept the second decision for nine years.

The third thing he had decided in the cabin had been the smallest of the three at the time of the deciding and had become, in nine years, the largest. He had decided that he would, for the remainder of his career, pay — privately, through whatever channel was available, at whatever price was asked — for the protection of the men he had been training to be officers and the men he was training to be officers. He had paid the small private arrangement at the Brooklyn waterfront with the Norwegian-American businessman called Gjesdal since '74. He had paid Larsen the *Bergljot* survivor at the Bowery corner since '76 (when he had heard, by Wexelsen's quiet word, that Larsen had stayed at New York and was at the corner and was warning Norwegian sailors). He had paid the Stavanger annual letter to the wife of the deserter who had shipped out of Brooklyn in '76 and had not come back, because the wife at Stavanger did not know she was a widow and the captain had decided that her not-knowing was the small piece of mercy he could continue to provide her at the price of two pages of careful captain's prose each year.

The three private arrangements had become, over nine years, the captain's small private institutions. They had cost him — in the aggregate, across nine years — perhaps four hundred dollars American out of his own pocket beyond what he billed the company.

He had not, in the nine years, told anyone except his wife at Bergen about any of the three.

She had told him, the first time he had described the arrangements at their kitchen table in Bergen in '76, that they were the right arrangements and that she was glad he had made them. She had not asked him to expand on his reasoning. She had not, in seven years since, raised the subject again. She had, from the autumn of '76, paid the small monthly drafts the bank manager handled on his behalf without comment. The bank manager, a Bergen man of his generation, had also not commented. The arrangements had become, by the small Norwegian competence with which the people who knew about them administered them, invisible to the parts of his life that did not need to know.

---

He had not, in the nine years, expected to have to describe any of the arrangements to a first mate.

The first mates of the *President Daae* had, since '74, been Tellefsen — for the last eight years — and a Bergen man named Bredesen for the year between the inquiry and Tellefsen's signing. Bredesen had not been the kind of man Thoresen would have told. Tellefsen had not been the kind of man Thoresen would have told either; Tellefsen was the second kind of officer, the steady-second-rank-for-life kind, who did the work admirably and did not need to know the captain's three private institutions in order to do it.

Hestby was the third kind.

Hestby was, Thoresen had registered at the first interview at the Bergen quay in October, the young first mate the company had been sending him intermittently since the early '70s — the young first mate the company believed was going to be a captain, who was being deliberately routed through Thoresen's bark for the rougher half of the trade education, and who would, in five or seven or ten years, be at his own quarterdeck on his own bark with his own first mate at the rail. Thoresen had had four such young first mates in the years before Hestby. Of the four, two had become captains; one had been killed in a Bay of Biscay storm in '79; one had left the trade after his second voyage with Thoresen and had become — by Thoresen's last hearing — a clerk at a Stavanger insurance office.

Two of four had become captains.

The two who had become captains had not, before they became captains, learned about the three private arrangements. Thoresen had not told them. Their voyages had not, by the small ordinary luck of a Brooklyn stop, generated the situations that would have required the telling. They had finished their two voyages with him and had gone on to their own captaincies, and had — Thoresen suspected — built their own private arrangements at their own foreign ports without ever knowing that they were continuing a pattern Thoresen had begun.

Hestby was the first young first mate who had, by the small accident of a re-stamp at the Marine Underwriters' office and a missed ferry, generated the situation that had required the telling.

Thoresen had told him.

He had, at the morning interview, told him as little as the situation permitted — only the existence of the arrangement, only the small operational fact that the arrangement covered the loss of the pocket-book — and Hestby had taken the telling without pressing for more. The not-pressing had been, in itself, a piece of information about the kind of officer Hestby was. The two captains-of-four had also been the not-pressing kind. The other two had pressed; the one who had died in Biscay had pressed at the second week of the second voyage with such force that Thoresen had had to reprimand him; the clerk-who-had-left had pressed at the second voyage and then, finding the answers Thoresen would give insufficient, had left the trade.

Hestby had not pressed.

Hestby had taken the telling. He had said *Yes, sir.* He had gone to the deck.

That was, by Thoresen's nine-year accounting, the right answer.

---

Thoresen sat at his cabin desk at four bells of the second night-watch on the Wednesday of the third week of the Brooklyn stop.

The cabin was quiet. Tellefsen was on deck. The discharge of the petroleum was three days from completion; the loading of the small return cargo (Brooklyn-manufactured tools, oil-lamps, a small consignment of tobacco) would begin on the Friday. The bark would lift for the homeward leg in the second week of January. Thoresen had been at the cabin for two hours doing the small administrative work the captain did at the end of a Brooklyn stop — the small letters to the company at Bergen, the small consular forms, the small private letter to the Stavanger widow that he composed each Brooklyn stop and posted from the Bergen office on his return.

He had finished the company letters.

He had finished the consular forms.

He sat now with the small private letter to the Stavanger widow — Mrs. Asbjørnsen — in front of him. The letter was the seventh of the year. It was the same letter he had been composing at each Brooklyn stop since '76, with the small specific variations the year required. The letter said, in its standard form: *Mrs. Asbjørnsen — Your husband has not, by my information at this Brooklyn stop, made his return to a Norwegian-flag vessel during this voyage. I will continue, as I have for these years, to keep the inquiry running through the Brooklyn-Norwegian community. I am sorry to have no further news to give you. Your servant — G. Thoresen, Master, bark* President Daae.

He had been composing the letter for seven years.

He had not, in seven years, allowed himself to think — at the moment of the composing — that the letter was a small lie. The lie was the small part of the letter that implied his husband Asbjørnsen might still, in some imaginable future Brooklyn stop, return to a Norwegian-flag vessel. Thoresen knew, by Gjesdal's quiet word in '78, that Asbjørnsen had died at Brooklyn in the autumn of '77 of a fever that had taken several Norwegian-American sailors in that month, that the body had been buried at the Norwegian-American cemetery at Greenwood, and that the body had not been claimed by any family because the family did not know to claim it. Gjesdal had told Thoresen at the Brooklyn stop of '78. Thoresen had decided, at the cabin desk that year, that he would not relay the news to Mrs. Asbjørnsen. He had been continuing the letter ever since.

The reasoning had been the reasoning of a man who had decided, in 1874, that his career would be the career of a captain who paid privately for the consequences of decisions he had made publicly. Mrs. Asbjørnsen, Thoresen had concluded, would not be better served by knowing that her husband was buried at Greenwood. She would be better served by continuing to receive the annual letter that allowed her, in some part of herself, to imagine that her husband was somewhere on a Norwegian-American coaster, between voyages, perhaps about to return. The continuing letter was the small mercy the captain could provide. The cost was the sustained small lie. The captain had decided, in '78, that the cost was acceptable.

He composed the letter for the year.

He blotted it.

He folded it. He put it in the small envelope addressed to Mrs. Asbjørnsen at Stavanger. He sealed it with his small captain's wax-seal. He put the envelope in the inside pocket of his coat where the small private letters lived until Bergen.

---

He sat back in the chair.

He did not, in the next several minutes, do anything specific. He looked at the small framed photograph of his wife on the desk — the photograph that had been taken at Bergen in '79, in which she was at the small chair in the parlour with her left hand on the table beside her, in the small careful Norwegian-portrait pose of a woman of her generation. She was, in the photograph, fifty-two. She was now fifty-six. He had been married to her for thirty-three years.

He thought about her. He thought about the kitchen table at Bergen at which he had told her about the three arrangements in '76. He thought about the way she had said, that evening, that the arrangements were the right arrangements. He thought about the small letter from her that had reached him in the ordinary mail at the consul's office on the Tuesday of this week — a short letter, the small careful Bergen-housewife letter she sent him at every Brooklyn stop, with the small ordinary news of the household and the small reference she always made at the end to the children (now all of them grown, two of them married, the youngest at the Bergen polytechnic doing a course in some new kind of engineering).

He thought about his wife and Mrs. Asbjørnsen at the same time.

He thought: *my wife knows what I have decided. Mrs. Asbjørnsen does not. The two of them — both Norwegian women of the same generation — have, by my single decision in '78, been arranged into different categories of Bergen-and-Stavanger womanhood. My wife is the woman who knows. Mrs. Asbjørnsen is the woman who does not. The not-knowing is the mercy I am providing her at the price of the small annual letter. The knowing is the burden I am providing my wife at the price of seven years of her not-mentioning the burden at any kitchen table in either of our marriages.*

He thought: *I have, by my decisions across nine years, distributed knowledge unequally among the women in my life and at my profession's edge. The distribution is the captain's distribution. I have not, in nine years, found a better one.*

He looked at the photograph for another minute.

Then he opened the small leather-bound notebook on the bookshelf — the one Hestby had registered at the first interview as *the captain's notebook* — and he turned to the page where he kept the running record of the men he was currently sailing with.

The page had Hestby's name at the top, with the small column of three or four lines he had added since the first interview. The lines were short. They said, in his hand:

> *Hestby, O. Finnøy. 25. F. Brun's recommendation.*
>
> *First int.: composed. Did not press. Took the framing.*
>
> *Storm-handling third week: dog-watches. Correct. Did not show.*
>
> *Brace incident week 2: corrected forward. Did not push back.*
>
> *Bowery incident wk 10: re-stamp delay, missed ferry, alley with Peder. Gjesdal applied the arrangement. Officer untouched. The telling at the morning interview: did not press.*

He looked at the page for some seconds.

He picked up the pen.

He wrote, below the existing lines, in the same small careful captain's hand:

> *Conclusion at week 11 of voyage 1: this is the second of the four in the company's young-officer rotation since '74 who will, in five-to-seven years, be a captain Brun would sign on with. The first was Lothe in '77. This is the second.*

He blotted the page.

He closed the notebook.

He put the notebook back on the bookshelf in the small precise position it had been in for nine years.

---

He sat for another minute.

He thought about Hestby at the morning interview after the Bowery — the small composed face, the *Yes, sir,* the going to the deck without pressing. He thought about the difference between Hestby and the four previous young first mates. The two who had become captains had been like Hestby in the not-pressing. The two who had not been captains had been the pressing kind. The pressing kind, Thoresen had concluded, were the kind who needed to understand the captain in order to obey him; the not-pressing kind were the kind who obeyed first and built their understanding by observation across the years afterward. The first kind — by Thoresen's hard reading — burned out. The second kind made captains.

Hestby was the second kind.

Hestby was also — and this was the small thing Thoresen had not, in the nine years, articulated to himself before this Wednesday evening at the cabin desk — the first young first mate who, by his small composed presence at the chart-table at four bells of the morning watch each day, had begun to soften the captain's morning by being the man whose face was not afraid of the captain. The two captain-becoming young first mates before Hestby — Lothe and the man before Lothe — had been afraid of the captain at the morning interview. The fear had been, by Thoresen's reading, a function of their youth and of the captain's deliberate calibration. The fear had not been a problem; it had been an instrument. The instrument had worked.

Hestby was not afraid of the captain.

He was, by his bearing at the chart-table, the kind of young man who had been afraid of a worse captain in his eighteenth year and had, in the seven years since, processed the fear into the small flat composure that was now his daily register. Thoresen had not, until this Wednesday evening, known about Hestby's eighteenth year. The notebook on his bookshelf had only the small line *Brun's recommendation,* and Brun did not recommend a man on the basis of the man's eighteenth year. Brun recommended on the basis of competence. The competence Brun had recommended Hestby for was, Thoresen now understood at the desk, not the same thing as the not-being-afraid that Hestby brought to the chart-table each morning. The not-being-afraid had been built by some prior captain. Brun had refined it. Hestby brought it now to the *Daae.*

It softened, Thoresen registered at the desk with the small specific clarity of a man who had been in the cabin alone for some hours, the captain's morning.

The captain had not, in nine years, had a softened morning.

The captain had not, until this Wednesday evening, known he had wanted one.

He thought about the wanting for some seconds.

He decided, in the small flat way he decided most things at the cabin desk, that the wanting was a piece of him he was going to let exist for the remainder of the voyage and that he was not going to, by any small captain's adjustment, suppress.

He turned down the lamp.

He went to bed.

---

In the morning at four bells of the morning watch Hestby came to the chart-table for the daily report.

He gave the report in the small composed voice he had been giving it in for eleven weeks. The petroleum discharge was on schedule. The small return cargo would begin loading on the Friday. The crew was holding. The new common seamen had stopped, by the carpenter's quiet word, being the captain's projects; they were now competent. The bark was ready, by the small daily checklist Thoresen had been giving Hestby since the first week, for the homeward lift in eight days.

Thoresen took the report.

He did not, at the report, do anything different from what he had been doing for eleven weeks.

But at the small moment when Hestby was about to go forward, the captain looked up at him for two seconds longer than the small protocol of the morning interview required.

Hestby noticed.

He did not, by his small composed face, comment.

He went forward.

The cabin was again the captain's.

Thoresen did not, after Hestby had gone, write anything in the small leather-bound notebook. The two-second look at the morning interview was not the kind of thing the notebook recorded. The two-second look was the kind of thing the captain had decided, the previous evening at the desk, that he was going to let exist.

He had let it exist.

The morning was the morning.

The voyage went on.
