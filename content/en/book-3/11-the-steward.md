The bark lifted Brooklyn on the second Wednesday of January.

The homeward Atlantic crossing was not the crossing the outbound had been. The outbound had been the standard October-November westbound petroleum bark — fair winds for most of it, one bad storm at the Grand Banks, forty-two days. The homeward was a January-February eastbound bark with a partial cargo of Brooklyn-manufactured tools and oil-lamps and tobacco — light cargo, the bark riding high, and the wind systems of the North Atlantic in the deep winter giving the bark exactly the kind of mixed weather Tellefsen had warned Olav at the morning's officer's mess on the second day of the homeward to expect.

They had two storms in the first ten days. The first was an eight, the second a nine. The bark held. The crew worked. By the eleventh day they were past the worst of the winter weather and were running on a steady westerly at seven knots toward the European approaches.

It was on the fourteenth day of the homeward — at four bells of the second night-watch on a Tuesday — that the steward came at Olav with the whiskey-bottle.

---

The steward of the *President Daae* was a Bergen man of thirty-eight named Hov.

He had been the steward on the bark for three years. He was, by Tellefsen's quiet appraisal at the first week of the voyage, a competent steward in the small ordinary measures by which a Norwegian deep-water bark's steward was measured — he kept the captain's cabin clean, he served the meals on time, he did not steal from the lockers, he did not shirk. He had, however, by Tellefsen's same appraisal, the small particular weakness a deep-water steward sometimes acquired in middle age, which was the small private liking for whiskey he carried in a flask under his apron and consulted at intervals across the long watches he kept alone in the galley.

The flask was, by Thoresen's longstanding policy, permitted. The captain knew about it. He had elected not to make of it a captain's matter.

The whiskey-bottle was different.

Hov had, on the morning of the day in question, gone ashore at the small Cornish fishing village of St Just-in-Roseland (which the bark had passed close to in the small fair morning the previous day for a brief water-and-fresh-vegetables stop the captain had ordered as a precaution against the usual North Atlantic winter scurvy) and had bought, with the small private money he kept in his sea-chest, a full bottle of an English whiskey he had not previously had access to. He had brought the bottle aboard. He had, through the day, drunk most of it. By four bells of the second night-watch he was at the galley, more drunk than he had been at any point in his three years on the bark, and he had decided — by some small private logic of his accumulated grievances against the *Daae*'s small daily disciplines — that he was going to settle a particular grievance against the first mate.

The grievance was a small one. Olav had, that morning at the breakfast service, returned to Hov a piece of bread that Hov had served too dry. Hov had taken the bread back without comment. He had, at the galley, replaced it with a fresher piece. Olav had eaten the fresher piece. The exchange had been the small ordinary exchange between a first mate and a steward at a breakfast service. Olav had not, at the time, registered any tension. He had not registered, until later, that Hov had registered tension. He had not, at the time, known about the bottle in the steward's chest.

He learned about it at four bells of the second night-watch when Hov came up the forward companionway, in his apron with the bottle at his hip, and crossed the deck to where Olav was at the windward rail with a chart-correction in his hand.

---

Olav had the deck. Tellefsen was below at his cabin. Holm the bosun was forward at the windlass with the second mate of the night-watch checking a line that had begun to fray. The deck at the windward rail at that moment — apart from Olav and the man at the wheel ten feet aft — was empty.

Hov came across the deck. He came with the small unsteady walk of a heavily drunk man who had decided, in the deck's failing light, that the small unsteadiness was the price he was willing to pay to reach the man at the rail.

He stopped about three feet from Olav.

He did not, for some seconds, say anything.

Olav turned. He saw the steward. He saw the bottle in the steward's hand — held by the neck, swung idly at his side, full to about a quarter. He saw, in the small lamplight from the binnacle, the steward's face, which had the small particular flushed loosened expression of a man who had been at the bottle since the previous noon and had now, at four bells, reached the point of decision a heavy drinker reached after a sustained day's drinking.

Olav understood, in the count of two breaths, what was about to happen.

He understood it because he had, on the *Sigrid* in '76 at eighteen, seen Captain Gjermund's mate come at the carpenter at the same hour with the same kind of unsteady walk and the same kind of decision in the face. The mate had not, in that case, had a bottle. He had had a marlinspike. The carpenter had taken the marlinspike across the ribs at the moment Olav had, from the foretop, seen the mate's intention without having time to call the alarm. The carpenter had survived.

Olav did not, at the rail, have a marlinspike to dodge. He had a bottle.

He set down the chart-correction on the cap-rail beside him. He turned his body to face Hov fully. He kept his hands at his sides.

He spoke in his ordinary first mate's voice — neither louder nor quieter than the morning's voice, with the same small Bergen calibration he had been practising for eleven weeks.

"Hov."

"Hestby."

"You have been at the bottle."

"I have been at the bottle."

"You will give me the bottle."

"I will not give you the bottle."

"Then you will go below to your bunk. I will come down with you."

"I will not go below."

Olav considered.

He had, at the moment, three options. He could call the bosun forward — a shout of perhaps the volume Holm at the windlass would hear — and the bosun would come at a run and the situation would resolve with two men plus Olav against a heavily drunk steward with a bottle. He could attempt the bottle himself — step in, take the steward's wrist, twist the bottle loose. He could wait.

The third was, by the calculation a first mate made at the moment, the option that would best preserve Hov's dignity in the small Norwegian-bark community Hov was going to have to live in for the remaining year of the voyage. The first option would humiliate Hov publicly. The second would, by the arithmetic of the steward's drunkenness, probably involve Olav taking some part of the bottle. The third — waiting — would, by Olav's reading of the steward's face, allow the steward perhaps thirty more seconds to either close the situation himself by sitting down on a coil of rope or to escalate by swinging the bottle.

Olav waited.

The steward did not, in the next five seconds, sit down on a coil of rope.

He swung the bottle.

He swung it at Olav's head with the small unsteady coordination of a heavily drunk man whose aim was off by perhaps four inches but whose arc was — given that Olav had been standing three feet away — going to take the bottle into the side of Olav's head if Olav did not move.

Olav moved.

He moved by stepping inside the arc — toward Hov, not away from him — which was the move he had learned at the *Sigrid* foretop in '76 when Haakon had told him on his second day that the move a man made against a swung weapon was always inside the arc, because the arc had its full force at the end and the inside of the arc had no force at all. He stepped inside. The bottle came past the back of his head with the small whistle a heavy bottle made when it missed by inches in still air. Olav's hands came up — by the small body-knowing the *Sigrid* had given him at eighteen — to the steward's wrist and to the steward's elbow.

He took the wrist. He twisted the elbow. The bottle, at the end of an arm that had no leverage left at it, fell from the steward's hand. It hit the deck. It did not break — the deck was wood, and the bottle was a heavy English whiskey-bottle, and the angle was wrong for breaking — and rolled toward the cap-rail.

Olav held Hov by the wrist.

Hov did not, after the loss of the bottle, struggle. He stood for some seconds with his wrist in Olav's hand and his other hand at his side and his face — Olav could now see in the lamplight — going from the small flushed loosened expression to the small slack expression of a heavily drunk man who had, in the count of perhaps four seconds, understood what he had just done and had begun the small private arithmetic of what the consequences were going to be.

Olav let go of his wrist.

Hov did not move.

Olav said, in the same first mate's voice:

"Hov. Sit down on that coil of rope."

Hov sat down on the coil of rope.

Olav crossed to where the bottle had rolled. He picked it up. He uncorked it. He poured the remaining quarter into the scuppers. He put the empty bottle on the cap-rail.

He turned back to Hov.

"Hov."

"Hestby."

"You will, at four bells of the morning watch, come to the captain at the chart-table. I will be at the chart-table with you. You will tell the captain what you did. You will accept the discipline the captain assigns. The discipline will not be — by my best assessment of the captain — the discipline he would assign if I had not been in the way of the bottle. I was in the way. The bottle did not, by an accident of physics, take me. The captain will, by the small private arithmetic he keeps, take the not-taking into account."

Hov did not, for some seconds, answer.

Then he said:

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"I am sorry."

"You are sorry. Yes. We will see whether the captain takes the sorry."

"I do not, ordinarily, do this."

"I know. Tellefsen has told me. The bottle was the bottle."

"Yes."

"Go below to your bunk. Do not come up until the morning. The bosun will come for you at four bells."

"Yes."

Hov stood up — unsteadily, with his eyes on the deck — and walked to the forward companionway and went below. Olav watched him go. He turned to the man at the wheel — who had, throughout the exchange, stood at his post without speaking, because the man at the wheel did not interrupt a first mate's handling of a steward — and he said:

"You saw."

"I saw."

"Your name."

"Sigurdsen."

"Sigurdsen, you will not, at the watch's end, speak of this to any of the watch coming on. You will speak of it only to the bosun at six bells when I will tell him about it. Is that understood."

"Yes, sir."

"Good."

Olav picked up the chart-correction from the cap-rail. He picked up the empty bottle from the cap-rail. He went forward to find Holm.

---

He told Holm at the windlass at six bells.

The bosun took the news without expression. He listened. He nodded.

"Hov."

"Hov."

"He has been at the bottle for years. Not this much."

"Not this much."

"It was the Cornish bottle."

"It was the Cornish bottle."

"He will, at four bells of the morning, come to the captain."

"He will come."

"I will bring him. The captain will hear him. The captain will assign."

"Yes."

"Hestby."

"Bosun."

"You did not call me forward."

"I did not."

"Why not."

"Because the situation was a steward-and-first-mate situation. Calling the bosun forward would have made it a steward-and-bosun-and-first-mate situation. The steward will, in twelve months, still be the steward of this bark. The cleanest handling for the twelve months was the smallest handling for tonight. I gave it the smallest handling I could give it."

Holm looked at him for some seconds.

He nodded.

"Good. I have been at the *Daae* for ten years. I have not, in the ten years, seen a first mate handle a deck-incident in the smallest way it could be handled at the first attempt. The first mates have, all of them, called me forward. You did not. The not-calling is the thing the captain will register at four bells of the morning. He will not say so. The notebook on the bookshelf will say so."

"Yes."

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"You are the kind of first mate this bark has been waiting for, for nine years."

He went back to his line.

Olav stood at the windlass for a moment.

He did not, at the windlass, know what to do with what Holm had just said. He had not — in the count of seconds during the exchange — calculated his handling of Hov as a thing the bark or the captain or the bosun would register as the smallest possible handling. He had calculated it as the handling that would keep Hov dignified across twelve months. The smaller handling had been a side-effect of the dignity calculation. Holm had read it as the primary calculation. The reading was, Olav understood, perhaps more accurate than his own.

He went back to his deck.

The watch ended at eight bells. Tellefsen came up to relieve him. Olav handed off the deck. He did not, at the handing-off, tell Tellefsen about Hov. The telling would happen at the officer's mess at breakfast, which was the small Tellefsen-Olav daily exchange that handled the news of the night. He went below to his cabin.

---

In his cabin, before bed, he wrote in the small bound book.

> *14th day of homeward. Steward Hov came at me with a Cornish whiskey-bottle at four bells of the second night-watch. The bottle missed by inches. I took it from his hand. I emptied it into the scuppers. I sent him below. The bosun will bring him to the chart-table at four bells of the morning.*
>
> *Holm at the windlass said the not-calling-the-bosun was the right call. He said I am the kind of first mate the bark has been waiting for, for nine years. I do not know what to do with the saying. I will write it down so I have it.*
>
> *The Sigrid foretop in '76 — Haakon's lesson about the inside of the arc — was the lesson that took the bottle off my head tonight. Eight years between the lesson and its use. The body remembered.*

He blotted the page.

He blew out the lamp.

He slept the small uneven sleep of a man who had, in the count of perhaps a minute on a deck of a North Atlantic bark in winter, taken a piece of the trade his career had been waiting for him to take and had taken it in the way the trade required him to take it.

---

At four bells of the morning watch he and Holm and Hov stood at the chart-table in the captain's cabin.

Thoresen was at his desk. Olav had, before Hov was brought in, given the captain the small report of the previous night's events in the small flat first mate's report-voice. Thoresen had taken the report. He had asked one question — *was the bottle full or near-empty* — and Olav had said *near-empty.* Thoresen had nodded once. Then Holm had brought Hov in.

Hov was sober. He was, Olav saw at the small gray morning light through the cabin's window, deeply pale and deeply ashamed. He stood at the chart-table without looking up.

Thoresen looked at him.

"Hov."

"Captain."

"You came at the first mate with a bottle last night."

"Yes, captain."

"You missed."

"Yes, captain."

"Mr. Hestby took the bottle from your hand and emptied it into the scuppers."

"Yes, captain."

"You are sober now."

"Yes, captain."

Thoresen sat back in his chair.

He did not, for some seconds, speak.

Then he said:

"Hov. The discipline I would normally assign for a steward who came at the first mate with a bottle on a Norwegian deep-water bark is the discipline of dismissal at the next port and of a notation in the company's records that would prevent you from holding a steward's berth on any Bergen ship for ten years. That is the discipline. You know it is the discipline. The first mate knows it is the discipline. The bosun knows it. Three of the four men in this cabin know it."

"Yes, captain."

"I am not assigning the discipline."

Hov looked up.

"Captain?"

"I am not assigning the discipline because the first mate's small report this morning was the small report of a man who took the bottle off his own head, sent you below without breaking your dignity in front of the watch, and brought you to me at four bells with the request that I take the not-taking-of-his-head into account in the discipline I would ordinarily assign. He did not make the request in those words. He made it by the way he gave the report. I am taking it. The discipline I am assigning is this. You will be confined to the galley and your bunk for the remainder of the voyage. You will not be ashore at Bergen at the pay-off in March; you will sleep on the bark for that fortnight while the bark is in port. You will not be permitted any whiskey-bottles on the bark for the second outbound voyage in April. The flask you keep under your apron is permitted as before. If this incident or anything resembling it occurs again, the discipline I am suspending today will be applied without warning or appeal. Is that understood."

"Yes, captain."

"You will, in my view, also do something for the first mate that is not the captain's discipline but is the steward's debt. The thing is yours to choose. I am not specifying it. You will do it on your own account. Do not, in choosing it, embarrass the first mate by making the doing public. The doing should be the small private kind. Is that also understood."

"Yes, captain."

"You may go to your galley. Holm, take him."

Holm took Hov out.

Thoresen sat at the desk.

He did not, for some seconds, look at Olav.

Then he said:

"Hestby."

"Sir."

"You handled it."

"Yes, sir."

"You will, in your career, handle similar events. The events are part of the trade. I am not going to tell you you handled it well. Telling you would be the kind of telling Brun gives. I am not Brun. I am telling you instead that I have, this morning before you came in, written four lines about the handling in the small notebook on the bookshelf. The lines will, in three years or five, be the lines a Bergen company office reads when it is considering you for a captaincy. That is the recording. The recording is the praise."

"Yes, sir."

"You may go to the deck."

Olav went to the deck.

He took his watch.

The bark ran on east toward Bergen at seven knots in the small clean January morning that had, by the small accident of weather, become the kind of morning a first mate wanted at the morning after he had, in the count of perhaps a minute, taken a piece of the trade he had been waiting for the trade to give him.

The bottle was at the cap-rail still. Sigurdsen at the wheel — relieved at eight bells, now back on the morning watch — had, at six bells, asked Olav whether the bottle could be cleaned and used at the steward's table for vinegar. Olav had said yes. Hov, by Olav's small private later registering, had taken the bottle from the cap-rail at the breakfast service, washed it at the galley, and had begun, in the small reformative gesture only a steward would think to make, the work of converting it to a vinegar-cruet.

Olav had not, at the breakfast, registered the conversion in any way Hov could see.

He had, in his head, registered it.

The captain's *the doing should be the small private kind* had been honored by Hov in the smallest possible way. The vinegar-cruet would, for the remaining year of the voyage, sit at the steward's table at every meal as the small daily reminder of the night the bottle had not taken the first mate's head.

That was, by the small economy of a Norwegian deep-water bark, the steward's debt being paid.

The bark ran east.
