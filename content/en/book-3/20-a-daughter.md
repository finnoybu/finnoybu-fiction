The daughter was born at the cottage at Finnøy on the morning of the ninth of June, 1886.

Olav was not, at the moment of the birth, in the cottage. He was at the boat-shed at his father's, as his mother and Mrs Lindøy had arranged across the previous two weeks. The arrangement a Norwegian island household of their period made at the birth of a first child had placed the husband at the boat-shed and the father-in-law at the woodpile and the young wife's brother-in-law (Eirik, who had come over from Mosterøy on the Monday at Marit's word) at the small outside garden-fence with the Norwegian-island work of not-being-present-in-the-birth-room that the men of their generation had been given for generations.

Mrs Hestby was at the bedside.

Mrs Lindøy was at the bedside.

Marit was at the kitchen with the small clean towels.

The Stavanger midwife — a Mrs Pettersen, whom Mrs Hestby had arranged for at the small Stavanger office six weeks before, who had come across on the morning coaster on the seventh and had stayed at the small spare bed at Jens's house since — was at the position the midwife took at a birth, which was the foot of the bed with her hands free.

The birth took, by the kitchen clock Mrs Hestby had brought over from Jens's, six hours.

---

At half past eleven on the morning of the ninth Mrs Hestby came to the boat-shed door and said, in her small composed voice:

"Olav."

"Yes, mother."

"You have a daughter."

He set down the plane.

He came to the door.

"Olava."

"Olava is well. The birth was long but clean. Mrs Pettersen is pleased. Your daughter is five pounds and ten ounces and has a full head of dark hair."

"I may come."

"You may come now."

He came up the path with his mother. His father, at the woodpile, did not come with them; his father had the rule of his own generation that the first meeting between the newly-delivered husband and his wife and child should be without a grandfather in the room, and Jens had kept the rule since his own first child's birth in '59. Eirik, at the garden-fence, also stayed. Marit, at the kitchen, had put the small water to boil for a tea and did not, at the coming-up-the-path, break the small ceremony.

Olav came into the cottage.

Mrs Lindøy was at the kitchen. She stood aside at the door of the bedroom.

Mrs Pettersen was packing her small midwife's bag at the small parlour-table. She looked up. She nodded once at Olav. She did not speak.

Olav went into the bedroom.

Olava was propped up on the small pillows his mother and Mrs Lindøy had arranged. She was pale and tired and the small sweat of the birth was still at her hairline. She was in the small clean nightdress Mrs Hestby had brought over from Jens's that morning. Her eyes, when she turned them to the door, had the exhausted-bright look of a twenty-five-year-old Norwegian woman who had just, in the six hours, done the thing her body had been preparing for for nine months.

At her breast was the small wrapped bundle of the child.

She said, in the small quiet voice of a woman who had been working for six hours and did not yet have much voice left:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"Come and meet her."

He came to the bed.

He sat, carefully, on the edge of the bed beside her.

He looked at the child.

The child was the small new human creature of the kind he had, in his twenty-seven and three months, never before been this close to. She was very small. She was red at the cheeks and pale at the forehead. Her eyes were closed. Her hair, at the top of her head, was the dark hair Mrs Hestby had described — not the small pale-blond a Norwegian baby of the parish often had, but a definite small dark head, which Mrs Lindøy had said at the kitchen a moment before was the captain's hair-colour as he had been at his birth in 1817, her father's mother's record.

The child was breathing.

Olav registered the breathing with the same small careful attention he had registered the breath of the crew of the *Daae* at the bare-poles run in the force-eleven storm of the third voyage. The registering was the professional-listening a deep-water first mate did at the moments at which the continuation of someone else's life was the fact at issue. He had not expected to bring the professional-listening to the cottage's small bedroom. He had brought it. The listening worked the same way.

The child was breathing fine.

He put his hand on Olava's hand, very gently.

He said:

"Olava."

"Yes."

"Thank you."

"You are welcome."

"The name."

"Anna."

"Anna."

"As we said."

"As we said. Anna Hestby."

"Anna Hestbye, at the parish register, in the old spelling."

"At the register, Hestbye. At the kitchen, Hestby. At the small baptismal line we will sign on the Sunday two weeks from now, Hestby."

"Yes."

"Olav."

"Yes."

"She has hair like my father."

"Mrs Lindøy said so."

"I had not, in the nine months, allowed myself to imagine what the hair would be. The hair is what the hair is. I am pleased."

"I am pleased too."

He sat, carefully, beside her.

The child slept at her breast.

---

Mrs Hestby brought the tea at the small door a few minutes later. She did not come in. She set the tray at the small table beside the door. She said, quietly:

"Olav. You have ten minutes. Then she sleeps. Then you go and tell your father."

"Yes, mother."

She went out.

Olav took the tea from the tray. He gave it to Olava at the bedside. She drank from it. The drinking was slow. She was tired.

He sat with her for the ten minutes.

He did not, in the ten minutes, say anything large. The conversation the ten minutes held was the kind of conversation a husband and his wife had at the bedside in the small exhausted-bright minutes after a first child's birth — the conversation of the small practical things: when the midwife would come back the next day for the small check; whether Mrs Lindøy would stay at the cottage through the week; when Mrs Hestby would begin the small supper-cycle she had been planning; what the baptism Sunday arrangement would be.

They worked out the small practical things.

He kissed her on the forehead at the end of the ten minutes.

He stood.

He went out.

---

At the garden-fence his father was still at the woodpile.

Olav came up to him.

"Father."

"Olav."

"I have a daughter."

"Yes."

"Anna."

"Anna."

"At the old spelling, Hestbye."

"Good. The line continues. Mrs Pettersen?"

"Pleased."

"Olava?"

"Well. Tired. The birth was six hours."

"Good."

He set down the axe he had been splitting with.

He came to Olav. He put his hand — his good hand, the one that was not his plane-hand — on the back of Olav's neck. He held it there for the count of perhaps four breaths. That was the Hestby household's gesture at moments like this one. It had been his father's gesture at Olav's own birth in '59 (his mother had later told him). It had been his grandfather's gesture at his father's birth in '33 (his great-uncle's testimony). The gesture was the gesture. Jens held it. Then he let go.

He went into the cottage to meet his granddaughter.

Olav stayed at the garden-fence.

He looked at the channel.

The channel in the middle of the morning of the ninth of June was the small channel the cottage's view included — a narrow strip of grey-blue running between the island and the mainland, with the small Mosterøy coast a darker shape on the far side.

He registered, at the channel:

*A daughter. She has her grandfather's hair. She is five pounds and ten ounces. She is at her mother's breast. She will, by Pastor Kleven's next Sunday arithmetic, be baptized Anna Hestbye at the Hestby church on the hill on the third Sunday of June. She will be, from the baptism, a Hestbye in the parish register and a Hestby at the household. She will, like her father and like her father's father and like her father's grandfather, be a Hestby at both spellings in the two ways the parish keeps the two spellings.*

*The line continues.*

*The old Olav who was at the Stavanger quay in June of '81 with the wool coat and the first kiss could not, at eighteen, have imagined this morning. He had, at eighteen, imagined a life at sea. He had imagined the specific voyages he might sail. He had not, at eighteen, imagined a small morning at the garden-fence of a cottage he had built on a small share of his father's land with a daughter asleep at her mother's breast inside the cottage and his own father inside now holding the daughter for the first time. The eighteen-year-old Olav had not imagined it because the trade had not yet shown him the shape of the life that would make the imagination possible.*

*The trade has shown him the shape.*

*The shape is this morning.*

He turned from the fence.

He went back into the cottage.

His father was at the small bedroom door with Mrs Hestby beside him. Jens had, in the way a Hestby grandfather had been given his granddaughter at this moment for four generations, held her briefly and given her back.

Olav stood at the door for a moment.

He did not, at the door, say anything.

He took the small dark-haired bundle of Anna Hestbye into his own arms for the first time.

She was warm. She weighed five pounds and ten ounces. She was asleep. She did not, in his arms, wake.

He held her for the count of perhaps two minutes.

Then he gave her back to Olava at the bed.

---

The household came together at the cottage that afternoon for the small first family supper of the daughter's day.

Mrs Lindøy, Marit, Eirik, Mrs Hestby, Jens, Olav. Olava stayed in the bedroom; the supper was brought to her on the small tray Mrs Hestby had prepared. The others ate at the cottage's small parlour-table that had been Mrs Hestby's writing-desk in the '60s.

The supper was quiet.

The captain, at Lindøy, had been told by the small Monday coaster's messenger (Aanestad's grandson) who had come across at the midday to carry the news from Mrs Lindøy to Lindøy. The captain would come across on the Wednesday coaster for the small grandfather-meeting. The Thursday would be the small Hestby-Lindøy family supper at the cottage. The Sunday after would be the baptism.

The small household-choreography ran, across the afternoon, at the small pace Mrs Hestby had designed.

At six Jens stood up at the small table.

"Good supper, Olava's mother. Good supper, Mrs Hestby." (He named both his wife and his daughter-in-law's mother in the same breath, at the small Jens convention for the formal occasions.)

"Thank you, Jens."

"Good day, Olav."

"Yes, father."

"I am going back to the workbench for the small last hour of the day. You stay with your wife."

"Yes, father."

He took his coat from the hook. He went out.

The small afternoon went on.

---

That night at the small bedside — Mrs Lindøy and Mrs Hestby and Marit had gone back to their respective kitchens at half past eight, and the cottage was the cottage's — Olav sat at the small chair beside the bed with Olava and Anna at the small bed. Anna was asleep. Olava was, finally, after the long exhausting day, sleeping. The lamp at the bedside was low.

Olav did not sleep.

He sat at the chair for perhaps the first hour of the small night. He watched Anna's breath. He watched Olava's breath. The two breaths were in a small unsynchronized pair — Olava's slow deep adult breath, Anna's small fast new-human one — and the pair was the sound the cottage's bedroom would have in it for the foreseeable years.

He thought, at the chair:

*I am twenty-seven years and three months. Olava is twenty-five and a quarter. Anna is half a day. My father at my birth was twenty-seven. My grandfather at my father's birth was thirty-eight. My great-grandfather at my grandfather's birth was forty-seven. The men of the line have been, across the line, growing younger at the first child. The line is shortening forward. Anna, if she is born on this pattern to her own child, will at twenty-three or twenty-four have her first. That places the next generation of Hestbys at approximately 1910. I will be, in 1910, fifty-two. I will, by the Bergen bank's actuarial tables, see the next generation's baptism.*

*I will, if the small actuarial reasonings hold, also see my own grandchildren's births.*

*I had not, at eighteen, imagined this.*

*I have, at twenty-seven and three months, imagined it.*

He sat with the thought for another minute.

He blew out the lamp.

He got into the bed beside Olava — carefully, so as not to wake the child — and he slept for the first night as a father.

The cottage at Finnøy on the ninth-into-tenth of June of '86 held its three occupants — one of them half a day old — in the small quiet a Norwegian island cottage held on the first night of its first child's life.

The line continued.

The small first land year had made a second person at the kitchen.

The third person was asleep between them at the bed.

Olav slept.
