The years between 1911 and 1918 were the years the children became adults.

Anna at Stavanger with Henrik Berg and their four children (the fourth — a small girl, Helga — was born in the summer of 1912); by 1918 Anna was thirty-two. She taught at a Stavanger primary-school three mornings a week. She brought her children across on the small Tuesday coaster to Finnøy one Tuesday a month in the summers. She wrote Olava a letter every Friday. She was, by the small family observation, the kind of woman her mother had been at the same age — quiet, competent, the small watchful observer of her own household.

Jens the younger was at Bergen through to 1914, when he took a first mate's berth on a Bergen-Rotterdam bark and moved to a small lodging at Sandviken. He married, in 1916, a Bergen woman named Ragnhild Haugen — by small coincidence Olav did not mention, she was a distant cousin (by her grandmother's side) of Sven Haugen of the *Semiramis;* the coincidence had been registered by Olav at the small moment Ragnhild's small family-tree was named at the engagement supper, and had been kept by him in the interior register that still ran for such things. He did not tell Olava the coincidence. The not-telling was the form. He did, however, at the cottage's workbench on the Sunday after the engagement supper, write a small additional line on the small Haakon-sheet of '11 that now lived in the drawer:

> *Addendum, 19 November 1916. Jens the younger is engaged to Ragnhild Haugen of Bergen, distant cousin of Sven Haugen of Sandviken. Not told to Olava. The form holds.*

The drawer now had six items.

Jens the younger and Ragnhild married at Bergen in the spring of 1917. They had their first child — a boy, Olav Hestby the second — at the small Bergen lodging in the late autumn of 1918.

Ingrid, the youngest, did not marry through these years. She was, at twenty in 1911, at the small parish-school teaching her mother had done; by 1914 she had taken the small Stavanger teacher-training course Olava had identified for her, and by 1915 she was at the Stavanger town-school teaching the small fourth-form class. She lived at the small Lindøy household for the weekdays (Mrs Lindøy had died in 1913 at the age of seventy-nine, and the captain — still alive, by the small small arithmetic of his stubborn longevity, at ninety-six — needed the small weekday company Ingrid provided at the Lindøy kitchen); she came across to Finnøy on the weekends. Ingrid, by the small family observation, was going to be the small single-daughter the household always produced at this generation, the one who did not marry because the marrying had not arrived and the work of the parish-school and the aging grandfather had filled the life she had built for herself.

She was not, by Olava's careful observation across the small evening coffees at Lindøy when Olava came over to visit, unhappy.

She was the Ingrid her great-aunt had been in 1870-76 before Ingrid's death at the small Lindøy illness in '76. The aunt had lived to thirty-eight. Ingrid the younger had the same small shape.

---

Olav retired from the *Helga* in the autumn of 1910.

He had, by the arithmetic Houlder's office made at the two-hundred-round mark of his captaincy, been at the *Helga* for fifteen years by the September of 1910. Houlder had offered him the retirement arrangement on generous terms — a pension from the owner-share arrangement of two hundred kroner a quarter, to continue for ten years past retirement or until Olav's death, whichever came first — and Olav had taken it. His last *Helga* round lifted Stavanger on the second Wednesday of September and made Bergen on the small clean morning of the seventeenth. At Bergen the small Houlder office had a farewell supper at the Exchange; the new captain — a Haugesund man named Bjorn Eliassen, who had been Olav's first mate for four years — stood at Olav's right at the small table and at the supper's end took Olav's small *Helga*-captain's cap from Olav's head in the ritual a Stavanger coastal company used for its retiring masters. The cap went in a glass case at the Houlder office at the wall where the seven previous retired masters' caps hung.

Olav's cap was the eighth.

He came home to Finnøy on the small Friday coaster from Bergen.

He was at the cottage on the Saturday morning. At the cottage he wrote a small single-line entry in the small archive-sheet he had started at the Asiatic-Society-book of 1881 — an archive-sheet that had, across thirty years, accumulated perhaps fifteen entries total, one for each small structural change in the trade-shape of his life:

> *17 September 1910. Retired from the Helga of Stavanger. Captain Bjorn Eliassen is the new master. The cap is at the Houlder office wall.*

He blotted the page.

He closed the small archive-book.

He put it back in the small leather folder.

---

The small retirement years that followed were the retirement years a Norwegian island retired captain of his kind had.

He was at the cottage's workbench on most mornings. He worked — at the small leisure of a man who did not need to — on the small carpentry that the larger house and the cottage always required. He built, in the summer of '12, a small new porch on the cottage's south side; in the summer of '14, a small new shed for the small cold-weather firewood. He went to the small Lindøy-Hestby Sunday suppers that had rotated between the two households across the decades. He went to the small Hestby church on the hill on most Sundays with Olava, where Pastor Øverbye was nearing his own retirement at sixty-eight.

He read.

He read, across the retirement years, at a scale he had not been able to read at across the working decades. He read the small Stavanger papers daily. He read the small Bergen weekly on the Tuesdays. He read, at Olava's suggestion in the winter of '13, the small Norwegian translation of a Russian novel Olava had borrowed from the Stavanger library — a large book that took him all of January and February to work through. He read, after the Russian, a small Norwegian translation of a Greek poem Olava had borrowed at her second Stavanger visit. He read a small local history of Stavanger shipping that had been published in '10. He read, in the small evenings when Olava was at her writing-desk with her own small work, whatever small books came to the household through the small Lindøy-Hestby-Schmidt annual small exchanges.

He had not, in his first thirty-five years at sea, read much.

He made up, in his retirement years, some of the small reading he had not done.

---

Olava, across the same years, did the work of an island retired-captain's wife of her generation.

She continued the small parish-school letter-mornings for the small youngest children, through 1915 when the teacher the parish had been training at Stavanger arrived at the Finnøy school and Olava turned over the work. She kept the small writing-desk correspondence with her adult daughter and her newly-married son and her not-yet-married daughter. She went to Mrs Hestby's (Mrs Hestby died in the autumn of 1914 at ninety-one, the second-oldest parish-death of the year) at the small Tuesday afternoons until the death. She arranged, across the six months after Mrs Hestby's death, the work of closing Mrs Hestby's kitchen and transferring the household-items (the grandmother-candlestick that had been at the Lindøy side in the 1840s, the small blue Bergen kitchen-plate, the two spoons Mrs Hestby had used since 1858) to the new places they would live in the three Hestby-daughter households.

She managed Ingrid's small weekday absence at Lindøy with Captain Lindøy by the small schedule she worked out with Ingrid at the summer of '13.

She managed the small correspondence with Jens the younger and Ragnhild at Bergen.

She kept, across these years, her own small private indent at her own small personal-notebook — the small notebook that had succeeded the three voyage-ledgers of the 1880s and the small engagement-year notebook of 1880. The new notebook was her own ongoing small record of the household's years. She wrote in it, at the small writing-desk, perhaps once a fortnight. She did not share it with anyone.

---

In the autumn of 1917 — the year Olav was fifty-nine and Olava fifty-seven — Captain Lindøy died at ninety-nine at the small back bedroom at Lindøy.

The captain had been, across the forty-four years of Olav's knowing him, the fixed point of the Lindøy household. He had managed the wooden hand across the decades and the Lindøy household across the decades and the Christmas morning of 1879 at which he had, from the parlor of the Lindøy house, been the unseen father while Olava and Olav had made the Christmas agreement at the back bedroom above the kitchen. He died at the small back bedroom with Ingrid at the bedside and Olava at the door, and Olav's father's brother at the small kitchen working out — in the small last specific Lindøy-Hestby co-operation — the small telegram to Jens the younger at Bergen and to Henrik Berg at Stavanger. The body went to the Lindøy-church burial on the Saturday.

The captain's wooden hand was the one small private thing Olav watched Olava pack, at the small Tuesday afternoon two weeks after the funeral, into a small wooden box for the transport across the channel. The wooden hand was, by the captain's will, going to Jens the younger — the grandson who bore the Hestby name but whose face, at twenty-nine at the funeral, had the captain's stubborn-eye that none of the other three grandchildren had.

Jens the younger received the wooden hand at Bergen in the December of 1917.

The wooden hand was, from December 1917, at the small Bergen lodging's parlor as a mantelpiece object. It would, across the decades, remain in the Jens Hestby the younger line.

---

At the supper at the larger house on the evening of the small Saturday of the Lindøy funeral, Olava said, in the small composed voice she had used at the small structural moments for thirty-eight years:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"My father is gone."

"Yes."

"I am, from tonight, without parents."

"I am — by the small arithmetic of my own parent-death-dates — without parents from the autumn of 1914 when Mrs Hestby died. You are from tonight in the same state I have been in for three years."

"Yes."

"Olav."

"Yes."

"We are, at fifty-nine and fifty-seven, the old generation at the two kitchens. Our children are the middle generation at Stavanger and Bergen. Anna's oldest is at eleven and at the Stavanger school; that child is the beginning of the small new generation."

"Yes."

"I had not, at twenty in November of 1880, worked the small arithmetic forward to this Saturday."

"No. I had not either."

"We have, across thirty-seven years of the marriage, become the old generation. The two of us are, at the small Saturday supper at the larger house at Finnøy in the autumn of 1917, the pair the families now look to as the grandparents at the two channels."

"Yes, Olava."

"Good."

She did not, at the table, say more.

They finished the supper.

They went to bed at ten.

The house at Finnøy on the Saturday evening of the autumn of 1917 held its three occupants (Ingrid had come back from Lindøy for the funeral and was at the small upstairs bedroom) in the small ordinary quiet of a Norwegian island household at the close of a parental generation.

The small new generation had begun at Stavanger and Bergen.

The small old generation had settled at Finnøy.

The form held.

The household went on.
