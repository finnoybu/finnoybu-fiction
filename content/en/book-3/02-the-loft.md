The wedding-day dinner ran until four o'clock.

By four o'clock the families had eaten the herring and the smoked mutton and the bread and had had their coffee and their two pieces each of the small honey-cake. The captain had told the one wedding-day story he was going to tell, which was the story of his own father's wedding at the Lindøy church in 1810, in which the pastor of the time had — by family memory — fallen halfway through the service and had been propped up at the altar by the captain's uncles for the remaining ten minutes. The story was the captain's standard wedding contribution; he had told it, Olav had been informed, at every Lindøy family wedding for forty years. He told it at this one. The table laughed. He drank the small glass of his own aquavit Mrs. Lindøy poured him at the end of it.

After the story the small business of the dinner's ending began.

Mrs. Lindøy and Olav's mother began clearing plates. Marit and Eirik — who had come over with the Schmidt family on the morning boat — took the cake-plates to the larder. Olav's father and Captain Lindøy went out to the porch with the captain's pipe to discuss something they had been quietly waiting all afternoon to discuss alone, which was — Olav understood from the way they walked out — the sale of the small share Captain Lindøy held in a coastal smack at Lindøy that he had decided, with the wedding done, to transfer to Olav as part of the small dowry the captain had been planning for some months and had not yet, at this hour, formally raised.

The kitchen, at the cleared table, was Olav and Olava and the two mothers and Marit.

Mrs. Lindøy looked at her older daughter once. She looked at Olav. She set the dishcloth on the rim of the basin. She said, in the small composed voice she used at the moments she had been preparing for a long time:

"Olava."

"Yes."

"Take your husband up to the loft."

"Yes."

"The lamp at the loft is full. The water-pitcher is on the washstand. The bed has been turned. You will not be disturbed."

"Yes, Mother."

Mrs. Lindøy turned to her dishes.

Olava stood. She took Olav's hand. She walked him out of the kitchen and up the stairs to the loft.

---

The loft was the loft Olav had slept in for the four months of the engagement when he had been at Lindøy, and now — for the first time, since they were at Finnøy — it was the loft of his father's house, which Olav had slept in every night of his life until he had gone to sea and that Olava had slept in only twice (once on a Christmas visit in '81 when his parents had been at Stavanger, once on a single afternoon nap during the Easter visit). The loft at Finnøy was not the loft at Lindøy. It was smaller. It had the window at the gable end that gave onto the boat-shed and the channel beyond. The bed was the narrow bed Olav had slept in alone since he was sixteen, and the bed had been turned — by his mother, in the morning before he was up — into a wider bed with a second pallet and an extra blanket and a pillow that had not been there in the morning.

His mother had done the turning. She had not announced it. She had simply done it, the way a Norwegian mother of her generation prepared a son's loft on the morning of the son's wedding for the bride who would, that night, sleep in it.

Olava saw the bed. She did not, on the seeing, say anything.

She went to the window. She looked at the channel. The channel in the late afternoon of the fourteenth of March had the particular gray-light Olav had been carrying in his head for fifteen months at sea — flat, low, the early-evening gray of a Norwegian island before the lamps came on.

"Olav."

"Yes."

"Come and look."

He came to the window beside her.

They stood at the window for some minutes without speaking. The Lindøy coast was a darker gray across the channel. A small boat — one of the Mosterøy fishermen, Olav guessed by the stem — was working its way home with a single lateen sail and the small steady purpose of a man who had been at his nets since dawn. The air at the window was cold.

Olava took her hand from the window-sill and put it on his.

"I thought, this morning at the jetty," she said, "of standing at this window and looking at the channel from the Finnøy side. I had not, in the year and a half, looked at it from the Finnøy side. The wool coat had only ever looked at it from the Lindøy side. I am, this evening, looking at it from the Finnøy side for the first time."

"Yes."

"That is what marrying is, in part. It is the side of the channel one looks at."

"Yes."

She turned from the window. She came into the room, took off her cap, hung it on the hook by the door, came to the bed, sat on the edge of it. She undid the small pearl button at the collar of her dress. She did this without ceremony, in the small composed practical motions of a woman who had decided, before she came up the stairs, what she was going to do and at what pace she was going to do it.

She looked at Olav at the window.

"Olav."

"Yes."

"Come."

He came.

---

He had not, in the four months between the homecoming and the wedding, allowed himself to picture this evening with any specificity.

He had pictured it in general — the loft, the lamp, the night — but he had not pictured the small particular things, because he had understood at some level that the picturing would have either set up an expectation the actual evening would not match, or it would have anticipated the evening to a degree that would have spoiled the actual having of it. He had let the evening, in the months of the engagement, be a thing his head approached only at the edges.

The evening was the evening.

It was tender. It was brief — by the small private register a man and a woman of their generation kept for the first night of a marriage — and it was present. Neither of them was an experienced person in the things their bodies were now doing. Both of them had read the small careful books their mothers had given them in the weeks before the wedding (her mother had given Olava one in November; his mother had given Olav one in February); both of them had asked the small respectful questions of the older married women in the families who were authorized to be asked (Marit's older cousin at Mosterøy had answered Olava's; Olav's uncle's wife had answered Olav's, in the small kitchen at Mosterøy on a Sunday in January when Olav had walked over for the conversation his mother had explicitly told him to have); both of them had come to the loft this evening with the small composed plan of being kind to each other at the moments at which kindness was the thing the moments required.

They were kind.

The kindness was not what Olav had imagined — he had imagined nothing specific to be kind about — but it was, when the moments came at which the kindness mattered, the thing the two of them were able to give each other. Olava was, in particular, kind to him at one moment in which a less calibrated woman would have laughed and a less prepared woman would have apologized. She did neither. She simply made a small motion with her hand to indicate the small mechanical adjustment that needed to be made, and the adjustment was made, and the moment continued. The smallness of her motion — the un-fuss of it — was, he understood at the moment, the small lesson the marriage was teaching him at hour one of its existence: she would not make of his small confusions anything they did not need to be made into. He registered it. He filed it. He did not, at the moment, say so.

Afterward they lay beside each other in the small wider bed his mother had made for them.

The lamp was low. The window was dark. The channel outside was the channel.

Olava said, after a while:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"I do not think it is going to be the kind of thing the books said it would be on the first night, every night."

"No."

"I think it is going to be its own kind of thing each night, and the each-night is going to be what it is by the way the day was, and the way the day was is going to depend on the small things the day was made of."

"Yes."

"That is what I was thinking on the way up the stairs."

"It is what I am thinking now."

"Good."

She put her head on his shoulder. The position was, he registered, a position she had rehearsed in her head some evening in the four months — perhaps the Easter Saturday at the bench, perhaps the Christmas-Eve garden, perhaps an evening in February at the Lindøy kitchen window when she had been alone — and that she had decided was the position the first night was going to end on. She held the position for some time. He did not move. He did not, in fact, want to move.

After perhaps fifteen minutes she said:

"I am cold."

"I will get the second blanket."

"No. Stay. I am cold and I am not asking you to fix the cold. I am only telling you. The fixing is a separate question."

"Yes."

"This — the having the cold and telling you about it — this is also part of it, I think."

"Yes."

"I will, in a minute, get the second blanket myself."

"Yes."

She did not, in the minute, get the second blanket. She lay against him and was cold. He was warm. The small physical fact of his warmth flowing slowly into her side under the one blanket was the conversation the two of them, after the brief tender main event of the evening, were having instead of speaking. He understood at the conversation that this — the lying and the warming — was, in some small way he had not previously had the vocabulary for, the thing his mother had been preparing for since the morning he had been baptized in the Hestby church.

Eventually Olava reached down and pulled up the second blanket.

She tucked it around them both.

She put her head back on his shoulder.

She slept, slowly, the small careful sleep of a woman who had been awake since five in the morning of her wedding day and who had, by ten in the evening of it, used up the day's preparations and was now in the part of the day for which no preparations had been made. He lay beside her. He did not sleep at once. He listened to the small things — the wind on the gable end, the small creak of the loft's beams in the cold, the distant sound of the kitchen down the stairs where his mother and Mrs. Lindøy were, by some unstated agreement, washing up together — and he registered, with the small flat clarity he used for the moments of his life that he intended to remember in their particulars, the first night of his marriage as it actually was, which was the first night of his marriage as he was going to remember it for the rest of his life.

He thought, briefly, of all the men he had been in the catalogue at the loft on the night before the wedding.

He thought: *they are all here. They are not at this moment in the loft with me; they are in me. Olava has, by her small kind motion at the moment of the small mechanical adjustment, married all of them at once. I do not have to introduce them to her. She married them in the dark.*

He closed his eyes.

He slept against her shoulder, slowly, the small first sleep of a married man on the wedding night that, at the moment of the sleeping, had become — without ceremony, without the trumpets the picturing-mind would have put on the moment — the ordinary first night of an ordinary good marriage.

In the kitchen below, his mother and Mrs. Lindøy washed the last of the cake-plates and put them away in the cupboard and turned down the lamp and went each to her own arrangement for the night — Mrs. Lindøy to the back room with the captain, Olav's mother to the side bed with Jens — and the house at Finnøy on the night of the fourteenth of March, 1883, held in its quiet the new married couple and the four parents who had given them to each other and the small Marit-and-Eirik party who had bedded down in the parlor with cushions, and the wedding day passed at last into the wedding's first night, which was the night the marriage began for real.

The bark *Semiramis,* by Brun's letter to Olav at the end of February, had lifted Bergen on the morning of the previous Friday for the run to Calcutta with a different first mate.

Sven, by Torgerson's letter from Sandviken at the same time, was at his apartment with Hanne and the boy Jonas, between voyages, due to sail on a North Sea coaster in April.

Haakon, by no source Olav had any contact with, was — Olav had to assume — at Shields or at Bergen on the *Norden,* doing the work he had been doing for five years.

Anders, by no source, was at Stockholm.

Olav slept against Olava's shoulder.

The drawer at the boat-shed held the two letters.

The wedding had been done.
