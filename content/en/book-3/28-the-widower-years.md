The years between 1924 and 1933 were the small widower-years of Olav's life.

Ingrid stayed at the larger house with him. She was thirty-four at Olava's death and had not married. She would not, across the nine years that followed, marry. She kept the housekeeping the larger house required and the parish-school work she had been doing since '15 and the daily companionship a Norwegian island unmarried daughter gave a widowed father of her generation. She did it without the resentment the position would have produced in some daughters; Ingrid had her own life at the parish school and at the small Lindøy-Hestby letter-correspondence-circle she had maintained across the years, and the sharing of the larger house's kitchen with her father was the small ordinary extension of the life she had chosen.

Anna came across from Stavanger one Tuesday a month with her children, as she had for years.

Jens the younger and Ragnhild brought Olav the second across from Bergen twice a year — once at Christmas and once at the summer. Olav the second was six at Olava's death, and ten by the spring of '28, and fifteen by the summer of '33. He was a reader of books, as his aunt Ingrid was. He was not, by the small family observation of the later years, going to be a sailor; he had his mother's eye for numbers and would, by his parents' small careful planning, go to the Bergen commercial-school at sixteen and into the Bergen shipping-office world from there. Jens the younger, at the kitchen-table conversations at the summer visits, was reconciled to this. The small Hestby-sailor line had gone two generations and had, at the third, turned to the shore. This was — Jens the younger said at the small supper of the summer of '31 — the Norwegian-Bergen pattern of his generation's families. The shipping trades produced sailors for the fathers and bookkeepers for the sons and, by the grandsons, office-men.

Olav received the small trend without protest.

He had, across his retirement years since 1910, been the widower-of-the-trade he had been part of. The *Helga* run had closed in 1919 when Houlder's son had retired the bark at forty-one years of service and had broken her up at a Stavanger scrapyard. Bjorn Eliassen — the new master who had succeeded Olav in '10 — had retired in '21 and had died in '23 in a Stavanger illness. The small Houlder office had closed in '27 when the Houlder grandson — Helge Houlder's son Sigurd, who had inherited the firm at the Helge Houlder death in '19 — had decided that the small-coaster trade was no longer a viable business for a second-generation Stavanger family to continue. The Houlder office's assets were sold to a Bergen firm. The trade Olav had spent his working life in was, by the small arithmetic of the twentieth century's industrial replacement of the Norwegian sail-trade with steam and engine, being dismantled in pieces around him.

He did not, in his private register, mourn the dismantling.

He was sixty-nine at the moment he registered that the trade of his working years was entirely gone. Sixty-nine was not an age at which a man mourned. It was an age at which a man observed.

---

Across the widower-years he went to the cottage on most mornings.

The small workbench was the same workbench. The small drawer under it held the same six items it had held since the addendum of November 1916 — Haakon's letter, Anders's letter, the 1897 Bergen clipping, the 1897 Anders sheet, the 1911 Haakon sheet, the 1916 addendum note. Olav did not, across the widower-years, add to the drawer. There was nothing to add. The men whose names the drawer held had been dead for decades. The arithmetic of the drawer had, by Olava's death in '24, become the archive that would not grow further.

He did not, across the nine years, re-read any of the drawer's items.

He knelt at the drawer perhaps once a year, at some anniversary moment he did not plan in advance but found himself keeping — the first Tuesday of May of each year, for example, which had been the day he had moved the drawer's contents from Jens's boat-shed to the cottage in 1885 and which had, by the accumulated forty years of May-first-Tuesdays since, become the day he checked the drawer's contents to verify that the physical condition of the papers was holding across the decades.

The papers were holding.

Haakon's letter had, by 1933, been in the drawer for fifty-two years. Anders's for fifty-one. The small clipping for thirty-six. The items were yellowed. The ink was faded at the older ones. But the papers were intact.

He did not open any of them.

---

In the summer of 1928 Ingrid brought home from the small Stavanger library a small book on the history of the Norwegian Lutheran parish system across the nineteenth century.

She had, across the small evenings of the widower-years, taken to bringing Olav books she thought he might want to read. The Norwegian-parish-history book was the kind of book that had been her small guess at his interest. She was right — Olav read it over two weeks at the small evening parlour-corner, with the small lamp at his elbow, and at the end of the two weeks he had read the small chapter the book contained about the Hestby parish.

The chapter was four pages.

The four pages described the Hestby church on the hill — the 1812 building; the wooden gate Peder Hestby had carved at the commissioning; Pastor Kleven's forty-nine-year tenure from 1848 to 1897; the archival keeping-of-the-old-spelling Kleven had initiated in '52 and had maintained until his death. The small chapter named, as one of the small examples of the parish's keeping of the old, the small Hestbye graveyard plot where the nineteenth-century Hestbyes had been buried with the old spelling on their stones while their living descendants had signed the parish register in the modern spelling at the same century.

The small chapter was, Olav registered at the second reading, a historical naming of the motif his own life had been the centre of.

He took the small book.

He walked up to the churchyard.

---

It was a Tuesday afternoon in the second week of September of 1928.

Olav was sixty-nine and seven months. He walked with the slower pace he had acquired across the widower-years. He had a small stick now — the small thin walking-stick his uncle's son had carved for him at the gift-of-the-seventieth-birthday the previous spring — and he used it on the small uphill portion of the path.

He came to the small *P.H. 1812* gate.

He opened it.

He walked into the churchyard.

He went to the small family-plot at the south edge.

The plot had, by 1928, ten stones. The stones — by Pastor Kleven's keeping and by Pastors Øverbye's and Nordenhaug's continuation of the keeping — were all in the old Hestbye spelling:

*Peder Hestbye, 1748–1834* (his great-grandfather)
*Anna Hestbye, 1760–1842* (his great-grandmother, beside Peder)
*Jens Hestbye, 1795–1849* (his grandfather)
*Sigrid Hestbye, 1836–1839* (his father's sister, who the brig *Sigrid* of his eighteen-year-old voyage had been named for by the family-naming convention of the mid-nineteenth century)
*Ole Hestbye, 1812–1892* (his great-uncle, who had been alive with the prematurely-cut stone at the churchyard visit of '83 and had finally caught up to his stone in '92)
*Anna Hestbye, 1801–1872* (his grandmother)
*Jens Hestbye, 1826–1908* (his father)
*Anna Hestbye, 1823–1914* (his mother; she and his father had shared the same small stone-style, beside each other)
*Olava Hestbye, 1860–1924* (his wife)
*[awaiting]*

The eleventh space — the place the Stavanger mason would someday put the stone of *Olav Hestbye, 1858–[date]* — was, in the Hestbye plot-arrangement, to the right of Olava's stone. The space was, at the September afternoon of 1928, empty grass. The Stavanger mason had, by the small arrangement Olav had made with him in 1925 at the time of ordering Olava's stone, a pre-paid engagement for the eleventh stone whenever the date arrived. The mason would, by the same arrangement, continue the old-spelling that Pastor Kleven had started in '52 and that the three pastors of the parish had continued across the seventy-six years since.

Olav would, at the time, be *Olav Hestbye* on the stone.

He would, at the parish register, still be *Olav Hestby* in his own signature hand, which was the last signature he had put on a parish document — at Olava's funeral in '24, on the form Pastor Nordenhaug had laid on the small chapel table for him to sign as the surviving spouse — and which would be the small signature his children and grandchildren would inherit as the written name of the family in the twentieth century.

The two spellings at the two places. The old on the stone. The new on the paper. The Hestby/Hestbye the two-spelling bargain Pastor Kleven had named to Olav in October of '82 at the banns.

Olav stood at the small family-plot.

He looked at the stones.

He read each name in the order he read them (he had, across the visits of the widower-years, read them always in the same order — from the oldest to the newest, from Peder of 1748 to Olava of 1860, in the chronology his head kept).

He stopped at Olava's stone.

He said — softly, in the voice he used only at the graveside — the one sentence he said at each visit:

"Olava."

He did not say more. The voice had, across the years of the widower-visits, been the register the graveside-moment required of him.

He stood for some minutes.

He turned from the plot.

He walked out of the churchyard.

---

On the walk back down the path he registered, at about the halfway point where the path crossed the small stream:

*I am in the years between the visits and my own addition to the plot. The years will, by the Bergen bank's actuarial tables, be ten or fifteen more. I will, in those years, continue the routine the widower-years have become. I will be at the cottage's workbench in the mornings and at the small parlour-corner in the evenings. I will have Ingrid at the kitchen. I will have the small Tuesday-visits from Anna and the small twice-yearly visits from Jens the younger. I will have the small Sunday suppers at the Schmidt side when the Mosterøy round of the rotation falls.*

*I will have the churchyard visits.*

*I will have the workbench drawer that will hold its six items for the remaining years unchanged.*

*I will be, at eighty, the Norwegian island widower a life of my kind produces.*

*I will not, at eighty, be sorry for the life.*

He did not, at the halfway point, allow the thought to expand into the small easy sentimentality. He kept the small flat register.

He walked down.

He came back to the larger house at half past four.

Ingrid was at the kitchen with the small afternoon tea.

"Father."

"Yes, Ingrid."

"You were at the churchyard."

"I was."

"Tea."

"Tea."

He sat at the kitchen. She brought the small mug.

They drank the small afternoon tea together.

That was the ordinary evening.

---

The years continued.

In the winter of '31 Olav had a illness — a chest-cough that held him at the bedroom for three weeks — and recovered. In the summer of '32 Ingrid had a illness — a digestive trouble the Stavanger doctor treated — and recovered. In the autumn of '33 Anna's oldest child, who was twenty-one, announced her engagement to a Stavanger telegraph-clerk. In the winter of '33 Jens the younger took over as the chief clerk at the small Bergen shipping-office where he had been the senior accountant for seven years. In the spring of '34 Olav's great-grandson — Anna's first daughter's first child, a small boy named Olav Hestby the third by the family-name-pattern — was born at Stavanger.

The line went on.

The small cottage drawer held its six items through all of it.

The form — the form that had been the marriage's and had, at Olava's death, become Olav's alone — held.

Olav was, at the summer of '34, seventy-six.

The small widower-years continued.

The final years of the life were ahead.
