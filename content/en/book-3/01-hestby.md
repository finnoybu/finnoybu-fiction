The bell at the Hestby church rang at half past ten on the morning of the fourteenth of March, 1883.

Olav heard it from the kitchen at his father's house, where he had been since six o'clock — washed, shaved by his uncle's hand on a small stool by the kitchen window, and dressed in the wool coat his mother had brushed and aired for the previous fortnight in the small careful Finnøy way that meant a coat would, at the moment of its wearing, give off the particular sweet hay-smell a Finnøy woman of his mother's generation produced from a coat by airing alone. The coat fit. He had not, in the eighteen months since he had last worn it, lost the chest his father had built into him with the spring's labor at the boats. The coat fit at the shoulders the way the tailor at Stavanger had cut it to fit at the shoulders, and Olav, standing at the kitchen window with his mother's hand for a moment briefly at the back of his collar to check the lay of it, registered that the moment was the moment a man's mother had been preparing for since the morning he had been baptized in the church the bell of which was now ringing across the valley.

His mother said:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"You will go up. Your father is at the gate. He has my flask in his coat pocket. He will not give it to you until you are at the door of the church. The walk is the walk you and your father have to walk."

"Yes."

"I will follow with your aunt and your uncle. We will be at the porch by ten before eleven."

"Yes."

"Olava is at the jetty by now. I had Marit's word from the Sunday boat."

"Yes."

She did not, at the kitchen door, hold his face. His mother had decided — Olav had understood by the second day of being home — that the wedding morning was not the day for the gestures that were Olava's. She had her own gesture for the morning: the brief small hand at the back of his collar, the smoothing of the wool with two fingers along the seam. She did it once. She let him go.

He went out.

---

His father was at the gate. The gate was the gate of the small lower garden that opened onto the path up to the church. Jens Hestby was in his Sunday coat, the one he had worn for thirty years to weddings and funerals at this church and that had, by now, worn thin at the cuffs, the way a Norwegian Sunday coat wears when it is worn only on the days the family stood as witnesses. He had his pipe out. He was not smoking it. He was looking down the path toward the harbor and the Lindøy channel, and Olav understood, joining him at the gate, that his father had been looking that way since six o'clock.

"Father."

"Olav."

"We will go up?"

"We will go up."

They started along the path.

The path went past the small lower meadow, then past the boat-shed (where Olav's great-grandfather had built his first boat in 1834, a year before he had died), then past the small stone-walled paddock his father kept for the one cow they had kept since the war, then up the slope toward the church. The slope was the slope Olav had walked five hundred times — to confirmation, to his grandmother's funeral, to the Christmas Eve services of his childhood, to the small Sunday services his mother had brought him to until he was twelve and had decided, with his father's quiet half-permission, to spend his Sunday mornings at the boat-shed instead — and it was, on the morning of the fourteenth of March in his twenty-fifth year, a slope that had become, by repetition, a thing his feet walked without his having to ask them.

His father walked half a pace behind him.

Olav understood, half-way up, that the half-pace was deliberate. His father had been walking a pace ahead of him for twenty-five years and had, on this morning, fallen back one pace to the position a father took when he was no longer the family's lead man and the son was becoming the lead. The half-pace was Jens's small private gesture of the handing-over. He did not, Olav understood, intend to comment on it. He had simply moved.

At the gate of the churchyard — at the *P.H. 1812* of his great-grandfather's initials — Jens stopped.

He took his flask from his coat pocket.

He unscrewed the cap. He passed the flask to Olav.

"One swallow," he said.

"One swallow," Olav said.

He took the swallow. The flask held the small Finnøy aquavit his uncle made each autumn from the stoneberry crop, which his mother had, by long custom, been saving in a small cask in the cellar for occasions of this particular kind. The aquavit was sharp and clean and warm. It went down the way the small hard truths a man took on a wedding morning went down. He gave the flask back. His father took his own swallow. He capped the flask. He put it back in the inside pocket.

"Now," Jens said, "we go in."

They went through the gate.

---

The churchyard had its small Sunday-best look. Pastor Kleven had had the snow cleared along the path the week before; the early grass on either side of the path was still last year's, brown and beaten flat, but the path itself was swept. The church door was open. Two of Olav's cousins from Mosterøy — boys of eleven and thirteen who had been put up to the small ceremonial work of greeting — stood on either side of the door with their caps off.

They went through the door.

Inside the church the lamps were lit. The altar at the far end carried, that morning, a small arrangement of dried winter flowers Mrs. Lindøy had sent across on the Friday with Marit and that Pastor Kleven's wife had set in two simple brass holders that the church had owned since 1858. The pew on the right side — the bridegroom's side, by parish custom — held Olav's aunts and uncles and three older cousins. The pew on the left was empty; it was for the Lindøy party, which had not yet arrived.

Pastor Kleven was at the altar in his vestments. He nodded to Olav and to Jens. He did not, in the small formal way that was his Sunday register, smile. He gestured to the front pew on the bridegroom's side.

"Sit," he said.

They sat.

---

The Lindøy party came up at five before eleven.

Olav heard them before he saw them — the boots of perhaps a dozen people on the path outside, the small low voices of a Lindøy family in a Hestby churchyard. He did not turn. He had been told, by Pastor Kleven's wife at the rehearsal on the Tuesday, that the bridegroom did not turn at the entrance of the bridal party; he sat, and the party arranged itself, and the pastor signaled when the moment had come.

He sat.

He heard the boots come into the porch. He heard them stop. He heard the small rustle of coats being arranged, of Mrs. Lindøy speaking quietly to Marit, of Captain Lindøy's boots on the wooden floor of the porch as he took his wooden hand out of his coat pocket and prepared, with the small particular attention a father gave to a thing a father did once in a daughter's life, to walk her down the aisle of a church that was not his parish.

The pastor cleared his throat. He raised his hand.

Olav stood.

He turned.

Olava was at the back of the church on her father's good arm.

She was in a dress of dark gray wool that Mrs. Lindøy had been sewing since January — high-collared, long-sleeved, with the small pale blue piping at the throat that was Mrs. Lindøy's particular small signature on every important dress she had made for either of her daughters since Marit's confirmation. Her hair was up under a plain white cap. She had no veil. Norwegian Lutheran weddings of their parish did not, as a rule, use veils; the bride came into the church with her face open and her father at her elbow.

Olav had not, in the four months he had known the wedding was coming, been able to picture her in the dress. He had tried; he had not managed it. He understood now why he had not managed it. He had not been able to picture it because the dress, on Olava, was not a thing his picturing-mind had access to. The dress was a thing that had to be seen.

He saw it.

He saw her.

He saw her father's wooden hand in the coat-pocket of the side facing the pew, and his good hand at her elbow, and the small careful slowness with which the captain was bringing his daughter up the aisle of a parish church on an island that was not his — the slowness of a man who had decided, in his sixty-fifth year, that the aisle was going to be walked at the pace he had decided it would be walked at, and that no one — not the pastor, not the families, not the gathered cousins — was going to hurry him.

He brought her to the altar.

He took his wooden hand out of his coat pocket. He took her hand from his good arm. He put it, with the small careful formality the Lindøy household used at the moments that mattered, into Olav's hand.

"Hestby," he said.

"Captain."

"My daughter."

"Yes."

The captain stepped back. He took his place in the front pew on the left side, beside Mrs. Lindøy, who had taken her own seat by the small invisible Lindøy choreography Olav had not been able to track.

Pastor Kleven raised his book.

---

The service was the service.

It was the Norwegian Lutheran wedding service Pastor Kleven had read at this altar — Olav's father had said over breakfast on the Wednesday — perhaps four hundred times in the thirty-five years of his ministry. It was the service Olav's parents had been married by in 1858, that his uncle and aunt had been married by in 1869, that two of his cousins had been married by in the previous decade. It was the service every Norwegian Lutheran of the parish came to the altar expecting to hear in the order in which it had always been said. Pastor Kleven read it without his book — he held the book open as a courtesy — and the cadence was the cadence Olav had known since he had stood beside his mother at his uncle's wedding at the age of eleven.

The vows were the vows.

Olav said them in the voice he had been practicing in his head for the four months of the engagement — his ordinary voice, neither louder nor smaller than at the supper table — and Olava said hers in the same voice, the small composed voice she had used at the back garden bench on Christmas Eve and at every Saturday-evening hour of the engagement.

The pastor pronounced them man and wife at six minutes past eleven by the watch Jens Hestby was holding in his hand at the front pew on the right side.

He turned them to the altar.

He brought out the parish register from the small side table where he had laid it before the service.

He opened it to the page he had prepared at the rehearsal.

The page had Olav's banns entry in the priest's old hand: *Olav Hestbye of Finnøy, parish of Hestbye, age twenty-four years and seven months, navigator,* with Olava's matching: *Olava Lindøye of Lindøy, parish of Lindøy, age twenty-two years and seven months.*

Below the entries were two clean lines for their signatures.

Pastor Kleven offered the pen to Olav.

Olav took the pen. He bent to the page. He signed *Olav Hestby* in the modern hand he had been signing his name in since he was sixteen.

He passed the pen to Olava.

She took it. She bent to the page. She signed *Olava Hestby* — the modern spelling, taking his surname — in the small composed hand she had used in her letters to him through the eighteen months of the voyage.

The pastor took the pen back. He blotted the two signatures. He looked at the page. He nodded, once.

He said, in his Sunday register:

"The two of you are now, in the eyes of this parish and in the eyes of the church and in the eyes of the families who have witnessed it, husband and wife. The register will hold the record. The bell will ring. Go in peace."

The bell rang.

---

They walked out of the church together.

At the porch — under the carved arch his great-grandfather had put up in 1812 — Olava stopped and turned to him. Around them the families were pulling on their coats, Marit was wiping her eyes briefly with the back of her hand, the captain was looking at the sky with the small particular small look a father had after the moment he had stopped being his daughter's nearest man.

Olava put both her hands on Olav's face on the cheekbones.

She held his face for a count of breaths he did not, this time, count. The counting had been the form of the earlier holds. The wedding-morning hold did not need to be counted; the wedding had counted it.

Then she said, quietly enough that only he heard:

"Hestby."

"Hestby."

She had not, until that moment, called him by his name without his given name in front of it. The name was the name she would call him by at the kitchen table at Finnøy on the Saturday after Easter when she would call him from the boat-shed for supper. The name was the name she would use, for the rest of their lives, for the version of him that was not the boy from Landa or the navigator from Stavanger or the man at the wool-coat quay in June of '81. The name was the name of the man she had married.

He took her hand from his cheek. He kissed it.

"Hestby," he said.

"Yes."

She turned, with him, and they walked down through the churchyard gate together, past the *P.H. 1812,* down the slope past the small stone-walled paddock and the boat-shed and the lower meadow, to the house where his mother had set the kitchen table for the family's small wedding-day dinner — herring, smoked mutton, the particular slow-rising bread Mrs. Lindøy had sent across on the morning's boat with Marit, the small honey-cake his mother had been planning since January — and where the families would, for the next four hours, do the small Norwegian work of celebrating a marriage at a kitchen table on an island in the channel.

The bell at the Hestby church rang once more, at noon, as Pastor Kleven left the building and locked the door behind him.

By that hour Olav and Olava were at the kitchen table. They were married.

The day had begun.
