The discharge of the petroleum at the Red Hook wharf began at six bells of the morning watch on the Tuesday after the bark warped in.

It was not work the crew did. Brooklyn in 1883 had its own dock-labor — a workforce of perhaps three thousand men along the petroleum waterfront, organized by the small jealous unions of the trade and supplied to the wharves at piecework rates by the Hook's labor-contractors — and the *Daae*'s petroleum cargo was discharged by a gang of twenty Brooklyn men under a foreman named Riley, who took the gang from one bark to the next along the wharf in a small steady industrial rhythm Tellefsen said had not, in his eight years on the bark, varied by an hour. The crew's work, during the discharge, was the small ordinary keeping of the bark — watch-keeping, small repair, the constant scrubbing of decks that had been at sea for six weeks — and the daily small administrative work the first mate did at the company agent's office at Pearl Street.

Olav had the Pearl Street work.

Tellefsen had the deck.

This was, Tellefsen had said at the small officer's mess on the morning of the second day, the standard division: a first mate on his first Brooklyn was sent to the company office to be introduced to the agents and to handle the small daily commercial errands a deep-water bark generated at her foreign port, while the second mate ran the deck so the first mate could learn the ashore part of the trade. By Olav's third Brooklyn it would be reversed — Olav the captain's man at the office, the new first mate at the deck — but for this voyage the office was Olav's classroom.

The classroom started on the Tuesday at the consul's office at Bergen Avenue, where Olav left his first letter home.

---

The Norwegian consul's office at Brooklyn was on the upper floor of a brick building at the corner of Bergen Avenue and Smith Street, in a part of the city the local Norwegian community called Atlantic Avenue Brooklyn or, less officially, *Lille Bergen* — a half-square-mile of brick buildings, warehouses, and small shops that had, since the petroleum trade had brought regular Norwegian shipping to Brooklyn in the 1870s, become the small permanent Norwegian neighbourhood of New York. The consul was a man named Wexelsen who had been in Brooklyn twenty years and ran the office with his wife (also Norwegian, Stavanger-born) and two younger Norwegian-American clerks. The office handled the mail, the seamen's papers, the small consular interventions the petroleum-trade crews periodically required. Wexelsen had been at the post for fifteen years.

Olav came up the stairs to the office at ten on the Tuesday morning with his Brooklyn letter to Olava in his coat pocket.

The office had a small waiting bench by the door, a long counter, and behind the counter the two clerks at their desks. Wexelsen was at the inner office. The wife — Olav guessed her at fifty — was at a table near the window with the morning's mail bag. She looked up when he came in.

"Norwegian?"

"Norwegian."

"Off the *Daae*?"

"Off the *Daae.*"

"First voyage?"

"First Brooklyn."

"Then you are the new first mate. Mr. Wexelsen had the bark on the schedule and was watching for you. Sit. He will see you in a minute."

She gestured to the bench. Olav sat.

He looked around the office. It was clean. The walls had a printed list of the Norwegian-flagged ships expected at the Brooklyn ports across the next quarter, a small framed lithograph of the Norwegian royal arms (which Olav understood to be a polite consular formality and not a political statement), and a framed list of the seamen's lodging-houses in Brooklyn that the consul considered acceptable for Norwegian crews ashore. The list had perhaps fifteen names. Olav read them. Two were marked with a small red star — the consul's particular recommendation. Three were marked with a small black cross — the consul's particular warning.

The bench at his side held two other men.

The first was an older Norwegian sailor — perhaps fifty-five, weathered, in a coat that had been the coat of a long-serving deep-water hand for at least a decade — who was waiting with a small folder of papers in his lap. He nodded at Olav. He did not speak.

The second was a young Norwegian-American clerk of perhaps twenty-two, in a brown wool suit, with the small careful diction of an immigrant's son who had been born in Brooklyn but had been raised speaking Norwegian at home and English at school and had come to the consul's office on some small errand for an Brooklyn-Norwegian uncle. He glanced at Olav. He did not speak either.

After perhaps four minutes the inner door opened. Wexelsen came out.

Wexelsen was in his early sixties. He had a square head and a small grey beard and the small careful manner of a Norwegian consular officer who had managed his post — by all signs successfully — for a long time. He looked at Olav. He nodded.

"Hestby."

"Mr. Wexelsen."

"Captain Thoresen sent your articles ahead with the morning runner. I have your record. Come in."

Olav followed him into the inner office.

---

The inner office was small. A desk; a chair; a bookshelf of consular reports and English-Norwegian dictionaries and a small set of the *Norsk Sjøfartstidende* for the past five years; a window that gave onto Smith Street where, at this hour, the small Brooklyn morning was at its small Brooklyn business.

Wexelsen sat. He gestured to the chair.

Olav sat.

"Hestby. The consul's office at Brooklyn handles two things for the *Daae* crews. The mail, which is bundled and posted on the Cunard's Wednesday departures, reaches Bergen in two weeks and Stavanger in two and a half. Your letters home will go on the runner at five each weekday. The second thing is the consular protection. Norwegian seamen ashore in Brooklyn are protected by the Norwegian-Swedish consular treaty of 1875 to the extent that the New York courts choose to enforce it, which is — by my fifteen years of office — about half the cases that reach the courts. The remaining half, the consul handles by other means, which I will not describe at the first interview but which you will, in three weeks at Brooklyn, hear about from the older men of your crew."

"Yes."

"Your crew. Twenty common seamen. Fifteen previous *Daae* hands. They know Brooklyn. They will go to the lodging-houses on the consul's list at red star — there are two — and will, mostly, return to the bark on time. The five new hands are the unknowns. Tellefsen will tell you which lodging-houses he is sending them to. If any of them disappear, you bring me their names and the date and the lodging-house. I will, if it is possible, locate them. If it is not possible, I will tell you so. I do not, in this office, pretend to powers I do not have."

"I understand."

"You. The first mate. You will ashore at the consul's recommended lodging or at the bark. If you ashore at the lodging, you ashore at the Norwegian-American Seamen's Mission on Hicks Street, which is the red-star lodging on the list. The mission is run by Pastor Fredriksen, a Bergen man, three nights at fifty cents American. The other red-star is the Hook Hotel, which is more comfortable but is a sailors' commercial place; I do not recommend it to first mates on their first Brooklyn. You will, by my recommendation, take the mission."

"Yes."

"You will not, on your free evenings, walk east of Court Street without a man you trust. You will not walk in the Bowery — which is a Manhattan district, not a Brooklyn one, but I am naming it because the Bowery is the place a young Norwegian first mate at his first Brooklyn most often gets the idea to visit, and the Bowery is the place from which a young Norwegian first mate at his first Brooklyn most often does not return on time. The Bowery is the consul's particular do-not. I am telling you this in the office on the Tuesday morning so that you know I have told you."

"Yes, Mr. Wexelsen."

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"You are married."

"Yes. Married in March of this year."

"Wife at home in Norway?"

"At Finnøy, by Stavanger."

"Then I will say to you the thing I say to first mates on their first Brooklyn who are married. The thing is this. The Brooklyn of the petroleum trade in the year 1883 is a city in which a Norwegian first mate at his first foreign port can, if he wishes to, lose more of himself to the lower streets than he intends to. I have, in fifteen years, seen this happen perhaps thirty times. I have, in the same fifteen years, seen perhaps fifteen marriages end at home as a result. The wives at home do not know that the marriages have ended; they are still corresponding with the husbands. The husbands know. The husbands carry the knowledge for the rest of their lives. I am telling you this because I am the consul and the consul tells the new first mates this on the Tuesday morning of their first Brooklyn. I am not telling you you are the kind of first mate who would do this. I am telling you so that you know I have told you."

Olav listened.

He took the speech for what it was — Wexelsen's small careful warning, the kind of warning a consul in his position had to give to every new first mate on their first Brooklyn regardless of the man's character, a warning that was the consular equivalent of Sundberg's three-risks speech at Calcutta two years before. He took it. He registered it.

He said:

"Thank you, Mr. Wexelsen."

"You are welcome. The mission is at Hicks Street and Carroll. Pastor Fredriksen takes Norwegian sailors from Sunday evening through Wednesday morning. You may take the rest of your evenings at the bark. Your letter home goes on the runner at five today, by my office's stamp, and reaches Bergen in two weeks. Your wife will read it the second week of January."

"Yes."

"Welcome to Brooklyn, Mr. Hestby."

He stood. Olav stood. They shook hands. Olav left the office, gave the letter to the older Norwegian woman at the mail desk on his way out, took her stamp on the cover, and went down the stairs.

---

He walked back to the bark by way of Atlantic Avenue.

The walk was about a mile and a half. It took him through *Lille Bergen* and across the small commercial district at Hoyt Street and down the slope of Smith Street to the Hook waterfront. He walked slowly. He looked at the city as he walked. The buildings were brick where Norway's were wood; the street-lamps were closer-spaced; the women on the streets were dressed in a Brooklyn winter style he had not before seen — long dark coats, hats with small dark feathers, the small careful Brooklyn winter wear of a city that did not have Norway's snow but did have the wet cold of the Atlantic seaboard in early December.

The men he passed were of every kind. The Norwegian men he registered by their walks before he registered them by their faces; he had learned, at Calcutta and at Cape Town, that a Norwegian sailor walked in a particular way that distinguished him from any other nationality at first sight, and the distinguishing carried in any port. The American men were more varied. The Irish workmen of the petroleum yards. The Italian fruit-vendors at the corner of Court and President. The black workers at the Hook docks who had come up to Brooklyn from the southern states in the years after the war. Two Chinese clerks at a small laundry on Smith Street. The mix at Brooklyn was more complex than the mix at Calcutta had been; at Calcutta the colonial administration had organized the mix into legible classes; at Brooklyn the mix was the mix that an industrial American city in 1883 had let assemble itself, and the assembling was — Olav registered as he walked — a different kind of arrangement than any port he had been in.

He was not, he understood by the half-mile, in a city he had a category for.

He registered that too.

He walked on.

At the foot of Smith Street he turned onto the Hook waterfront. The petroleum wharves stretched for half a mile to the south. The *Daae* was at the third wharf along. He came up to her gangway at half past noon.

Tellefsen was at the rail.

"Hestby."

"Tellefsen."

"Wexelsen told you the speech."

"He told me the speech."

"He tells it to every new first mate. He has, by my count, told it five times in my eight years at the *Daae.* Two of the five took it. Two were already the kind of man it did not need to be told to. One was not."

"The one who was not?"

"He was the first mate before me, in '74. The wife at Bergen still writes him at the bark. She does not know that he sailed on a different bark in '76 and went to Buenos Aires and did not come back. The captain — Thoresen, whose previous bark was the *Bergljot* in '74, the same year your father told you about — has, since '76, written annually to that wife at Stavanger confirming the man's continued absence in the small careful captain's hand a captain uses for that particular kind of correspondence."

"Thoresen does that."

"Thoresen does that. He does not advertise it. I tell you because the captain has the small private side his shouting does not show. The notebook on his bookshelf is one piece of him; the annual letter to a Bergen widow who does not know she is one is the other. You will, in eighteen months, learn both."

"Yes."

"Welcome to Brooklyn, Hestby."

He smiled — a small sharp Tellefsen smile that Olav had not seen before — and went forward.

---

That night in his cabin Olav wrote two paragraphs in the small bound book.

> *Brooklyn day two. The consul's speech. The mission at Hicks and Carroll for the ashore-nights. The petroleum waterfront from the consul's office back to the wharf. The mix is its own mix. The city does not have a category I have a category for.*
>
> *Tellefsen on Thoresen's annual letter to the Bergen widow whose husband sailed away in 1876 and did not come back. The captain has the small private side. I will, by Tellefsen's reckoning, learn both pieces in eighteen months.*

He blotted the page.

He thought, briefly, of Olava at the kitchen at Finnøy at this hour. He thought of Mrs. Hestby at the stove. He thought of the letter that would reach them by the second week of January.

He thought of the lodging-house at Hicks and Carroll where he would, by Sunday evening, take the small clean Norwegian-American Seamen's Mission room Pastor Fredriksen kept for first-mates.

He blew out the lamp.

He slept the small uneasy sleep of a man at his first Brooklyn night with the consul's speech in his head and the small specific knowledge that the city outside the cabin window was a city that had — by Tellefsen's frank statement — the capacity to take a Norwegian first mate's marriage if the first mate was the kind of man who let it.

He had decided, in the cabin, that he was not the kind of man who let it.

He held to the decision through the night.

The bark lay at the wharf. The discharge would resume at six bells of the morning watch. The petroleum-smell of Red Hook drifted in through the cabin's small ventilator. The small constant flares of the Brooklyn refineries lit the cabin's low ceiling in a faint wavering yellow Olav had not seen the like of in any other port.

He slept against the yellow.

The first Brooklyn night was held.
