In the second week of the Brooklyn stop a small piece of company business required Olav to cross to Manhattan.

The business was the small administrative kind. Hofmoe at Bergen had instructed Wexelsen at Brooklyn to instruct Thoresen on the *Daae* to obtain — at the Manhattan Marine Underwriters' office on Whitehall Street — a duplicate of an insurance certificate for the bark's third-quarter petroleum cargo that the original of had been mislaid by a clerk at Bergen the previous summer. Thoresen sent Olav.

Tellefsen, at the morning's officer's mess, gave him the small careful Manhattan-instructions: ferry from the foot of Furman Street, walk south to Whitehall, the office on the fourth floor, the four o'clock ferry back. *You will, by Wexelsen's recommendation, not be in Manhattan after dark. The forbidding is the lower streets. The looking is the looking.* Olav took the speech. He took the half past nine ferry.

The errand at the Whitehall Building took thirty-five minutes. By eleven he had the duplicate certificate in an envelope in his coat. The four o'clock ferry was five hours away.

He walked north. He looked at the city — the financial district, Wall Street, the new Brooklyn Bridge from the Manhattan side, City Hall, the small commercial streets above. He registered the city the way he had registered the Strand at Calcutta and the Strand at Cape Town, with the small careful Norwegian-sailor attention that did not let his face do anything but committed the streets to memory.

By two o'clock he was at the southern end of a long broad commercial street that the small map called the Bowery.

He stood at the corner of Bowery and Worth Street.

The Bowery in daylight was a working commercial street with the small theaters and saloons and dance-halls of a New York district that had, by the small map's notations and by Wexelsen's afternoon warning, the reputation of being a different street at night. He was at the consul's particular do-not. He was at it in daylight. He looked at it for the count of perhaps a minute.

Then he walked back south. He had decided, at the corner, that the looking was the limit of what he was going to take from the Bowery.

---

The decision was the right decision. The execution was where the afternoon failed.

He was at the Whitehall ferry landing by half past three, with the four o'clock ferry comfortably ahead of him. He was about to step onto the landing when a clerk from the Marine Underwriters' office — a man he recognized from the morning's errand — came up to him on the dock with a small embarrassed look and a paper in his hand.

"Mr. Hestby."

"Yes."

"There has been a small office error on the certificate. A figure was transposed. The clerk who copied has corrected it. I have, in this paper, the corrected page. I would have sent it across to Brooklyn by tomorrow morning's runner, but I saw you on the dock and thought to give it to you directly. We will need the original certificate back. I have the corrected duplicate here."

"Yes."

"It will, I am afraid, take perhaps fifteen minutes at the office to do the small re-stamping and the witnessing. The office is, as you know, four floors up. I am sorry."

"It is the work."

"It is the work. Thank you."

They walked back to the Whitehall Building. They went up the four flights. The clerk attended to the re-stamping at his desk while a senior man who had not been at the morning's errand witnessed the corrected duplicate and signed for the receipt of the original. The fifteen minutes Olav had been told would be the duration became, by the small unhurried American office economy, twenty-five minutes. By the time Olav came out of the building at the corner of Whitehall and Pearl it was a quarter past four.

The four o'clock ferry was gone.

The next ferry was at five.

He was going to be at the bark by half past seven instead of half past six. The supper would have started without him. Tellefsen would assume the office had taken longer than expected. The captain would not be told.

He understood, at the corner, that the small re-stamping had cost him an hour and that the hour had not, by his own arithmetic, made him late in any consequential sense. He had time. He would take the five o'clock ferry. He would be at the bark for the second half of the supper. The day would close more or less as planned.

He decided to walk to a different ferry — the Battery landing, which the small map said was a quarter-mile south of Whitehall — because the Battery ferry departures were more frequent and he might catch one earlier than five. He turned south.

He had walked perhaps two blocks when the small fact of the December dusk became the dominant fact of the afternoon. The sun was already down behind the buildings. The street-lamps were being lit. The light at the corner of South Street was the small failing yellow light of a Manhattan winter evening at quarter past four. Olav was, he understood as he walked, no longer in daylight.

The map he had been using — Tellefsen's small pocket map — did not, as he checked it at a lamp-post, show the Battery landing as clearly as he had thought. He had taken the wrong street. He was, he registered, somewhere between Whitehall and the Battery but on a small side street that the map did not mark. He turned west to find a wider street. He turned again. He was, by the third turn, no longer certain which direction the river was.

It was the sort of small navigational error a sailor on land made in a city he did not know in the failing light of a December afternoon. It was not, by itself, dangerous. He needed only to find a wider street, ask directions of a passing pedestrian, take a cab if he could find one, get to either ferry landing.

He walked another two blocks.

He came out onto a wider street.

He did not, at the corner, know which street it was. He looked for a sign. The signs at the corners of this part of the city were small wooden plaques nailed to the buildings, often in poor repair. The one at this corner had been painted over by a poster for a circus. He could not read it.

He turned right.

He walked another block.

He came around a corner into a small alley that was, in the failing light, suddenly not the kind of alley a man wanted to be in.

Three men were at the far end of it.

They had been waiting for him.

---

He understood this in the count of one breath.

He understood it because he had — walking the wider street for the previous three blocks — registered, without registering it consciously, that two men had been at his shoulder for the last block and a half, and that they had been keeping the pace he was keeping rather than passing him, and that they had been, at intervals, looking at each other in the way men did when they were communicating about a man walking ahead of them. The two men were now behind him. The three men at the end of the alley were ahead of him. The alley was perhaps thirty feet long, narrow, with no door in either side. He had come around the corner and into it without seeing the three men until he had come around. The two men behind him had — he understood as he heard their footsteps stop at the corner — been guiding him to this alley for the last three blocks.

The largest of the three men at the end of the alley took a step forward.

He was perhaps thirty-five. He was tall and broad. He had the small careful look of a man who had done this before and would, by his pattern, do it again on the next sailor who walked the wrong street at this hour. He spoke, in English with a heavy Brooklyn accent that suggested an Irish father and a Manhattan upbringing:

"Sailor."

Olav did not, for some seconds, answer.

"Sailor. We saw you on the Bowery this afternoon. We had thought you was Swedish. Now we hear you was at the underwriters' on Whitehall. That ain't Swedish. That's Norwegian. The Norwegian off the Brooklyn bark."

Olav understood, in the count of two breaths, that the men had been watching him since two o'clock. The afternoon-watching pattern Larsen the *Bergljot* survivor had not, this morning, warned Olav about — because Olav had not, this morning, met Larsen — was the pattern that had operated on him. The men had identified him at the Bowery in the afternoon. They had not, by their preference, attempted him at midnight. They had taken the small accidental gift of the office's twenty-five extra minutes and the Battery wrong-turn and were attempting him at the small early-evening hour at which a Norwegian first mate could be alone in an alley between the financial district and the harbor.

He thought, with the small flat clarity his interior used at moments at which the body was about to be tested:

*This is the moment. The moment is going to be over in two minutes either way. I have my pocket-book. I have the certificate. I have my body. They have decided to take some combination of the three. The combination they take will depend on what I do in the next ten seconds.*

He raised his hands slightly — palms out, the gesture a man made to indicate he was not reaching for a weapon — and he said, in his own English (which was the careful English of a Norwegian navigation-school graduate who had been ashore in English-speaking ports four times in seven years):

"I have a pocket-book. I will give it to you. The papers in the coat are not yours to want. They are a company document. I am willing to give the pocket-book."

The Brooklyn man laughed once.

It was a particular laugh.

It was the laugh of a man who had, by long practice in similar alleys, found that a Norwegian who offered the pocket-book at the first request was a Norwegian who could be relieved of the pocket-book *and* of certain other things by a small additional application of force, because the offering was the sign that the body would not put up the fight that the body of an Irish dock-hand would have put up. The laugh was the laugh of a man who had calibrated his trade by exactly such offerings.

He took a step closer.

The two men behind Olav stepped into the alley.

The largest man drew, from the inside of his coat, a small leather sap of the kind a Manhattan thief used at this hour for the blow to the side of the head a man could be relieved of consciousness by without, as a rule, dying.

Olav put his back against the alley wall.

He understood, with the same flat clarity, that the next ten seconds were going to involve more than the pocket-book, that the offering had been the wrong move, that the body was going to take the sap, that the sap might or might not — depending on the angle — be survivable.

The largest man raised the sap.

A voice from the corner where the two men had come in — the Manhattan end of the alley — said, in Norwegian:

"Stop."

The voice was the voice of a man who was perhaps fifty.

The three men in the alley turned. The two men at the corner turned.

A man stood at the corner of the alley.

He was, Olav saw in the failing light, a Norwegian. He was about fifty. He was in the dark coat of a Brooklyn-Norwegian businessman of some standing. He had a small gray beard. He was, Olav registered in the second second, holding nothing in his hands — no weapon, no stick — and was therefore relying on something other than physical force to make the *stop* he had just given.

He spoke a sentence in English to the largest of the three men. The English was Brooklyn-accented but not Brooklyn-native; it was the English of a Norwegian who had been in the city for thirty years.

"Peder. This one is mine."

The largest man — Peder — looked at him.

"Gjesdal. You don't get to call this one."

"This one I do call. He is the new first mate of the *President Daae.* The Daae's captain has been protected by my arrangement at the Brooklyn waterfront for nine years. The arrangement covers the captain's officers. Take the pocket-book if you want it. Do not touch the man. The arrangement is the arrangement. You know it is the arrangement."

Peder looked at the older Norwegian.

He looked at Olav. He looked at the sap in his hand.

He lowered the sap.

He looked back at the older Norwegian.

"Gjesdal. The Norwegian arrangement on the waterfront does not run inside the Bowery's afternoon-watching. We watched him on the Bowery today. He walked it. We had him."

"You had him before you knew he was *Daae.* Now you know. The arrangement applies. Take the pocket-book if you want it. Leave the man."

Peder considered for what was — by Olav's stretched count of the seconds — perhaps six.

He stepped forward. He took Olav's pocket-book from Olav's coat with the small practiced motion of a man who had taken several hundred pocket-books in his career. He stepped back. He looked at the older Norwegian. He nodded once. He turned and walked out the Brooklyn end of the alley with his two men. The two men behind Olav followed.

The alley emptied.

---

The older Norwegian came down the alley toward Olav.

Olav did not, for some seconds, move from the wall.

The older man stopped about three feet from him.

"Are you hurt."

"I am not hurt."

"Good. Walk with me."

He turned and walked back to the corner of the alley. Olav followed him on legs that were, he registered, not fully reliable for the first ten steps. They came out onto the wider street. The older man led him north for two blocks, then west, then onto Broadway. They walked at the pace of two Norwegians who knew where they were going.

The older man did not, for the four blocks, speak.

At the corner of Broadway and a small side street that Olav did not register the name of, he stopped. He turned to Olav.

"Hestby."

"You know my name."

"I know your name. Wexelsen at Brooklyn told me a fortnight ago you had come to the city. The Norwegian-American community at Brooklyn is small and the consul talks to the Norwegian-American businessmen who matter at it. I am one of the men who matter. My name is Gjesdal — different Gjesdal from the Peder you just met. I am, by my own arrangement with the city's underworld, the man the Norwegian shipping captains pay a small annual sum to in exchange for the protection of their officers when their officers walk the wrong streets. The captain of the *Daae* has been paying me for nine years. The fee covers exactly the situation you were just in."

"Thoresen."

"Thoresen. He has, for eighteen years, paid for his officers' safety at New York. The Bergljot incident in '74 — which I assume you know about, because every Norwegian sailor of your generation knows about it — was the incident that taught him the lesson. He has, since '74, been the captain who pays me. He has not advertised it. He pays through Wexelsen's office at the consular ledger; the money is filed under *miscellaneous Norwegian-American assistance* in the consul's report. I have never met Thoresen face to face. I have, on his account, walked thirty-one Norwegian first mates and second mates and chief stewards out of various Manhattan alleys in the nine years. You are the thirty-second."

Olav did not, for some seconds, speak.

He thought: *Thoresen pays for the protection. Thoresen pays for it through the consul. The consul pays Larsen at the Bowery corner with five dollars American at every Brooklyn arrival. The captain who shouted at me for the brace and who calibrates upward across the voyage is the same captain who has been paying — privately, through the consul, at his own expense — for the safety of the officers his shouting has been training for nine years.*

He thought: *I am the thirty-second.*

He said:

"Thank you, Mr. Gjesdal."

"You are welcome. The thanks belong to Thoresen. I am the instrument; he is the funder. You may, at your own discretion, mention to him at the bark that you met me. He will not, in my experience, bring it up himself. He prefers the men he funds the protection for to learn about the protection by being protected."

"I understand."

"I will walk you to the Battery ferry. The next departure is in ten minutes. You will be at Brooklyn by half past six."

"Yes."

They walked to the Battery. Gjesdal did not, on the walk, speak further. At the ferry landing he raised his hat once in a Norwegian-businessman's farewell and turned and walked back into the city.

Olav took the ferry.

---

He came up the *Daae*'s gangway at twenty to seven.

Tellefsen was at the rail.

"You are late."

"I was held at the office. The certificate had a transposition. I was held to re-stamp."

Tellefsen looked at him.

He looked at Olav's coat — at the place at the lapel where the pocket-book had been until twenty minutes earlier and where the small bulge of it was now missing.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"Your pocket-book."

"It is at a Manhattan alley. It was taken by a man named Peder. A Norwegian businessman called Gjesdal — different Gjesdal — intervened on Captain Thoresen's account. I was not hurt. The certificate is in my coat."

Tellefsen did not, for some seconds, answer.

Then he said:

"You met Gjesdal."

"I met Gjesdal."

"You will tell the captain at the morning interview tomorrow."

"I will tell him."

"Good. Go below. Eat the supper the steward has held back for you. I will hear the rest at the officer's mess."

Olav went below.

---

In his cabin, before the supper, he wrote two short paragraphs in the small bound book.

> *Manhattan. Held at the office on a re-stamp. Missed the four o'clock ferry. Walked the wrong streets in the failing light at the wrong hour. Was identified by men who had watched me on the Bowery this afternoon. They had me in an alley with a sap. A Norwegian businessman called Gjesdal arrived at the corner and named me as a Daae officer protected by Thoresen's nine-year private arrangement with the city's underworld. The sap was lowered. The pocket-book was taken. The body was not.*
>
> *I am the thirty-second. Thoresen has been paying through the consul for nine years. Larsen at the Bowery corner is paid five dollars at each arrival from the same source. The captain who shouts is the same captain who pays. I had not, before tonight, understood that the two pieces of him were so directly tied. The notebook on the bookshelf is one piece. The annual letter to the Bergen widow is another. The Gjesdal arrangement is the third. The captain has, by my eight weeks' observation, more pieces than the surface shows.*

He blotted the page.

He went up to the supper.

The steward had held back a plate of cold mutton and bread. Olav ate it at the small officer's table. Tellefsen, across from him, did not press for the story. The story would come at the officer's mess afterward. The supper was the supper.

In the morning at four bells of the morning watch he reported to Thoresen at the chart-table.

He told the captain the story.

Thoresen listened. He did not interrupt. When Olav was done, the captain said only:

"You met Gjesdal."

"Yes, sir."

"He would have told you about the arrangement."

"He told me, sir."

"He should not, by my preference, have told you. But he did. So you know. That is the situation. The arrangement is a piece of the captain's job that the captain does not, in the ordinary course of a voyage, mention to the first mate. It is part of why the wages are what they are. It is part of why I take the company's three-dollar deduction at each pay-off in the line of *miscellaneous expenses unspecified.* You will, when you are a captain, do something similar at the foreign port your trade puts you at. The trade, Hestby, has more pieces than the surface shows."

He did not, after the speech, look at Olav for some seconds.

Then he said:

"Pocket-book."

"Taken, sir."

"How much."

"Thirty-two dollars American."

"You will have a fresh advance from the company purse this afternoon. The thirty-two will be filed at the consul's office tonight as a *miscellaneous reimbursable.* You will not pay it back. The arrangement covers the loss."

"Yes, sir."

"Hestby."

"Sir."

"You will not, in the next eighteen months, return to Manhattan."

"No, sir."

"Good. You may go to the deck."

Olav went to the deck.

He took his watch.

The petroleum continued to come out of the hold. The bark continued to prepare for the return crossing of the North Atlantic. The Brooklyn day was the Brooklyn day.

He had, at twenty-five at his second week at his first foreign American port, learned the lesson the city had to teach him at one alley.

He had, by Thoresen's nine-year arrangement, learned it without paying the full price.

The full price would have been the use of his right hand for the rest of his career, by Tellefsen's earlier tally; or, by the angle of Peder's sap, perhaps his life.

He had paid thirty-two dollars and the knowledge that the captain who shouted at him at the brace was the same captain who had, since '74, been paying for the safety of the men he shouted at.

The trade had more pieces than the surface showed.

He went to the work.
