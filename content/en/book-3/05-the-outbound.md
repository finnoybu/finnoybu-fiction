The mate of the *President Daae* was a man named Tellefsen.

He came back from the company office at three o'clock on the Tuesday afternoon, as Thoresen had said he would, and found Olav at the foredeck where Olav had been since two — having stowed his chest in the small cabin that had been assigned to him as the first mate's berth (a separate cabin, narrow, with its own door and a fixed bunk and a small writing-desk that bolted to the bulkhead), having taken his short walk of the bark from stem to stern noting what he could see noting from the deck of a vessel he would be at sea on for the next eighteen months, and having come to rest at the foredeck to wait for the man he would be working under.

Tellefsen was about forty. He was tall and thin, with the small grey-blond beard a Bergen man of his generation grew because his father had grown it. His eyes were clear and a little tired, in the way the eyes of a long-serving second-rank officer often were — a man who had been a mate for fifteen years and had concluded he would be a mate at sixty too, and who had reconciled himself to the conclusion. He came up the gangway, looked at Olav, and put out his hand.

"Hestby."

"Tellefsen."

"They told me at the office you would be early. I am glad you were."

"Thank you."

"I will tell you the things you need to know now and not in front of the captain. The captain prefers the mate to know everything before he is asked."

"Yes."

Tellefsen led him aft along the deck to the small space behind the wheelhouse — a kind of half-shelter where the binnacle cover was kept and where two officers could speak without being overheard — and he laid out, in the next twenty minutes, the small careful catalogue Olav understood was the operating manual a man like Tellefsen handed to a man like Olav at the start of a voyage with a man like Thoresen.

The captain ate alone in his cabin at all three meals.

The captain came up at eight bells of the morning watch, at noon for the sight, and at the changing of the dog-watches in the afternoon. At other times he was below or at his cabin, and the deck was the mate's.

The captain shouted at the first mate when the captain was displeased with the deck. The first mate did not shout back. The first mate took the shouting and, after, went forward and quietly corrected whatever the captain had been displeased about.

The captain did not, in Tellefsen's eight years on the *Daae,* strike the first mate. He had struck a previous first mate (in the autumn of '76 in mid-Atlantic) and had been reported by the second mate to the company at Bergen, and there had been a small private warning. He had not, since, struck the first mate. He had, twice in eight years, struck a common seaman.

The crew of the *President Daae* was twenty common seamen. Of the twenty, Tellefsen said, fifteen had sailed with Thoresen at least once before. Five were new this voyage. The five would be the captain's projects in the first month.

The petroleum was the cargo. It was carried in the hold in the standard wooden barrels of the trade, which leaked at the seams — they always did — and which therefore meant that the bark smelled of petroleum at all times below the deck, and that any open flame below the deck was forbidden by the company's articles. The galley fire was permitted only because the galley was on the deck, with its iron-walled stove and its small permitted hearth. Smoking below was a flogging offense. Tellefsen did not, in his career, smoke. Olav did not smoke either; this was, Tellefsen noted, useful.

The Atlantic crossing in October-November was a five-to-six week passage to Brooklyn. The Brooklyn stop was perhaps three to four weeks, depending on the discharge schedule. The return was a January-February crossing of the North Atlantic, which was the difficult one. They would, weather permitting, lift Brooklyn in late January for a Bergen arrival in early March. Then a fortnight at Bergen for refit and re-loading. Then a second outbound to Brooklyn in the spring, summer at Brooklyn, return in the autumn of '84. Then a fortnight at Bergen. Then a third outbound in the late autumn of '84 for a winter Brooklyn stay and a return in the spring of '85.

Three crossings of the Atlantic westbound. Three eastbound. April 1885 sign-off. That was the shape.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"You have a wife at Lindøy."

"At Finnøy now. Married in March."

"Then you are the second married man on the bark. The captain is married — at Bergen, three children grown. I am not. The carpenter is. The bosun is not. The sailmaker is. The rest of the men are common seamen, and a deep-water common seaman in the petroleum trade does not, as a rule, marry."

"I will write home."

"Write what you can. The mail at Brooklyn is two weeks each way to Norway. Your wife will have your letter posted from the Brooklyn consul about a month after the Atlantic crossing. Then again at the second Brooklyn stop. Then at the third. She will have, in eighteen months, perhaps six letters from you of the kind a wife wants to read."

"I understand."

"Good. Captain wants you at four bells."

"Yes."

Tellefsen went forward.

---

At four bells of the afternoon watch Olav reported to Captain Thoresen in the captain's cabin.

The cabin was, in size and arrangement, much the same as Brun's on the *Semiramis* — a desk, a chair, a chart-table, a small bookshelf — but the small differences from Brun's were the differences that defined the man. Brun's chart-table had been clean. Thoresen's had a coffee-cup ring on the corner that had been there long enough to have become permanent. Brun's bookshelf had held twelve volumes — pilots' guides, the Norwegian Maritime Code, a Bible, a copy of the *Asiatic Society Journal* of 1873. Thoresen's held five — a pilot's guide, the Maritime Code, a Bible, a slim volume of Christmas-cards of the kind a wife sends a captain at sea, and a small leather-bound notebook of Thoresen's own that lay closed on top of the rest.

Thoresen was at the desk. He did not stand.

"Hestby."

"Captain."

"Sit."

Olav sat.

Thoresen looked at him for what Olav understood — by Brun's standard — was the captain's grading-look. Thoresen's grading-look was different from Brun's. Brun's had been the look of a man who was confirming an opinion already formed; Thoresen's was the look of a man who was forming the opinion at the moment of looking, and who would, depending on what he saw, either give the man his approval-rating now or assign him a project.

Thoresen did not speak for some seconds.

Then he said:

"I have read your articles. I know your reputation from Brun. I know your sign-off was at the top mark Brun gives a common seaman. I know the *Semiramis* East India was a single eighteen-month voyage. You are twenty-five years old. You are married five months. You have been a navigator with a certificate for three years. You have not, before today, been a first mate."

"That is correct, sir."

"Then I will tell you what the first mate is on this bark. The first mate is the man who runs the deck when I am not on it. The first mate is the man the crew speaks to. The first mate is the man who tells me what I need to know when I come up at eight bells, at noon, and at the changing of the dog-watches. The first mate is the man I shout at when I am displeased with the deck. The first mate does not shout back. He goes forward and corrects. Is that understood."

"Yes, sir."

"Tellefsen has been the first mate on this bark for eight years. He has chosen, this voyage, to be the second mate so that the company could send me a man it wants to develop. The man it wants to develop is you. Tellefsen does not resent the change; he is forty and content. You will, however, be the first mate of a bark on which the first mate has been Tellefsen for eight years, and the crew has the habit of Tellefsen, and you will earn the crew by your handling of the deck and not by your title. Tellefsen will help you. He will not undercut you. He is not that kind of man. But he is not going to do your job for you. Is that understood."

"Yes, sir."

"You have come from a ship under Brun. Brun is the kind of captain who teaches by quiet. I am not the kind of captain who teaches by quiet. I teach by shouting, by example, and by the small private notebook I keep on the bookshelf behind me. The notebook has the names of every officer who has sailed under me for thirty years. After your articles are signed off in April of '85, your name will be in that notebook with three or four lines of my assessment. Those three or four lines will, in the small Norwegian deep-water community, reach Bergen and Stavanger and Christiania within two years of your sign-off. They will determine the captaincies you can apply for. I am telling you this on the first afternoon so that you understand the stakes."

"Yes, sir."

"I will say one more thing on the first afternoon, Hestby."

"Yes, sir."

"I am not Brun. I will not be the captain Brun was for you. I will be a captain you will, at twenty-five, learn the rougher half of the trade from. You will, in the eighteen months, find me unpleasant at moments. You will find the bark unpleasant at moments. You will, if you do the job, leave this bark in April of '85 a different first mate than the one who came up the gangway today. Whether you leave a better one or a worse one is — by my notebook's record over thirty years — about even odds. I am telling you the odds. I am not interested in pretending the odds are better than they are."

"Yes, sir."

"You may go to your cabin."

"Yes, sir."

Olav stood. He left the cabin. He went forward.

---

He stood at the foredeck rail for some minutes.

He thought, at the rail, about the captain. He thought about Brun. He thought about his father at the morning's rail at the bark — the half-pace fallen-back in March, the one-sentence raising of the hand from the warehouse corner an hour ago. He thought about Olava at the kitchen table at Finnøy at this hour, with his mother across the table from her, doing whatever the small autumn evening at the kitchen had become.

He did not, in the thinking, allow himself to feel anything specific about the captain.

He had taken Thoresen's three-or-four-lines-in-the-notebook speech as the small honest captain's framing it had been. The captain was telling him the stakes; the captain was not pretending to be a different captain than he was. That was, Olav had to admit, more than some captains gave at the first afternoon. Brun had not, on the first day, framed the eighteen-months as a notebook entry. Brun had framed them as a ledger entry between two professionals. Thoresen was framing them as a record between a man and his own assessment of men. The framing was different. The framing might, Olav suspected, mean less protection at moments when the captain decided protection was not the cost-effective option.

He registered the thought.

He went below to his cabin.

In his cabin he sat at the small fixed desk for some minutes.

He took out a sheet of paper from the leather folder. He took the small bound book — the second one, the one he had bought at Stavanger in February to replace the first — and laid it beside the paper. He opened the small bound book.

He wrote, in his own hand, on the first clean page:

> *17 October 1883. Bergen, on the President Daae. Atlantic petroleum, Capt Thoresen. Eighteen months. The captain spoke clearly at the first interview. The trade will be different from Brun's. I am the first mate. Tellefsen the previous, now second. Lift tomorrow at six.*

He blotted the page.

He closed the book.

Then he wrote a short letter to Olava. The letter said he had reported, the captain had been clear at the first interview, the bark would lift on the morning tide, the mate (Tellefsen) was decent, and he was well. He sealed the letter with the red wafer. He addressed it to Mrs. Olav Hestby, Finnøy, Stavanger.

He took the letter on deck and gave it to Tellefsen, who would, Tellefsen said, hand it to a Bergen company runner before the bark lifted. The letter would be at Stavanger by the Friday coaster and at Finnøy by the following Monday.

He went back to his cabin.

He slept, the small uneven sleep of a man on the night before his first deep-water voyage as a first mate.

---

The bark lifted on the morning tide at six.

The harbor pilot took her out of Byfjorden in the grey October dawn. The crew worked the cables and the topsails as a crew that had been at this work together for years, with the small calibrated shorthand a long-serving deep-water crew used in the small operations of leaving a port: a half-word, a hand signal, the unhurried knowing where the next pull was. Olav stood on the quarterdeck with Thoresen and Tellefsen. He observed. He did not, on the first morning, give an order.

Thoresen did the giving. He gave the orders in a measured Bergen voice that was neither shouting nor quiet — the voice of a captain who had decided, by experience, that the lifting of a bark from her port was not a moment for either kind of voice but for the third kind, which was the captain's clear declarative middle register that the crew's ears had calibrated to obey.

The bark cleared the inner harbor at half past six.

The pilot left her at the outer light at half past seven.

The wind was a steady northerly. The bark set her course south-southwest down the coast. By eight bells of the morning watch she was making seven knots on a quartering breeze.

Olav, at the rail, watched the Norwegian coast go past at the slightly faster rate that an eight-month homecoming had taught him to feel as the rate at which a bark left her country.

He thought, briefly, of the same coast going past in June of '81 on the *Semiramis.*

He thought, briefly, of Olava at the kitchen at Finnøy at this hour — eight in the morning of a Wednesday in October, the first mug of coffee, the first slice of bread, the small familiar work of a Norwegian island woman starting her day.

He thought: *she will have my Tuesday letter on the Monday. She will have it five days from now. She will know I am at sea.*

He thought: *that is the rope.*

He turned from the rail. He went to the captain's chart-table to begin the work of being a first mate.

---

The bark made the latitude of the Lofoten by the third day. The wind held. The crossing was, by Tellefsen's quiet assessment over the first three days at the small officer's mess they shared in the deckhouse, an unusually fair October crossing for a westbound petroleum bark — the kind of opening a captain whose notebook was watched by company offices welcomed because it gave him no occasion to demonstrate his capacity for handling a bad one.

By the fifth day Olav had the deck.

Thoresen had handed it to him at four bells of the afternoon watch on the Sunday with the words, "You have it, Hestby," and had gone below. Tellefsen, at the rail, had nodded once at Olav and had taken his own quarter-hour's leisure at the windward side. Olav had stood at the captain's place on the quarterdeck — three feet from the binnacle, the same three feet Brun had stood at a hundred times in front of him — and had given his first orders as the man on watch on a Norwegian deep-water bark in the North Atlantic.

The orders were small ones. A trim adjustment at the main braces. A correction to the helm. A sending-aloft of two of the new common seamen to inspect a shroud the bosun had asked about.

The crew obeyed the orders without question.

Olav understood, at the first hour of his first solo watch as a first mate, that the obedience was the small everyday miracle a deep-water bark gave its first mate the moment the captain handed him the deck — that the crew, having been trained by the captain to obey the captain, transferred the obedience to the man the captain put in charge without re-examining whether the man deserved it. The earning, Tellefsen had said the previous afternoon, came later. The first hour was the gift.

He took the gift.

He gave the orders.

The bark ran on west-southwest at seven and a half knots into the open Atlantic.

That night in his cabin — the captain having taken the deck back at eight bells of the second dog-watch — Olav wrote a single line in the small bound book:

> *22 October 1883. Fifth day. Thoresen handed me the deck at four bells of the afternoon. I had it for four hours. I gave the orders. The crew obeyed. The bark ran west.*

He closed the book.

He blew out the lamp.

He slept, the small better sleep of a first mate who had been given the deck for four hours and had not, in the four hours, broken the deck.

The Atlantic ran under the bark.

The voyage was under way.
