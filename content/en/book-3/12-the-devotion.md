The bark made Bergen on the second Tuesday of March, 1884.

The crossing's last week had been the small clean homeward week a January-February eastbound bark hoped for and rarely got. The two storms of the first half had been the worst of it. The second half had been steady westerlies, fair seas, the bark making her seven knots almost without variation hour after hour. Olav had stood his watches. Tellefsen had stood his. Thoresen had taken the noon sights and the morning interviews and had, by the small daily calibration he kept of his own discipline, shouted at the first mate twice in the four weeks — once in the third week for a mis-trimmed topsail brace that Olav had not noticed (a two-minute shouting; Olav had not pushed back), and once in the fifth week for a small administrative error in the company log that Olav had let stand for half a day before correcting (a one-and-a-half minute shouting, which Olav understood as the captain's small downward calibration in Olav's favor across the voyage).

Hov the steward had, across the four homeward weeks, become a different steward.

The change had been the change of a man who had, on the morning after the four-bells-of-the-morning interview at the captain's chart-table, woken up sober, hungover, deeply ashamed, and entirely committed to the reformation Olav and Thoresen between them had given him the room to make.

The vinegar-cruet had been the first piece of the reformation.

Hov had, in the fortnight after the bottle incident, washed and re-purposed the empty Cornish bottle into a small clean cruet that sat at the centre of the steward's table at every meal. The cruet had no label. It carried the steward's vinegar — the small white English vinegar Hov made each Brooklyn stop from a Brooklyn-purchased mother-vinegar he kept in a stoppered jar in the galley — and the cruet was, by Hov's careful daily attention to it, always full and always clean and always at the same precise position at the centre of the table. The crew of the *Daae* had registered the cruet by the second week. They had not, by the small calibrated tact of a Norwegian deep-water crew that had been told nothing officially about the bottle incident, said anything about it. The cruet was the cruet. The fact of the cruet was the fact of what had happened. The two were the same and were not separately referred to.

This was the first piece. The second piece was harder for Olav to manage.

---

The second piece was Hov's quiet sustained attentiveness to Olav across the four weeks.

It began at the morning after the captain's interview. Hov, at the breakfast service, had brought Olav his coffee at the small officer's table with the small careful steward's gesture of a man who was, by his hand's slight extra slowness, communicating to the man he was serving that the serving was being done with a particular intention behind it. He had set the coffee down. He had paused for the count of one breath. He had said, in his ordinary steward's voice:

"Coffee, Mr. Hestby."

"Thank you, Hov."

He had moved on to Tellefsen.

The exchange had been, on its surface, the exchange that happened a hundred times a day on a Norwegian deep-water bark. But the count-of-one-breath pause had been a thing the surface did not contain. Olav had registered it. Tellefsen, across the table, had also registered it; Olav had seen Tellefsen's eye flick to Hov for a fraction of a second and then back to his own coffee. Tellefsen had not, at the breakfast or at any later meal, mentioned the pause.

The pause continued at every meal for the four weeks.

It continued at every other small steward-and-first-mate exchange. When Olav came up to the deck in the morning Hov was, at the galley door, available with a small mug of coffee a quarter of a minute earlier than the previous voyages' steward had been. When Olav went below to his cabin in the evening Hov had, at some small earlier hour, refilled the small water-pitcher on the cabin's washstand and laid out a clean towel. When Olav had — in the third week, on the cold afternoon after the second storm — torn the cuff of his coat against a brace, Hov had taken the coat from his cabin's hook in the night and had returned it before the morning watch with the cuff invisibly mended.

The cuff was the moment Olav understood what the second piece was going to require him to manage.

He had not asked Hov to mend the cuff. He had not registered that the cuff was tearable; he had hung the coat at the hook in the evening with the small tear at the cuff and had intended, at the morning, to mention it to Tellefsen and to take it down to the carpenter's small mending kit at his own first opportunity. Hov had taken it. Hov had mended it. Hov had returned it before Olav had to ask.

The mending was perfect. The cuff was indistinguishable from new.

Olav had registered the mending at the morning when he had put the coat on. He had stood at his cabin's small mirror with the coat at his shoulders and had registered, with the small flat clarity his interior used for the small structural moments, that Hov was — by these small daily acts — paying the steward's debt the captain had named.

He had, in the cabin, also registered that the paying had a shape Olav had not been prepared for.

The shape was the shape of a man who had, in the count of perhaps a minute on a North Atlantic deck, become so afraid of the consequences he had nearly visited on the man at the rail that the entire remainder of his service was now organized around the small daily un-doing of the bottle-incident — and that the un-doing had become, by the second week, the steward's central architecture of the voyage.

The mending was perfect.

It was also, Olav understood at the cabin mirror, too perfect.

---

He spoke to Tellefsen about it at the small officer's mess that evening.

"Tellefsen."

"Hestby."

"Hov mended the cuff of my coat."

"I saw the cuff."

"He did not need to. I had not asked. The mending is invisible."

"The mending is invisible. I noticed it at supper."

"He has been doing the small things. The coffee a quarter-minute earlier. The water-pitcher. The towel. The cuff."

"Yes."

"It is, on its face, a steward doing his work better. It is, in the four weeks since the captain's interview, more than that."

"It is more than that. It is a steward who has been told by the captain to find his own way of paying the debt and who has, by the second day, decided the way he is going to pay it. The way is by becoming the steward of one specific officer above all others. The officer is you. You will, for the remaining year of the voyage, be the steward's primary attention."

"That is what I am asking about."

"Yes. I see that."

"How do I manage it."

Tellefsen considered.

He drank the small mug of coffee Hov had brought to the mess at the start of the meal. He set it down.

"Hestby. The captain handles this kind of thing in a specific way. The captain does not, when a man on his bark develops an attachment to him of the sort that a man can develop for an officer he has been spared by, address the attachment. The captain accepts the attachment as the small private fact of the man's recovery and uses the attachment to keep the man functional for the rest of the voyage. The attachment is, in the captain's reading, the small private mechanism by which a man who has nearly broken his career repairs himself. The captain does not, by the small intervention that would feel kind, take the mechanism away from the man. The man needs the mechanism. The captain lets him have it."

"I am to let Hov have the attentiveness."

"You are to let him have it. You are, also, to not encourage it, and to not make of it a thing he can in any way mistake for a private intimacy of the sort that would be — for him as much as for you — unbearable. The line is fine. The line is the line a first mate of your kind has to learn. You will, in the eleven months remaining on this voyage, find the line by trial. I will not, in advance, give you the line. The line is yours."

"Yes."

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"You will also, at no point in the eleven months, describe the small daily acts as devotion. You may register them in your own head as devotion if you like. You will not say the word aloud. The word, said aloud, would change the shape of what Hov is doing into a thing he would have to defend or disclaim, and Hov is not in a position to do either. The acts are, in the working register of the bark, the steward serving the officer with care. That is the surface. The surface is what the bark uses. The not-surface is yours."

"I understand."

"Good."

He went back to his coffee.

---

Olav practised the line for the remainder of the homeward.

He thanked Hov at each meal in the same small ordinary first-mate's voice he used for Tellefsen at the same exchanges. He did not thank him more warmly. He did not thank him less warmly. He maintained, as Tellefsen had advised, the small ordinary working register of the bark. Hov continued the small daily acts. The acts continued to be perfect. The cruet continued to be at the centre of the steward's table.

At the noon-sight on the last Wednesday of February, with the bark perhaps six days from the Bergen approaches, the captain looked up from the binnacle at Olav.

"Hestby."

"Sir."

"The cruet."

"Yes, sir."

"The cuff."

"Yes, sir."

"The coffee a quarter-minute earlier."

"Yes, sir."

"You are letting him do them."

"I am letting him do them."

"Without encouragement."

"Without encouragement."

"Without saying the word."

"Without saying the word."

"Good. The handling is the handling. He will, by Bergen, have settled into what he is now. He will continue the acts for the remainder of the voyage. You will, in eleven months, have the cleanest first mate's coat on any Bergen bark this year. That is the cost of having handled the bottle the way you handled it. The cost is borne by Hov and by you in equal small daily measures. I am telling you so that you know I have noticed."

"Yes, sir."

"You may go forward, Hestby."

Olav went forward.

He understood, at the deck, that Thoresen had — by the register of his eight-word naming of the three small daily acts — given Olav the captain's confirmation that the line Olav had been finding by trial across the four weeks was the line the captain himself would have found. The confirmation was, in Thoresen's economy, the highest praise the captain gave a first mate for the handling of a piece of the trade that did not have a name in any of the books.

He took it.

He went to his watch.

---

The bark made Bergen on the morning tide of the second Tuesday of March.

The discharge of the small return cargo — the tools, the oil-lamps, the tobacco — began that afternoon and was complete by the Friday. The bark would lay at Bergen for two weeks for the small refit and the loading of the second outbound cargo. Thoresen had given Olav and Tellefsen alternating ashore-leave; Olav would take the first week, Tellefsen the second. Olav would, in his ashore-week, take the Friday coaster south to Stavanger and the Monday coaster across to Finnøy. He would have, by the company's calendar, six days at home before he had to be back at Bergen for the second outbound's lifting in the third week of March.

He came down the gangway on the Friday morning with his small ashore-bag and the small leather folder under his arm.

Hov was at the galley door.

"Hestby."

"Hov."

"Six days at Finnøy."

"Six days."

"You will be back the third week of March."

"Yes."

"Good. I will, in the six days, have the cabin ready for the second outbound. The wash will be done. The water-pitcher will be filled. The new towel from the Bergen ship-chandler will be on the washstand. The coat will be brushed."

"Yes."

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"I have, in the six days, also a thing I want to do for your wife at Finnøy that is not the cabin. It is the small parcel of preserves I have been making at the Brooklyn galley for the last week. A jar of plum, a jar of apple. Both Brooklyn fruit. They will keep through the spring. I am asking your permission to send them on the coaster with you."

Olav considered.

He had not, in the four weeks of practising the line, been prepared for Hov to extend the small daily acts to Olava. The line was harder here — Olava was not the bark's officer, not Hov's responsibility, not — strictly — within the steward's debt the captain had named. The preserves were a piece of Hov reaching past the bark's working register into Olav's life ashore.

He thought about it for the count of three seconds.

He thought: *if I refuse the preserves, I make Hov defend the offering. That is the thing Tellefsen warned against. If I accept the preserves, I extend the line by perhaps six inches. If the next Brooklyn brings a second extension and the one after that a third, the line will, by the end of the voyage, be at my kitchen table at Finnøy. That is too far.*

He said:

"Hov. The preserves are kind. I will take the plum. I will not take the apple. The plum I will give to my wife at Finnøy with your name on the jar. The apple you will keep at the galley for the second outbound's first storm, when the crew will need a small sweet thing. That is the arrangement."

Hov looked at him for a moment.

He nodded.

"The plum, then."

"The plum."

He went back into the galley. He came out with one small jar wrapped in a square of clean linen. He gave it to Olav. The jar was warm from the galley.

Olav took it. He put it in his ashore-bag.

"Thank you, Hov."

"You are welcome, Hestby."

"Six days."

"Six days. I will see you at the gangway on the Wednesday of the third week of March."

He went back into the galley.

Olav came down the gangway. He walked along the Bergen quay in the small clear March morning to the Stavanger coaster.

He thought, walking, that the line he had practised across the four weeks of the homeward had, at the gangway, been tested for the first time at the line's hardest place — the place where the steward's debt extended past the bark's working register into Olav's life ashore — and that he had, by accepting half the offering and re-routing the other half back into the bark's economy, found the calibration the line required at that place.

The plum jar was in his bag.

The apple jar was at the galley.

The line had held.

---

He took the coaster south.

By the small Norwegian-coaster economy of these years he was at Stavanger on the Friday evening, at the Tau quay on the Saturday morning, at the Finnøy jetty by the Saturday afternoon. His father met him at the jetty. Olava was at the path with her mother — Mrs. Lindøy had come over for the week's visit a fortnight before — and Mrs. Hestby was at the kitchen door with the small honey-cake she had been planning since the Friday of the previous week when Hofmoe's letter to the household had arrived with the bark's expected Bergen date.

He came up the path.

He gave Olava the plum jar at the kitchen door. He explained, in the small careful first mate's voice he had learned to use for explaining bark-events to his household, that the jar was from the bark's steward, a Bergen man named Hov, who had asked to send it. He did not explain the bottle incident. He did not, by his own accounting, owe Olava the bottle incident at the kitchen door at the moment of the homecoming. The bottle incident was a piece of the voyage that would, like other pieces, sit in the small private back of his head for the rest of his life and would not, in his lifetime, be named at any kitchen table.

Olava took the jar.

She thanked him in the small careful voice of a wife who had been told the necessary surface of the news and who registered, by the smallness of the surface, that a more complicated story sat under it.

She did not, by her habit, ask.

She set the jar on the kitchen shelf beside the small grandmother-candlestick and said:

"It is a good plum."

That was the first word of the first homecoming.

The six days began.
