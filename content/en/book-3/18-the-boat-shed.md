Olav came down the stairs at the small ordinary hour he had been coming down the stairs at his father's house since he was eleven.

It was half past six on the Tuesday morning of the second Tuesday of April of '85. The house was in its ordinary pre-breakfast quiet. His father was, by the small signs — the coat at the door, the boots gone from the kitchen mat, the mug at the table with the small ring of coffee at the bottom — already at the boat-shed. His mother was at the stove with the morning's bread. Olava was still upstairs in the loft; she had, across the long night of the talking, slept a slightly later sleep than he had, and he had, in getting up at the small turn of her breath that said she was not yet surfacing, decided to let her sleep another hour.

He took his coffee at the table. His mother did not speak much; she had read the small signs of the morning's descent and understood that the early hour belonged to father and son for a moment, and then to Olava whenever she came down.

He drank the coffee.

He took his coat from the door.

He went down the path to the boat-shed.

---

His father was at the workbench.

Jens Hestby was, at the workbench at seven in the April morning, at the small specific position he had been in for thirty-five years of mornings — the small particular left-shoulder-against-the-post stance, the small specific hand-on-the-plane grip — and the workbench had on it, this morning, the small planking for the next boat. The next boat was a repair job for a Stjernerøy man who had brought her in at the end of March; the plank had been cut the previous Saturday. Jens was hand-planing the long edge.

He did not, at Olav's coming in, look up.

Olav came to the other side of the bench. He did not, for a moment, speak.

He looked at his father's hands. He looked at the plank. He looked at the small careful motion of the plane.

He said, after a moment:

"Father."

"Olav."

"Can I take the second plane."

"Take it."

He took the second plane from the rack on the wall.

He came to the bench.

He started at the other end of the plank.

They worked together for the next hour without speaking.

---

This was, Olav registered at the first twenty minutes of the planing, the small return to the small specific rhythm the boat-shed had for him. The rhythm had been established in his eleventh year when his father had first put the small plane in his hand; it had been preserved across the five years of his deep-water voyages (even when he was not at the shed, the hand-memory of the shed had been kept); it had been exercised at the six days of the first homecoming in March of '84 and at the ten days of the second in October of '84. It was now being exercised again.

The rhythm was the thing the boat-shed gave him that no other place could.

He did not, at the rhythm, think about the loft of the previous night. The loft had been — he had registered at the top of the path on the walk down — a thing he was going to keep for himself across the morning. He was not going to bring it into the boat-shed. The boat-shed was the boat-shed. The loft was the loft. The two were the small clean-separate rooms of his life at Finnøy and his discipline would be to keep them separate.

He planed.

He registered, after about forty minutes, that the small drawer under the underside of the workbench was at his right hand.

He did not, in the registering, break the rhythm of the planing. He planed another five minutes.

Then he said, without looking up:

"Father."

"Yes."

"The drawer."

"Yes."

"Have you touched it."

"I have not touched it."

"In the two years and a month."

"In the two years and a month. It has been where it has been since the May you put the thing in it in '81."

"You have been at the workbench three thousand mornings."

"Approximately three thousand. I have been at the workbench without touching the drawer at any of them. The drawer is not a drawer I have the right to touch. The drawer is yours."

"Yes."

"Olav."

"Yes."

"I am not, at this workbench this morning, asking. I have not, at any of the three thousand mornings, asked. I am saying only that I have known, since '81, that the drawer has a thing in it. I have known since I came into the shed for the small morning's work on the day after you came down to Stavanger in June of '81 and I saw the drawer was not in the position it had been in when we closed the shed the previous Saturday. The thing in the drawer has been there since then. I do not know what the thing is. I have, across the two and a half years, not at any point asked to know."

Olav did not, for some seconds, answer.

He planed.

His father planed.

Then Olav said:

"Father."

"Yes."

"There are two things in the drawer now."

"Yes."

"Since '82."

"Since '82. I know. I saw the drawer was in a different position in the autumn of '82 when you came back from the *Semiramis*."

"Yes."

"I have not asked."

"You have not asked."

"Olav."

"Yes."

"I will not, at the workbench, ever ask. The drawer is yours. I am telling you this morning only because you are home and the drawer is now — by the small arithmetic of the cottage's completion in four weeks — a drawer that is, in effect, at a workbench that will be used by me alone across the rest of my life, because you will be at the cottage's workbench when you have one. The drawer at this workbench will, after the cottage, be at a workbench you are not at on any given day. I am telling you because I do not want, across the rest of my life, you to come across the channel and wonder whether the drawer has been touched while you were not here. The drawer will not be touched. The drawer will be left as it is. I am telling you so that you know."

"Thank you, Father."

"You are welcome."

"I will, when the cottage is built, take the drawer's contents to the cottage's workbench."

"That would be my advice, if I were giving advice. You are a married man with your own workbench coming. The drawer's contents belong at your workbench."

"I will do it in May."

"Good."

They did not, for the rest of the planing, speak again.

They finished the long edge at a quarter to nine. Jens brought out the second plank. They started on the second plank.

---

At nine Olav's mother came down to the boat-shed with the small coffee-tray.

She had, by the small signs, registered that the father and son were at the workbench and had decided to bring the second coffee down to the shed rather than call them up. This was the small specific gesture his mother used at the mornings at which she did not want to break the rhythm. She set the tray on the small side-table at the door, and she said at the door:

"The coffee."

"Thank you," they said together.

She looked at Olav for a beat.

"Olava is up."

"Good."

"She is having her coffee at the table. I have told her there is no hurry."

"Thank you, Mother."

She went back up the path.

Olav and his father took their mugs from the tray. They drank at the bench.

Olav said, after the second sip:

"Father."

"Yes."

"Last night at the loft."

"Yes."

"I do not have to explain."

"No. Your mother's arrangement. She has been planning the small specifics for a month. The loft was the room. The loft was the right room. I did not, in the small arrangement, participate beyond staying at the boat-shed an extra hour on the Saturday so your mother could turn the bed without me in the kitchen's way."

"You knew."

"I knew. Your mother told me the broad shape in March. The broad shape was a shape I did not need the particulars of. I have — by your mother's arrangement and by my own small instinct for what a first night of a long homecoming requires of a household — stayed at the boat-shed later than usual on the Saturday and left the house to the loft on the Monday evening. That is what a father of a son of your generation does. That is what my father would have done for me in '58. That is what his father would have done for him in whatever year of his own homecoming. The particulars stay with the married couple. The staying-out-of-the-way is the household's contribution."

"Thank you."

"You are welcome."

"Father."

"Yes."

"I did not, in the last two years, know how to tell you how grateful I am for the household's contribution."

"You have not had to tell me. I have known. Your mother has known. We have been, at the household, grateful that you and Olava have been the kind of son and daughter-in-law who did not need to be told to be grateful to each other's households. You have done the work of a husband and she has done the work of a wife, and the households have, by the small signs we have read, been recognized in the doing. That is the contribution's reward. It is enough."

"Yes."

They drank the rest of their coffee at the bench.

They went back to the planing.

---

At ten o'clock Olava came down to the boat-shed.

She was at the door.

Olav looked up.

She had the small grey wool dress on, and her hair was up under the small white cap she wore at the mornings at Finnøy, and she had a small jar in her hand. The jar was a jar of preserves — apricot, by the colour through the glass — that Mrs Hestby had finished over the winter.

"Olav."

"Yes."

"Your mother sent down the mid-morning bread and preserves for the boat-shed. I said I would bring them."

"Good."

"Jens."

"Olava."

"The bread is at the kitchen. I wanted to ask the two of you whether the mid-morning bread should be at the kitchen or down here."

"Here," Jens said. "The planing is clean work; we will not soil the bread."

"I will bring it down."

"Thank you."

She set the jar on the small side-table. She did not immediately turn to go back up; she looked at the bench for a moment, and at the two men at it, and at the small two-man rhythm they had been keeping for the three hours since Olav had come in.

Then she said, in a small register Olav had not heard her use before:

"The two of you at the bench."

"Yes," Jens said.

"This is what the shed looks like when the son is home."

"Yes."

"It has been the shed I have been imagining across the eighteen months when I heard Jens at the workbench through the kitchen window."

"That was the image you had."

"That was the image I had."

She looked at Olav. She looked at Jens. She did not say more. She turned and went back up the path.

Jens watched her go for a second.

He turned back to the plank.

"Good wife, Olav."

"I know."

"I mean more than the first sense of it. I mean she has been at the window during the eighteen months looking at the shed and keeping, in her head, the image of the shed that the son's return would fill. I know because your mother was at the same window keeping the same image for the six months when I was away in '57. The two Norwegian wives at the window of a Finnøy kitchen at the same image across a generation. That is a shape."

"Yes."

"Good wife."

"Yes."

He went back to the planing.

---

They worked, with Olava bringing the bread down at ten-thirty and the three of them eating the bread and the preserves at the small bench-corner together, until noon.

At noon the three of them came up to the house for the noon dinner.

Mrs Hestby had the small Tuesday dinner ready. They sat at the four places at the table.

The four of them ate.

This was, Olav registered at the second bite, the first four-place noon-dinner of the married household since the wedding reception supper of March of '83. Every other four-place dinner the parents-plus-the-married-couple had had — at Christmases, at Easters, at the small visit from Lindøy — had had Mrs Lindøy or the captain at the fifth place or more. The four-place Tuesday noon-dinner was the first of its kind.

He registered it.

He did not, at the table, say so.

---

The afternoon ran to the small ordinary pattern his father and he had been working out at the shed for the month: Jens at the boat-shed for the carpentry; Olav at the shed with him for the mornings; Olav at the household for the afternoons; Olava at the kitchen with Mrs Hestby for most hours. The pattern would, Olav understood at the Tuesday afternoon of his second day home, become the household's pattern for the four weeks until the cottage was ready.

He was, at the pattern, home.

He did not, at the pattern, need anything else.

He did not, in the workbench afternoon, open the drawer.

He had told his father at the morning that he would, in May, take the drawer's contents to the cottage's workbench. He would do it then. He would not, in the interim, open the drawer to check on its contents, because the contents were the contents, and the check would be the small indulgence a man of his discipline had decided not to take.

The drawer held.

The house held.

---

That evening at the supper table — now the small ordinary four-place supper of the household — his mother said, in the small register she used when she had been saving a sentence across the day:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"The cottage in four weeks."

"Yes."

"I have been talking to Olava across the day about the small household-moves. There are some things — the grandmother-candlestick, the leather folder with your letters from the voyages, the small writing-desk that was mine before it was hers — that will go across on the Tuesdays of the four weeks. The larger things will go on the Saturday of the cottage's readiness. The arrangement is the arrangement we have been planning."

"Yes."

"I want you to take the drawer's contents across on the first Tuesday of May."

Olav looked at her.

Jens, across the table, did not look up from his plate.

Olava, beside Olav, took a small breath.

His mother did not, at the sentence, look at him. She looked at her own plate. She continued:

"The first Tuesday of May is the Tuesday before the cottage's readiness on the Saturday. The morning of the first Tuesday is the morning I have decided is the right morning for the small specific move. Jens will be at the boat-shed for the full morning. Olava will be at the household with me. You will be alone at the shed. You will take the drawer's contents. You will put them in the small leather wallet you have been keeping for small transport purposes. You will take them across the path to the cottage. You will put them in the small drawer under the cottage's workbench, which Jens has, at my small suggestion, built into the cottage's workbench in the same position the drawer at this shed is. I have told Jens. Jens has built it. The cottage's workbench drawer is waiting."

She looked at Olav once.

"I am not asking, Olav."

"No."

"I am telling you only that the drawer at the cottage has been built in the same position and that the first Tuesday of May is the move's day."

"Yes, Mother."

"That is all."

She went back to her plate.

---

Olav, at the table, did not speak for some seconds.

He understood — with the small flat clarity his interior used for the small structural moments — that his mother had, by the small careful arrangement of her announcement at the Tuesday supper-table, given him the small specific household-plan for the continuation of the drawer's discipline across the move to the cottage. The drawer had been, at his father's boat-shed, at a small specific position. The drawer at the cottage's workbench would be, by Jens's hand on his mother's small suggestion, at the same specific position. The discipline would be continuous. The move would be the small specific handing-over the son's marriage required, and the mother had designed it.

His mother had, in designing it, done what Mrs Hestby of the '57 November had done and what Mrs Thoresen of the '74 spring had done. She had administered the not-asking. She had administered it at the scale of the household's architecture. The drawer at the cottage would, by her small specific design, be the same drawer in a new location — not replaced, not inventoried, just moved.

He said, in the small composed voice he had been practising at the supper:

"Thank you, Mother."

"You are welcome."

"I will make the move on the first Tuesday of May."

"Good. The leather wallet is in the parlour cupboard."

"Yes."

"Good."

She did not, at the table, say more about it. She asked Olava to pass the bread. Olava passed it. The supper went on.

---

That night Olav lay in the married-bedroom upstairs — not the loft; the loft had been the Monday-night room, and the bedroom was the ordinary married-at-home room — with Olava beside him.

They did not, this night, talk about the loft. The loft had been the loft. The ordinary married nights of the marriage were, from this evening forward, the ordinary married nights.

Olava said, in the quiet of the small bedroom after the lamp had been turned down:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"Your mother's announcement."

"Yes."

"You understood it."

"I understood it."

"Good. I understood it too. I am telling you only so you know I am in the same understanding."

"Yes."

"The drawer will move on the first Tuesday of May."

"The drawer will move."

"I will be at the household with your mother across the morning. I will not come across to the cottage until the afternoon. The morning will be yours alone with the drawer's move."

"Yes."

"Goodnight, Olav."

"Goodnight, Olava."

They slept.

The house at Finnøy at ten o'clock on the Tuesday evening of the second Tuesday of April of '85 held its four occupants in the small ordinary quiet of a Norwegian island household two days into the son's full return, and the morning's planing and the noon-dinner and the supper-announcement had together been the small specific second day of the new ordinary.

The first Tuesday of May was four weeks away.

The cottage was ready in four weeks.

The drawer would move.

The new decade of the marriage had begun.
