The first Tuesday of May was the fifth of the month.

Jens was at the boat-shed at six, as he had been every morning for thirty-five years. Olava was at the kitchen at six-thirty with Mrs Hestby, beginning the small Tuesday bread. Olav came down the stairs at half past six. He took his coffee. He said nothing at the table. His mother said nothing at the stove. Olava said nothing at the window. The household had, by the choreography his mother had designed at the previous week's Tuesday supper, arranged the morning such that the three people who were not making the move made themselves small about it.

At seven Olav went down the path to the boat-shed.

His father was at the workbench on a repair for a Mosterøy hand. He looked up briefly when Olav came in. He nodded once. He did not speak. He took his coat from the hook by the door and he walked out of the shed up the path to the small upper paddock to check on the cow, which he had not needed to check on at that hour in several years but which was the small pretext the morning required.

Olav was alone at the shed.

He took the small leather wallet from the inside pocket of his coat. He had brought it down from the parlor cupboard the previous evening at his mother's quiet suggestion. It was the wallet he had used for his school papers as a boy.

He went to the workbench. He knelt at the underside. He slid his hand into the small drawer under the bench.

The two letters were where they had been since the second Tuesday of November of '82.

He took them out in the order they had gone in — Haakon's first, Anders's second — and he laid them flat in the small leather wallet without unfolding them. Haakon's was folded once, the way Haakon had folded it in May of '81; Anders's was folded once, the way Olav had folded it the morning it had come to Finnøy in November of '82. Neither wafer had been re-broken. Neither letter had been re-read. Olav did not re-read either now. The move was a move, not a reading.

He closed the wallet.

He put the wallet in the inside pocket of his coat.

He stood at the workbench for a moment with his palm on the top of the bench above the small empty drawer — the same way he had stood when he had first stopped at the workbench on the morning of the first Wednesday home from the *Semiramis,* when he had decided not to open the drawer. The drawer was, this morning, empty for the first time in four years.

He closed the small drawer.

He went out of the boat-shed.

---

He walked the quarter-mile along the lane to the cottage.

The cottage had been finished by Jens and Olav's uncle and Olav in the last eighteen months. It was a small three-room cottage with a kitchen, a main room, and a bedroom, with a small loft above for the children that were not yet anyone. It had, on its south-facing side, a small boat-shed of its own — built into the same small stone foundation the main cottage sat on, an arrangement Olav's uncle had designed at Olav's request for the purpose of giving Olav a workbench he could call his own.

The cottage's door was open. He had left it open at the small inspection he had made the previous Saturday. The small workbench his uncle had built for him was in the small boat-shed, with the small drawer under the underside built at the same position it had been at his father's bench.

The drawer was empty.

He knelt at the underside.

He slid the two letters out of the wallet. He laid them in the small drawer in the same order they had been in — Haakon's first, Anders's second — with the inch of space between them he had arranged at Finnøy in November of '82. The drawer accepted them. The drawer was, by Jens's careful copying, the same drawer in its proportions and in its position.

He closed the drawer.

He stood at the cottage's workbench for a moment.

He did not, at the workbench, say anything aloud. He had not, at the boat-shed at Finnøy, either. The move had been a silent move, and the installation at the cottage was a silent installation. The drawer at the cottage would, from the fifth of May of '85 forward, hold the two letters.

He walked out of the small boat-shed.

He closed the cottage's door.

He walked back along the lane to his father's house.

At the kitchen door Olava was at the window.

She looked at him.

He nodded, once.

She nodded back.

That was the full of the ceremony.

---

The Saturday after the drawer-move, the household transferred.

Olav's uncle brought the small cart from Mosterøy for the larger items — the grandmother-candlestick, the small writing-desk Mrs Hestby had given Olava, the small oak chest Mrs Lindøy had sent across with Marit the previous summer, the small bed-frame that had been Olav's at the upstairs bedroom, the two chairs, the small wall-clock Olava had brought from Lindøy. The cart made four trips between Jens's house and the cottage across the Saturday afternoon. By six in the evening the cottage had everything it needed to be a household.

Mrs Hestby came down to the cottage at seven for the small first-supper.

Mrs Lindøy had not, by Olava's request the previous week, come across from Lindøy for this first-supper. Olava had wanted the first-supper to be the small four-person supper of Olav, Olava, Jens, and Mrs Hestby — the same four people as the Tuesday noon-dinner of the third week in April, only now at the cottage rather than at Jens's house. Mrs Lindøy had understood and had written a small note wishing the cottage's first supper well.

The four of them ate at the cottage's small table.

Olava had made the small meal. It was her first full supper cooked at her own kitchen as the head of her own household. She had made pickled herring, boiled potatoes, the small flatbread she had learned from Mrs Hestby across the eighteen months, and a small honey-cake Mrs Hestby had sent across the lane in the afternoon as a gift for the cottage's first evening.

They ate.

Jens said at the end of the supper — in the small Jens voice he used at the formal occasions — "Olava."

"Yes, Jens."

"This is a good supper."

"Thank you, Jens."

"You have made a good household."

"Thank you."

"Good."

He did not, at the table, say more.

That was the small first-supper.

---

Mrs Hestby and Jens walked back up the lane at half past eight.

Olav and Olava did the small first-evening's dishes at the small kitchen sink.

The cottage was, in the small lamplight, the cottage.

They did not, that first evening, go up to the small loft — the cottage's loft was, by the small design of the cottage, a storeroom, not a bedroom — and they slept in the bedroom on the small married-bed-frame Olav's uncle had moved across that afternoon.

It was, Olav registered at the small bed on the first night at the cottage, the small bed he had been in at his father's house since the homecoming. The same bed. The small household had moved the bed. The bed was the bed.

Olava said beside him, in the quiet:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"The cottage."

"The cottage."

"It is ours."

"It is ours."

"Good."

They slept.

---

The summer of '85 went at the pace a Finnøy summer went at a married household's first year.

Olav was at the boat-shed at his father's from seven to noon on most weekdays. He was at the cottage's small workbench in the afternoons, working on the small projects the cottage itself still required — the small adjustment to the kitchen door, the small shelf Olava wanted at the small parlor-corner, the small repair to the west wall where a stone had settled. He was at the small Lindøy jetty on most Saturdays for the small Lindøy visits the Christmas-of-'79 agreement had required the marriage to keep across the years. He was, at the kitchen table at the cottage at suppers, with Olava.

Olava was at the cottage's kitchen for the hours he was at the boat-shed, and at Mrs Hestby's kitchen for one afternoon a week, and at the small parish work Pastor Kleven's wife had found for her by the middle of June — the reading-help Mrs Kleven had arranged at the parish school where Olava had begun to teach the smallest children their letters on the Tuesday and Friday mornings. The reading-help was, by Mrs Kleven's judgment, Olava's small gift — she had been reading and writing at a high standard since she was nine, and the parish had identified her as the woman who could help the five-year-olds at the small quiet pace they needed — and it gave Olava two mornings a week of work outside the household that had become, across the first summer, the addition to her life at the cottage that made the cottage the cottage rather than the extension of Mrs Hestby's kitchen.

---

In the first week of July Olav's uncle came to the cottage on a small Sunday afternoon visit.

He had with him a small proposal.

"Olav."

"Uncle."

"I have been holding a quarter share in a small smack out of Stavanger — the *Solveig,* a twelve-ton coaster that runs from Stavanger to the small Ryfylke ports — for six years. The Stavanger man who held the other three quarters has decided, at fifty-eight, to retire from the run. He is selling his three quarters at a small fair price. I have been considering buying the three quarters myself. I have concluded, in the week of thinking about it, that I am sixty-two and do not want to run a smack alone for the years I have left. I would like to propose, instead, that you buy two of the three quarters and that I buy one, which combined with my existing quarter gives me two quarters. The smack would be ours, Olav. You and I would run her together at the Ryfylke runs, the way my father and I ran the small coaster at Ryfylke in the '50s. I am asking you because I would like, at sixty-two, to have the work of my last working years be with you at the Ryfylke ports."

Olav considered.

It was the small proposal he had been, without entirely articulating it to himself, half-expecting since the homecoming. His uncle was his father's older brother; had, since Olav was nine, been the small second-father who had taught him the pieces of the trade his father had not taught him; had said at the small cottage-planning table in the spring of '83 that he would be at Olav's side for whatever the married man's trade became. The smack was the kind of proposal a small second-father made at sixty-two.

He said:

"Uncle."

"Yes."

"The figure for the two quarters."

"Eighteen hundred kroner."

Olav had, at the pay-off at Bergen in April, come home with four thousand kroner from the *Daae.* He had, after the cottage's completion costs and the small summer's household expenses, about three thousand two hundred kroner in the small leather folder at the cottage's writing-desk. Eighteen hundred was a large portion. It was, by his uncle's careful naming of it at the small Sunday afternoon, the portion that was the full share of the proposal.

He said:

"Uncle."

"Yes."

"I will take the two quarters at eighteen hundred."

"Good."

"I will, at the small Stavanger notary's office on the Thursday of next week, make the transfer."

"I will come with you on the Thursday."

"Good."

That was the small meeting.

They went back to the small Sunday afternoon.

---

The *Solveig* was a small twelve-ton Ryfylke coaster that had been built at a Stavanger yard in '76 and had run the Stavanger-to-Sand route for nine years under the same captain-owner. She had, by Olav's small inspection of her at the Stavanger quay on the Thursday of the transfer, the small careful upkeep a smack of her size could expect under a competent captain-owner — her standing rigging was sound, her hull seams were tight, her small single-sheet rig was well-worn but well-maintained. She would, by Olav's assessment, run another decade before she needed a major refit.

He and his uncle ran her together from the small end of July.

The running was not at-sea in the deep-water sense. The *Solveig* did not go beyond the Ryfylke, and the longest single run she made was a six-hour run from Stavanger to Sand. She was, as coastal smacks went, a small local boat doing small local work. The cargo was the mixed cargo of a small coaster — small boxes of goods for the Ryfylke shops, small passenger traffic between the islands, small boxes of mail for the Ryfylke post-offices, occasionally a small heavy item like a piece of farm machinery being brought up from Stavanger for a Ryfylke farmer. The wages were the wages of a small coaster's quarter-share — perhaps a hundred kroner a month in the good months, less in the winter.

It was not the trade of a deep-water bark.

It was, Olav registered at the second week of running her, the trade he had at twenty-five on the afternoon of Brun's speech at the Calcutta customhouse stairs understood he would eventually walk into — the small coastal trade of a Norwegian married man who came home to his own kitchen at most evenings and who had, by the accumulated years of the deep-water voyages, the competence to run any boat in any weather the Ryfylke could give him. The trade was a small version of the trade he had been walking toward since '81. It was not, he understood at the second week, the final version. The final version — by Brun's prediction of '82 and Thoresen's notebook line of '83 and Holm's *waiting for, for nine years* of the same year — would come in some year between now and 1895, when Olav would, he suspected, take his own captain's ticket at Stavanger and would sign on as the master of a small Stavanger coaster and, after some years at that, perhaps a larger one.

But not now.

Now was the *Solveig* with his uncle, on the Ryfylke, at home most evenings.

He was content with it.

---

Olava was — the first summer at the cottage revealed — more than content with it.

Olava had, across the summer, settled into the domestic rhythm of a married Finnøy woman of her generation with the small particular additional work of the parish school two mornings a week. She was, in the evenings at the cottage, quieter than she had been at Mrs Hestby's kitchen in the eighteen months — not unhappy, just less talkative; the quieter-evening a woman who was running her own kitchen was at night because the running had already consumed the day's small supply of talking. She was, at the mornings, the small composed early-riser who had already done the bread before Olav came in from the small morning boat-shed work.

She was happy.

Olav registered the happiness in the small signs his eighteen months of first-mate observation had taught him to register. The set of her shoulders at the small kitchen table. The small laugh she produced at the Sunday dinners with his parents at Mrs Hestby's. The small unhurried pace of her walking at the path.

He did not, to her, name the happiness.

He registered it.

---

At the end of September, on the small Sunday evening of the last Sunday of the month, Olav and Olava sat at the small parlor-corner of the cottage after Jens and Mrs Hestby had gone back up the lane from the small Sunday supper.

Olava said, in the small quiet voice she used when she had a thing to say:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"There is a thing I want to tell you."

"Tell me."

"I am perhaps with child."

He did not, for a beat, speak.

He had not, in the five months since the cottage, allowed himself the specific expectation of the announcement. He had known, in the back of his head, that the announcement was a thing the marriage's first year was likely to produce at some point; he had not, in the small front of his head, held the date of the announcement at any particular week.

He said:

"Olava."

"Yes."

"How do you know."

"I do not, yet, know with certainty. I have, by my small careful arithmetic of the small women's signs, reason to believe. Mrs Lindøy, when I mentioned the possibility at her kitchen last Saturday, said the signs were the signs. I will know with certainty in another month."

"A month."

"A month, perhaps six weeks."

"If you are."

"If I am, the child will come in the early part of June."

"Olava."

"Yes."

"I am glad."

"I am glad too."

"Are you."

"I am."

He took her hand. She let him.

They sat at the small parlor-corner for some minutes without speaking.

---

Olav did not, that night in bed, write anything.

He had not, since the small bound book of the *Daae*'s voyage had closed at the Bergen rooming-house in April, been keeping the small bound book. The small bound book had been a book of the voyages. He was, from April, at home. At-home life did not, by his small rule, require the small bound book. The ledger was Olava's. The book was closed.

He lay in the small bed with Olava's back against his chest.

He thought, at the ceiling:

*If she is, the child will come in the early part of June. The child will be the first of, I suspect, two or three. The child will be a Hestby. The child's name, if it is a boy, will be Jens — my father's name, the family's oldest name. The child's name, if it is a girl, will be Anna — my mother's name, my grandmother's name, the family's oldest woman's name.*

*The Hestby church on the hill will, in the baptism of the child, put another Hestby in the register. The baptismal name will, by Pastor Kleven's keeping, be in the old spelling: Hestbye.*

*The line continues.*

*I am home.*

He did not, in the thinking, allow the thought to expand into the small easy sentimentality it would have accepted if he had let it. He had learned, across the eighteen months, to keep the small flat register for the small structural moments; he kept it now.

He listened to Olava's breath slow into sleep.

He slept.

The cottage at Finnøy on the last Sunday evening of September of '85 held its two occupants, and perhaps a third one in the beginning, in the small ordinary quiet of a married household at the close of its first full summer at its own address.

The first land year was ending.

The second land year would bring, if the small women's signs had read correctly, a small first Hestby of the next generation to the kitchen door.
