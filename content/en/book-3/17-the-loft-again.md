Olava came across on the Monday coaster at three o'clock in the afternoon.

Olav was at the jetty. He had walked down the path alone at two; his mother had stayed at the kitchen with the small bread she was finishing for the afternoon, and his father had stayed at the boat-shed with the small carpentry he was finishing for the afternoon, and by the small unspoken arrangement of the household the jetty had been left, at three o'clock, for Olav alone.

The coaster came up the channel.

He watched it come up.

He had, at the jetty on the second Monday of April in the small April light of a Norwegian spring afternoon, the particular small feeling a man had at the jetty when he was waiting for his wife to step off a coaster after a thirty-hour separation that was, by the small arithmetic of the eighteen months, the shortest separation he had had from her in his married life. The feeling was not the feeling of the homecoming at Stavanger on the Saturday. The Saturday had been the formal homecoming, with the parents at the awning and the captain beside them and the small public arrangement of a Norwegian island family at a quay. The Monday was different. The Monday was the first time in eighteen months he would see her alone, without the families at the edges of the moment.

He was alone at the jetty.

The coaster tied up at the boards at twenty past three. The gangway went over.

Olava came down.

She was in the wool coat.

---

She crossed the small distance between them.

She did not, on the boards, put her hands on his face. She had spent the gesture at Stavanger on the Saturday, by her own economy, and the Monday afternoon was not the hour for it. She came up to him and she took his hand — his hand, not his arm — and she stood beside him with her hand in his and she looked at his face for a moment.

Then she said, quietly:

"Come up."

He came up with her.

They walked up the path from the jetty to the house in silence.

The house was at the top of the path, as it had been since 1812. His mother was at the kitchen window; she saw them coming; she moved away from the window and went to the stove to begin the small final preparation of the Monday supper. His father was at the boat-shed; he would, by the small arrangement of the household, stay at the boat-shed until the small bell his mother rang at six for the supper.

At the door Olava stopped for a moment.

She took off her cap. She hung it on the peg. She undid the small pearl button at the collar of her wool coat. She took off the coat. She hung it on the hook that had been her hook at the small corner since the summer of '83.

She looked at Olav at the kitchen door.

"I am home."

"You are home."

"Yes."

She went into the kitchen to his mother.

---

The supper was the Monday supper.

His father came in from the boat-shed at six. He washed his hands at the small basin by the door. He sat at his place at the table. His mother had laid four plates — herring, potatoes, the bread, a small piece of smoked mutton she had been saving for the Monday — and the small Monday-supper of the household was, for the first time in eighteen months, a four-place supper with the son and his wife at their ordinary seats opposite the parents.

They ate.

They talked, at the supper, about the small news of the household. His father reported on the small carpentry he had finished that afternoon — a repair on the door of the small upper paddock that had been sagging since the winter. His mother reported on the small bread. Olava reported on the Lindøy household's small news of the morning: Marit and Eirik were coming over on the Wednesday coaster for the small supper the household was planning; Mrs Lindøy was baking the small butter-bread for the Wednesday; the captain had sent a small greeting.

Olav did not, at the table, tell the long-letter version of the eighteen months. He had decided at the Saturday supper that the long version would, by the slow accretion of days, reach his parents in the proportions the two women chose. He had not, at the Saturday supper, told them much at all. He had, at the Monday supper, told them a little more — the Brooklyn winter weather at the third voyage; the force-eleven storm on the homeward; Hov's vinegar-cruet, without the whiskey-bottle behind it. The small careful ration he was giving his parents was the ration.

Olava, at the supper, did not press him to tell more.

She knew. She had the long letter.

---

At half past seven his mother, at the stove, said:

"Olav. Olava."

"Yes, mother."

"The loft."

Olava looked at her.

Olav looked at her.

His mother was at the stove with her back to the table. She had been, across the day, finishing the small arrangement she had been designing for some weeks. The arrangement had included, by his mother's small private decision on the Saturday evening after the dishes, that the Monday evening's bedroom for Olav and Olava would be the loft and not the upstairs married-bedroom. The loft was the wedding-night room. The married-bedroom was the ordinary married-at-home room. The Monday night was not the ordinary. The Monday night was the first night of Olava and Olav at Finnøy together after eighteen months. His mother had decided that the first night required the loft.

She had turned the bed in the loft that afternoon.

She had made it wide.

She had put in the second pallet, the extra blanket, the pillow.

She had done the same motions she had done on the wedding morning in March of '83.

She did not, at the stove, explain any of this.

She said only:

"The loft."

Olava said, in her small composed voice:

"Yes, Mother."

She stood. She took her small overnight-bag — which Olav's father had put, that afternoon on her arrival, at the bottom of the stairs — and she went up.

Olav stood.

He looked at his mother's back at the stove for a beat.

He followed Olava up.

---

The loft was the same small room it had been on the wedding night in March of '83.

The window at the gable end gave onto the boat-shed and the channel beyond. The bed was the narrow bed he had slept in alone until he was sixteen, which his mother had turned that afternoon into the wider married-night bed with the second pallet and the extra blanket. The lamp was on the small table at the foot of the bed. The water-pitcher on the washstand was full. The towel was clean.

Olava set her bag on the small chair.

She went to the window. She looked at the channel.

"Olav."

"Yes."

"Come and look."

He came.

They stood at the window for a minute without speaking.

She said, after the minute:

"I looked at this channel from this window twice in the twenty-five months since the wedding. Once on the wedding night. Once on the Christmas morning of '83, when I came up to see the small view I had only seen once. The second time — the Christmas — was the time I decided, in my head, that the next time I stood at this window it would be on the first night of your homecoming from the *Daae,* and that the first-night-of-the-homecoming would be the night I had been preparing for across the eighteen months."

"The night you had been preparing for."

"Yes."

"Olava."

"Yes."

She turned from the window.

"I have been preparing, Olav, across the eighteen months, for the night my husband came home from a two-year voyage and I had him to myself in a room with a door that closed. The preparing has been the small private preparing of a wife who is twenty-four years old and has been married for just over two years and has had her husband, in the days of the marriage that were days rather than letters, for about eight weeks total across those two years. The preparing has been a preparing of the body. The preparing has been a preparing of the particular small things I wanted to do when he came home. I am telling you about the preparing now because I want you, before we turn down the lamp, to know that the preparing is what has been happening on my side of the eighteen months in addition to the letters and the ledger and the kitchen at Finnøy."

He looked at her.

She was, in the lamp's small light, the twenty-four-year-old Olava of the wool coat at the Stavanger quay with her hand at the back of his neck for six breaths, and she was the Olava of the March-of-'83 loft who had handled the small mechanical adjustment without fuss, and she was the Olava of the Christmas midnight bench in '82 who had named the marriage's form, and she was also — at the window of the loft in April of '85 — the Olava who had decided at the midnight-bench Christmas of '83 that this specific first night of this specific homecoming was the night the body she had been preparing would be given to him.

He understood, at the window, what she was telling him.

He said:

"Olava."

"Yes."

"Tell me what the preparing was."

She did not, for a moment, answer.

Then she said, in the small quiet voice she had used at the Christmas bench:

"I will tell you at the bed. Not at the window. The window is the place I say this at. The bed is the place I do it at. Come."

She crossed to the bed.

She sat on the edge of the bed. She undid the small pearl button at the collar of her dress — the small gray wool dress her mother had been letting her borrow from the family's Sunday-best chest since the wedding — and she did this without ceremony, the way she had done at the wedding night in March of '83, with the small composed practical motions of a woman who had decided what she was going to do at what pace.

She looked at Olav at the window.

"Olav."

"Yes."

"Come."

He came.

---

What happened in the loft across the hour that followed was the thing Olav had been waiting for across the eighteen months.

It was the thing Olava had been preparing for.

The two were not, by any exact measure, the same thing. Olav had been waiting, across the long letters and the short letters and the six nights at the Brooklyn wharf, for the moment at which he came back into the room with Olava and the room was a room they had earned the right to be private in; he had not been waiting, specifically, for the body's particulars — the particulars had been, in his head, part of the larger waiting but not the specific end of it. Olava had been preparing, across the same eighteen months, at the specific end. She had been thinking about the particulars. She had been reading the small careful books her mother had given her at the wedding in a second pass she had not done at the first night. She had been imagining, in the small private register a twenty-four-year-old Norwegian wife of her generation imagined in, the physical things she wanted to do and wanted to have done.

At the loft, across the hour, she taught him the particulars she had been imagining.

She taught him by doing. She taught him by small quiet directions — a hand moved to a particular place, a breath taken at a particular moment, a pause held at a particular beat. The directions were not words. The directions were the small careful wordless guidance of a wife who had decided, during the eighteen months, that the first night of the homecoming would be the night she stopped waiting for the husband to work out what she wanted and instead showed him directly, with her body in his hands, what she wanted.

Olav received the teaching.

He received it because the husband he had been at the wedding night in March of '83 — the twenty-four-year-old navigator-newly-home-from-the-*Semiramis*-East-India, composed but inexperienced, kind by instinct but cautious by temperament — had been replaced, across the eighteen months of being at sea under Thoresen and handling Hov's small daily devotion and writing the long letter at the Brooklyn wharf, by a husband who was twenty-five and seven months and had learned — not in bed, but in the small careful calibrations of being a first mate for eighteen months — how to follow the small wordless directions a partner gave when the partner knew what the partner wanted.

He followed.

He followed in the ways her hand guided. He followed at the pace her breath set. He followed in the small particulars she had been, across the eighteen months, thinking about.

The particulars were, by any register appropriate to the loft at Finnøy on the second Monday of April of '85, the particulars of a married couple of their generation at the moment at which the marriage was consummating itself in the fuller sense the wedding night in March of '83 had not yet quite been — the sense in which the two of them were, as bodies, learning the specific shape of each other's specific wants.

It was, across the hour, a quiet scene.

It was also — by the small precise register Olav's body was in the hour taking in and the small precise register Olava's body was offering — the most specific thing either of them had done with the other's body in the two years and one month of the marriage.

She showed him what she wanted.

He gave her what she had shown.

He gave it slowly. He gave it attentively. He gave it with the small careful attentiveness he had learned, in the eighteen months, to give to the daily needs of a crew of twenty-four men whose daily work depended on his seeing what they needed without their having to ask — the attentiveness, transposed to the body of his wife at the loft, that was the competence of the first mate whose year Thoresen had written in the notebook as a year of a man who had learned the small calibrations of care.

The giving took, in the body's time, perhaps forty minutes.

It took, in the small clock's time, perhaps twenty.

The difference — between the body's time and the clock's — was, Olav registered at some point in the hour, the small difference that made the specific hour he was giving to Olava in the loft a different hour from any other hour they had had in a bed together.

She, at the end of the giving, lay beside him on the small wider bed his mother had made for them.

She said, in her small composed voice:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"That was what I had been preparing for."

"Yes."

"I had not, before the preparing, known to ask for it. I had to prepare myself to know how to ask. I have, by the eighteen months of preparing, known how to ask tonight."

"You asked clearly."

"I asked, tonight, without using words. Words were not the way I had decided to ask. I had decided, across the eighteen months, that the asking would be with the body. The body was what I had been preparing."

"I received the asking."

"You received it. You gave the answer the asking asked for."

"Yes."

"Olav."

"Yes."

"That is what the marriage, in the body, is going to be from tonight forward. The preparing will not be the preparing of eighteen months every time. It will be the small daily preparing of two people who share a bed. But the shape of tonight — the small wordless asking, the small careful receiving — that shape is the shape I want the marriage to have in the body for the rest of our lives."

"That shape."

"That shape."

"Olava."

"Yes."

"I will hold the shape. I will hold the shape because the shape is a shape my body has learned to hold tonight and the holding is the body-knowing the marriage has, by the eighteen months and by this hour at the loft, given us both."

"Yes."

She put her head on his shoulder.

She did not, for some minutes, say more.

He lay with her head on his shoulder and the lamp turned low and the small April window dark at the gable end and the house below them quiet.

---

Across the next hour they did not sleep.

They talked. The talking was not the talking of the wedding night in '83 when Olava had talked to Olav about feeling cold; this was a different kind of talking. She told him small particulars of the eighteen months — the small things Mrs Hestby had said at the kitchen in November that Olava had not written into the letters because the letters were not the place for them, the small things Mrs Lindøy had said at her kitchen visits that Olava had also kept back. She told him about the Tuesdays when the Brooklyn letters had been expected and the Tuesdays when she had let herself go down to the window earlier than she would ordinarily have. She told him about the small private indent in the ledger and about what she had been writing in it.

He told her, in return, small particulars of the eighteen months he had not been able to put in the letters. He told her about Thoresen's three institutions — the notebook, the Asbjørnsen letter, the Gjesdal arrangement — with the small careful calibration of what could be shared without breaking the confidences the captain had placed on him. He told her about Hov at a higher level of particularity than the Monday supper had permitted. He told her about the long letter's composition across the six nights and about why, at the fifth night at the wharf, he had decided the paragraph on himself had to be the letter's load-bearing paragraph.

She listened.

She did not, at the listening, press.

---

At some point in the telling, Olava said:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"There is a thing I want to say about the letter I wrote you in reply."

"Tell me."

"The reply named you as the captain-to-be. The naming was, by the ledger's arithmetic, the phrase I had decided to use for you in my own head. I have been using it, in my head, since the letter was posted. I have not used it aloud — not even to myself, at the kitchen — because the phrase is the small private phrase the marriage's discipline has assigned to the inside of my head only."

"Yes."

"I am telling you tonight because the loft is the place where the inside of my head and the outside of my mouth can be, for the first time in eighteen months, the same place. The captain-to-be is the phrase. I am telling you. I will not, after tonight, say it aloud again. It goes back to the inside of my head at the kitchen-table's morning tomorrow."

"Olava."

"Yes."

"Thank you."

"You are welcome."

She kissed him on the shoulder — a kiss, not at the mouth — and then she lay her head back where it had been.

---

They slept, at last, at half past midnight.

The lamp had burned low for the hour of the talking. Olav reached across and turned it out. The loft went dark.

In the darkness Olava said, very quietly:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"I am glad."

"I am glad."

"Good."

That was the last word.

They slept.

The house at Finnøy at half past midnight on the second Monday-into-Tuesday of April of '85 held its four occupants — the parents in the back bedroom; the two newly-rejoined married occupants in the loft — in the small quiet a Norwegian island household held on the first night of the son's full return from a two-year voyage.

The voyage was done. The marriage had moved, across the hour at the loft, into the next shape of its second decade. The next decade was ahead. The loft would not, by Mrs Hestby's small unstated plan, be the nightly room — the married-bedroom would; the cottage in four weeks would be the eventual household — but the loft had, on the April Monday of the homecoming, been the room the second decade's first night had been spent in.

The two of them slept.

The loft held them.

In the morning they would come down to the kitchen for the small ordinary breakfast.

The ordinary would, from the breakfast forward, be the marriage's ordinary.

The specific night was done.

The specific night would be remembered for the rest of their lives.
