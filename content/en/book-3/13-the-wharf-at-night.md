The second voyage of the *President Daae* — lift Bergen the third week of March, '84; arrive Brooklyn the fourth week of April; lay at Brooklyn through the summer for an unusually long six-week refit Thoresen had agreed to with the company in exchange for the bark's owner-share of a small additional Brooklyn-to-New-Orleans run; lift Brooklyn for the homeward in the third week of August; arrive Bergen the second week of October — was the voyage that gave the *Daae*'s officers and crew the small steady summer no Atlantic petroleum bark of her year ordinarily got.

The summer had been the kind of summer that built a bark's interior cohesion. The crew had, by the third week of the Brooklyn stay, settled into the small careful Brooklyn-Norwegian routine the consul's lodging-list and Pastor Fredriksen's mission permitted. Hov continued the small daily acts; the vinegar-cruet sat at the steward's table; the line Olav had been calibrating since the previous spring held. Holm and Tellefsen between them ran the deck during the long Brooklyn weeks while the carpenter and the sailmaker did their small refit work below. Thoresen took the small commercial business of the New Orleans run with the same captain's-economy he took everything. By the August lift the *Daae* was, in the small private opinion of Tellefsen at the officer's mess, the cleanest petroleum bark on the Brooklyn waterfront.

The homeward in September had been fair.

The Bergen arrival in October had given Olav a small ten-day window at home in late October — Mrs. Hestby's birthday on the twenty-eighth fell within it, and Mrs. Lindøy and Marit and Eirik had all come over from Lindøy for the Sunday tea, and the cottage's roof had gone on in the second week (Olav's uncle's work, with Olav at the rafters for two days of the week before he had to go back to Bergen) — and had then ended at the gangway for the third lift on the second Tuesday of November.

The third voyage was the winter voyage.

It was, by the small hard arithmetic of a Norwegian deep-water bark in the petroleum trade, the voyage everyone on the bark had been quietly bracing for since the spring. The North Atlantic in late November and early December was a worse North Atlantic than the one in late October. The Brooklyn stay in January would be the bark's coldest. The return in February-March would face the second-worst stretch of the Atlantic year. The voyage would be eighteen weeks at the kindest, twenty-two at the more honest reckoning.

It was the voyage Thoresen had described to Olav at the chart-table at Bergen on the morning of the lift as *the one that pays the wage you have been paid all year for.*

It was, by any measure, the voyage Olav had been the first mate on for eighteen months and had now, in the third Brooklyn week of December '84, settled into the cold-and-grey of.

---

The bark made the Red Hook wharf at the Brooklyn approach on the fifteenth of December, '84.

The crossing had been thirty-six days — fast for a winter westbound — with two storms, one of which had been the worst storm of Olav's career to date, a force-ten North Atlantic gale on the twenty-first day that had taken three days to ride out and that had left the bark's crew with the exhaustion a force-ten left in the body for a fortnight after. Thoresen had handled the storm by the captain's-economy he had been refining for forty years. Olav had handled the deck during the dog-watches Thoresen gave him. Holm had been, as ever, where he needed to be. The bark had held. The crew had held. The petroleum cargo had stayed in its stowage. The two of the four new common seamen of the voyage who had not previously been in a force-ten had not, by the calibration the bark's culture provided, broken; they had, by the storm's end, become part of the crew the bark had been making them into for thirty-five days.

By the third week at the wharf the small daily Brooklyn routine of the discharge was established.

Tellefsen had the deck.

Olav had the small administrative work at the consul's office and at the company agent's at Pearl Street. He took the morning ferry across to Manhattan three times a week for the agent's business; he was at the consul's office at Bergen Avenue twice a week. He had, by the small care he had been practising since the previous voyage, stayed inside the consul's geographic limits without exception. Wexelsen, who had had him in the office now five times across three voyages, addressed him by his name without title, which was — by Wexelsen's small consular economy — the small sign Olav had been registered at the office as a man the consul did not need to deliver the warning-speech to a third time.

The evenings at the bark were the evenings.

He took, by long-established preference, his suppers at the small officer's mess with Tellefsen. He did the small captain's-log work afterward at the chart-table. He went to his cabin by half past nine. He read for an hour from one of the three books he had brought on the third voyage — the small Norwegian translation of an English navigation handbook of '78, the small *Norsk Sjøfartstidende* of November '84 he had brought from Bergen, and a small volume of Bjørnson's stories Olava had given him in October at the home week with the small careful inscription *for the night watches between Bergen and home* in the cover. He blew out the lamp by half past ten.

He had been keeping this rhythm for seventeen days when, on the night of the first day of January, '85, he sat at the small fixed desk in his cabin and began, on a sheet of paper from the leather folder, to write the longest letter to Olava he had ever written.

---

The letter took him six nights.

He wrote it in pieces. He wrote, on the first night, the small ordinary opening — the bark was at the Red Hook wharf, the discharge would finish by the second week of January, the lift for the homeward would be the third week — and then he set the pen down and looked at the paper for some minutes and decided that the letter was not, this time, going to be the small ordinary letter the previous letters of this voyage had been.

The deciding had a reason.

The reason was that it was the first day of January, '85, and that the *President Daae*'s pay-off at Bergen was, by Hofmoe's last letter to the bark, set for the second Wednesday of April. Three months and one week. The voyage that had begun on a quay at Bergen in October of '83 was — as he sat at the small fixed desk on the first night of January '85 — within sight of its end. The end was no longer a date he was holding in his head against an empty horizon. The end had begun to be, instead, a date toward which the bark and the captain and the crew and the first mate were all visibly walking.

The walking-toward had changed something in him.

He had begun to register, across the third voyage and especially across the nineteen days at the Brooklyn wharf since the arrival, the things he was going to take home from the voyage that he had not yet found the language for at home. He had taken home, after the first voyage, the small ordinary news of the bark and the story of the brace incident and the story of the Bowery and Hov's plum-jar. He had taken home, after the second voyage, the small ordinary news of the bark and the story of the New Orleans run and the small report Tellefsen had given him about a Bergen man at the consul's office who had been a cousin of Mrs. Lindøy's and had asked Olav to convey the cousin's regards. The two homecomings had been, in their particulars, small careful well-edited reports of the kind Olav had been writing in his letters for eighteen months.

He had not, in either homecoming, taken home the larger pieces.

The larger pieces were the pieces of what he was becoming as a first mate. The captain's three institutions. The notebook on the bookshelf. Tellefsen's calibration of the captain's shouting. Holm's bosun-judgment. Hov's small daily devotion. The Brooklyn-Norwegian community's small careful operation across the city. The ways in which the trade Olav had signed for at twenty-five was forming him, by twenty-six and a half, into the man who would — by Brun's prediction at the Calcutta customhouse-stairs and Thoresen's notebook entry and Holm's *the kind of first mate this bark has been waiting for, for nine years* — be a captain before forty.

He had not, at home, been able to take the larger pieces in.

He had not been able to because the homecomings had been short, and the small careful first mate's voice had been the wrong voice for the larger pieces, and Olava — by her own discipline — had not pressed.

He had, sitting at the desk on the first night of January '85, decided that the letter he was going to write across the next six nights would take the larger pieces in.

---

He wrote them across the six nights in pieces.

He wrote, on the first night, after the small ordinary opening, a paragraph about the captain. He named Thoresen as the captain who had told him at the first interview that he would be unpleasant and had been exactly that and no more, and he named the calibration-curve Tellefsen had described, and he named the way Thoresen ran his deck. He did not, in the first night's paragraph, name the Bowery rescue or the captain's three institutions; the Bowery had been in his second-week letter of the first voyage in a small careful version, and the institutions were not yet ready to be named. He named only the captain as a captain.

He wrote, on the second night, the paragraph about Tellefsen. He named the second mate as the man who had been the previous first mate for eight years and had stepped down for Olav's training; he named the small daily generosity of Tellefsen's not-undercutting; he named the calibration speeches at the officer's mess; he named the small Tellefsen smile on the morning Olav had taken the bottle from Hov.

He wrote, on the third night, the paragraph about Holm. He named the bosun as the bark's third witness; he named the bosun's *the kind of first mate this bark has been waiting for, for nine years;* he named the way the bosun moved on a deck.

He wrote, on the fourth night, the paragraph about Hov. He named the steward as the man who had nearly taken Olav's head with a Cornish whiskey-bottle on the homeward of the first voyage and who had, by his own decision in the four weeks afterward, become the small daily devotee of the first mate's coat and cabin. He named the vinegar-cruet. He named the line he had been calibrating with Hov for fourteen months.

He wrote, on the fifth night, the paragraph about himself. He named — for the first time in any letter to Olava in his life — the kind of first mate he had become by the eighteen months of these three voyages. He named the weight of the captain's notebook entry. He named the body's having grown into the trade. He named Brun's prediction at the customhouse stairs at Calcutta in '82 and Thoresen's notebook line at Brooklyn in '83 and Holm's *waiting for, for nine years* line in the third week of the homeward of the first voyage. He named the small uncomfortable fact that the three independent assessments had been the same assessment, and that the assessment was that Olav was, by the small careful arithmetic of the trade, going to be a captain.

He named, also, the small fact that the becoming-a-captain was not, in his own head, a thing he had asked for — that the becoming had been done to him by the trade, that he had only walked into the openings the trade had presented him with, that the openings had now closed around him in a way that meant the captaincy was the thing he was going to do with the rest of his career whether or not he had originally asked for it.

He wrote, on the sixth night, the paragraph that closed the letter.

The closing paragraph was the hardest to write. He had been turning it in his head across the sixth day. He had decided, by the supper at the officer's mess that evening, what it had to say. He came back to his cabin. He sat at the desk. He wrote:

> *Olava. The voyage will end at Bergen on the second Wednesday of April. I will be at Lindøy by the second Saturday of April and at Finnøy by the third Sunday. The cottage will be ready, by your last letter, by the second week of May.*
>
> *I have, in this letter, told you the larger pieces of the voyage that I have not — across the six homecomings of the eighteen months since the wedding — been able to bring to the kitchen table or to the bench in the back garden. The reason I have not been able is the reason of a man who has, in the trade he is in, been forming into a kind of man whose forming-up cannot be described in the small careful first mate's voice the daily letters of the voyage have been written in. I have, in this letter, used a different voice. The voice is not, I think, the voice I will use at the kitchen table at Finnøy in April. It is a voice the wharf at Brooklyn at one in the morning of the first week of '85, alone in a cabin, has produced.*
>
> *I am writing the letter because I have understood, in the third week of this Brooklyn, that there are now — by the eighteen months — pieces of me you do not have access to that I had not, when I went away, intended to keep from you. I had not intended to keep them because at the engagement I had promised that my carrying would be visible to you in its outline. The outline, I now register, has grown larger than the outline I had at the engagement. I am writing to give you the new outline.*
>
> *The new outline is this. I am the first mate of the* President Daae *of Bergen. I am, by the small private accounting of three captains and a bosun and a notebook on a bookshelf, going to be a captain before I am forty. The captaincy is the trade I have walked into. The captaincy will, in the years between now and then, take me at intervals to sea on voyages of the kind this voyage has been. The voyages will be the voyages. I will write the letters from each one. I will come home in the spring of '85 and I will live at the cottage with you and I will work at the boats with my father, and I will, in some year between now and 1890, take another berth, and the berth after that, and the berth after that, and the small steady accretion of berths will be the small steady accretion by which I become the captain the trade has decided I am to be.*
>
> *I am telling you all of this because the man who comes home to you in April will be a different man from the man who left in October of '83. I have been changing, across the eighteen months, in directions I did not entirely anticipate when I signed the articles. The directions are not — I want to say — directions you would have asked me not to walk in. They are directions the trade has walked me in. I am, by the choosing of the trade, the kind of husband whose career will have a shape. The shape is the shape of a Norwegian deep-water captain. You will have married, in retrospect, that shape.*
>
> *This is the letter I have not, across the eighteen months, written.*
>
> *I am writing it tonight because the voyage's end is in sight and because the wharf at Brooklyn at one in the morning is the kind of place at which a man writes the letters the kitchen table cannot.*
>
> *I am not asking, in this letter, anything you have not already given.*
>
> *I am only telling you that the pieces are larger than the outline I had agreed to bring home. The new outline is the outline above. The form of the marriage holds. The not-asking holds. The drawer in the boat-shed continues to hold what the drawer has held. The captaincy is the new piece. I am giving it to you in this letter because it is, of the new pieces, the one I am able to give.*
>
> *Yours, Olav.*

He blotted the page.

He looked at the letter.

He folded the six pieces — six sheets of paper, more than he had ever sent in a single letter to Olava in his life — into a single folded packet. He sealed the packet with the red wafer. He addressed it to *Mrs. Olav Hestby, Finnøy, Stavanger, Norway.* He wrote, in the upper left, *Bark President Daae, Brooklyn, January 1885.*

He sat for some minutes with the letter in his hands.

He understood, at the desk, that he had — across the six nights — written the letter the small bound book he had bought at the Asiatic Society stall at Calcutta in October of '81 had been waiting for him to write. The small bound book had closed in March of '83 at the loft at Finnøy on the night before the wedding. The letter to Olava on the wharf at Brooklyn at one in the morning of the first week of January '85 was the small bound book reopened and re-closed in a different form — addressed not to himself but to the woman who had married the man the small bound book had been written by.

He understood, also, that the letter would be — by the terms of the marriage's discipline — the longest single thing he would ever write to Olava in his life.

He was not, he decided at the desk, going to write a longer one.

He put the letter in his coat pocket.

He blew out the lamp.

He slept the small clean sleep of a man who had completed, across six nights at a wharf in a foreign winter, the piece of the marriage's correspondence that the eighteen months had been quietly asking him to complete.

---

In the morning he gave the letter to Wexelsen at the consul's office.

Wexelsen weighed the packet in his hand. He looked at Olav.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"This is heavier than your usual."

"It is."

"Six sheets?"

"Six sheets."

"It will need the additional postage."

"I will pay it."

"You will pay it. The Cunard's Saturday will take it. Bergen by the third week of January. Lindøy by the fourth."

"Good."

Wexelsen stamped the cover. He set the letter in the outbound tray.

He looked at Olav for a beat.

"Hestby."

"Yes, Mr. Wexelsen."

"You have, by my consul's-tally across the eighteen months, not had a single letter or other item I would have flagged for the wife at home. The discipline has held. I am not the kind of consul who praises a first mate at the office. I am the kind of consul who notes it. I have noted it. The note is in the consul's tally. The tally will, when you are presented with a captaincy in some Bergen office in the years to come, be the small private piece the company looks at."

"Thank you, Mr. Wexelsen."

"You are welcome. The Saturday Cunard."

"The Saturday Cunard."

He went out.

---

He walked back to the bark by way of Atlantic Avenue.

The Brooklyn morning of the second week of January was cold — a clean dry cold, the kind of cold a New York harbor gave on the days the wind came down off the snow-fields of the upstate counties — and the small *Lille Bergen* district was at its small Brooklyn winter business. The Norwegian-American men at the small storefronts nodded at him as he passed; he nodded back. He had become, by his five visits across three voyages, a small known face in the district. He registered the registering. He did not, by the small careful self-discipline he had been keeping, let the registering soften the line he had been keeping with the city.

He came to the bark at the wharf by ten.

He went up the gangway.

The morning's discharge was in progress. Tellefsen had the deck. Hov was at the galley. Holm was at the foredeck with the carpenter on a small wooden adjustment.

Olav went forward to take his watch.

The letter was in the outbound tray at the consul's office. The Cunard would lift on the Saturday. By the fourth week of January the letter would be in Olava's hand at the kitchen table at Finnøy.

He had given her, in the six sheets, the new outline.

He had given her what he could.

The voyage's end was, by Hofmoe's calendar, twelve weeks away.

He took his watch.
