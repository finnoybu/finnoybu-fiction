The letter came on a Thursday in the last week of April 1876, on the kind of pale cold afternoon the Finnøy spring produced before it had made up its mind to be spring.

It came up the Finnøy road from Tau on the afternoon post, carried by the postman Bjørgulf Haugen who had been walking the route since his father had been the postman before him, and it was a letter that Bjørgulf had been — Olav understood later — expecting. Bjørgulf had, at the kitchen door, handed the letter to Olav's mother with the small half-bow he gave letters he knew to be more than a letter, and he had said, *Mrs Hestby,* and he had gone back down the road.

The letter was from Stavanger. The address was written in a hand Olav's mother did not know. The seal was a seal Olav's mother did not know. She brought it into the kitchen where Olav was at the table with a slate, and she set it on the table between them the way a woman sets a small heavy thing down that she does not wish to carry any further into the room. She did not open it. She put the small knife for opening letters beside it.

"Your father will open it," she said.

"Yes."

"When he comes in from the boats."

"Yes."

She went to the stove. She stirred the pot. She did not, for the next half hour until Olav's father came in from the boats, look at the letter again.

---

His father came in at five.

He took off his coat. He washed his hands at the basin. He sat at the table across from Olav and looked at the letter on the table between them without picking it up.

"From whom."

"I do not know. It is a Stavanger hand."

"The seal."

"I do not know the seal. A shipping seal, perhaps."

His father picked up the letter. He opened it with the small knife. He read it once. He set it down. He read it twice. He set it down again.

He said to Olav's mother:

"Inger."

"Yes."

"It is the *Sigrid.*"

His mother did not, at this, say anything. She was at the stove. Her back was to the table. Her hand at the pot did not stop stirring. After a moment she said:

"When."

"The middle of August. The master is a man called Gjermund. The brig is a Stavanger registry. The run is the southern run — Lisbon, the Carolinas, a port or two in the Caribbean, home by the following summer. The wage is ten kroner a month and the dietary. The term is the voyage."

"Who made the recommendation."

"Tolvsen."

His mother stirred for another minute. Then she stopped. She turned.

"Tolvsen."

"Yes. He put the word in at Stavanger in February. I did not tell you."

"You did not tell me."

"No."

"Why."

"Because I did not, in February, know whether Tolvsen was serious about it or whether Tolvsen was, in his small particular way, making a gesture he did not mean to follow through on. I did not wish to produce a thing in the kitchen that might not, in the summer, come to anything. It has come to a thing. I am telling you now."

His mother turned back to the pot.

She stirred.

She stirred for another full minute.

Then she said, without turning:

"He is seventeen."

"He is."

"Eighteen in August."

"Eighteen in August."

"The middle of August."

"The middle of August."

"He will be eighteen on the ship."

"He will."

His father did not, in these answerings, look at Olav. His father was looking at his mother's back.

"Jens."

"Yes."

"Ten kroner is not a good wage."

"Ten kroner is the wage a captain gives a young man on a first southern voyage. The wage will be higher on the second voyage. The wage will be higher still on the third. The wage is the wage a man takes because the wage is not what is being offered."

"What is being offered."

"The voyage."

His mother stirred.

"Jens."

"Yes."

"Tolvsen has been your man for thirty years. Tolvsen recommends who Tolvsen recommends. I know Tolvsen. I am not asking about Tolvsen. I am asking — the captain. Gjermund. What kind of a captain."

His father was silent for a long moment.

"I do not know the captain. I have heard — among the men at Tau this winter — two things about him. One thing is that he is a navigator. The other is that he is a man who has his own manner. The men did not elaborate on the manner. I do not, myself, know what the manner is."

"Jens."

"Yes."

"That is not an answer."

"It is the answer I have."

---

The three of them had supper at six.

Olav had sat in his chair since four. He had, since his mother had set the letter down, not said anything. He had not been invited to. The letter had been — in the small arithmetic of a kitchen in a Finnøy farmhouse in the spring of 1876 — a thing for his father and his mother to discuss before it was a thing for Olav. The deciding would, Olav understood at seventeen without having been told, not be his deciding. It would be his mother and father's deciding. He would be told.

This was the way the household had always worked. It was also the way households in the island villages at this latitude had worked for the three generations Olav had heard his grandfather reference.

He sat at the table.

His mother served the pot — a small stew of winter-stored mutton and the last of the late roots, with the small piece of salt cod his father had brought in from the boats that morning — and she put a ladle of it in his father's bowl and a ladle in Olav's and a ladle in her own. She sat.

They said the grace. His mother's grace was the grace she had been saying since Olav had been four. His father did not say a grace; he said *amen* at the end of his mother's. Olav said *amen* at the end too.

They ate.

His father ate in the way he ate — slowly, with his bread broken into small pieces and held at the edge of the bowl. His mother ate without tasting. Olav ate without noticing the eating.

Halfway through the bowl his mother put her spoon down.

She said:

"Jens."

"Yes."

"I will give you the answer. I will give it to you tonight at the supper so that you do not need to ask me for it, and so that Olav, sitting here, does not need to wait on my small private answering before the three of us talk at the table. I am saying yes. I am saying yes to the *Sigrid.* I am not saying yes because I am pleased. I am saying yes because I am not the kind of mother who says no to a voyage because I am not pleased. I have been a sailor's wife long enough. I have been a sailor's daughter. My son will be a sailor. My son's cousins are sailors. The *Sigrid* is a voyage. The voyage will be the voyage it is. He will go. When he comes back we will see what we will see."

She picked up her spoon.

She ate two bites.

She put the spoon down again.

"Jens."

"Yes."

"Tell him what you told Tolvsen."

"What did I tell Tolvsen."

"In February. When you gave Tolvsen the word to put in at Stavanger. Tell him what you said to Tolvsen about him."

His father looked at Olav.

"I said," his father said, "that my son was a boy who was careful at a line, who did not speak when the speaking was not his speaking, who had learned to row a boat at seven and had not, in the ten years since, put a boat on a rock. I said he was a boy who could, at a ship that wanted a first-voyager, be shown the work once and would do the work. I said the wage was not the issue. I said, to Tolvsen, *find him a voyage that will teach him and not kill him.*"

His father looked back at his plate.

"That is what I said to Tolvsen in February."

---

After supper his father went out to the boat-shed. The April evening was clear and cold; the channel held the last pale light at the mouth of the fjord, and the gulls had stopped speaking for the day.

There was, that evening, a repair he had been putting off for a week — a small piece of caulking at the stern of the boat Olav's uncle had been lending them since March — and the repair was the kind of repair his father went to the shed in the evening for because the repair required the bench and the bench required the shed and the shed was where his father went after supper on the evenings a thing had been decided at the table.

Olav went out with him.

He had not been told to. He had not been told not to. He had, in the final minute of the supper as his mother cleared the bowls, understood that his father was going to the shed and that the going was the going a father did after a table-decision had been made, and that the going without the son was a thing the father would do only if the son did not, on his own decision, come out.

Olav came out.

His father did not, at the shed door, comment on Olav's coming. He opened the door. He lit the lamp on the bench — the small brass lamp his father had been using since Olav had been six. He put on the leather apron he wore at the caulking work. He bent to the boat.

Olav held the lamp for him.

The two of them did not speak for perhaps half an hour.

His father caulked the piece he had been meaning to caulk. The caulking took the time it took. It was a two-man work at one stage — the tightening of the small mallet against the seam while the other man held the seam — and at that stage his father held out the mallet to Olav without looking and Olav took it and they did the stage together. After the tightening his father took the mallet back. He finished the seam. He set down the tools. He wiped his hands on a rag.

"Olav."

"Yes."

"Put the lamp down."

Olav put the lamp down on the bench.

"Sit."

Olav sat on the small stool his father kept at the bench for the end of evenings.

His father did not sit. He stood at the bench with his back to Olav, looking at the lamp.

"I am going to tell you a thing," his father said, "that I would not tell you if you were not about to go. I am telling you because you are going, and because a boy going to the *Sigrid* in August should have this with him when he goes."

"Yes, father."

"The sea is not the thing you are being sent for. The sea is the thing the captain uses to get you to the thing you are being sent for. The thing you are being sent for is your own arithmetic. What you are in the fourth month of the voyage is what you are, for the rest of your life, going to have available to you to be. You will be there a long time. You will not be there as long as the calendar says. The calendar will say a year, perhaps more. The fourth month will be the one that decides it. I am telling you this so that you do not, at the fourth month, believe that you are at the middle of a thing. You are not at the middle. You are at the thing."

He stopped. He turned. He looked at Olav.

"That is all. I do not have more. I have had this — in me — for nineteen years. I have been waiting for a son to whom I could give it. You are the son."

"Yes, father."

"Good."

He picked up the mallet again.

"The seam wants another pass."

He did another pass at the seam. Olav held the lamp for him. When the second pass was done his father wiped his hands again and put the tools away and blew out the lamp and went out of the shed.

They did not speak on the walk back to the house.

His mother was at the kitchen table when they came in. She had the letter on the table in front of her. She had been, Olav saw, re-reading it. She looked up when they came in.

"Jens."

"Yes."

"I will write the answer tomorrow."

"Yes."

"Olav."

"Yes, mother."

"Go up to bed. You will need your rest. The *Sigrid* will need it."

He went up to bed.

---

On the Sunday after the letter — a clear cool May morning with the channel full of light — Olav walked to the Hestby church for the service.

He walked with his mother. His father was already at the church; his father had gone early to help the sexton with a stove that had been making trouble. Olav and his mother walked the road from the farm to the church at the pace Olav's mother walked roads — slow, steady, with the small considering pauses she made at the places along the road she had been making them at since she had been a girl.

At the church wall — the low stone wall that ran along the road and that the children of Finnøy had been climbing on for two hundred years — Olav stopped.

His mother did not, at the stopping, urge him on. She said *I will go in,* and she went in.

Olav stood at the wall.

Beyond the wall, in the graveyard, were the stones. There were perhaps two hundred of them, some standing, some leaning, some lying. At the back of the graveyard, against the east wall, were the stones of the Hestby family. Per's stone — the brother who had died in November of 1869, when Olav had been eleven — was the newest of them. Behind Per's stone, in the second row from the wall, was the stone of Olav's grandfather, who had died in '64. Behind the grandfather's stone, in the third row, was the stone of Olav's great-grandfather Gunnar Hestby, who had died in '38 at the age of seventy-seven, having been a captain in the merchant marine of his time on a run between Stavanger and Hamburg and having, across the twelve years of his retirement, made for himself on Finnøy a small reputation as a man who could be trusted with the kind of thing a younger man did not wish to tell his own father about.

Olav had never known Gunnar. Olav had known, all his life, the stone.

He looked at the stone.

He thought, at the wall on the May morning in 1876, that the man under the stone — a man who had gone to sea at fifteen in 1775 and had not come home until he was thirty-two — would, if he had been standing at the wall beside Olav, have said nothing and would have looked at the same sea Olav was looking at and would, perhaps, have put one hand briefly on the back of Olav's neck and would have walked into the church ahead of him.

The man under the stone had not, of course, put a hand on Olav's neck.

The thinking had been Olav's thinking.

The thinking was, Olav understood at seventeen without having been told, the kind of thinking a boy did at a wall on the Sunday before a first southern voyage.

He stood at the wall for another minute.

Then he went into the church.

The minister was at the pulpit. The hymn had begun. His mother was in her pew. His father was at the stove at the back, still working at it. Olav took his place beside his mother. He sang the hymn.

He did not, in the singing, look back at the wall.

He knew the stone was there.

That was, he understood on the May morning, what stones were for.
