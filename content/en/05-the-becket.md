title: "The Becket"

They cleared the Tagus on the next morning's tide and stood out into the Atlantic. The wind was northerly and steady. The captain had decided, evidently, that they were now to take a partial cargo across the ocean — to Wilmington, in the Carolinas — for a return load of pitch-pine and naval stores. This was a longer voyage than the one Olav had signed for. Olav had signed for *Lisbon and back.* The captain had not asked the crew about the change of orders. He did not, the carpenter said, do that.

Olav did not protest. He had decided two nights before that he was not going to make this voyage — that he would, in some port, in some way, leave it — and so the captain's reroute did not alter his plans, only his timeline. Wilmington was as good a place to desert as any. Better, perhaps. He had heard there were boarding houses in Wilmington that did not ask too many questions.

He went forward and worked the brig down the Iberian coast. He did not look at Haakon. He did not look at the captain. He looked at the rope and the deck and the sail, and he kept his hands moving, and he did not let his face do anything his face did not need to do.

---

On the third day out from Lisbon they were caught by a calm.

The wind died at noon and did not come back. The brig sat on a glassy ocean under a brass-colored sky and rocked very slightly on the swell. The crew, at first, was glad of the rest. By the afternoon they were bored. By the evening they were uncomfortable; in a calm a ship's smells come up through her decks, and a brig with five days of unwashed coal-blackened crew and a half-stowed salt cargo is not a ship one wishes to be becalmed on for long.

The captain came up on deck at eight bells of the second dog-watch. His coat was new-pressed; his beard was wet from washing. The smile was on him. Olav, who had not addressed him since the salt-hold, kept his eyes on his rope.

"Mr. Hestby."

"Sir."

"There is no wind. There will be no wind tonight. I should like you to do me a service."

"Sir."

"The royal sheet on the starboard side has chafed itself badly through the block at the yardarm. The block is hanging by a thread. If we are sighted by a pilot at first light and try to set the royal with that block in the state it is in, we will lose the sheet, the corner of the sail, and possibly the yard. I would like a fresh becket seized on the block tonight."

"Yes, sir."

"You will go aloft. The carpenter will give you what you need. Be quick."

The captain smiled and turned away.

Olav went to the rope-locker. The carpenter — without looking up from the bench at which he was sharpening something — handed him a length of small hemp for the seizing, a marlinspike, and a fid. The carpenter did not say anything. He did not look at Olav's face. He kept his eyes on the chisel-edge under his thumb. Olav understood, without understanding why he understood, that the carpenter would have looked at his face if he had been able to look at it without showing what was on his own.

He went to the foot of the foremast. The shrouds were waiting for him, dark against the brass sky.

Haakon was on the deck. He was at the windlass with no work obviously to hand. He looked up when Olav passed him. He looked at Olav's face. He looked at the captain's back across the deck. He looked back at Olav.

He did not say anything.

Olav climbed.

---

He went up the main shrouds because it was easier for the next stage, then the futtocks, then the foretop platform, then the topmast shrouds. The topmast shrouds were thinner than the lowers and they swayed in the calm with the small motion of the swell. He climbed past the topmast crosstrees and up the topgallant rigging, which was thinner still, and then up the royal rigging, which was a single line on each side and which a man climbed by hooking his arm through it and walking himself up against it like a child climbing a frayed rope in a barn.

He reached the royal yard.

The royal yard is the highest yard on a brig — above the topgallant, just below the truck. To work at the starboard yardarm a man must walk himself out along the footrope, balancing his belly across the yard and letting his feet take his weight on the rope below. The footrope is seized to the yard at each end with a *becket* — a small loop of marline lashed through an eyebolt at the inboard end and another at the yardarm. The two beckets together hold the footrope at the height it needs to be. If either gives at sea the rope drops free, and the man on it falls.

Olav worked his way out onto the footrope. The yardarm was perhaps sixty feet of climb above the deck. The brig beneath him rocked very slightly in the swell. He clamped his belly to the yard and felt the footrope take his weight, and he started toward the starboard yardarm.

He had got perhaps half the way out when the becket gave.

It did not give all the way. It gave a half-inch — the distance between a man's life and the deck — and then held. Olav, who had been about to take his next step, felt the rope drop under his foot. His belly stayed on the yard. His arms, by the kind of reflex a body learns from a year on the coast, hooked over the jackstay along the top of the spar. He did not fall.

He hung there for perhaps four seconds, listening to his own breath. The rope had not parted. It had only loosened. He looked, very carefully, along the footrope toward the starboard becket.

The becket had not chafed. It had not worn. It had been cut.

The cut was clean and it was halfway through the marline, and the marline had given the rest of the way under his weight as he had walked out along the rope. The remaining half held. It held because marline is stronger than a man's weight if it is whole, and even halfway-cut it sometimes holds, but a half-cut becket will eventually go, and the captain had counted, evidently, on the *eventually.*

Olav hung at the royal yard of the brig in a calm in the Atlantic and understood, with a clarity that came at him from the back of his head and walked all the way down his spine, that the captain had cut the becket. Or had ordered the becket cut. Or — and this was the worst possibility, because it was the one Olav could not prove was wrong — had merely seen a frayed becket in passing some afternoon and chosen not to mention it, and then chosen to send the new boy aloft to discover it.

He understood, also, that he could not complete the captain's order. He could not move further out toward the yardarm; the second half of the becket would give. The block would have to wait.

He took his weight off the footrope and worked his way back along the yard toward the topmast, hand over hand on the jackstay, his feet finding the parts of the footrope that were closer to the inboard becket where the rope was still sound. He reached the topmast. He gripped the topmast with both arms.

He took the cut end of the becket — the half he had been able to reach when he had freed his foot — and he wrapped it once around his belt and tied it.

Then he started down.

He came down faster than he had gone up. The calm held; nothing pressed him into the rigging or pressed him out of it. At the topmast crosstrees he paused for a moment. He looked down.

Haakon was at the foot of the foremast. He had not moved. He was looking up.

Olav came down to him.

---

He stood on the deck. His legs were not, he discovered, working. He had to lean against the foremast.

The captain was on the quarterdeck. The captain was looking at him.

Olav crossed the deck — he did not remember crossing it — and stopped at the foot of the quarterdeck ladder.

"Sir."

"Mr. Hestby. Is the becket seized?"

"Sir, the becket gave way."

"The becket gave way."

"The starboard becket on the royal-yard footrope, sir. It was cut. Halfway through. It gave under my weight as I walked out toward the yardarm. The remaining half held me. I came back along the jackstay."

He took the cut piece from his belt. He laid it on the deck at the foot of the ladder.

The captain looked at it for some time. The smile had not moved. It would not move; Olav understood this now, that the smile was the captain's face and that the captain's face was the smile, and that whatever passed behind it would not show.

"Boatswain," the captain said, without turning his head.

"Sir."

"Send up a fresh becket on the starboard royal-yardarm. Have it ready for the morning watch."

"Aye, sir."

The captain looked at Olav again.

"Mr. Hestby."

"Sir."

"You have done me a service. A man could have fallen from that yardarm tomorrow, and we would have lost him, and we would have lost a sail in addition." The smile, briefly, did something almost like animation. "You have a good eye."

"Yes, sir."

"You may go below."

Olav did not move. He could not, he found, decide whether to thank the captain or to spit on him. His mouth had not yet chosen. He stood at the foot of the quarterdeck ladder until he understood that standing there was conspicuous, and then he turned and walked aft along the deck and into the forecastle.

---

Haakon was at the foot of the foremast as Olav came down the ladder. He had a tin mug in his hand. He held it out without speaking. Olav took it. It was coffee. There was no rum in it. Olav was, he found, glad of that; he did not want to feel anything just now that he could not feel without help.

"Drink," Haakon said.

Olav drank. The coffee was hot. His hands shook around the mug.

Haakon watched the shake. He did not comment on it. After a moment he reached out and took the mug back, held it himself while Olav got his hands steady, and gave it back. Olav drank the rest.

"How far out," Haakon said.

"Half the way to the yardarm."

"And the becket."

"Cut. Halfway through."

Haakon let his breath out, slowly, through his nose. He looked up at the foremast. The royal yard, from the deck, was a thin black line against a brass sky.

"And it held," Haakon said.

"It held."

"And you came down."

"And I came down."

Haakon nodded. He did not look at Olav.

"You are alive," he said.

"Yes."

"Good."

He took the empty mug. He turned to go aft.

He stopped after one step.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"If a captain ever orders you out on a footrope at night, in a calm, on a brig that has been at sea long enough for a green hand to be the only one he sends — and you cannot refuse — you check both beckets before you put your weight on the rope. From the topmast. With your hand. Before you take your feet off the yard."

"Yes."

"I should have told you that before you went up."

"You did not know he would send me."

"I knew what he is. That was enough."

He looked at Olav, finally. The pale noon-blue eyes were very still.

"I am sorry," he said.

Then he went aft.

Olav stood at the foot of the foremast with the empty space the mug had left in his hand, and he thought:

*That is the second man on this ship who has tried to kill me. Or the first man twice. Or the first man only, and this time he had seen the cut becket and chosen not to see it.*

He thought: *I do not care which it was. I am going to live.*

And then, because the body insists on its small honesties even when the mind has gone elsewhere:

*Haakon said* sorry *to me.*

He had not heard the word from another man's mouth, said to him, since his mother had said it once when he was nine. He thought about this for a moment. Then he put it in the drawer.

The drawer was getting full.

He went below.
