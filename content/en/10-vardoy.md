The doctor at Stavanger was Dr. Due. He was a small man with a tobacco-colored beard. He listened to Olav's chest with a small wooden tube. He listened for a long time. He took the tube away. He put it on the table beside him. He took his spectacles off and polished them slowly with a corner of his coat.

"Mr. Hestby."

"Sir."

"I will not lie to you. I will also not tell you the thing you came here to be told. The thing you came here to be told is that you have it or you do not. I do not, today, know which. Your right lung is doing a thing your left lung is not doing. The thing your right lung is doing is small. It may be small because it is going to become big. It may be small because it is small. I cannot tell you, today, which it is."

"Sir."

"Three months from now I will be able to tell you. Six months from now I will be certain. You are nineteen. Your father is your father. Your brother died of it. These are facts I cannot do anything about. The fact I can do something about is your stomach. You will eat. You will eat butter and you will eat fat meat and you will eat the white of eggs. You will not work below in damp holds. You will rest when you can rest. You will sleep with your window open even in winter. These things are not a cure. There is no cure. These things are the things that, in some men, make the difference."

"Yes."

"Three months."

"Yes."

Olav and his father walked from Dr. Due's house to the Stavanger quay without speaking. They walked past the navigation school. Olav, who had once thought he might enroll in it, did not look at it. They walked past the Bethania chapel. They walked past the fish-market. At the quay they stood for some time without speaking.

"You will come home," his father said.

"No."

His father did not turn his head.

"You will go to sea."

"Yes."

"Why."

"Because at home I will sit by the window with the cough and I will think about Per until I am Per. The cough is a passenger. The thinking is what kills."

His father considered this. Olav could feel him considering it. After a long while his father nodded once, very slowly, the way a man agrees to a thing he wishes he could refuse.

"Three months," his father said. "Then home for the winter."

"Yes."

"You give me your word."

"Yes."

"I will hold you to it, Olav."

"Yes."

His father turned, and they walked back into the town to find a berth.

---

The berth was on a small schoonerbrig called the *Allegro,* trading north with salt to the Russian fishing-camps at Vardøy. The captain of the *Allegro* was a man named Brennevin, which was not, his crew told Olav later, his real name; his real name was something nobody used because he had so thoroughly become his nickname (the word means *liquor*) that the original name had wandered off and stayed lost. Brennevin was forty-five years old, dry-skinned, narrow-eyed, and the kind of captain who did not strike his men because striking cost a half-day of their labor. He believed in time. He believed in it so completely that he had moved the ship's chronometer to his own cabin so that he could determine, at any hour, whether the men on deck were moving with appropriate urgency.

He was not Captain Gjermund. He was a different kind of bad captain. He was the kind of bad captain whose badness was an accountancy of small subtractions rather than a sequence of large violences. Olav, signing the *Allegro*'s articles in a small office above a chandlery, understood this within five minutes of meeting him, and was grateful. A man with a soft cough did not need a Gjermund. He needed a Brennevin. A Brennevin would not kill him; a Brennevin would only require that he work to the chronometer for three months for a wage Brennevin would describe, on Sunday nights, as *generous in the circumstances.*

They sailed on a Tuesday. Olav at the rail watched Stavanger thin out for the second time in his life. He did not, this time, look for the green of Finnøy beyond the green of the town. He had been told the green of Finnøy was waiting for him. He preferred to know it was waiting for him without seeing it.

---

The voyage north took eleven days.

The first three were Norwegian coast — fjord and rock and the long blue line of the mountains — and Olav had something like pleasure in them. He had not seen the Norwegian coast from the sea in two years, and he had forgotten how the light worked on it: the way the sun struck a rock-face above a fjord and made the rock briefly the color of butter. He stood at the rail in the off-watches and ate the herring his mother had wrapped in oiled paper for him. He coughed less. The air, he thought, was perhaps doing him good already.

By the fifth day they were past Trondheim. By the seventh they were past the Lofoten. By the ninth they were inside the Arctic Circle and the night was no longer a night but a kind of grey twilight that did not deepen. By the eleventh they made Vardøy.

Vardøy is the easternmost town in Norway. It sits on a small island in a sound between two larger islands at the top of the country, and the country, by the time you reach it, has stopped being green. The grass is the color of old hemp. The houses are grey. The boats in the harbor are grey. The fish-racks behind the houses are grey, hung with grey-and-yellow stockfish and white-and-grey gulls. The water in the sound is grey. The sky in summer is grey-with-too-much-light, and the sun does not set, and the absence of darkness becomes, after the second day, a kind of weather pressing on the back of the head.

Olav stepped onto the wharf at Vardøy at three o'clock in the morning of an afternoon-colored night. He set his sea-chest down. He looked around him at the grey. He understood, with a clarity he had not had at Stavanger, that he had come to a place that did not believe in the recovery of anything.

---

The work was salt.

The Russian fishermen at Vardøy bought salt to cure their cod, and they bought it day and night because they fished day and night, because there was no night, and because their season was short. The *Allegro* had come north with a hundred and twenty tons of it. The captain — Brennevin, who in Vardøy had managed to acquire a watch fob he wore over his waistcoat in addition to the chronometer in his cabin — sold the salt by the bushel to whichever Russian boat tied up at the *Allegro*'s side, and the bushel was Olav's to fill.

Olav filled bushels. He filled bushels for sixteen hours a day for the first week. The salt was wet and heavy and it cracked the skin at the corners of his hands. The cough, which had quieted on the passage north, came back on the second day at Vardøy and stayed. He worked through it. He had nowhere to go.

He did not sing. He had sung at the tarring in Wilmington because singing was a way of being unbroken in the captain's hearing. He had no breath now for that kind of unbrokenness. The Finnøy songs were in the drawer with the rest.

In the second week he caught a chill on the ship's bottom — Brennevin had decided, since they were moored, that the *Allegro* could be re-coppered, and Olav had been the man with the strength left for the job — and the chill went down into his chest the way a stone goes into a pond. He was, that night, hot to the touch. He did not tell Brennevin. He continued, in the morning, to fill bushels.

There was a doctor in Vardøy. He was a Finnish man of perhaps sixty who had married a Norwegian woman thirty years before and had stayed. Olav saw him on a Sunday afternoon, in a small consulting-room above the post office, with a window that looked out at the unending grey light over the sound.

The doctor listened to Olav's chest with the same kind of small wooden tube Dr. Due had used. He listened for the same long time. He took the tube away. He looked at Olav the way Dr. Due had looked at him.

"You have what?" he said. His Norwegian was good but it had the flat Finnish *r* in it.

"A cough. I am told it is not yet tuberculosis."

"Who told you this."

"A doctor at Stavanger."

"Stavanger." The Finn made a small sound. "Stavanger says these things. Stavanger is fond of the word *not yet.*"

"Is it not yet?"

The Finn did not answer. He looked at Olav for a long time. He looked at Olav's hands and at the place under his jaw and at the sweat at his hairline.

"You are working in the salt," he said.

"Yes."

"Stop."

"I cannot."

"Stop."

"I cannot stop. The captain will not discharge me unless I find a replacement. There is no replacement at Vardøy."

"There is always a replacement at Vardøy. The captain does not want to find one. The captain wants the salt sold and the boy in the water-bushel and the boy can be replaced from the salt-pile when the boy is dead. This is Vardøy."

He took his spectacles off. He polished them. He put them on. The gesture was so exactly Dr. Due's that Olav, in his fever, briefly thought he had been transported back to Stavanger and was being told the same thing a second time.

"I will not tell you what you have," the Finn said. "Stavanger has told you. I do not need to repeat Stavanger. I will tell you instead that you must leave this place. You are not getting better here. You are not staying the same. This is not a place where men get better."

"I have signed for three months."

"You have signed for as long as you can survive. You do the arithmetic."

Olav did the arithmetic.

He paid the Finn, who did not want to be paid, and walked out of the consulting-room into the grey light, and went down to the wharf, and stood on the wharf for an hour, and did not know, for the length of that hour, what he was going to do.

He had a cough. He had no replacement to give Brennevin. He had no money for passage south. He had given his father his word he would come home in three months, and the three months were not yet up, and he would not come home in any case because to come home now without wages was to come home with one more failure — to bring the body Per's body had become, with no money to set against it.

He sat on the chest at the wharf. He coughed into his sleeve. The sleeve came away with a small wet spot on it that he did not, in the grey light, want to look at.

He did not look at it.

He looked at the sound. The sun, which in this place did not move, hung in its same grey position above the same grey water. Two Russian men in rubber boots were tarring a hull at the next wharf. A gull stood on a piling and did nothing.

It was at perhaps the second hour that the steamer came in.

She rounded the point at the east end of the sound and came up the channel under reduced steam, and Olav, sitting on the chest, watched her come because there was nothing else to watch and because she was the first thing in three weeks at Vardøy that was not grey.

She was white.

She was a small steamer of perhaps three hundred tons, her hull painted a clean white with a thin black sheer-line, and she had a single buff-colored funnel and a name in black letters across her bow that, as she came nearer the wharf, Olav could read.

The name was *Diana.*
