---
title: "A Cough"
id: 11
slug: a-cough
---

He came home in late summer, on a coastal smack out of Stavanger that made Finnøy at low water and put him off at the small dock at Tau where his father had once mended nets. He had not, in the year and a quarter since the Wilmington dawn, told his family he was coming, and so there was no one at the dock to meet him. He stood for a long moment with his sea-chest at his feet and his head down. The smack was already underway again, working out into the channel; he could hear the steam-engine shifting through her below-deck.

He had practiced, on the long passage back, what he would say. None of the sentences he had practiced felt like sentences he could say.

He picked up the chest. He started to walk.

He had a cough. He had had it for nine months, and it had become, in that time, less a cough than a small ongoing weather inside him — a low warm presence behind his ribs that asserted itself when he climbed a stair, when he ate, when he laughed (which had become, for him, a thing he tried not to do because of what it cost). He had not seen a doctor for it in Boston or in New York or in Quebec or in Liverpool. He had not seen a doctor for it because to see a doctor under the name Vaageland was to put a body to a paper that had no body, and he had been busy keeping the body and the paper apart.

At Liverpool he had at last allowed himself, for the first time since the boarding house at Wilmington, to be Olav Hestby on a piece of paper. He had signed onto a Norwegian timber-bark for a coastwise run home. The captain had not asked questions; nobody at Liverpool asked questions of a young Norwegian sailor with a clean wool coat and a recommendation in his pocket from Mrs. Hovland. The captain had been kind enough.

But Olav had spent the passage in the rigging with his own cough, and by the time he made Stavanger he could feel the little wet thickening at the bottom of each breath that he had been trying not to know was there. He had said nothing to the captain. He had said nothing to the steward. He had paid his coastwise fare and had walked off the bark at the Stavanger quay with the chest on his shoulder and the coat over his arm, and he had sat for half an hour on a bench by the ferry-house before he could make himself board the Tau smack.

He did not know what he was going to say to his father.

The road from Tau up to the house went past the church.

---

Hestby church was not a large church. It was a white wooden building set into a slight rise above the road, with a small graveyard behind it and a low stone wall running along the road that the children of Finnøy had been climbing on for two hundred years. Olav had been baptized in it. His mother had been confirmed in it. His father had been married in it. His older brother — Per, who had died of the cough when Olav was eleven — was buried behind it.

He stopped at the wall. He set the chest down. He looked at the church.

He had not, he realized, looked at it before. Not the way he was looking at it now. As a child it had been the building one went into on Sundays. As a sailor it had been the small white shape that the brig had passed at four miles of distance on the way out of the roads. As Olav Vaageland it had been a thing he was running away from. As Olav Hestby with a cough at the wall, he was looking at it for the first time as a piece of stone with his name on it.

He stood at the wall for some time. He did not go in. He did not know why he did not go in. He picked up the chest. He went past the church. He went up the road to the house.

---

His father was at the back of the house, at the woodpile, when Olav came into the yard. His father heard the gate before he saw who it was, and turned, and stood with the axe in his hand and looked at his son for what Olav at the time thought was perhaps two seconds and what he understood later was much longer.

"Olav," his father said.

"Father."

His father set the axe in the chopping-block. He came across the yard. He did not embrace Olav. He stood in front of him and put one hand, briefly, on the back of Olav's neck — the same hand-place from the morning at the Stavanger quay, but the hand was older now, and Olav's neck was thinner, and the hand could feel that it was thinner — and then he stepped back.

"Where did you come from."

"Liverpool, last week. I worked a coaster home."

"Under what name."

"Under mine."

His father nodded once. He did not ask the question that had to be asked next. He did not ask it because he had decided, in those long-feeling two seconds at the woodpile, that he was not going to ask it; the asking of it was Olav's to do, in his own time, in his own words, when he was ready.

"Your mother is in the house," he said. "Go in."

Olav went in.

---

His mother was at the table, with a bowl of summer beans in her lap, snapping the ends off them with her thumbnail. She looked up when the door opened and she did not, for the count of two breaths, recognize the man at the door. Then she did. She put the bowl down on the table and she stood up and she crossed the kitchen and she put both her hands on his face, on his cheekbones, and she said his name once, very quietly — *Olav* — and then she said it again, *Olav,* and then she pulled his head down to her shoulder and she did not weep, because they were not, his family, a family that wept. She held him against her shoulder for some time, with her hands tight on the back of his head, until she felt his shoulders shake against her, and then — only then, because she was a mother and she knew the difference — she let him go.

"Sit down."

He sat.

"You are thinner."

"I am tired."

"You are thinner than tired."

He did not answer. She was looking at his face. She was looking at the small dark circles under his eyes, and at the place under his jaw where, when he had been a boy and had had the bread-fevers, the glands had come up. The glands were not up now. But she was looking at the place where they would come up, and she understood — Olav saw her understand — that she was looking for something she had looked for in another son's face once.

"Your father is coming in," she said.

"Yes."

"You will eat with us. We will speak after."

"Yes."

His father came in. His father did not look at Olav's face the way his mother had looked at it. His father looked at Olav's hands and at the way his ribs lifted his shirt when he breathed, and he made his calculation and he kept it to himself.

They ate. The beans were good. Olav had not eaten green vegetables in nine months, and the taste of summer through them was a thing his mouth did not know what to do with. He ate slowly. He ate everything. Then he sat with his hands in his lap and he watched the light move across the kitchen wall and he listened to his father pour coffee and he understood that he was at home and that being at home was a thing he had not allowed himself to want.

---

After supper his father took him out to the woodpile.

There was no work at the woodpile. The work had been done that morning. His father took him out to the woodpile because the woodpile was where the men in this house went when there was a thing to be said that could not be said inside.

His father split a piece of birch in half. Then he split one of the halves in half. Then he set the axe down.

"How long," he said.

"Since Wilmington."

"Wilmington was when."

"July of last year."

His father looked at the woodpile. He did not look at Olav.

"You did not write."

"I could not write. The captain at Wilmington would have written to the consul. Any letter would have been seen. I could not let my name be at home and at sea at once."

"You are sure of this."

"I am sure of this."

His father nodded.

"And the cough."

"It started in the boarding house. The widow gave me a syrup for it. The syrup helped for a month. Then it stopped helping."

"Have you seen a doctor."

"Not under my name."

"Not under any name?"

"Not under any name."

His father split another piece of birch. The wood was dry; it cracked clean.

"You will see Dr. Due in Stavanger on Monday. I will go with you."

"Yes."

"Olav."

"Yes."

His father turned then, and looked at him. His father's face was the face Olav had carried in his head for three years across an ocean — the face that had set him a mark on the back of his neck at the Stavanger quay — and the face was older now, and the eyes were a different thing in it. The eyes were not the eyes of a man releasing an animal. The eyes were the eyes of a man who had once been released and had come back.

"I have not asked you," his father said, "why you came home."

"No."

"I am not going to ask you."

"No."

"But I am going to tell you a thing." His father set the axe down on the chopping-block, very carefully, the way a man sets down a glass he has decided not to drop. "There is no shame in it. There is no shame in the leaving and there is no shame in the coming back. I came back. Tolvsen has likely told you I sailed on the *Aase.* What he has not told you, because he does not know, is that I left her at Newcastle and walked on foot to Hull and worked a herring smack home under a name I had borrowed from a man who had died of the fever in our forecastle the week before. There is no shame in it. There is only the cough sometimes, after. The cough was mine for two years. It went."

Olav looked at his father.

"You have never said this."

"I have not said it because there was not a thing to say it about. There is a thing to say it about now."

"Yes."

"You will get well. I do not say it to comfort you. I say it because I have lived it. The cough is a passenger. It rides for a while. It gets off."

He picked the axe up again. He split another piece of birch. He did not look at Olav.

"Go in," he said. "Your mother has put your old bed in the loft."

Olav went in.

---

He climbed the loft stair. The bed was where it had been when he was sixteen — under the small dormer window that looked north over the channel toward Lindøy. He undressed and lay down. The mattress was the mattress he had slept on for fifteen years; it had a hollow worn into it that fit his hip exactly, even now, even with the weight he had lost. The hollow was a small physical mercy. He put his hand on the wall above the bed where, as a boy of nine, he had carved his initials with his uncle's pocket-knife.

The initials were O.H. The pocket-knife had been dull. The H had three crosspieces because he had not been able to get the cuts to line up.

He looked at the H for some time.

He coughed once, into the pillow.

Then, in his own bed, in his own house, with his father splitting wood downstairs in the failing light, he allowed himself a thing he had not allowed himself in a year. He thought about Haakon. He thought about Haakon's hammock three down from his on a brig that was now somewhere in the Baltic or somewhere off Hamburg or somewhere he could not, from the loft window in Finnøy, picture. He thought about Haakon's thumb on his wrist at the foretop. He thought about Haakon's shoulder against his under the brown wool blanket in the lee of the galley.

He thought about all of it for as long as the body would let him. Then he stopped, because the body had begun to want to cough again and the cough at home was a different cough than the cough at sea, and he could not, in the loft, afford it.

He put it away the way he had been taught.

The drawer, he found, was still where he had left it.

He went to sleep.
