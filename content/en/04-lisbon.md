The brig made Lisbon on the eleventh day. The wind, which had carried them down the Bay of Biscay with a steadiness Oliver had begun to take for granted, gave them a final favor at the mouth of the Tagus and let them in on a quartering breeze that the pilot — a small dark man with a Portuguese hat and English of a kind — said was the best he had seen in a fortnight.

Oliver was at the larboard rail. The city came at him through a haze of heat and dust and a kind of smell he had not encountered before — a great composite smell of olives, rotting fish, citrus, smoke, animal, ocean-mud, baking stone, and underneath all of it a sweetness like wine spilled in a warm room and not cleaned up. The buildings climbed the hills in pale terraces. The roofs were red. The light was the color of a coin held up to a candle.

"Portugal," Haakon said, beside him. He had come to the rail without Oliver noticing.

"Yes."

"You will see worse cities. You will not see better light."

Oliver did not answer. He was busy looking. He was also busy not looking at Haakon's hand on the rail, which was perhaps four inches from his own, and which, since the night of the squall four days before, he had been training himself not to look at.

The drawer.

He looked at the city.

---

The discharge of coal took five days.

This is one sentence to write and a thousand sentences to live. The coal came up out of the holds in canvas slings, hauled by hand, swung over the side, lowered to the lighters, dumped, and the slings hauled back up. The coal was wet. The coal was sharp. The coal got in the seams of the planking, the seams of the gunwales, the seams of a man's clothes, the seams of his teeth. By the second day the brig and her crew were the same color: a uniform soft black that did not so much darken a thing as redefine its surface.

Captain Gjermund did not work the coal. Captain Gjermund went ashore in a shore-boat at eight bells of the morning watch and was, the carpenter said with a face that admitted nothing, *negotiating with the agent.* He was negotiating with the agent until midnight. He was negotiating with the agent again the next day, and the day after that. The negotiations, evidently, required brandy in considerable quantity. They also required that nothing else aboard the brig be brought to him for decision.

Solvaag ran the ship. Solvaag was a poor substitute for a captain and an excellent substitute for an angry dog. The crew worked. They worked because Solvaag would beat them with a knotted rope-end if they did not, and the rope-end was on the rail by him and he had used it twice already, on the cabin boy and on Mathis, and so the men kept their hands moving and their mouths shut and they let the coal come up and the coal go over and the coal go down to the lighter, and at sundown the steward brought up tin mugs of coffee and biscuit, and they ate standing up with black hands, and then they slept the sleep of men whose bodies do not so much rest as stop.

Oliver slept the same.

On the fifth night the captain sent down for Oliver and Nils.

---

Nils was a deckhand from Hogganvik. He was twenty, slow of speech, and had taken half his life to learn to read; his stammer came on him hard when he was startled, and Solvaag liked to startle him. Oliver had spoken to him perhaps three times. Nils had on those occasions seemed to like him; it was hard to know with Nils.

The order came down through the boatswain. *The captain wants the new boy and Hogganvik in the shore-boat at ten o'clock to bring him aboard.*

Oliver took this as a duty and dressed for it. Nils took this as a duty and looked frightened. They went down into the boat at ten and rowed the half-mile to the customhouse stairs, where the captain had said he would meet them. The captain was not at the stairs. The captain was not at the stairs at half past ten, or eleven, or half past eleven. The night turned. The fog came in off the river. They sat in the boat with the painter held by Nils against the iron ring at the stairhead, and they did not speak.

At twelve o'clock Oliver said, "We should go back."

Nils shook his head.

"He told us ten."

"He is not coming."

"If we go back he will say we deserted."

"He is not coming, Nils."

Nils looked at him. The fog had wet his hair flat against his head. He looked very young suddenly, and not slow at all.

"I know," he said. "But we wait."

They waited.

At one o'clock the captain came down the customhouse stairs. He was supported on each side by a man whose face Oliver did not see clearly in the lamplight. They were both Portuguese. They poured him into the boat with the practiced courtesy of men who had done this for him before. The Portuguese laughed. The captain, settling onto the stern thwart, did not laugh. He looked at Oliver and Nils with the dead-still smile that Oliver had first seen at noon in the mess, and he said, in Norwegian, very quietly:

"Take me home."

Oliver took an oar. Nils took an oar. They rowed.

---

At the brig the captain hoisted himself onto the deck without help and stood swaying for a moment. Then he turned and looked down at Nils, who was making the shore-boat fast.

"Hogganvik."

"Sir."

"You let me sit at those stairs for three hours."

The brig was quiet. The forecastle was asleep. Solvaag was below, drunk too, by all evidence. The watch was a single man at the gangway who was now finding things to look at on the far side of the deck.

"Sir," Nils said. "We were at the stairs at ten, sir. As you said."

"You let me sit at those stairs for three hours."

"Sir, we —"

The captain lifted the rope-end from the rail.

Oliver, on the deck behind the captain, did not move. He had a moment to move in. He had perhaps two seconds. The captain raised the rope. Nils made a sound that was not a word. The captain brought the rope down.

The first blow took Nils across the shoulder. The second took him across the side of the head. Nils went down to one knee with his hand up, palm open, the way a child raises a hand to ask for a thing it cannot name. The captain hit him a third time. The third blow, which was full-armed and which Oliver heard clearly above the small water-noises of the harbor, took Nils across the temple. Nils went the rest of the way down. He did not put his hand out to catch himself. His head met the planking with the kind of sound a melon makes when set on a stone.

The captain stood breathing for a long second. Then he stepped over Nils and went to his cabin and shut the door.

Oliver, on the deck, did not move.

---

He moved when the deck-watch moved. The watch came across slowly, knelt by Nils, and turned him onto his back. Nils was breathing. There was blood on the planking from his ear and a great deal of blood from the broken skin at his temple, and his eyes were open and not seeing, but he was breathing.

"Get the steward," the watch said.

Oliver got the steward.

Tolvsen came up in his nightshirt with a rag and a pail of water. He looked at Nils. He looked at the cabin door. He did not look at Oliver. He cleaned the blood off Nils's face. He felt with his thick fingers along the side of Nils's head. He sat back on his heels.

"He will live," he said. "Take him below. Put him in my bunk; mine is wider than his."

Oliver and the watch carried Nils below. They put him in the steward's bunk. Oliver wiped the blood off Nils's forehead with the edge of a blanket. Nils, halfway through this, opened his eyes and looked at Oliver, and said, quite clearly:

"I knew."

"Knew what."

"That he would do it."

Then he closed his eyes, and his breathing settled into a kind of deeper rhythm that made Oliver, for a moment, think he had stopped breathing. He had not. He had only gone into the place that men go after a beating like that, where the body takes itself out of the conversation.

Oliver sat with him for some time. He had been told to do nothing else. He did nothing else.

He thought, with a clarity he had not had before:

*If I had moved when I had the moment to move, the captain would have hit me too. Maybe instead. Maybe also. I do not know which, and I will never know, because I did not move.*

He thought: *I have just learned what I would do.*

He sat with Nils for the rest of the night.

---

The salt loading began two days later. They had taken on a partial cargo of salt from the Setubal lighters at one of the wharves up the Tagus, for a coastwise run to ports the captain had not yet named, and the salt was being struck down through the main hatch into the lower hold. Oliver was on the deck with the slings. Captain Gjermund — sober, freshly shaved, smiling — was at the hatch coaming, supervising.

The captain had decided that the salt could be stowed faster if the men did not bother to trim it level in the lower hold, but rather poured it in heaps and let the next sling come down on top. This is not how salt is stowed. This is how a ship is made dangerously cranky in a seaway. Oliver, who had spent six months on a coastal smack with a master who had taught him salt, looked at the captain's smile and said, before he had decided to:

"Sir. The salt should be trimmed."

The captain looked at him.

The smile did not change.

"Mr. Hestby," the captain said. "You are eighteen years old. You have been at sea on this brig for two weeks. You are not yet in a position to instruct me on the stowage of salt."

"No, sir."

"Get back to the slings."

Oliver got back to the slings. He worked the slings for an hour. The salt continued to come in unstowed. The brig, even at the dock, took on a small list to starboard that was not the list she had had at sunrise.

At the end of the hour the captain came down into the hold, picked up a long-handled salt-shovel from where it lay against the bulkhead, and called Oliver down with him.

"Now," the captain said. "I will show you how I want the salt stowed. Step here."

Oliver stepped where the captain pointed. The captain stood behind him with the shovel in his hand. The hold was lit by a single lamp hung from the deck-beam above. The captain's face, in the lamplight, was the face Oliver had seen at the customhouse stairs holding the rope-end above Nils. The smile had not moved.

"Bend down," the captain said. "Look at the way the salt has fallen here. You see how it has piled?"

Oliver bent down.

He bent down because he did not know what else to do, and because he had been ordered to. He bent down knowing — he would later think on this for years, at watches when there was nothing else to think about — knowing perfectly well, as he bent, that the captain was going to hit him on the back of the head with the shovel.

He did not flatten himself out. He did not pretend he had not understood the order. He bent.

But he tilted his head, in the act of bending, just enough that when the shovel came down — and it came down, swinging up from the floor of the hold, with the captain's whole body behind it — the iron blade did not strike his head. It struck the iron post that held up the deck-beam above the lamp. The shovel rang. The lamp swung. A piece of the shovel-blade broke off and fell into the salt.

The captain stared at the broken shovel.

Oliver stared at the captain.

Neither of them moved for what felt like a long time and was perhaps three seconds.

Then the captain put the broken shovel against the bulkhead, very carefully, as a man might put down a glass he was afraid to drop, and he climbed up out of the hold without looking back.

Oliver stayed in the hold with the broken shovel and the salt and the swinging lamp.

When he was sure the captain was gone he sat down on the salt and put his head on his knees, and he did not weep, because weeping was not the thing that came; what came was a kind of cold trembling that started in his thighs and worked its way up through his ribs into his teeth, and stayed there for a quarter of an hour before it let him stand up.

When he stood up he understood three things, in this order:

One — that he was not going to die on this voyage by his own hand or by accident.

Two — that he was going to die on this voyage if he did not desert it.

Three — that he was going to desert it, somehow, somewhere, soon. He did not know how. He did not know where. He knew only that he would.

He went up the ladder. The hold was below him. The salt was below him. The captain was on the quarterdeck, smiling at the carpenter.

Oliver did not look at the captain. He went forward and he picked up the slings and he went back to work.

That night, in his hammock, he turned on his side toward the wall of the forecastle and he put his hand on the canvas and he thought, very clearly:

*I am going to live.*

He had not had a use for those words before that night. He had a use for them now.

He thought it again. *I am going to live.* It was not an oath. It was a plan.
