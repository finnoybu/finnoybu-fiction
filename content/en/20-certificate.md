He was given the certificate in a ceremony at the school on the eighth of June.

The ceremony was in the long hall, with the hundred and twenty students in three rows, and the four instructors at a table at the front, and the school dean — a man named Gabrielson, who had been the dean since before any of the students had been born — at the lectern with a pile of certificates beside him. Each certificate was a single sheet of heavy cream-colored paper with the school's seal in red wax at the bottom and the candidate's name in black ink at the top. The certificates were given out in alphabetical order. Olav was the third. He walked the length of the hall with the eyes of a hundred and seventeen other young men on his back, accepted the certificate from Gabrielson, accepted Gabrielson's small dry handshake, and walked back to his place. He did not, in the walking, allow his face to register anything.

When the ceremony was over the students went outside into the school courtyard with their certificates in their hands. The afternoon was warm. Some of the students were already drinking, from a small cask of beer one of the older students had brought in for the occasion. Olav did not drink. He stood at the edge of the courtyard for some time with the certificate in his hand, looking at his name on the heavy paper.

*Olav Hestby, of Finnøy, had on the eighth day of June 1866 been examined and found qualified to serve as a navigator on a vessel of any tonnage in the merchant marine of His Majesty the King of Sweden and Norway.*

He read the sentence twice.

He put the certificate into the leather folder Mrs. Kindingstad's husband had given him as a leaving gift, the previous evening, with the small dry observation that *a man's papers are a man's papers and they should be carried like papers.* He took the folder back to Mrs. Kindingstad's. He laid it on the desk. He sat at the desk for a while and looked at it.

The certificate was a paper. The paper was qualified. The qualified paper was for a vessel he did not yet have. He had, in the room at Mrs. Kindingstad's, no berth, no offer, no captain interested in him for any voyage, and the certificate would not, by being on his desk, produce one. The certificate was a permission, not a hire.

He left for Finnøy the next morning.

---

His father met him at the gate.

His father did not, this time, ask to see the certificate. He had been told, in Olav's last letter, that the certificate was due on the eighth and that Olav would come home on the ninth. He had been at the gate, presumably, every afternoon for some days. He stepped aside, let Olav into the yard, and said, with the small evenness of a man who has been preparing the sentence for two months:

"Show me later."

"Yes."

His mother, in the kitchen, did the same. She did not, when he came in, ask after the paper. She gave him bread and butter and coffee. She asked him whether he had eaten on the smack. She told him Aagot was at the back garden. She did not look at the leather folder he had set on the kitchen chair beside him.

He understood, sitting at the kitchen table, that his family had, while he was away, agreed amongst themselves not to make the certificate the event of his return. The certificate was a piece of paper. The return was the event. The certificate could be looked at, by his father, that evening, when the looking would be a small private ceremony of two men at the woodpile rather than a public one in the kitchen.

That evening at the woodpile his father split nothing. He set the axe in the chopping-block as he had done in May of the year before. He held out his hand. Olav put the certificate in it.

His father read it.

His father read it twice.

His father folded it carefully, in three, the way a man folds a piece of paper he means to preserve, and handed it back to Olav.

"Halvar Sars was right about you."

"I do not know yet whether he was right."

"He was right. The school does not give the paper to the wrong man. The paper says you are a navigator. You are a navigator."

"I am a navigator without a berth."

"That is the next problem. It is not today's problem."

"No."

"What is today's problem."

Olav thought.

"That the paper is in my hand and there is no place for it to go."

"Then put it on the shelf above the kitchen window. Beside the school letter. The shelf has been waiting for it for ten months. The shelf will hold it until the next problem comes."

Olav put the certificate on the shelf above the kitchen window the next morning. The shelf was, after he had put it there, a different shelf. There were now two papers on it. The papers were the inventory of his next ten years.

---

He went to Lindøy for the day, the following Saturday.

He took the small boat. He did not, on the way, plan what he would say. Olava met him at the jetty. The captain was at the boat-shed; the mother was in the kitchen; Marit was with the schoolmaster's son, walking the path to the high pasture. Olava and Olav had, by the small unspoken stage management of the household, an hour together at the back garden bench.

She asked to see the certificate. He showed her.

She read it twice.

She did not, when she had read it, hand it back immediately. She held it for a moment. She turned it so the light caught the red wax of the seal. She studied the seal. Then she handed it back.

"Olav Hestby of Finnøy."

"Yes."

"It is a good paper."

"Yes."

"You are not yet a navigator."

"No."

"You will be when you have a berth."

"Yes."

"You will write to me when you have one."

"Yes."

"I will write to you in the meantime as I have been writing."

"Yes."

She looked at him. The grey-channel eyes. She did not, today, lift her chin. She did not, today, perform any of the small physical things she had performed on Christmas morning in the back room above her mother's kitchen. She had, evidently, decided that today's encounter was a different kind of encounter. Today's encounter was the day his certificate had become an object that could be held in a Lindøy garden by a girl who had decided to marry him. The encounter was the certificate, not the marriage. The marriage had already been arranged.

He stayed at Lindøy for the rest of the day. They had supper with her family. The captain, at the table, asked him three questions about the exam. The mother served him a piece of brown bread that was the same bread she had served him in August. Marit, returned from her walk with the schoolmaster's son, did not speak of the schoolmaster's son but had on her face the look of a girl who had spent the afternoon at a thing she had been waiting to do.

Olav sailed home in the long June evening light. He thought, on the boat, about the certificate on the shelf above the kitchen window, and about Olava's hand on the paper at the back garden bench, and about the next problem.

The next problem was a berth.

In late June a postcard came from Bergen. It was from Hamre. It said: *I have found Sars. He keeps eggs. He sends his regards.* Olav put the postcard in the leather folder beside the certificate.
