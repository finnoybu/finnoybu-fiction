He was given the certificate in a ceremony at the school on the eighth of June. The ceremony was the kind of ceremony an institution holds when the institution does not know what else to do with the day on which the year ends. The students stood in three rows. The dean read out the names. Each man walked the length of the hall, accepted a single sheet of heavy paper with a red wax seal, accepted a small dry handshake, and walked back to his place. Olav was the third in the alphabet. He did not, in the walking, allow his face to register anything.

That afternoon at Mrs. Kindingstad's he sat at the desk with the certificate in front of him.

*Olav Hestby, of Finnøy, had on the eighth day of June 1880 been examined and found qualified to serve as a navigator on a vessel of any tonnage in the merchant marine of His Majesty the King of Sweden and Norway.*

He read the sentence twice. He put the certificate in the leather folder Mrs. Kindingstad's husband had given him as a leaving gift, with the small dry observation that *a man's papers are a man's papers and they should be carried like papers.* The folder was empty before. The folder was no longer empty. The qualified paper was for a vessel he did not yet have.

He left for Finnøy the next morning.

---

His father met him at the gate. *Show me later,* he said. His mother did not, in the kitchen, ask after the paper. She gave him bread and butter and coffee. The certificate sat in its folder on the kitchen chair beside him. Nobody looked at it. Olav understood, sitting at the table, that his family had agreed amongst themselves not to make the certificate the event of his return.

That evening at the woodpile his father split nothing. He set the axe in the chopping-block. He held out his hand. Olav put the certificate in it.

His father read it twice. He folded it in three. He handed it back.

"Halvar Sars was right about you."

"I do not know yet whether he was right."

"He was right. The school does not give the paper to the wrong man."

"I am a navigator without a berth."

"Then put the certificate on the shelf above the kitchen window beside the school letter. The shelf has been waiting for it for ten months."

Olav put the certificate on the shelf the next morning. The shelf, after he had put it there, had two papers on it.

---

He sailed to Lindøy the following Saturday in the small boat his father had lent him.

Olava met him at the jetty. They walked up to the house. The captain was at the boat-shed; the mother was in the kitchen; Marit was on the path to the high pasture with the schoolmaster's son. Olava and Olav had, by the small unspoken stage management of the household, an hour together at the back garden bench.

She asked to see the certificate. He showed her. She read it twice. She turned it to the light to look at the seal. She handed it back.

She did not, in the handing back, look at the certificate. She looked at him.

"Your letters have been shorter, this last month."

"Have they."

"Yes."

"I have not noticed."

"You have. Your letter of the second week of February was three-quarters of a page. Your letter of the fourth week of February was a quarter of a page. You wrote me on Saturday instead of Wednesday in the second week. I have noticed. I am not, today, asking you why. I am telling you that I have noticed, and that I have decided not to ask, and that I am telling you the deciding so that you know I have decided."

Olav looked at her. The grey-channel eyes. He had not, in the three weeks since he had landed at Stavanger or the four weeks before that of the cough, expected this conversation. He had, perhaps, expected the certificate to perform the work of distraction.

"The cough came back in February. It is gone now. I did not write about it because I was not sure, in February, that it was the cough. I am sure now."

She was quiet for a moment.

"Thank you for telling me."

"You did not ask."

"No. I did not."

"I told you anyway."

"You did. I am noting it."

She did not, in the noting, smile. She did not, in the noting, indicate whether she was pleased that he had told her. She was, with this small admission of his, doing a piece of accounting that was not, today, going to be performed visibly.

He understood, sitting beside her on the bench in the long June light, that he had — by telling her about the cough without her asking — done a small thing that was being entered into a ledger she kept somewhere in her head, and that the ledger was a ledger he had not, until this afternoon, known existed. The ledger was the ledger of how much of him he had been willing to give to her without being asked. The certificate, he understood now, was not the chapter's subject. The ledger was. The ledger had a first entry now. It had had no entries until today.

She picked up the certificate again. She handed it back to him.

"Put this on your father's shelf. We will, when you have a berth, put it in a frame. You will be a navigator in a frame in some kitchen we have not yet found."

"Yes."

"You will write to me about the cough the next time you do not know it is the cough. You will write to me whether or not you are sure. The not-being-sure is also a thing I will note. You will let me decide what I will do with the noting."

"Yes."

"Now we will go in to my mother."

They went in to her mother.

---

Olav sailed home in the long June evening light. He thought, on the boat, about the certificate on the shelf above the kitchen window, and about Olava's ledger, and about the next problem.

The next problem was a berth.

In late June a postcard came from Bergen. It was from Hamre. It said: *I have found Sars. He keeps eggs. He sends his regards.* Olav put the postcard in the leather folder beside the certificate.
