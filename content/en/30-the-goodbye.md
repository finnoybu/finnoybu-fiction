He went to Lindøy for the last time on the second Saturday in June.

The weather was the kind of light June weather that made the channel look temporary — as though the world had not yet decided whether the season was going to be the season it usually was, and the islands were holding their breath to find out. He took the small boat his father had been letting him use since he had come home from school. He sailed it across in two hours and ten minutes. He had timed the crossings through the autumn and the winter and the spring; this was the eleventh and the last.

Olava met him at the jetty. She was in the same wool coat she had worn at Christmas, despite the warmth; she had explained, the previous month, that the coat was the coat in which she felt most ready for things she did not want to be ready for. She stood at the boards with her hands at her sides. She did not, when he stepped ashore, cross the small distance between them. She let him cross it.

He crossed it.

She put both her hands on his face, on the cheekbones, the way she had at the jetty after the engagement. She held his face. She did not, this time, count the breaths. Olav understood, in the holding, that the count had been a Christmas-month gesture and that this was a different gesture, and that the difference was that today the holding had no end-time set on it. It went on. It went on long enough that his father's small boat, tied at the bollard behind him, swung once on its painter at the wash of a passing smack, and Olava did not, in the swinging, take her hands away.

She let go. She did not look at him as she did. She turned and walked up the path toward the house. He took his sea-chest off the boat and followed her.

---

That evening at the Lindøy supper table the captain spoke of nothing of consequence. He spoke of his brother in Drammen and of the railway share, which had at last paid a small dividend in May. The dividend was, the captain said, the size of a piece of brown cheese. He had, accordingly, spent it on a piece of brown cheese, on the principle that a man should not let his investments wander far from their purpose. Olava's mother said, without looking up from her plate, that he had spent it on the cheese because she had told him to spend it on the cheese, and that the principle he had cited was a principle she had handed to him at the moment of the spending. The captain did not contest this. He looked, for a moment, at his living hand and his wooden hand on the table beside it, and then he passed the bread.

Marit had walked over from the schoolmaster's house and was eating with them. She was now sixteen and engaged, in her own quieter way, to the schoolmaster's son — a thing that had happened over the spring without much announcement, in the manner of most things at Lindøy. She looked at Olav across the table. She did not ask after the voyage. She passed the bread when it was wanted.

Olava ate beside her father. Olava did not, at supper, look at Olav at all. The not-looking was, by then, a thing they both understood to be the form a woman of her tradition adopted on the night before the man she loved went to sea for eighteen months. The form was the form. They were both, by the second course, doing the work it required of them.

After supper Marit washed the dishes and her mother dried them. The captain went to the parlor with his carving. Olav and Olava had, by the small unspoken stage management that had governed every visit since August, an hour together at the back garden bench.

They sat at the bench in the long June evening light. The bench was the bench they had sat at the Saturday after his certificate, when she had told him about the ledger. The light was the same light. The bench remembered the previous occasion. They both, without speaking of it, allowed it to remember.

"You will write," she said.

"Yes."

"Once a month. From every port you make. The letters will be late. I have decided to count the lateness as part of the letters."

"Yes."

"You will not, in any port, do anything you would have to write to me about that you would rather not write to me about. I am trusting that you will understand the form of this without my naming the contents of it."

"Yes."

"You will come home in November of next year."

"I will try."

"You will come home in November of next year, Olav."

"I will come home in November of next year."

"Good."

She was quiet for a moment. Then she said:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"Tell me one thing about the *Sigrid* you have not told me."

He did not, for a moment, answer. The *Sigrid* was not the territory of the conditions. The *Sigrid* was the country she had agreed at Christmas not to ask him about. He understood that she was not asking him about that country now. She was asking him for one piece of the smaller country that surrounded it — the country of weather and rope and the small sounds of a ship at sea. She was asking him, before the eighteen months, for an instalment of the kind of small thing a husband tells his wife when she asks.

He thought.

"There was a galley boy," he said. "A boy of fifteen named Mathis. He had a voice clean as a bell. The opsang at the cast-off in Stavanger was his — he started it and the rest of the crew picked it up. I have not, since the morning we cast off, heard a voice like that. I do not know what became of him. He would, by now, be twenty-four."

She did not, for a long moment, say anything. Then she put her hand, briefly, on his forearm where it rested on the bench beside her. She did not, in putting her hand there, look at him. She looked at the channel.

"Thank you, Olav."

"Yes."

She took her hand away.

They sat without speaking after that for some time. The light fell. The dog came down from the door of the house and lay at Olava's feet and looked at Olav with the kind of look an old dog gives a man it has decided not to outlive. Olav did not, when Olava reached down and put her hand on the back of the dog's head, look at her hand or her face. He looked at the channel. The channel was the same channel.

When the lamp went on in the kitchen behind them Olava stood up. She did not, in standing, look at him. She walked back to the house. He sat on the bench for another quarter-hour. Then he too got up and went in.

---

The next morning was the kind of morning the channel sometimes produced in June when it could not decide between fog and sun and arrived at both at once. The captain rowed Olav to the small steamer that ran on Mondays from the Lindøy roads to Stavanger. Olava and her mother and Marit walked down to the jetty to see them off.

At the jetty Olava did not put her hands on his face this time. She had used the gesture twice — at midsummer two years before at Landa, then yesterday on the boards — and had decided, evidently, that to use it a third time on the morning of departure would be to make it routine, and she did not want to make it routine. She put her hand instead on the cuff of his coat at the wrist — briefly, the way a woman straightens a man's collar — and said:

"Go, Olav."

"Yes."

"Be at sea by tonight."

"Yes."

"Come home."

"Yes."

She let go. The captain, who had been standing at the boat with the oars across his knees, did not look at the exchange. The captain's wife looked at it, briefly, and looked away. Marit looked at it for a longer beat and made of her face the small open expression of a girl who has just witnessed a thing she will, in two years, be witnessing on her own jetty in her own dress.

Olav stepped down into the boat. The captain pushed off. Olav did not, as the boat made way out into the roads, look back at the jetty. He had decided, the previous day, that not-looking-back was the parting-form he would use; the looking would be a thing he would do when he had cleared the channel and could no longer see the jetty.

He cleared the channel an hour later. He did not, by then, look back. The looking, he understood, was a piece of property he had taken from himself by signing the *Semiramis* articles; he had given the looking to the eighteen months. The looking would be returned to him in November of next year if he was, at that month, still alive to receive it.

The Stavanger smack made the harbor at noon. Olav stepped onto the Stavanger quay with his sea-chest at his feet. His mother and father were there, as they had said they would be. They had come on the morning's smack from Tau. They were standing at the place his mother had stood the day he had left for school, and the place his father had stood to receive him after the *Diana,* and they were doing what they had done at both of those previous standings — which was nothing, because at the moment of a man's departure a Norwegian family of their kind did nothing, because the doing had all been done in the months and years before, at the woodpile and in the garden and at the kitchen table.

His father said *Olav.*

His mother put her hand on the back of his neck.

He hoisted the sea-chest onto his shoulder. He turned to walk down the quay toward the third wharf, where the *Semiramis* lay.

He took four steps before his mother's hand closed on his arm.

"Olav."

He turned.

Olava was at the head of the quay. She was in the wool coat. Marit was a step behind her. They had come on a fisherman's boat from Mosterøy that had crossed an hour after the morning steamer — Olava had decided, after the Lindøy jetty, that she had not yet said the thing she had meant to say.

She crossed the quay to him. She did not speak as she crossed. The half-dozen dockworkers at the third wharf paused at their work. His father set down the sea-chest he had picked up and looked aside at the harbor. His mother let go of his arm.

Olava stopped in front of him. She put both her hands on his face on the cheekbones, the third time. Then she kissed him.

The kiss was brief. It was on the mouth. It was the first kiss of their engagement, and it was also the first kiss of either of their lives. She did not pull back fast. She did not pull back slow. She pulled back on the count, perhaps, of three.

She put her forehead against his for the space of one breath. Then she stepped back. She did not speak. He did not speak. She walked back to Marit at the head of the quay.

Olav picked up the sea-chest. He walked down to the third wharf, set it at the foot of the gangway, and looked back.

His mother and father and Olava and Marit stood in a line at the head of the quay. Olava had not moved since she had stepped back from him. The wool coat in the noon light was the same wool coat it had been in the November dusk when the engagement paper had come into her father's pocket.

He raised his hand once. She raised hers.

He went up the gangway.
