The brig made Lisbon on the eleventh day. The wind, which had carried them down the Bay of Biscay with a steadiness Olav had begun to take for granted, gave them a final favor at the mouth of the Tagus and let them in on a quartering breeze that the pilot — a small dark man with a Portuguese hat and English of a kind — said was the best he had seen in a fortnight.

Olav was at the larboard rail. The city came at him through a haze of heat and dust and a kind of smell he had not encountered before — a great composite smell, olives and citrus and baking stone and rotting fish, and underneath all of it a sweetness like wine spilled in a warm room and not cleaned up. The buildings climbed the hills in pale terraces. The roofs were red. The light was the color of a coin held up to a candle.

"Portugal," Haakon said, beside him. He had come to the rail without Olav noticing.

"Yes."

"You will see worse cities. You will not see better light."

Olav did not answer. He was busy looking. He was also busy not looking at Haakon's hand on the rail, which was perhaps four inches from his own, and which, since the night of the squall four days before, he had been training himself not to look at.

The drawer.

He looked at the city.

---

The discharge of coal took five days.

The coal came up out of the holds in canvas slings, hauled by hand, swung over the side, lowered to the lighters, dumped, and the slings hauled back up. The coal was wet. The coal was sharp. The coal got in the seams of the planking, the seams of the gunwales, the seams of a man's clothes, the seams of his teeth. By the second day the brig and her crew were the same color: a uniform soft black that did not so much darken a thing as redefine its surface.

Captain Gjermund did not work the coal. Captain Gjermund went ashore in a shore-boat at eight bells of the morning watch and was, the carpenter said with a face that admitted nothing, *negotiating with the agent.* He was negotiating with the agent until midnight. He was negotiating with the agent again the next day, and the day after that. The negotiations, evidently, required brandy in considerable quantity. They also required that nothing else aboard the brig be brought to him for decision.

Solvaag ran the ship. Solvaag was a poor substitute for a captain and an excellent substitute for an angry dog. The crew worked. They worked because Solvaag would beat them with a knotted rope-end if they did not, and the rope-end was on the rail by him and he had used it twice already, on the cabin boy and on Mathis, and so the men kept their hands moving and their mouths shut and they let the coal come up and the coal go over and the coal go down to the lighter, and at sundown the steward brought up tin mugs of coffee and biscuit, and they ate standing up with black hands, and then they slept the sleep of men whose bodies do not so much rest as stop.

Olav slept the same.

On the fifth night the captain sent down for Olav and Nils.

---

Nils was a deckhand from Hogganvik. He was twenty, slow of speech, and had taken half his life to learn to read; his stammer came on him hard when he was startled, and Solvaag liked to startle him. Olav had spoken to him perhaps three times. Nils had on those occasions seemed to like him; it was hard to know with Nils.

The order came down through the boatswain. *The captain wants the new boy and Hogganvik in the shore-boat at ten o'clock to bring him aboard.*

Olav took this as a duty and dressed for it. Nils took this as a duty and looked frightened. They went down into the boat at ten and rowed the half-mile to the customhouse stairs, where the captain had said he would meet them. The captain was not at the stairs. The captain was not at the stairs at half past ten, or eleven, or half past eleven. The night turned. The fog came in off the river. They sat in the boat with the painter held by Nils against the iron ring at the stairhead, and they did not speak.

At twelve o'clock Olav said, "We should go back."

Nils shook his head.

"He told us ten."

"He is not coming."

"If we go back he will say we deserted."

"He is not coming, Nils."

Nils looked at him. The fog had wet his hair flat against his head. He looked very young suddenly, and not slow at all.

"I know," he said. "But we wait."

They waited.

The lamps along the customhouse stairs were oil-lamps in iron brackets, and the fog took the light from them and made of each lamp a small dim haloed thing that did not so much illuminate the stairs as confirm that the stairs were there. Olav could not, after the first half-hour, see the top of the stairs from the boat. He could see the lowest two steps and the iron ring and the painter, and he could see Nils's hand on the painter, and he could see the water sliding past the boat with the small dark slap of a tide that was about to turn.

Nils did not stammer at the boat. Nils stammered when Solvaag startled him, or when the captain looked at him, or when a question came to him faster than he could turn it; Nils did not stammer on a boat in a fog at midnight beside a Norwegian boy he had spoken to perhaps three times. Olav understood, sitting beside him, that the not-stammering was the most Nils had ever shown him. He noted it. He did not, then, know what to do with the noting.

Once, at perhaps half past eleven, a small boat passed them on its way out of the harbor, with a single oar working at the stern and a Portuguese voice talking quietly to itself. The boat went past without seeing them. Olav watched it go. Nils watched it go. Neither of them said anything about it.

At one o'clock the captain came down the customhouse stairs. He was supported on each side by a man whose face Olav did not see clearly in the lamplight. They were both Portuguese. They poured him into the boat with the practiced courtesy of men who had done this for him before. The Portuguese laughed. The captain, settling onto the stern thwart, did not laugh. He looked at Olav and Nils with the dead-still smile that Olav had first seen at noon in the mess, and he said, in Norwegian, very quietly:

"Take me home."

Olav took an oar. Nils took an oar. They rowed.

---

At the brig the captain hoisted himself onto the deck without help and stood swaying for a moment. Then he turned and looked down at Nils, who was making the shore-boat fast.

"Hogganvik."

"Sir."

"You let me sit at those stairs for three hours."

The brig was quiet. The forecastle was asleep. Solvaag was below, drunk too, by all evidence. The watch was a single man at the gangway who was now finding things to look at on the far side of the deck.

"Sir," Nils said. "We were at the stairs at ten, sir. As you said."

"You let me sit at those stairs for three hours."

"Sir, we —"

The captain lifted the rope-end from the rail.

Olav, on the deck behind the captain, did not move. He had a moment to move in. He had perhaps two seconds. The captain raised the rope. Nils made a sound that was not a word. The captain brought the rope down.

The first blow took Nils across the shoulder. The second took him across the side of the head. Nils went down to one knee with his hand up, palm open, the way a child raises a hand to ask for a thing it cannot name. The captain hit him a third time. The third blow, which was full-armed and which Olav heard clearly above the small water-noises of the harbor, took Nils across the temple. Nils went the rest of the way down. He did not put his hand out to catch himself. His head met the planking with the kind of sound a melon makes when set on a stone.

The captain stood breathing for a long second. Then he stepped over Nils and went to his cabin and shut the door.

Olav, on the deck, did not move.

He had counted the steps without meaning to count them. The captain had taken three from the gangway to where Nils was. The captain had taken two seconds to lift the rope-end. The captain had taken — Olav would later count this back, again and again, in the years that followed — perhaps a half-second between the lifting and the first blow, in which Olav, who was eighteen years old and had hands and feet and a coat over a wool shirt, could have moved.

He did not move.

He did not know, at the moment of the not-moving, why. He had not, that morning, decided he would not move; he had not, ever, decided such a thing in advance. The not-moving was not a decision. It was a thing his body did without consulting him, the way the body does the breathing when a man is asleep.

The body had decided. The body had decided that the man in front of it with the rope-end in his hand was a thing the body did not interpose itself between, and the body had held the place it stood in, and the body had not asked Olav whether the holding was the holding he wanted.

Olav, who would for the rest of his life carry the small cold weight of this fact against his ribs, learned in the half-second of the captain's lift that the body did not, when it came to it, always do what a man wanted it to do.

---

He moved when the deck-watch moved. The watch came across slowly, knelt by Nils, and turned him onto his back. Nils was breathing. There was blood on the planking from his ear and a great deal of blood from the broken skin at his temple, and his eyes were open and not seeing, but he was breathing.

"Get the steward," the watch said.

Olav got the steward.

Tolvsen came up in his nightshirt with a rag and a pail of water. He looked at Nils. He looked at the cabin door. He did not look at Olav. He cleaned the blood off Nils's face. He felt with his thick fingers along the side of Nils's head. He sat back on his heels.

"He will live," he said. "Take him below. Put him in my bunk; mine is wider than his."

Olav and the watch carried Nils below. They put him in the steward's bunk. Olav wiped the blood off Nils's forehead with the edge of a blanket. Nils, halfway through this, opened his eyes and looked at Olav, and said, quite clearly:

"I knew."

"Knew what."

"That he would do it."

Then he closed his eyes, and his breathing settled into a kind of deeper rhythm that made Olav, for a moment, think he had stopped breathing. He had not. He had only gone into the place that men go after a beating like that, where the body takes itself out of the conversation.

Olav sat with him for some time. He had been told to do nothing else. He did nothing else.

He thought about the moment he had not moved.

He thought about it for the count of perhaps ten breaths. Then he thought about Nils sitting in the boat beside him at the customhouse stairs in the fog, not stammering. He thought about Nils's hand on the painter at the iron ring, and about Nils's *I know* in the boat at twelve o'clock, and about Nils's *I knew* on the steward's bunk three minutes before. He thought about how Nils, who had been the slowest man in the forecastle since they had cleared the Skagerrak, had been, in the matter of the captain, faster than Olav by every measure that counted.

Nils had known the beating was coming.

Nils had known it the way a man knows a thing he has been around all his life. He had known it the way Olav, who had grown up beside salt water, knew when the tide was about to turn without looking at the water. The knowing was a knowing of the kind one did not learn at any school.

Olav, who had been at the school the captain ran since the morning of the cast-off, had not yet learned the knowing. He had learned, this night, that the knowing was a thing he was going to have to learn. He had learned, also, that the knowing was a thing some men in this trade learned by being beaten and some men learned by watching beatings; and that the men who learned by watching had a small permanent piece of cold in their chest that the men who learned by being beaten did not have.

Nils, in the steward's bunk, breathed his slow breath.

Olav sat beside him with his hands on his knees. He did not, in the rest of the night, weep. He did not, in the rest of the night, sleep. He sat with Nils because the steward had told him to and because the not-sitting would have required him to go up on deck, and the deck was the place where the captain was, and Olav was not, that night, willing to be on a deck the captain might walk onto.

He sat with Nils until the morning watch.

---

The salt loading began two days later. They had taken on a partial cargo of salt from the Setubal lighters at one of the wharves up the Tagus, for a coastwise run to ports the captain had not yet named, and the salt was being struck down through the main hatch into the lower hold. Olav was on the deck with the slings. Captain Gjermund — sober, freshly shaved, smiling — was at the hatch coaming, supervising.

The captain had decided that the salt could be stowed faster if the men did not bother to trim it level in the lower hold, but rather poured it in heaps and let the next sling come down on top. This is not how salt is stowed. This is how a ship is made dangerously cranky in a seaway. Olav, who had spent six months on a coastal smack with a master who had taught him salt, looked at the captain's smile and said, before he had decided to:

"Sir. The salt should be trimmed."

The captain looked at him.

The smile did not change.

"Mr. Hestby," the captain said. "You are eighteen years old. You have been at sea on this brig for two weeks. You are not yet in a position to instruct me on the stowage of salt."

"No, sir."

"Get back to the slings."

Olav got back to the slings. He worked the slings for an hour. The salt continued to come in unstowed. The brig, even at the dock, took on a small list to starboard that was not the list she had had at sunrise.

At the end of the hour the captain came down into the hold, picked up a long-handled salt-shovel from where it lay against the bulkhead, and called Olav down with him.

"Now," the captain said. "I will show you how I want the salt stowed. Step here."

Olav stepped where the captain pointed. The captain stood behind him with the shovel in his hand. The hold was lit by a single lamp hung from the deck-beam above. The captain's face, in the lamplight, was the face Olav had seen at the customhouse stairs holding the rope-end above Nils. The smile had not moved.

"Bend down," the captain said. "Look at the way the salt has fallen here. You see how it has piled?"

Olav bent down.

He bent down because he did not know what else to do, and because he had been ordered to. He bent down knowing — he would later think on this for years, at watches when there was nothing else to think about — knowing perfectly well, as he bent, that the captain was going to hit him on the back of the head with the shovel.

He did not flatten himself out. He did not pretend he had not understood the order. He bent.

But he tilted his head, in the act of bending, just enough that when the shovel came down — and it came down, swinging up from the floor of the hold, with the captain's whole body behind it — the iron blade did not strike his head. It struck the iron post that held up the deck-beam above the lamp. The shovel rang. The lamp swung. A piece of the shovel-blade broke off and fell into the salt.

The captain stared at the broken shovel.

Olav stared at the captain.

Neither of them moved for what felt like a long time and was perhaps three seconds.

Then the captain put the broken shovel against the bulkhead, very carefully, as a man might put down a glass he was afraid to drop, and he climbed up out of the hold without looking back.

Olav stayed in the hold with the broken shovel and the salt and the swinging lamp.

When he was sure the captain was gone he sat down on the salt and put his head on his knees, and he did not weep, because weeping was not the thing that came; what came was a kind of cold trembling that started in his thighs and worked its way up through his ribs into his teeth, and stayed there for a quarter of an hour before it let him stand up.

When he stood up he thought of Nils. He had not moved that night. He had bent today. The man he was becoming on this ship was not the man he wanted to be.

He understood three things, in this order:

One — that he was not going to die on this voyage by his own hand or by accident.

Two — that he was going to die on this voyage if he did not desert it.

Three — that he was going to desert it, somehow, somewhere, soon. He did not know how. He did not know where. He knew only that he would.

He went up the ladder. The hold was below him. The salt was below him. The captain was on the quarterdeck, smiling at the carpenter.

Olav did not look at the captain. He went forward and he picked up the slings and he went back to work.

That night, in his hammock, he turned on his side toward the wall of the forecastle and he put his hand on the canvas and he thought, very clearly:

*I am going to live.*

He had not had a use for those words before that night. He had a use for them now.

He thought it again. *I am going to live.* It was not an oath. It was a plan.
