The *Semiramis* was not the brig.

She was a bark of about a thousand tons, a deep-water carrier with the kind of clean lines a ship has only when she has been built within the previous five years for the kind of voyage Olav had not, until now, signed for. Her hull was painted black with a thin red sheer-line. Her three masts were taller than any masts Olav had been close to in his life. Her standing rigging was newly tarred. Her decks had been holystoned that morning. She smelled of pine pitch and of the lime-wash they used on the inside of the bulwarks against marine growth, and underneath those of the sour particular smell of timber that has been brought from a forest to a wharf to be shipped to a country where there are no longer enough trees.

Olav came up the gangway with the sea-chest on his shoulder. A mate at the rail — a tall man of about thirty-five with a Bergen accent — looked at his papers and pointed him forward.

"Common seaman?"

"Yes."

"Forecastle. Berth six on the larboard side. Captain wants to see you in the cabin at four bells of the afternoon watch. Stow your chest, get yourself looked at by the bosun, and report aft at four bells. You are early. We do not lift until tomorrow morning."

"Yes."

The mate did not, in saying any of this, look at Olav for longer than was required. He returned to the papers in his hand. Olav went forward.

The forecastle of the *Semiramis* was twice the size of the forecastle of the *Sigrid.* It had to be — there were eighteen common seamen on the bark, where the brig had carried perhaps a dozen. The hammocks were rigged in two banks, port and starboard, with a narrow walkway between them. Berth six on the larboard was Olav's; the chest fit under the hammock. He hung the hammock. He set out his blanket, his coat, his clean shirt. He did this in the small careful way a man does the same actions he has done at twenty in a different forecastle, and the carefulness was the carefulness of a man who had registered, without permitting himself to register it, that he had now twice in his life unpacked into a forecastle on the day before a long voyage, and that the second time was the same as the first time, and that the world had arranged for him to do this thing twice when most men did it once.

He did not let himself sit on the hammock for long.

He went to find the bosun.

---

The bosun of the *Semiramis* was a man of about fifty named Sundberg, from Karlskrona originally but a Bergen resident for thirty years, with a voice that was made to be heard from the foretop and a manner that was dry in the particular Swedish way that does not signal the dryness. He looked at Olav. He looked at Olav's chest measurement and his hands. He nodded once.

"Hestby."

"Yes."

"Certificate from Stavanger?"

"Last June."

"And you signed for common seaman."

"Yes."

"Captain told me. I was surprised. I am no longer surprised. You have decided to put yourself into the deep water for the first time, even though you carry the certificate of a navigator. That is one of the two reasons a man does it. The other is that he has no other berth. I do not, looking at you, think you had no other berth."

"I had another berth."

"Then you chose. Good. We will see what choosing buys you."

He did not, in saying it, smile or frown. He turned and walked aft. Olav followed him.

---

The captain's cabin on the *Semiramis* was on the after-deck, behind the wheelhouse, much like the saloon on the *Diana* except that everything in it was three degrees more formal. The panelling was darker. The carpet was newer. The brass fixtures were polished to the kind of brightness a sailor notices and a passenger does not. Captain Brun was at his desk with the ship's articles open in front of him.

Captain Brun was sixty years old. He had the upright carriage of a man who had been an officer for thirty-five years and had not, in any of those years, allowed himself to slouch in front of a junior. His beard was iron-grey and trimmed close. His eyes, when he raised them from the articles to look at Olav, were the kind of light grey that does not, by themselves, reveal anything; the revealing is all in the small movements at the edges of them, and those movements were minimal.

"Hestby."

"Yes, sir."

"Sit down."

Olav sat.

The captain did not look at him for some seconds. He read the articles. He turned a page. He read the next page. He looked up.

"Your certificate is with you?"

"Yes, sir."

"Show me."

Olav took the leather folder from his coat. He opened it. He took out the certificate. He laid it on the desk.

The captain read it. He looked at the seal. He turned it once to see the back. He handed it back.

"You signed for common seaman."

"Yes, sir."

"Why."

"Sir?"

"Why does a navigator with a year-old certificate sign for common seaman."

Olav had been preparing the answer to this question for a month. He had decided, at his desk in the loft at Finnøy in February, that he would tell the truth in the simplest form he could give it. He gave it now.

"Because I have not yet been to a deep-water port. The certificate was given to me on the basis of a year on the brig and three months on a steamer south from Vardøy. Neither was a deep-water voyage. I have been told by Captain Sars of the *Diana* that an officer's berth on a deep-water ship is the next step for me, and that I should not take it before I have rounded the Horn or come around the Cape of Good Hope at least once. I am taking your common seaman's berth because the voyage you are making is the voyage Sars described."

The captain looked at him for some seconds.

"Halvar Sars."

"Yes, sir."

"You sailed on the *Diana.*"

"Yes, sir. From Vardøy to Bergen, three years ago this summer."

"How are his eggs."

"He still keeps them, sir. As of last June."

"He has been keeping them for forty years. The day Sars stops keeping eggs is the day a man should worry."

He returned to the articles. He made a note in the margin in pencil. He looked up again.

"Hestby. I have decided three things about you in the last sixty seconds. First: you will work the common seaman's watch the entire voyage out and the entire voyage home. I do not promote on a single passage. Second: when we make Calcutta you will come ashore with me on the first day in the harbor, and you will draft for me the chart corrections we will need before we depart for Norway. The chart-work is yours; the discharge of the seaman's duty is also yours. You will do both. You will not, on this ship, have the luxury of being one or the other. Third: you will find that the work of an able seaman on a bark of this tonnage is harder than the work you did on the brig. You are six pounds under the weight you should be. You will eat what the steward gives you and you will not refuse the tea. Are these things understood."

"Yes, sir."

"Good. Mr. Sundberg has shown you your berth. You have the rest of the day to make yourself ready. We lift at the morning tide. Dismissed."

Olav stood. He took the certificate back into the leather folder. He took the folder back into his coat. He went to the door.

"Hestby."

"Sir."

"You are not the first navigator I have had as a common seaman."

"No, sir."

"You may, on the basis of how you handle yourself between here and Calcutta, be the third I have signed off as a first mate. Do you understand."

"Yes, sir."

"Good. Go forward."

Olav went forward.

---

They lifted at the morning tide on the Wednesday.

The departure was the kind of departure a deep-water bark makes from a Norwegian port in June: the steam-tug took her out of the inner harbor on a slack water, the pilot stayed aboard until they cleared the outer light, the captain stood on the quarterdeck with his glass and watched the harbor recede with the small even attention of a man who had said this particular goodbye perhaps two hundred times. Olav was on the foredeck with Sundberg and the other common seamen. They were sweating up the topsails as the tug let go.

The Stavanger roads opened out before them. The wind was light from the southwest. The bark took her course south.

Olav did not, on the leaving, allow himself to look back at the quay where his mother and father had stood the day before. He had said his goodbye. He had decided to say it once. He looked instead at the water and the rigging and the horizon ahead, and he registered, with the small particular calm a man gets at the start of a voyage he has chosen, that the next eighteen months were now under way.

The *Semiramis* cleared the outer light at noon.

By six bells of the afternoon watch she was past the Skudesnes, and the Norwegian coast was a thin grey line on her port quarter, and the wind was fair, and Olav was aloft with Sundberg loosing the main royal.

He worked the gasket. He shook the sail down. He came back to the deck.

That night in the forecastle he listened to the bark settle into the long roll of the open sea. He thought of the work he had done that afternoon at the royal yard. He thought it had been good work.

He thought, also, that the work the next morning would be different work, and that there would be work like it for the next eighteen months, and that he was — for the first time he could remember — looking forward to it.

He thought, also, of the small drawer in the workbench at Finnøy, and of the folded sheet inside it. He was not bound for the Tyne. The *Semiramis* would call at other ports — Calcutta and her sisters, ports of a different list. But the world she was going to had every kind of port in it, and a deep-water man saw what a coaster did not. He understood that he was looking forward, also, to ports he had not yet named.
