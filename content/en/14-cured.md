title: "Cured"

He came home on the Tau smack eight weeks after he had left Stavanger and three weeks before his promised return, with a sea-chest that was lighter than the one he had taken north and a face that, the man at the smack-tiller did not feel obliged to mention but did notice, was the face of a different boy.

Bergen had passed in a day. Captain Sars had walked him as far as the chandlery at the bottom of the wharf and had given him a folded letter to put in the pocket of his coat. *For the navigation school in the spring,* Sars had said, and had not elaborated on what was in the letter, and had patted him once on the shoulder, the way he had patted the rail of the *Diana* on the seventh night of the voyage south, and had turned and gone back up the wharf to his ship without looking back. Olav had understood, watching him go, that this was how Captain Sars said goodbye — the same way he said hello, and the same way he gave the next order, and the same way he wrote a letter — and that the consistency was its own form of farewell.

The coaster south to Stavanger had taken three days. Olav had paid his fare from the dollar-a-day Sars had paid him for the stockfish loading; there had been money left over. The captain of the coaster had been a man of forty named Tjaere, who was indifferent to passengers and who had let Olav sleep in a corner of the deck-house out of the wind. Olav had slept a great deal. He had eaten the bread and herring he bought at Bergen. He had not coughed.

He had not coughed, by the time the coaster made Stavanger, for three days in a row.

He noticed. He did not yet count the noticing for anything.

---

The Tau smack put him off at the dock on a slack water afternoon in late August. The man at the tiller was a Finnøy man — Olav had known him slightly as a boy — who recognized him as he stepped ashore and called out, *Olav, that you?* and Olav, with his sea-chest at his feet, said yes, and the smack-man looked at him for a moment with the look a man gives to a thing he had not expected to see again, and then shook himself out of it and said *welcome home, lad,* and pushed off and was gone.

Olav stood for a moment with the chest. The afternoon was warm. The road from the dock to the house went past the church, as it had in May.

He did not, this time, stop at the wall. He walked past the church without slowing.

He understood, walking past, that the not-stopping was as much a decision as the stopping had been. He had come past this wall in the autumn with a wet sleeve and a cough and a question he could not answer. He was coming past it now in August with a clear chest and the dollar-a-day money in his pocket and a letter from a Bergen captain in his coat. The wall was the same wall. The boy was a different boy. There was nothing to say at the wall today that would not be said better at the woodpile.

He went up the road to the house.

---

His father was at the gate.

His father had not been at the gate when Olav had arrived in May, and Olav understood, seeing him there, that his father had taken to standing at the gate in the late afternoons since May — perhaps every afternoon, perhaps only on days when a smack came in — and that the father had not, in eight weeks of standing at the gate, told Olav's mother he was doing it.

His father did not move when Olav came up the road. He did not move when Olav was within twenty feet. He waited until Olav was at the gate, with the chest at his feet, and then he opened the gate and stepped aside and said:

"Welcome home, Olav."

"Father."

His father looked at him. The looking was different from May. The looking was the looking of a man who had been told a thing about a man's face by his eyes and was doing, in the next two seconds, the small calculation that confirms the eyes have not lied. Olav let himself be looked at. His father's calculation took longer than Olav had expected and shorter than he had feared.

"Come in," his father said. "Your mother is in the garden. I will fetch her."

"I will fetch her."

"Yes," his father said. "You will."

---

The mother in the garden was as the mother in the kitchen had been in May, except that her hands were in dirt and her hair was tied back with a yellow cloth and her face, when she looked up at his shadow falling across her bean rows, was the face of a woman who had been waiting for this shadow without permitting herself to expect it.

She stood up. She did not cross the garden to him, this time. She wiped her hands on her apron. She looked at him from where she was, across the bean rows, for a long breath.

"You are not coughing," she said.

"No."

"How long."

"Three days. Maybe four. I do not know exactly when it stopped."

She nodded once. She wiped her hands again, although her hands were already as clean as they were going to get. She came across the bean rows then, slowly, so as not to step on the new shoots, and when she reached him she put her hands on his face again, on his cheekbones, and she looked for the place she had looked for in May, and she did not, this time, find what she was looking for. The glands had not come up. The dark circles were gone. The skin of his face had returned to the skin a young man's face was meant to have.

She let her hands drop.

"Go in to your father," she said. "I will come."

---

That evening at the woodpile his father split nothing. He had brought the axe with him out of habit, and he set it in the chopping-block and left it there and did not lift it again.

"How," he said.

"There was a steamer at Vardøy. The *Diana,* out of Bergen. The captain was an old man named Sars."

"Halvar Sars."

"You know him."

"I know of him. He is a good man. He kept eggs."

"He still keeps eggs."

His father almost smiled. The almost was small enough that Olav was not sure he had seen it.

"Sars took me on as a stockfish loader in exchange for passage to Bergen. He fed me. He let me sleep. The voyage south was twelve days. By the time we made Bergen the cough was, I thought, smaller. By the time I made the coaster to Stavanger it was smaller still. I have not coughed in three days. I do not know which day was the day."

"There is no day that is the day."

"No."

"The passenger gets off at the station the passenger chooses. The passenger does not consult the man he has been riding with."

"No."

His father looked at the woodpile.

"Yours got off in Bergen," he said.

"Or before."

"Or before. It does not matter. It is off."

"It is off."

His father picked up the axe. He weighed it in his hand. He set it back down.

"Sars said something to me on the *Diana,*" Olav said. "He said I should rest the winter at home and apply to the navigation school in the spring. He gave me a letter for the school. I did not know I had been thinking about the school until he named it."

"You had been thinking about it."

"Apparently."

"Then you will go."

"Yes."

"Olav."

"Yes."

His father did not look at him. He looked at the chopping-block.

"You will not, I think, be on a ship next summer. You will be at school. The summer after that you will be at sea. The summer after that you may be home again. I am telling you this because I have been counting the summers since you went away the first time, and I have not, in the counting, been wrong. Three summers. Then perhaps five summers in twelve. Then perhaps two summers in five. You will be home less and less, and I will be at the gate more and more, until you are home all the time and I am not at the gate at all. This is the way of it. I am only telling you so that you will know I have done the arithmetic and am not surprised."

He picked up the axe. He did not split anything with it. He held it in his hand for the count of perhaps three breaths, the way a man holds a thing he has been carrying for many years and has just remembered the weight of.

"Go in to your mother," he said.

Olav went in.

---

He climbed the loft stair. The bed was where it had been. The hollow in the mattress was where it had been. The H carved into the wall, with three crosspieces, was where it had been. He sat on the edge of the bed for some time without lying down. He looked at the wall.

He thought it carefully, the way a man thinks a sentence in a language he has just learned, to be sure he has the syllables in the right order: *I am cured.*

The body did not answer. The body does not answer this kind of sentence with anything except the absence of the cough, which is a quietness so small that the man who has been listening for it does not, at first, hear it.

He listened for it. He did not hear it.

He lay down.

The hollow fit him. It fit him better than it had in May because the body that fit it was now a body that had eggs in it and butter and sleep, and the hollow had not had to remember a thinner body. The hollow had remembered the body it had known when he was sixteen, and the body that came back from the *Diana* was, at last, that body or close to it, and the mattress and the body were, that evening, in agreement.

He went to sleep.
