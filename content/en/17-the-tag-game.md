title: "The Tag Game"

He was in the tag game by accident. He had been crossing the meadow to find Aagot, and the older girls at the alder had pressed her into the game and the game had needed an extra older player on the boys' side, and Aagot, twelve and freshly capable of small power, had pointed at Olav and said *him.* The girls had turned to him. He had not, at twenty, played tag in a meadow on Finnøy in eight years. He had also, at twenty, not learned how to refuse a thing twelve girls were asking him to do at once.

He played tag.

The game, as the older girls of Finnøy played it at midsummer at Landa, was not the tag he remembered. The tag he remembered was the tag of small children in a yard, in which one was *it* and chased the others. The tag the older girls played was the slower, stranger kind, in which the *it* gave a small wooden token — a *tag,* in the literal sense, a flat painted disk the size of a coin — to the next person, and the next person had to keep it for the count of one minute (kept by the eldest girl, who counted aloud) before passing it on. The disk was the *tag.* The game was a game of choosing — choosing whom you handed the disk to next, choosing in the middle of a meadow full of other young people, choosing without explaining why.

The first time the disk reached him he held it for the minute and gave it to one of the older boys at the water's edge. The boy took it without surprise.

The second time the disk reached him it was given to him by a girl he had not seen before.

She was perhaps eighteen. She was small, a little under five feet, with light brown hair the color of new pine and the kind of face that gathered all of the Finnøy summer light and did something unfair with it. She handed him the disk without speaking. She did not, when she had handed it to him, step back. She held the position of the handing, with her hand still where the disk had been, for one breath longer than the game required, and then she turned and ran.

He held the disk for the minute. He gave it to the older boy at the water's edge.

The third time the disk reached him it was given to him by the same girl.

She did not speak this time either. She put the disk into his hand. She held the position again. The breath was, this time, longer — a breath and a half, perhaps two. He could see the freckles that were beginning to come up on the bridge of her nose with the summer, and the small scar at the corner of her mouth that was the kind of scar a girl who has been a tomboy carries.

He held the disk for the minute.

He did not give it to the older boy at the water's edge.

He held the disk out to her. He held it out perhaps a second longer than the game required. She, in reaching for it, did not quite catch it. The disk fell into the grass between them. They both reached for it. Their hands met at the disk. Olava picked it up. She did not, in picking it up, look away from him. Her eyes were the kind of grey that under different light would have been blue, and under the meadow's afternoon light were the grey of the channel at four o'clock. She did not smile. She tilted her head, very slightly, to one side, the way a person does who has just been spoken to in a language they had not known the other person spoke.

She held the disk for the minute.

She gave it back to him.

The eldest girl, counting at the alder, called out the start of a new round. The disk passed back to her. Olav, no longer holding it, stood for a moment in the meadow, and the girl who had given it to him, no longer holding it either, stood across the meadow from him for the same moment, and they looked at each other across perhaps twenty feet of midsummer grass. Then the game moved on without them. They both rejoined it. They did not, for the rest of the game, hand each other the disk again. They did not need to.

---

When the game ended Aagot ran up to him with her face pink from running and her hair coming loose from its braid. "Olav."

"Yes."

"That was Olava."

"Olava."

"Olava Lindøy. From Lindøy. Her father is the old man on Lindøy who lost his hand to a rope at Brest in '57. She is at the Lindøy boats with her sister Marit. I told her you were my brother. She said *I know who he is.*"

"How does she know who I am."

"I do not know. I asked her. She said she knew. She said it as if knowing was nothing very complicated to do. Are you going to talk to her at the boats?"

"Aagot."

"What."

"Did you tell her I was twenty?"

"She did not ask. I think she had decided by that part of it."

"Decided what."

"I do not know," Aagot said. She looked at him with the look she gave him at the tiller of the boat — the look of a younger sister who is, perhaps, going to be older than her brother in some respects long before she expects to be — and she ran off again.

Olav stood in the meadow.

He had not, in the play of the game, allowed himself to feel anything. He felt it now.

What he felt was small and very specific. It was the feeling of having been handed a disk twice by a girl he did not know, and of having given it back to her once, and of having looked at her across twenty feet of grass for a moment and not looked away. The feeling was not the feeling he had expected, in the years of his life so far when he had thought about what feeling-something-for-a-girl might be like; the feeling did not have heat or hunger in it. It had instead a quality of *recognition,* of having met for the second time in his life a person whose presence in a room reorganized the room, and of being unsure, looking back across the meadow, whether the rearrangement was something the world was doing to him or something he was doing to the world.

The first person whose presence in a room had reorganized the room had been on a brig.

He had decided, that spring, not to think of the man on the brig at gatherings.

He kept the decision now. He did not, looking across the meadow at Olava Lindøy at the alder, allow himself to compare. He let the recognition be its own thing. He let it be, for the moment, the only thing.

---

The boats were drawn up at the Lindøy jetty when the long shadows came across the meadow. Olav, by the small calculation a young man makes when he has spent an afternoon at a midsummer gathering, found himself at the Lindøy jetty without remembering having decided to walk there. He found himself there because Aagot had gone there with Olava's sister Marit and had, in her twelve-year-old way, made a small project of reuniting them at the jetty. Aagot was talking with Marit about a chestnut horse on Stjernerøy. Olava was at the gunwale of her father's boat, putting the family hamper away.

Olav walked up to the boat. He did not know what he was going to say.

"Olava."

"Olav."

"Aagot tells me you knew who I was."

"I knew who you were."

"How."

She set the hamper down. She turned to face him. The grey-channel eyes again. She was perhaps a foot shorter than him; she did not, in being shorter, give the impression of being smaller. The smallness was a fact about her body. The other thing — the thing that had reorganized the meadow — was not a fact about her body. He could not have said what it was a fact about.

"My brother Asbjørn was at Stjernerøy with the Engelstads when your friend Thomas left," she said. "Thomas left in a small boat at midnight from Wilmington. Asbjørn knew the man who lent the boat. The man wrote to Asbjørn afterwards. Asbjørn told me about it the next year, when he came home for Christmas. I have known the name *Olav Hestby of Finnøy* for — eighteen months. Not two years. I am being generous to my own certainty. Eighteen months."

Olav did not, for some time, speak.

"You knew my name."

"For eighteen months."

"And not the rest of the story."

"No. The man who lent the boat did not know the rest. Asbjørn does not know the rest. Aagot, today, does not know the rest. I am not asking for the rest. I am only telling you why I knew your name."

"Yes."

"My brother died of the cough four years ago," she said. "I nursed him. I know the look of the men who have it and I know the look of the men who have had it and I know the look of the men who got out from under it. You have the third look. I noticed it across the meadow before I gave you the disk the first time."

"You gave me the disk the first time because of the look."

"Yes. I gave it to you the second time because you held the look while you held the disk."

She picked up a coil of rope from the boat's thwart. She did not, in picking it up, look away from him.

"My family is going home in a quarter of an hour. We have to load the boat. If you mean to write to me I am at Lindøy. The post comes once a week. Mention to the postmaster when you give him the letter that it is for *the captain's daughter,* and not for the other Olava on Lindøy who is the schoolmaster's wife. If you mean not to write to me, do not write."

She turned then, and went up the jetty toward her father's boat with the rope on her arm.

Olav stood at the gunwale for some time.

Aagot, who had been pretending across the jetty to be discussing the chestnut horse with Marit, came over.

"Olav."

"Yes."

"You are not, I think, going home as the same Olav who came to the meadow."

"No."

"That is what midsummer at Landa is for," Aagot said, with the small perfect authority of a twelve-year-old girl who has been to the gathering once and feels she has now grasped the institution. "Come on. Mother is at the alder. She will want to know."

"Aagot."

"Yes."

"Do not tell her yet."

"All right."

She took his hand. She walked him up the jetty toward the alder. At the alder his mother, who had been watching him from the moment he had begun walking toward the Lindøy jetty, did not say anything when he and Aagot arrived. She handed him a piece of bread with butter and honey on it and did not look at his face. She had decided, evidently, that she would let her son tell her what was in her son's face, when he was ready. That was the family way. The family was a family of people who waited.

He ate the bread.

He did not tell his mother yet.

---

On the boat home, with Aagot at the tiller and the wind off the south-east holding steady and the long Finnøy summer evening spreading out over the channel ahead of them, he sat in the middle of the boat with his hands on his knees. He looked at Lindøy across the channel, and at the smoke from the small fire at the Lindøy boats where the Lindøy family was eating its supper before lifting for home.

"Olav."

"Yes."

"You looked at her."

"Yes."

"You looked at her for a long time."

"Yes."

"Mother saw you looking. Father did not see, but Mother saw. Mother is going to want to know what we tell her tonight."

"Tell her she gave me the disk twice."

"That is not what Mother is going to want to know."

She turned the tiller a quarter-point to bring the boat onto its course for Finnøy. She did not, for the rest of the crossing, mention Olava again. She had said the thing she had been holding all afternoon. She was twelve. She had used it once and was satisfied.

He did the small private thing he had taught himself not to do at gatherings.

He thought of Haakon.

He thought of Haakon and of Olava in the same minute.

The wind held steady from the south-east. Aagot, at the tiller, was singing something quietly to herself. The Finnøy hills in the long evening light were the color they had been in his head across an ocean, and they were also, for the first time in five years, the color they actually were.
