The Atlantic ran nineteen days from Lisbon.

By the tenth day the brig was halfway across the northern passage, with the Azores a week astern and nothing but water ahead of her until the Carolina banks. The wind had settled into a steady northeasterly that carried her at seven knots and did not, in the count of three days, shift more than a point. The captain was pleased. The captain was, Olav had learned by the fourth day of the crossing, at his most dangerous when he was pleased.

The carpenter worked at his bench in the lee of the galley through most of the daylight hours. He was making — though he did not, on being asked, say what — a new fid for the boatswain and two new belaying pins for the starboard pinrail. The pinrail had lost a pin off the Azores when a sea had come over the rail; the carpenter was replacing it, and making the spare beside it as well, because a carpenter who had outlasted six captains was a man who made two of a thing when one of the thing had been lost.

Olav had been to the carpenter's bench twice in the ten days.

The first visit had been an errand — a small auger borrowed for deck-work Solvaag had set him. The carpenter had handed the auger across the bench without comment. Olav had said *thank you, sir* because he had not known what else to say, and the carpenter had nodded once without looking up, and the visit had been the whole of it.

The second visit had been on no errand.

It had been on the seventh evening, at the end of Olav's watch, when the carpenter had been at the bench alone finishing the second belaying pin and Olav had come forward with nothing in his hands. He had not, in walking forward, decided he was going to visit. He had been walking toward the forward hatch to go below. He had changed his line at the galley door without thinking about it. He had come to the bench and stood three feet from the carpenter, and said nothing.

The carpenter had not looked up.

The carpenter had, after perhaps a minute, said:

"Mr. Hestby."

"Sir."

"You are here because you have had a thing in you since Lisbon."

"Yes, sir."

"You will not tell me the thing."

"No, sir."

"Good. You will tell no man. The telling is not the work. The carrying is the work."

"Yes, sir."

"You may stand at the bench until the bell."

"Yes, sir."

Olav had stood at the bench until the bell. He had not spoken. The carpenter had not spoken. When eight had rung he had stepped back, and the carpenter had nodded once, and he had gone forward to the forecastle.

He had understood, in the four nights since, that the carpenter had given him the one thing the carpenter's trade permitted him to give a hand in Olav's condition — which was to let him stand at a bench while a working man worked, without asking anything of him, and without offering anything but the bench.

The carpenter was sixty-one. The carpenter had outlasted six captains. The carpenter knew.

---

On the eleventh night the wind held steady and the watch was the small ordinary watch a brig in the middle of the Atlantic on a fair trade kept. Four bells of the middle watch — two in the morning — came and went in the small even dark of a brig on a deep ocean at the pace her trade made her keep.

Olav was aloft at the foretop.

The mate — Solvaag — had, at the turn of the watch, said *Hestby, up the foremast, check the main topsail lift,* and Olav had taken the order without question, because by the eleventh night he had stopped questioning orders Solvaag gave him at the turn of any watch. The lift was — as it always was when Solvaag gave the order — fine. The lift was always fine. The order was the order. Solvaag gave it because Solvaag had decided, in the second week, that the best use of Olav was the use that kept Olav aloft in the dark between four bells and eight bells, where Olav was not on the deck and where Solvaag did not have to look at him.

Olav had come to accept the orders as a form of mercy, in their own way. The foretop in the middle of an Atlantic night was a place. The deck was another place. The foretop was, for the minutes Olav was at it, a place the captain and Solvaag were not.

He sat at the foretop platform at four bells of the middle watch of the eleventh night and he looked down at the deck.

The deck was the deck he had been looking down at for three weeks. The brig was on her course. The mate was aft. The man at the wheel was a hand named Kristensen who did not, by Olav's observation across the three weeks, speak to anyone. Kristensen was the wheel and nothing else. The binnacle light was the yellow point at the quarterdeck. The galley was dark — the steward had banked the fire at eight bells of the first watch. The carpenter's bench was at the lee of the galley, and the bench was dark too, because the carpenter had been below in his bunk since the end of the first dog-watch.

Or, rather: the carpenter had been below until the moment Olav, from the foretop, saw him come up the forward hatch.

The carpenter came up at four bells and a quarter. In his left hand was a lantern. In his right was a wedge of wood that Olav — from fifty feet above — could not precisely identify, but that was, by its shape, a spare belaying pin the carpenter had decided to shape at an hour the shaping would not wake the forecastle.

The carpenter went to his bench. He set the lantern at the corner. He set the wedge at the centre. He took a plane from the rack under the bench. He began, in the small still night-air of the brig on a steady trade, to plane the wedge.

The sound of the plane — a small continuous fine-edged shush — reached Olav at the foretop.

Olav did not, at first, think to move. He sat at the platform with his back to the foremast and his legs out along the cap and he listened to the shush of the plane and he watched the carpenter's small stooped figure in the lantern light at the lee of the galley.

Then Solvaag came forward.

Solvaag came along the starboard deck at the unsteady walk of a man who had, in the course of the watch, been at something the mate ought not to have been at. His left hand was on the pinrail as he walked. His right was at his side. He had, in the right, the marlinspike he used for the piece of work he had been sitting at aft.

Or the marlinspike was in his right hand because he had brought it with him for the walk forward.

Olav understood, in the count of the two breaths Solvaag took to cross the deck from the aft quarter to the galley, which of the two readings was the reading.

The mate's face was lit by the binnacle light as he passed it and by the galley lantern as he neared the carpenter's bench. The face was the face Olav had seen on the captain at the salt-hold with the shovel, and on the captain at the customhouse stairs at one in the morning. It was the unsteady face, the decision-made face, the face of a man who had walked a certain distance along a decision and who was walking the rest of it on momentum.

Olav was at the foretop.

Fifty feet above the deck, at the cap of the foremast, with the wind small and the air clear and the lantern at the carpenter's bench making the carpenter's small stooped figure visible to him at a vantage no man on the deck had, Olav saw — by the simple physics of line-of-sight — what no man on the deck would see in time:

Solvaag was going to hit the carpenter with the marlinspike.

He had two seconds, perhaps three, before the hitting would happen.

He called.

He called *Solvaag* and he called *carpenter* and he called *look up,* and he called them in the rising-volume register the foretop's wind and distance would take and drown, and he called them at the carpenter, because the carpenter was the man whose life was what calling was for. The carpenter — who had at his bench the plane in his hand and the wedge in the plane's groove and the continuous shush under his thumb — did not, in the two seconds between the first syllable of Olav's call and the arrival of the marlinspike, look up.

The foretop was too high for a shout.

Solvaag did not hear the shout. Solvaag would not have been deterred if he had.

The carpenter did not hear the shout. The plane and the wedge and the bench and the focused reach of an old man's work-attention had him.

The marlinspike came at the carpenter from Solvaag's right side, swung from the shoulder, at a rising arc that was meant — Olav registered, even in the half-second of its travel — for the carpenter's ribs rather than his head. Solvaag had kept what wits a man at his level of drunkenness kept. He had not gone for the head. The head was a killing-swing. The ribs were a hurting-swing. Solvaag had, in the evil arithmetic a drunk mate made at the turn of a watch, wanted the hurting.

The marlinspike took the carpenter across the ribs on his left side.

The carpenter made a sound. The sound was not a word. The plane fell out of his hand. The wedge rolled to the edge of the bench. The carpenter, who had been bent over the wedge, was knocked sideways onto the deck. He went down on his hip and then onto his shoulder. The lantern — which was not on the carpenter, but on the corner of the bench — stayed lit. The carpenter, on the deck, drew up his knees to his chest and did not, for the count of five breaths, move.

Solvaag stood at the bench with the marlinspike in his hand. He looked down at the carpenter. He looked up — not in Olav's direction, because Solvaag did not know Olav was there — but aft, at the quarterdeck, at the binnacle light. Nothing at the binnacle light moved. Kristensen at the wheel did not turn his head. Whether Kristensen had seen was a thing no one — and least of all Solvaag — was going to know for sure, because Kristensen did not, by the observation of a ship of three weeks' acquaintance, turn his head for any reason whatever.

Solvaag turned.

He walked back along the starboard deck to the aft quarter. He went below by the main companionway. He did not, on the walk back, sway.

Olav, at the foretop, had not moved since the call.

He came down the shrouds.

He came down faster than he had come up. He came down in the register of a man who had learned, in three weeks, that speed aloft in a fair trade at night was a thing the body could manage if the body did not let itself consider the fall. He reached the deck at the foot of the foremast. He crossed to the lee of the galley.

The carpenter was on the deck with his knees to his chest. He was breathing. The breathing had the catch in it a man's breathing had when a rib was either broken or near-broken and the man was not yet sure which. The carpenter's eyes were open. His hand was at his ribs.

"Sir."

The carpenter did not answer.

"Sir. The steward."

"No."

"Sir. The bosun."

"No. Hestby. Not."

"Sir —"

"Hestby."

The carpenter's voice — small and flat and level in the way only a carpenter of his age on a brig in the middle of an ocean at four bells of the middle watch could make a voice — stopped Olav at the bench.

"Yes, sir."

"Help me up. To my bench. Sit me at the stool. Do not call anyone. In five minutes I will walk to my bunk on my own feet. The morning will find me at my bench with a bruised rib. The morning will not find me on the deck with blood in my beard. Do you understand."

"Sir, the strike —"

"Missed the head. Did not miss the rib. The rib can be borne. The head cannot. I have the rib. Help me up."

Olav helped him up.

He put his hands under the carpenter's shoulders. The carpenter was heavier than he looked. The carpenter's breathing caught when Olav lifted him. The carpenter gave a sound through his teeth that was not the sound the marlinspike had made him give but was a companion sound. Olav got him onto the stool. The carpenter sat with both hands at the bench and his head down. His breath shortened and then, gradually, evened.

He raised his head.

"Hestby."

"Sir."

"You saw."

"From the foretop."

"From the foretop. Yes. That is why I did not turn. If I had turned it would have been to you. I did not know you were there. The not-knowing was a mercy. The mercy took the back of my head out of the strike."

"Sir."

"Sit down. At the bench. Not on the stool. On the deck at the end of the bench. Where no man at the binnacle can see you. Sit."

Olav sat.

The carpenter, at the bench, took the plane from where it had fallen on the deck and put it back in the rack under the bench. He took the wedge and put it at the centre of the bench. He picked up another tool — not the plane, a chisel — and set it in his hand. His hand was shaking. He did not, for some seconds, cut.

Then he cut.

The chisel took a thin curl off the wedge.

The sound of the chisel was not the sound of the plane. But the sound was a working sound. The sound was the working sound of a man at his bench at four bells of the middle watch on a brig in the middle of an Atlantic night.

"Sir," Olav said, after some time.

"Yes."

"What do I do."

"You do nothing."

"Sir."

"You do nothing because the doing of something is the thing the mate wants. The doing of something will bring the bosun. The bosun will bring the captain. The captain will take Solvaag's word over yours because the captain owns Solvaag and does not own you. The doing of something will put you on a yardarm tomorrow morning with a cut becket. The doing of nothing will put Solvaag below with the strike in him and the strike undone by nothing. The strike is undone by the morning. You do nothing, Hestby. You do nothing the way a man at your age learns to do nothing by doing it once and remembering."

"Sir."

"I have, in forty years at sea, been hit seven times with a marlinspike, a bucket-bail, a chain-end, a fid, and a loaded rope-end. I am not, at sixty-one, one of the men who were hit for the last time. I know the doing-nothing. The doing-nothing is what has kept me at my bench."

"Sir."

"You saw the arc."

Olav looked at him.

"Sir."

"You were at the foretop. You saw the marlinspike at the swing. You saw the carpenter at the bench. You saw the two come together. Tell me what you saw about the arc."

Olav did not, for a moment, know what the carpenter was asking.

He thought about it. He thought about what he had seen from the foretop. He had seen the whole of it — the swing from Solvaag's shoulder, the lift of the arm, the arc of the marlinspike coming up and then coming down across the six feet between Solvaag and the carpenter, the moment of contact at the carpenter's ribs, the follow-through of the arm, the stopping of the marlinspike at the end of the arm's reach.

He thought: the force was at the end of the swing.

He thought: the carpenter had been standing in the force of the end of the swing.

He thought: if the carpenter had, at the moment he was struck, stepped in — toward Solvaag — the marlinspike's end would have passed over his shoulder without force. The carpenter would have been under the end of the arc.

He thought of Haakon, at the lee of the galley on the second day, with the cold coffee in his hands and the blanket over his knees and his voice saying *you step toward the pivot. You pass under the end of the arc before the end of the arc passes through you.*

He understood, at the bench at four bells of the middle watch of the eleventh night of the crossing, that Haakon's lesson had been a lesson about a thing. The thing was a physics. The physics had been in his head as words. The physics was now in his body as a picture — the carpenter on the deck, the marlinspike's arc above the carpenter, the place on the carpenter's side where the end of the arc had taken him.

He had, at eighteen, received one half of a body-knowledge. He had received the words from Haakon on the second day. He had now received the picture from Solvaag on the eleventh night. The words and the picture, laid on each other, were a thing his body would — he understood this with the flat clarity the carpenter had been teaching him to have — keep.

"Sir," he said. "I saw the force at the end. I saw the inside of the arc empty."

The carpenter looked at him.

"Good," he said. "That is what you were on the foretop to see. You did not come down in time to stop the strike. You did come down in time to see what the strike was. The seeing is the thing you keep. The not-stopping is the thing you forgive yourself. You were at the foretop. You were fifty feet away. You called. Your call did not carry. That is the arithmetic. The arithmetic did not give you a different action. It gave you the seeing. The seeing is not a thing."

"Sir."

"Hestby."

"Yes, sir."

"You will, in the years of your life, see more marlinspikes. You will see some of them coming at you and some of them coming at other men. The men they come at will not, all of them, survive. You will, in the arithmetic of the seeing and the not-stopping, carry the ones that did not. You will carry me too. You will carry me even though I am, tonight, the one who lived. The seeing is the work. The carrying is the work. You have — at the foretop of the *Sigrid* tonight — done the work."

"Sir."

"Go below. To your hammock. Do not, on the way, stop at the main. Do not look aft. Go."

Olav stood up.

He went below.

At the forecastle hatch he did not — by the discipline the night had been teaching him — stop at the main or look aft. He went down.

He climbed into his hammock.

He lay in the dark.

He listened for Haakon's hammock three down from his. He could not, at first, locate it; the forecastle was a noise of breathing and the wind. Then he heard it. Three down, as always. Haakon was awake.

Haakon was always, when Olav came in from a thing, awake.

Olav did not — because he had been taught by the night and by the carpenter and by Haakon himself not to — make the sound a hammock made when a man asked a question with a hammock.

He lay still.

The brig ran. The wind held. The steady shush of the trade above the deck was the only sound in the forecastle for a long time.

He slept, eventually.

---

The carpenter was at his bench at six bells of the morning watch.

He moved — when he moved, which was slowly — in the careful way a man moved who had a bruised rib on his left side. He did not, at the bench, say anything unusual. He did not look different to a man who had not, the night before, been on the deck at four bells of the middle watch with Solvaag's marlinspike in the air above him. He was the carpenter. He was at his bench. He was planing a wedge.

Solvaag came up at eight bells for the morning watch. He did not, in passing the carpenter's bench, look at the carpenter. The carpenter did not, in his peripheral work-attention, look at Solvaag. The two men of the brig, that morning, did the thing they had, at the end of the middle watch the night before, agreed to do — which was not to do a thing.

The captain came up at eight bells and a quarter. The captain looked at the deck. The captain looked at the rigging. The captain looked at Solvaag. The captain did not look at the carpenter. The captain's smile was on him.

The captain — Olav understood, at the foot of the foremast where he had been sent to coil — knew.

The captain knew because the captain knew what his mate did. The captain did not need the report. The captain had the report built into Solvaag by the three years Solvaag had been on this brig.

The captain, by knowing, had chosen not to know.

The chosen-not-to-know was — Olav understood with the flat clarity the carpenter had taught him the night before — the thing that kept Solvaag at Solvaag's bench aft, and the carpenter at his bench forward, and the captain in the cabin between them. The brig ran on the chosen-not-to-know. The chosen-not-to-know was the weather the captain made.

Olav coiled.

He kept his hands moving. He kept his face doing what his face needed to do. He did not, in the coiling, let himself think the next thought past the one he had just finished.

Later — much later, in a lodging-house in Wilmington in November of 1876, with a deserter's wool coat over his chair and a woman named Mrs. Hovland in the kitchen — he would think the next thought.

The next thought, when he thought it, would be:

*I saw the marlinspike. I saw the arc. I saw the carpenter survive.*

*I did not, at the foretop, stop it.*

*I will not, at some port in some year the future has not yet named, be a man who does not stop something the second time.*

The thought would be the thought.

The thought would be a thing he put in the drawer, because the drawer was where things like it went, and because the thought was not yet — at eighteen, at the first Atlantic crossing, at the foot of the foremast on the morning after the night — a thought he had the shape to live inside.

He coiled.

The brig ran.

The crossing had eight more days.
