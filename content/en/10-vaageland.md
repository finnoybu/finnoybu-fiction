The widow's house held him through the first two weeks of October.

He had come into it on a Friday at midnight, with his sea-chest at his shoulder and Thomas at his side and a name in his mouth that did not yet sit right in his mouth, and he came out of it for good on a Tuesday morning sixteen days later with a coastwise berth in his pocket and the name as broken in as a new pair of boots — broken in but not yet, on the inside, not unfamiliar.

Thomas was, on the step of the widow's house at the Friday midnight, the first man Olav's interior had set apart from the rest of the world without yet having a name for the setting-apart. He had been, in the five months on the brig, the man whose steady voice across the salt-hold and the tarring and the royal yard had become the voice Olav listened for. The setting-apart was not the setting-apart Olav had registered in the lee of the galley in his second week at sea with another man whose name he had been carrying since. The two set-apartings were different. He did not, at eighteen on the step of the widow's house, have the vocabulary to say how. He had only the small fact that he had, by the Friday midnight, two men in the small specific place in him that, in the years to come, he would learn to name a drawer.

In the third week of the widow's house Thomas went out at dawn for the morning milk-errand and did not come back. The not-coming-back was the first thing the small specific place in Olav had ever been asked to keep without being told where the thing had gone. It kept. It kept for the sixty-five years after.

In between those two days — the Friday of arrival and the Tuesday of the first berth — Mrs. Hovland taught him to be Olav Vaageland.

She did not call it that. She did not call it anything. She had been doing this work for sailors who had decided to stop being themselves for a while since long before Olav had been born, and she had developed, over those years, a particular way of going about the teaching that did not announce itself as teaching. She would, at supper, ask the new boy what his mother's name had been; she would, at breakfast, ask whether the village he was naming himself for had a church and what colour it was painted; she would, in the parlour after the kitchen had been cleared, set him down at the long table with a piece of paper and ask him to write the names of his three nearest neighbours from the new village.

The point of all this, Olav understood by the third day, was not the answers. The point was that a man who could not produce the answers had not yet finished becoming the man whose answers they were. The widow asked the questions until the answering of them had stopped being effortful, and then she asked them once more and watched the boy's face for the small break in the look that meant he was still, somewhere, the other boy. When the small break stopped happening she stopped asking. That was the lesson.

Vaageland was a farm on the south side of Finnøy. Olav's mother had been born there in 1830. Her father had farmed it; her brother farmed it now. The farmhouse was white with a roof of red slate. The neighbours to the east were the Egelands. The neighbours to the west were the Lurens. The nearest church was at Hestby. He could not, the widow told him, name Hestby — Hestby was the church on his own actual papers, and a man whose borrowed name listed his actual baptismal church on its parish register would be caught the first time a Norwegian agent compared two pages. They settled, after some thought, on Tau as the Vaageland parish — Tau being a village close enough to the Vaageland farm that a son of it might plausibly have been baptised there, and far enough from Hestby that the two parishes did not, on a Norwegian shipping office's chart, sit side by side.

He learned Tau. He learned the names of three of its families. He learned that the Tau pastor in 1860 had been a man named Sandvik who had died of a chest fever in '63 and been replaced by a man named Lauritzen. He had never met either Sandvik or Lauritzen. He had never been to Tau church. The widow, who had also never been to Tau church, had a small grey notebook full of names and dates of Norwegian parishes that she had been collecting since 1851; she opened it to the Tau page and let him copy it twice into his head and once onto paper and then, with the small flat satisfaction of a woman who had done this many times, she took the paper back and put it in the kitchen stove.

"You will not need it written," she said. "If you need it written you have already lost the name. It must be in your head where the head does not have to look for it."

"Yes, ma'am."

"Olav."

"Yes, ma'am."

"You are not yet Olav Vaageland. You are still Olav Hestby in a borrowed coat. I will know when you have stopped being Olav Hestby in a borrowed coat. Until then, do not ship out. Do you understand."

"Yes, ma'am."

He did not, that week, ship out. He helped her in the kitchen. He carried wood from the back yard. He went, twice, with one of the other lodgers — a Dane in his thirties who had been at the house since June and who, the widow said, was not yet ready to ship out either — to the Wilmington market for fish and bread. He answered her questions at every meal. By the end of the second week he had stopped having to think about the answers. He had stopped, also, having to think about not flinching when the Dane called him *Vaageland* across the kitchen. The flinch had been there on the second day; on the eighth day it was small; on the fifteenth day it was not there at all.

On the morning of the sixteenth day Mrs. Hovland came to him at breakfast.

"Olav."

"Ma'am."

"You are ready."

"Am I."

"You are. Eat. There is a coastwise schooner at the upper wharf called the *Anna Maria* — Boston-Wilmington, freight and passengers, three weeks turnaround. Her master is a man named Jensen out of Bergen who does not ask too many questions and who is short an able seaman because his last one walked off in Boston and did not come back. I have spoken to Jensen. He will sign you on this afternoon under the name on your papers. The wage is poor. The food is what it is. The voyage is to Boston and back, and from Boston you may, if you wish, find a different berth. The first berth is the hard one. You will get through it."

"Yes, ma'am."

"Olav."

"Yes, ma'am."

"Do not, on any ship, tell another man your true name. Not in drink. Not in friendship. Not in any port where there is a Norwegian consulate. The rule is simple. The rule has no exceptions. The men who have come back here after a year and used a different name from the one they left under have all been the men who broke the rule once. Once is enough. Do you understand."

"Yes, ma'am."

"Good. Pack your chest. Jensen lifts at three."

He packed his chest. He paid her what he owed. She gave him a small piece of bread to take in his pocket, and a slip of paper with two names on it — *Mrs. Tjaere, Boston, North Square* and *Mrs. Iversen, Quebec, lower town* — and she said, *if you are ever stuck at either of those ports without a berth and without a friend, those are the houses. Tell them I sent you. They will not ask.*

She did not embrace him at the door. She put one hand briefly on the back of his neck — the place his father had used at the Stavanger quay three months before, the place his mother had used in the kitchen at Finnøy when he was eleven and his brother had died, the place that was, he understood for the first time as the widow's hand left it, the universal place a Norwegian gave a young man what could not be said — and she said, *go.*

He went.

---

The *Anna Maria* lifted at three on the afternoon tide.

She was a schooner of perhaps two hundred tons, two-masted, sloop-rigged forward, with a stained sail-plan and a captain who, as the widow had said, did not ask many questions and a crew of six who had been at sea long enough to know the difference between asking a question and noticing the absence of one. The mate, a man called Alm, took Olav's papers, looked at them, looked at Olav's face, looked back at the papers, and put them in the small deck-house drawer where the captain kept the papers of the watch. Alm did not, in any of the looking, say anything. The look had been the asking. The papers had been the answer.

Olav was Olav Vaageland for the next eleven months.

He was Vaageland on the *Anna Maria* for three coastwise runs between Wilmington and Boston, working freight — cotton north, dry goods south, lumber north, salt fish south. He was Vaageland on a Boston packet called the *Champion* through the winter of 1876 and into the spring of 1877, running between Boston and Halifax with passengers and the mail. He was Vaageland on a Quebec timber-bark called the *Augusta* in the summer of 1877, working a single voyage south to Boston with a cargo of dressed pine and back to Quebec with a cargo of barrels and tools.

In none of these berths did he tell another man his name.

He told the men he was from Vaageland on Finnøy. He told them his mother had been born there. He told them his father was a carpenter at Tau. He told them — when asked, which was rare — that the parish church of Tau was painted yellow and stood on a low rise above the road. The Tau church was, in fact, painted yellow; he had this on the widow's grey notebook. He had never seen it. He had never been to Tau. He could, by the end of the eleven months, talk about a village he had never set foot in for a quarter of an hour at a stretch without breaking a rhythm.

The cost of this was not large. The cost was that he could not, in any port, write home. He could not write to his mother to tell her he was alive. He could not write to his father to tell him he had not, in spite of three near-deaths in three months on a brig under a captain whose smile was the kind that ate men, drowned. He could not write to anyone whose name was on the rolls of any Norwegian parish church, because to write to such a person was to put his own true name in a postal record that any Norwegian consul in any of the ports he was about to be in could pull, six months later, when the *Sigrid*'s captain — Gjermund of the smile, who would by now have made his report — pulled the consul's hand to the page and named Olav Hestby of Finnøy, a deserter from the brig *Sigrid* at Wilmington, North Carolina, in the year 1876.

He did not write home. The not-writing was, by January, no longer a daily ache; by April it was a weekly one; by July it was the kind of standing absence a man learns to hold his weight against without noticing he is holding.

He thought, often, of Haakon. He thought of Haakon at the rate his body wanted to think of Haakon, which was perhaps once a day on the calmer voyages and not at all on the harder ones. He thought of him without permitting the thinking to turn into anything. He had Haakon's drawer, his own drawer, the drawer the widow had told him to make and in which he had put the boy he had been at Stavanger. The drawer was, by the spring of 1877, the largest piece of furniture in his interior.

He thought, sometimes, about whether Haakon had counted hammocks the morning he was gone. He decided the answer was yes. He decided this not because he had any way of knowing it but because the deciding was, on certain nights, a thing he needed to have decided.

He never permitted the deciding to be more than that.

The cough came on him in March of 1877, on the *Champion*, in a Halifax winter that had hardened into a kind of cold he had not understood was possible.

It came at first as a winter cold of the ordinary kind. He had had two of these on the *Anna Maria* in the autumn and had recovered from them in three or four days each, the way a young man with a body that did not yet know what could be done to it recovers. This one did not recover.

By the second week he had developed, at the bottom of each breath, a small wet thickening that he did not, on the second week, allow himself to name. He had heard the thickening once before — in the chest of his older brother, Per, in the loft of the Hestby house in the November of his eleventh year, in the three weeks before Per had stopped being a boy he could speak to and become a thing the family was waiting beside.

He had been eleven. He remembered the sound exactly.

He did not, on the *Champion* in Halifax in March of 1877, allow himself to call the sound by its name. He called it a winter cold. He called it the dampness of the forecastle. He called it the smoke from the stove. He worked through it. He had nowhere to go.

By the fourth week the captain of the *Champion* — a man called Snyder out of Halifax, who did not employ men he had to feed when they could not work — had taken him aside in the deck-house and said, *Vaageland, you are coughing harder than I would like. I will pay you off at the next port. You will find another berth.* Olav had nodded. He had not protested. He had understood, with the small flat clarity of a man who has been told a thing he had already privately decided, that the *Champion* was the last berth he was going to have under his own willingness.

He paid off at Boston in the second week of April. He stayed five days at Mrs. Tjaere's house in the North Square — the widow's note had been good; Mrs. Tjaere had taken him in without questions and had given him hot broth and bread for the five days at no charge after the second day — and he found a berth, on the fifth day, on the Quebec-bound brig *Augusta,* northward through the spring ice.

The cough went underground for the spring. The new air, the long days, the cleanness of the Quebec voyage — these helped. By June he had told himself, half-believing it, that the cold of Halifax had been the whole of the thing, and that he was on the other side of it.

---

In September of 1877 the *Augusta,* which had taken her timber to Boston in July and had returned to Quebec for a second cargo of dressed pine in August, was offered a charter to Liverpool. The owners — a Quebec firm — wanted the cargo at Liverpool by the end of October. The *Augusta*'s captain, a man called Berger, took the charter and began signing on the larger crew an Atlantic voyage required.

Olav considered the offer for two days.

To go to Liverpool was to go, for the first time in eleven months, within reach of a Norwegian shipping office. To sign on at Liverpool, after the *Augusta* paid off, for a Norwegian-bound coaster — to come home — was a thing he could, for the first time in eleven months, contemplate. The cough had not, on the spring and summer voyages, returned at the wet weight he had heard in March, but it had not gone either. It was a small intermittent presence in his chest at the end of long days. He understood, by the second day of considering, that he was going to come home, and that the coming home was a thing the body had, without consulting him, already decided.

He signed on for Liverpool.

The Atlantic crossing took thirty-one days. The cough was steadier on the crossing than it had been at Halifax — the *Augusta* was a drier ship than the *Champion,* and the wind was fair, and the captain Berger let his men sleep what they needed — but it was, by the time the bark made the Mersey on the fifteenth of October, at the bottom of every breath again. The wet thickness was small. It was not gone. He coughed once, into his sleeve, on the deck of the *Augusta* as she warped to her berth at the Liverpool dock, and he looked, briefly, at his sleeve, and he saw what he had hoped not to see; and then he folded the sleeve under and went forward to the work of paying off.

He was paid off at Liverpool on the seventeenth.

He stayed three nights at a Norwegian sailors' boarding house off the Goree Piazzas, run by a woman from Bergen who asked few questions and accepted his story about a mishandled set of papers without raising her eyebrows. On the second day he found a Norwegian timber-bark, the *Magnhild,* bound for Stavanger with a load of cured fish and a recommendation from the boarding-house woman that the captain — a man named Thorstad who had, in his youth, been a hand on the *Aase* under Olav's father's onetime master — was the sort of man who would take a young Norwegian back to Norway under his own true name without asking how it had come to be different from the name on a recent set of articles.

Thorstad took him.

The articles were signed on the eighteenth in Olav's true name. The hand that wrote *Olav Hestby of Finnøy* on the line was a hand that had not written the name on a set of articles in eleven months and three weeks. The writing of the name took longer than it should have. Thorstad watched. Thorstad did not say anything. Thorstad had, in his youth, walked off a Newcastle bark and back home under a borrowed name, and he had recognized the small hesitation of the pen that means a man is, at last, the man on the page again.

"Welcome aboard the *Magnhild,* Mr. Hestby."

"Thank you, sir."

"Stow your chest. The forecastle is below. We lift on Friday."

Olav stowed his chest. He went down into the forecastle. He stood for a moment in the warm dark of it — the same warm dark of every forecastle he had ever stood in — and he understood that he was a man on a Norwegian ship under his own name for the first time since the morning he had gone over the lee rail of the *Sigrid* at Wilmington, North Carolina, sixteen months before, and that the man on this Norwegian ship had a small wet thickness at the bottom of every breath that he was bringing home to his mother.

He coughed once, into his sleeve, in the warm dark.

He folded the sleeve under.

He climbed into a hammock that was not yet the hammock he had been assigned, and he lay there for a quarter of an hour with his hands folded across his chest, and he listened to the *Magnhild* shifting against her fenders at the Liverpool dock; and he thought, for the first time in eleven months, the sentence he had been not-thinking since the boarding house at Wilmington:

*I am Olav Hestby of Finnøy.*

He thought it once. He thought it twice. He thought it a third time, slowly, the way a man tries on a thing that does not yet sit right but that he intends, in time, to wear.

The drawer in him was where it had always been. It had not, in eleven months, gotten smaller. Vaageland was in it now too, folded once and put on top of the rest, the way the widow had folded a slip of paper of names and put it in the kitchen stove on the morning of the sixteenth day.

He closed his eyes.

He did not sleep.

He waited for the bell that, on the *Magnhild* on a Friday three days hence, would ring him out into a wind blowing east toward Norway.
