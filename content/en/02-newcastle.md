They came up the Tyne on a Thursday forenoon with a tug at the bow and the city showing itself by slow degrees through the coal-smoke.

The *Sigrid* had sailed from Stavanger to Copenhagen, lain three weeks discharging timber, crossed the Skaw with a northeast wind, and now had come for a return cargo of Newcastle coal. Olav was four months at sea. He had lost half a stone and gained the use of his hands. He had learned to sleep in the top bunk through the full range of the ship's motion, and to wake a minute before the watch-bell, and to make coffee in a swell without spilling more than a cup on the stove. He had learned also that the sea is not one thing but many — the short choppy water of the Kattegat that tires a boy in an hour, the long rolling swell of the open North that rocks him like a cradle, the short steep chop that gets up near a coast when the tide runs against a wind. He had come to prefer the long swell above the others, because in it a man had time to think between blows.

The tug brought them up through the narrow of the river between banks of shipyards and coal-staithes and chimneys. There were more chimneys than Olav had known were in the world. They stood in rows a hundred deep on both banks, and each of them gave off a smoke of its own color, brown and grey and blue and in one place yellow, and above the whole city the sky was the color of an old copper pot. Thorvald came and stood at the rail beside Olav and looked up at the sky and said, *It is a very English sky; you will get used to it.*

"My father was here once," Olav said.

"Most Norwegian sailors have been here once."

"He hurt his knee here."

"Some Norwegian sailors have done that, too." Thorvald did not look at him. "He was a sailor then."

"Yes."

"He did not become one after."

"No."

"Well then. Do not hurt your knee, Finnøybu."

The *Sigrid* was warped in at a wharf below a great black tip, where the coal would be loaded from above through a chute, and for the rest of that Thursday and all of Friday the crew worked at the trimming, which was the stowing of the coal into the hold in the manner that the ship would ride best with. The trimmers were local men in leather aprons, black from face to boot, and they came and went as the chute ran and the chute stopped, and they did not speak to the crew except in orders, and the orders were in a Tyneside English so thick that Olav could not understand them and Thorvald had to translate the first day. By Saturday noon the cargo was in and the hatches were battened, and the captain came forward to the forecastle with a handful of silver and gave each of the crew a full afternoon and evening ashore, to be back aboard by midnight.

"The Finnøybu is not to drink," Captain Dahl said.

"I do not drink, sir."

"He is not to drink and he is not to fight and he is not to find himself in any corner of the town he has not walked into on his own two legs and in his own sober mind."

"I will be careful."

"You will also not be alone. Thorvald goes with you as far as the yard gate. After that you may go where you please for three hours, and you will meet him at the yard gate at six by the great clock, and you will come back to the ship with him. Is this understood?"

"It is understood."

Thorvald walked him up the length of the wharf and through the yard and out by a small side-gate into a street of low brick houses, and stopped at the corner under a lamp that was not yet lit. The lamp-post had been painted a green that was almost black with soot. Thorvald took his pipe out of his pocket and did not light it.

"You have your father's business to do, I think."

"I did not tell you."

"You did. You told me yesterday at the rail. You did not know you were telling me." Thorvald tapped the stem of the pipe against his palm. "The yard you want is two streets down that way, and then one street over. It is called Hebburn. Walk carefully. There are carts."

"How do you know which yard?"

"Because there is only one your father would have been sent to from a Stavanger ship in eighteen fifty-six. I sailed out of this river for eleven years, Finnøybu. There are things one learns."

"Thank you."

"Meet me at the gate at six."

Olav walked in the direction Thorvald had pointed. The streets were wider than he had expected and the people in them were more numerous. Men in dark coats, men in caps, women in aprons standing at doors, children running in packs between the legs of horses. The carts were what Thorvald had warned him of. They came at speed and without warning from the cross-streets, and the drivers did not shout when they turned a corner. By the end of the first street he had understood that to stand still in this city was to be struck down, and he walked with his shoulders set and his eyes ahead. He turned where he had been told to turn. The second street gave onto the river again at a lower bend, and there across a stretch of open dock-ground he saw a yard with its gates wide open and a sign above the gate that said, in iron letters, HEBBURN & COMPANY.

He stood at the gate a long time before he went in.

The yard was not as large as he had thought it would be. It was a place of scaffolding and dry-docks and heaps of oakum, and a smell of hot tar and iron filings rose off the ground as if the ground itself had been at work. A gang of men were hauling a line of something heavy across the yard and singing a song with the pulling. The song was not a Norwegian song but it had the same shape as a Norwegian song, the shape of work done to a rhythm. A foreman in a bowler hat saw Olav at the gate and came over with the walk of a man who had decided to be patient about an interruption.

"You're lost, lad."

"I am not lost."

"Then you're looking for a man."

"I am looking for a place."

The foreman put his hands on his hips and looked Olav up and down and saw, Olav supposed, the cap and the jacket and the hands and the new-sailor's face, and something in the looking softened without its being in the man's power to soften.

"What place."

"The place where they put the planking on the beams for the big boats. I was told a Norwegian sailor was hurt there once. Nearly twenty years ago."

"He was killed?"

"No. He was sent home. He is my father."

The foreman looked past Olav at the dock-ground for a moment, and Olav knew him well enough, already, by the shape of the look, to keep his mouth shut for what was coming.

"Nineteen years ago a man named Abel Rutherford dropped a plank off the second staging of slip three. The plank took a Norwegian in the knee. I was sixteen years old that year and I fetched the water for the ones as carried him off. Come in. I will show you slip three."

They walked across the yard between the heaps of oakum. The slip was empty, with only a keel laid on the blocks and a scaffolding halfway up one side. The foreman pointed with his chin.

"He was about there. He walked after, I'm told."

"He walks. He uses a cane."

"Aye. Well."

Olav said nothing. He looked at the place. It was an unremarkable patch of ground with a ladder leaning on it and a heap of shavings to one side. He thought: my father was a man here, in this place, for some hours on one day, and he has walked the rest of his life because of those hours. He thought also: I could die here this afternoon in the same kind of hour, if a plank fell. He looked up at the scaffolding. No plank was going to fall. The scaffolding was empty on that side.

"Where are you from, lad?"

"An island called Finnøy. Off the west coast of Norway."

"How long at sea?"

"Four months."

"First voyage?"

"First voyage."

"Well." The foreman put his hand on Olav's shoulder, just once, and took it off again. "Tell him when you go home that the yard remembers. There's not many of us old enough for it now. But it remembers."

"I will tell him."

They walked back to the gate. The foreman pointed up the street toward a coffee-stand where Olav might sit for an hour without spending more than a penny, and Olav thanked him and went to the coffee-stand and sat on a box and drank a cup of the hot bitter coffee that Englishmen made, and looked at the light over the river. The light was not like the light over Stavangerfjord. The river was narrower, the banks were higher, the sky was closer to the chimneys, and the light had to find its way through all of it before it reached the water. And yet the water was water. It was water as the water at home was water, black where the current ran under a pier, brown where it turned on a mud-bar, silver in the one place where the sun had found a gap. He watched it for an hour. He thought of his father. He thought of Peder. He thought of nothing for a long stretch of minutes, which was something he had not yet learned to do on the ship, where there was always a bell coming, but which he learned on a box at a coffee-stand on the Tyne on a Saturday in late autumn of his eighteenth year.

He met Thorvald at the gate at six by the great clock on the yard wall.

"You found it."

"I found it."

"Good."

They walked back to the ship in the lamplight, which was yellow and steady and low enough that Olav could see each stone of the street as he put his foot on it. Thorvald did not ask him what he had seen. He said, after a while, *My father was a cooper at Ålborg. I saw the shop the last time I was in Denmark. The shop was a laundry now. There was a woman hanging sheets where he had used to hoop his barrels. She was a good-looking woman. I did not go in.*

"You did not go in."

"I thought about it. I thought it would be the same shop whether I went in or not."

"It would be."

"Yes."

They came to the ship. The ship was showing only a riding-light and a single lamp in the stern, and the crew were mostly already aboard, and Olav climbed the plank after Thorvald and went below and wrote in the small ledger his father had given him, in the back of the small Bible, the line *I was today at slip three of Hebburn & Company,* and the date, and nothing else. Then he shut the Bible. He went forward to the galley and saw that the cook who had been aboard for the trip and had looked at him with some dislike had left a pot of water simmering for the men's return, and he put more coffee in the grinder and ground it, and he had the pot ready when the first of them came down through the scuttle with his breath smelling of stout and his eyes shining, and the man thanked him and took his cup. He gave the coffee out to each of them as they came in. The last one was Henrik, who had drunk enough to be quiet rather than loud, and who took his cup without looking at Olav and went to his bunk.

Olav cleaned the galley. He turned down the stove. He went up on deck once before bed to see if the tide had turned, because he had learned to feel it through the hull and he wanted to check whether he was right.

The tide had turned.

He went below and climbed to his bunk and lay on his back in the dark and did not sleep for a long time, and then he slept.
