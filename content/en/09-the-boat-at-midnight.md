title: "The Boat at Midnight"

They went on Thursday at midnight.

This was the day Thomas had picked. The brig was due to finish loading Friday and to drop down the river on Saturday's tide. Thursday was the last night. Thursday was also the night Solvaag took his shore leave; the deck-watch at midnight would be the youngest man on board, the new boy from Bergen who had joined at Stavanger and who had not yet learned that he was supposed to do anything other than stand at the gangway and drink water from a tin cup. Thomas had calculated all of this. Thomas had calculated everything.

Olav had calculated nothing, because he had nothing to calculate. He had only to do what Thomas told him to do, when Thomas told him to do it, and to be ready.

He had been ready since Sunday. He had spent Monday and Tuesday and Wednesday tarring rigging with hands that were not quite his hands anymore — he could see them at the ends of his arms, doing the work, but they had begun to feel borrowed. He had been Olav Vaageland in his head, in private, since Sunday afternoon. He was practicing.

On Thursday afternoon he came down from the foretop for the last time. He set the tar-pail at the foot of the foremast. The brushes in it would dry stiff overnight; that was someone else's problem now. He stood for a moment in the shadow of the mast and looked at the brig the way he had not let himself look at her since Stavanger.

She was a brig. She was a thing of rope and pine and iron and pitch. She had a captain who had tried to kill him three times, by his count; she had a mate who had thrown a marlinspike at him; she had a carpenter who had given him a sentence to repeat aloft, and a steward who had served him gruel for two months without judging him. She had a boy named Mathis with a bell-clear voice. She had a boatswain from Hogganvik named Haakon Berg, who was twenty-five and missing the nail on his left thumb and who had once, in the lee of the galley, said *that is all it is.*

She was a brig. He had loved her the way a man loves a thing that has tried to teach him what he is.

He turned his back on her and went forward.

---

In the forecastle that evening Thomas passed him, brushed his shoulder once, did not speak. The signal was set. They would meet at the gangway at four bells of the first watch, ten o'clock, both already in their best wool, their bundles already by the gangway under a coil of rope where the deck-watch would not look. From the gangway it was a hundred yards to the small steamer below them on the next wharf, and the steamer's Swedish steward — a man named Bergström, whom Thomas had visited twice on Wednesday and once on Thursday morning — would be at the steamer's stern with a small boat and a length of muffled oar.

Olav lay in his hammock from supper until ten o'clock without moving. He did not sleep. He listened. He listened to the forecastle settle around him: Mathis already breathing the deep slow breath of a boy who had worked hard since dawn; the carpenter, two berths over, snoring once and stopping as he always did and snoring once more; Tolvsen the steward in his bunk forward, silent. Three hammocks down, Haakon, also silent. Olav could not tell if Haakon was asleep. He had the impression — he had had the impression all evening — that Haakon was awake, lying on his back with his hands folded on his chest, listening to the same things Olav was listening to.

He put it away. He did not have time for it.

At three bells of the watch — half past nine — he sat up in the hammock, pulled his bundle out from under his pillow, and slipped down to the deck of the forecastle. He did not look at Haakon's hammock. He did not look at any of the hammocks. He went up the ladder.

On deck the Wilmington night was warm and damp. The river smelled of pitch and pine-tar and a sweet vegetable rot from the marshlands across. The watch — a boy of perhaps sixteen with a loose belt and a dropped lower lip — was at the gangway with a tin cup, exactly as Thomas had predicted. He looked up when Olav came on deck.

"What."

"Air," Olav said. "I am going to be sick if I do not have it."

The boy nodded. He did not care. He went back to his cup.

Olav went forward, past the foremast, to the lee of the deckhouse. He found his bundle under the coil of rope. He crouched in the shadow and waited.

Two bells later — half an hour — Thomas came up from below. He did the same thing. He passed the watch with a story about needing to relieve himself over the side. The watch did not look up. Thomas joined Olav at the deckhouse.

"Now," Thomas said, very quietly.

They went over the lee rail.

The wharf was perhaps ten feet below the brig's main deck. There was a Jacob's ladder rigged for the loading; it had not been brought in since the day's work. They went down it one at a time. Their boots made no sound on the rope. At the bottom they did not run. Running drew attention. They walked down the wharf, two men with bundles, the way two men with bundles walked at any hour of any day in the lower wharves of any port city in the world. Nobody saw them. Nobody, more accurately, looked at them.

The steamer was at the next wharf, a small coast-runner of perhaps two hundred tons, painted black and white. Bergström was at the stern. He was a thin man of forty, sand-colored, with a face that had decided long ago not to be surprised by anything. He had a small dinghy alongside, oars already shipped, oar-locks muffled with strips of canvas. He held out his hand to Thomas without speaking.

Thomas put a small leather purse into his hand.

Bergström weighed the purse, briefly. He put it in his jacket. He nodded.

They went down into the dinghy. Bergström cast off the painter for them and gave the dinghy a small push.

"Up the river half a mile," Bergström said, in Swedish-accented Norwegian. "On the larboard side. There is a small landing under a magnolia tree. You will see the magnolia by its smell. The widow's house is the second up the lane. White door."

"Thank you," Thomas said.

"It was a good price," Bergström said, and stepped back into the shadow of his steamer's stern, and was gone.

---

Olav rowed bow. Thomas rowed stroke. The oars went into the water with the small soft sounds of muffled wood; there was no creak. The Cape Fear River flowed north here, away from the sea, with the tide; they pulled against the current but it was an easy current at the slack. The night was very dark. There was no moon. There were stars; Olav, who had been navigating by them for a month, knew the stars were there without looking up.

They did not speak.

Halfway up the half-mile Olav let his eyes go back, once, over his shoulder, to the brig.

She was a darker shape against the dark water. Her masts made a sketch against the stars. Olav thought: *I climbed that royal yard a week ago. I came down off it because Haakon told me to come down.*

He thought: *Haakon is in his hammock. He does not yet know.*

He thought: *Haakon will know at six bells of the morning watch, when I do not turn out for breakfast. He will know exactly. He will not say anything to anyone.*

He thought: *that is the kindness we are giving him.*

He thought: *that is also what we are taking from him.*

He turned his face back to the bow and went on rowing.

---

The magnolia tree was where Bergström had said it would be. Olav smelled it before he saw it — a thick sweet white smell that did not belong to anything he knew. He took the dinghy in to the small landing. They tied the painter to a piling. They climbed the bank and walked up a narrow lane between two unpainted board houses to the second house, which had a white door.

Thomas knocked. Three knocks, then two, then one. A long pause. Then the door opened a crack.

A woman of perhaps fifty was behind it. She was small and broad, with grey hair under a kerchief and eyes that were neither kind nor unkind. She looked at Thomas. She looked at Olav. She looked back at Thomas.

"Engelstad."

"Mrs. Hovland."

She opened the door.

"In."

They went in. She closed the door behind them and put a heavy iron bolt across it. The hallway was dim; one tin lamp burned on a side table. A long-case clock in the corner had the slow steady tick of a clock that had been keeping time for a hundred years and meant to keep it for a hundred more.

"Two beds," she said. "Top of the stairs. Right-hand door. There is bread and cheese on the kitchen table for tonight. Tomorrow we talk about the next ship. You will not go out in the lane after dark for any reason. You will not give your names to anyone but me. Is this understood."

"Yes," Thomas said.

She looked at Olav.

"Boy."

"Yes."

"You are very young."

"Eighteen."

"Eighteen." She looked at him for a long moment. "I will not ask why you are running. I do not ask. But I will tell you something for free. Wherever you go after this, you will think about what you ran from for the rest of your life. You will not stop thinking about it. The trick is to make yourself the kind of man who can think about a thing without it killing him. Some men learn to. Some do not. Which kind do you mean to be."

Olav considered this. He had not, until this moment, considered it. He had been busy not dying.

"The kind that can think about it," he said.

She nodded once, briefly, with the small satisfied air of a woman who had heard the right answer to a question she had asked many times.

"Good. Up you go."

They went up the stairs.

---

The room had two narrow beds, one window, a chest of drawers, and a small basin on a stand. The window faced the river. From the bed nearer the window — the one Olav, by silent agreement, took — he could see, between two trees and over a low roof, the masts of the brig. They were perhaps half a mile away. They were a small dark line against the slightly less dark sky. His brig. Their brig. The brig that was no longer his.

He took off his boots. He took off his shirt. He lay down on the bed in his trousers. The bed was straw and was wider than his hammock and was not moving with the river, and he was, he realized, more uncomfortable in it than he had been in his hammock for weeks.

Thomas, in the other bed, said nothing. After a moment he turned to the wall.

Olav lay on his back and looked at the ceiling.

He thought about Haakon, three hammocks down on the brig, who was — at this exact moment — perhaps still awake, perhaps just gone to sleep, perhaps lying with his hands on his chest in the same position Olav had spent the last three hours in.

He thought: *In six hours he will hear the bell. He will turn out. He will go to the rail to wash. He will be the third man at the table for breakfast. He will count, without meaning to. He will count three, four, five, and there will be no sixth, because the sixth is me. And he will know.*

He thought: *He will not say anything. He will go aft to his work. He will not look at the captain. He will not tell Solvaag. The captain will find out from the watch. The captain will rage. Haakon will be at the windlass with his back turned, and the carpenter will be at his bench with the door closed, and they will both know, both of them, both in the same way: a man left in the night who did not say goodbye.*

He thought: *Haakon will know that I did not say goodbye.*

He thought: *That is what I have done.*

He turned his face into the pillow. The pillow smelled of straw and lye and the long faint sweetness of an unfamiliar house. He pressed his face into it. He did not weep. What came was not weeping; what came was the cold trembling that had come in the salt-hold and at the foot of the foremast after the royal yard. He let it come. He let it pass through him. He let it pass.

When it was gone he turned onto his back again and opened his eyes.

The brig's masts were still in the window. They were still a line. They were the small last thing he could see of the man he had been when he had walked down the Stavanger quay with his sea-chest on his shoulder, two months and three near-deaths and one farmer-boatswain ago.

He looked at the masts for a long time.

He fell asleep.

When he woke at dawn, the masts were still there.

He looked at them.
