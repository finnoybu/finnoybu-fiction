The *Anna Maria* came up to her wharf at Wilmington on the twenty-third day of December 1876, in the evening, on a tide that ran slow and cold against the mud. She had been at sea longer than Olav's first run had taken — a gale off Hatteras had driven them south under bare poles for thirty hours, and the schooner had been tired by the time she came up river. The hands were tired. The captain, Jensen of Bergen, was at the wheel at the tying-up, and when the last hawser was fast he stood for a long moment without saying anything. Then he turned to the six of them at the rail and said:

"We will not lift again before the twenty-eighth. The wharf will hold us. I will be at the boarding house on Water Street through the week. If any of you wish to leave the ship, you will leave her by seven tomorrow morning. If any of you wish to stay aboard, you will eat from the galley as usual and will have the run of the ship but not the shore. Those are the terms. Decide by dawn."

He went below.

Olav had decided on the second day of the run, with the wet thickness at the bottom of his breathing that the gale had brought back, that if the *Anna Maria* held at Wilmington any time at all across the Christmas he would go to Mrs. Hovland's. He had been, across the ten weeks since October, putting the widow's house away inside him as a thing he did not permit himself to think about, because thinking about it would be to miss it; he permitted it tonight.

He went down to the forecastle and rolled his bundle. The bundle was small: one spare shirt, a razor, a gray wool sweater his mother had knitted for him that he had been afraid to put on at sea for fear of wearing it out. He put the sweater on. He put his coat over it.

He did not leave the ship until morning. To walk the Wilmington streets after dark, even at Christmas, was a thing Mrs. Hovland had told him not to do. He slept in the forecastle, in his hammock, with the bundle at his feet. He did not sleep well. Very far up the river a church bell struck eleven, and then twelve.

At seven in the morning he went ashore.

---

The way to the widow's house was the way he had been walking on paper for ten weeks. He had not walked it in body. He walked it now.

The streets of Wilmington on Christmas Eve morning were emptier than he had seen them at any time in his earlier stay — the warehouses shut, the black workers on the piers standing in small groups around coal-fires, the air cold and close with woodsmoke. He went by the route Thomas had taught him in October: down Water Street to the big warehouse, west two streets, south one, then north two lanes past the fish-market. The consulate building on Water Street was dark behind its windows. He did not look at the gold-painted letters on the second-floor glass. He walked around the building by the outer street as Thomas had told him to walk a dog around a kennel it had been beaten in.

At the magnolia landing he did not turn down to the river but continued up the lane. The magnolia tree was bare. The air did not hold its sweet white smell at December; the smell of the house he was walking to was woodsmoke and, faintly, salt cod boiling somewhere.

He came to the second house up the lane with the white door.

He knocked. Three knocks, then two, then one. The long pause. The door opened a crack.

Mrs. Hovland was behind it. She was smaller than Olav had remembered. Two and a half months was long enough, he understood in the moment of the opening, for the memory of a woman's size to drift. She had a gray apron over a dark wool dress. She looked at his face. Her face did not move.

"Vaageland."

"Mrs. Hovland."

"In."

He went in.

The hallway was as it had been. The long-case clock in the corner was on the same slow steady tick. One tin lamp burned on the side table. The smell of the house — lye, woodsmoke, old wool — was the smell he had put away in October and had, across ten weeks at sea, been waiting to smell again without knowing he had been waiting.

She closed the door behind him. She put the iron bolt across it. Then she looked at him again. Her face moved, this time: she looked him up and down, from his boots to his face, and she nodded once, briefly.

"You have eaten."

"I had a biscuit at the ship."

"That is not eating. Engelstad is in the kitchen. Go. I will be along."

He went to the kitchen.

---

Thomas was at the long table with a pot of coffee and a small loaf of dark bread and a slab of cheese. He was in shirtsleeves — the widow's kitchen was warm from the stove — and he was reading a newspaper the widow must have given him, a three-days-old Wilmington paper folded in half. He looked up when the kitchen door opened. He did not look surprised. He had been, perhaps, waiting.

He set the paper down.

"Hestby," he said.

The name — Olav's true name — did not belong in the widow's kitchen. The widow had been teaching him out of it for sixteen days in October. The widow, Olav understood, was still in the hallway; she had let him come through to the kitchen alone so that Thomas and he could be the only two people in any room anywhere at which Thomas could call him Hestby. The widow had, with the small calculation of a woman who had been doing this work since 1851, given them one minute.

"Engelstad."

"Sit down."

Olav sat.

Thomas poured him coffee in the widow's second mug. The coffee was weak, the way the widow's coffee had been weak in October; it was not the coffee the *Anna Maria*'s cook made, which was stronger than a man needed. He wrapped his hands around the mug. His hands, which had been cold since dawn, were glad.

"Gale off Hatteras," Thomas said.

"You heard."

"Two sailors at the wharf this morning. They came for the papers. They said the *Anna Maria* had come in last night. The *Anna Maria* in the papers meant you."

"Yes."

"Eat."

Thomas pushed the bread and cheese across. Olav ate.

Thomas did not speak while he ate. Thomas watched him. Thomas's wry quiet smile was at the corner of his mouth — the survival smile, not the kind smile — and it was aimed at nothing in particular. It was the smile Thomas wore at the widow's kitchen when he was not performing anything for anyone but himself.

After some time Thomas said:

"You have not said his name."

"No."

"You think about him."

"Yes."

"Do not say his name here either. The kitchen is the kitchen. The kitchen is for eating. The thinking you do for yourself."

"Yes."

Thomas pushed the coffee pot across the table and refilled Olav's mug.

"I have a thing to tell you," Thomas said. "Eat first."

Olav ate.

---

After the meal Thomas put on his coat and suggested a walk. Mrs. Hovland, in the hallway, did not object. She gave Olav a piece of bread to take in his pocket and said, at the door, *back by four,* without specifying why back by four. Olav understood *why back by four* was between the widow and Thomas, and that the question was not his to ask.

They went out into the Christmas Eve morning.

The day had warmed a little. The sky was the pale cold sky of a North Carolina December. They walked down the lane toward the river. At the magnolia-landing Thomas turned not upstream toward the consulate but downstream along a narrow path Olav had not walked in October. The path followed the bank. It went through a grove of cypresses whose gray beards hung low above the water, and out the other side to a small sandy place where a single piling stood in the water with no wharf attached, as though a wharf had been there once and had been taken away.

They sat on the piling's stump at the landward end.

Thomas took off his cap. He held it in his hands. He did not look at Olav.

"I have been thinking," he said, "about what to tell you at Christmas."

"Yes."

"I have decided to tell you a thing that does not have a conclusion. I am telling it to you so that you have it, for whenever you find yourself using it. I have nothing particular to teach you today. I am passing a thing along."

"Yes."

"There are three shapes," Thomas said, "that a man leaves a room in. One is the shape he chooses. He puts on his coat and he says *I am going,* and he is gone, and the room knows where he is. That is the first shape. The second is the shape he is chosen for. He is sent. He is given a paper. He is pressed. He is paid off. He is told to leave, and he leaves because he has been told, and the room knows where he is going because the room was the one doing the telling. That is the second shape. The third shape is the one he does not name. He does not choose the leaving and he is not sent. Something else moves him. He is, in some way the shape does not describe, gone. The room does not know. He does not know. He is gone."

Olav listened.

"Why are you telling me this."

"Because," Thomas said, "if you are ever told that a man has left a room, and you do not know which of the three shapes it was, you will have to carry the not-knowing. The not-knowing is bearable. I have learned it from Mrs. Hovland, who has been learning it for twenty-five years, and she has told me — I now tell you — that the not-knowing is the kind of weight a man gets used to, the way a sailor gets used to the weight of a wet coat. It is not light. It is not unbearable. It is a weight."

"Yes."

"I am not telling you to prepare you for a particular thing. I am telling you in the way I would teach a boy a piece of rigging he does not yet need to know. I am telling you because the teaching is the gift."

"Yes," Olav said.

He did not know, at the piling on the Cape Fear bank on Christmas Eve 1876, why Thomas had chosen this teaching for today. He understood, dimly, that Thomas was not speaking of himself. Thomas, sitting beside him, had his coat on and his cap in his hands and was a man who had decided to leave and had been, since October, waiting for the berth that would make the leaving possible. The berth had not come. Thomas had been, Olav saw now, waiting not for the berth but for something else that Thomas could not name.

---

They walked back to the widow's along the same path.

At the corner where the cypresses began — a narrow place where the path turned sharply between two roots of the larger tree, and where a man coming through had to step carefully over the higher root — Thomas stopped.

He put his hand on Olav's shoulder.

It was, Olav understood at the time and registered with the small flat clarity the brig had taught him, a practical hand. Thomas was warning him about the root. Thomas was, for one second, the man who had been watching out for Olav since Lisbon.

It was also, Olav understood a minute later when the path had opened again and Thomas's hand was back at his own side, not only a practical hand. It had been a practical hand and the other thing at once. The other thing had no name. The other thing was the presence Olav had been, across the past five months on the brig and the sixteen days at the widow's and the ten weeks on the *Anna Maria,* putting in the drawer in him that the widow and the brig had between them taught him to build.

The hand had been on the shoulder for perhaps three seconds.

The hand had come off the shoulder.

The path had opened.

They had walked on.

Olav had not flinched.

He had not flinched because there had not, in the three seconds, been a thing to flinch from. There had been only a hand on a shoulder at a difficult piece of path, placed there by a man who was, at twenty-three, a man who knew when to place a hand and when not to. The hand had been the right hand. The shoulder had been the right shoulder. The not-flinching had been, Olav understood as they walked the rest of the way back to the widow's, the right not-flinching.

He was eighteen. He did not know yet what he knew. He knew only that he had registered the hand, and that the hand was now in the drawer with the rest of Thomas.

---

The supper that night was the widow, Thomas, the Dane (who was still there), and Olav. The widow had made rømmegrøt with sour cream she had kept back since October and had put out salt cod softened in milk and a piece of smoked ham a parishioner of the Wilmington Lutheran church had given her in November. She said the small Christmas grace her mother had said in Kristiania in the 1840s. The Dane said *amen.* Olav said *amen* a second too late.

After supper the widow cleared. Olav went up to the room.

The room was the room. The two narrow beds, one window, the chest of drawers, the small basin. The window still faced the river. Through it, between two trees and over the low roof, he could see the masts of the *Anna Maria* at the wharf half a mile downriver.

Thomas came up at eight. He closed the door behind him. He did not light the lamp. He took off his coat. He sat on his bed — the same bed he had slept in since October.

Olav sat on his own bed.

They sat for some time without speaking.

After a while Thomas said:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"You will, in the year ahead, not always be at a ship. Sometimes you will be at a widow's house. Sometimes at a different sort of house. Sometimes you will not, for a week or a month, know what house you are at. When you are at one of the houses that is not the widow's, remember: the drawer you are building is the drawer you will open at the house you have not yet found. The drawer holds what it holds. The opening of it, when it comes, is not a thing you have to prepare for. It will prepare itself."

"Yes."

"That is all."

He lay down.

Olav lay down.

The room was quiet. Very faintly, from the town, came the sound of a single fiddle — the same kind of Christmas Eve fiddle Olav had heard from the lee of the deckhouse of the *Sigrid* the night before he and Thomas had gone over the rail. He thought about it for a moment. He let the thought come in and then go back out.

He slept.

---

At dawn on Christmas Day the widow was at the kitchen. The stove was going. The three lodgers came down. The widow had made another small piece of rømmegrøt and had put out the last of the ham and the bread.

After breakfast she said:

"Olav. Come to the parlor."

He went to the parlor.

She sat him in the chair by the window.

"I am going to tell you a thing Engelstad has not told you. He will not tell you. He does not, I believe, know how to tell you. I am telling you because a woman who has been doing this work since 1851 knows when a thing needs telling."

"Yes."

"Engelstad has not, since you left in October, accepted a berth that was offered him. There have been three. One a Savannah coaster, one a timber-bark for Quebec, one a Bergen schooner home. He has refused all three. He has given me three different reasons, none of which are the reason. The reason — in my judgment, which is not a judgment I share lightly — is that he has decided that the year he went to sea for, the year I have been teaching him the name for, has been a year he does not, in the end, believe in. He is at my house because my house is a place where the not-believing is allowed. He is not at my house because my house is on the way to another house he is going to. He is at my house because my house is the last house."

She looked at him.

"I am telling you this because you will, in the coming months, think about whether he has a berth, whether he has eaten, whether he is safe. Do not. He is being looked after. I am looking after him. I will look after him until he is no longer lookable-after. You are not to carry him at your ship. You are to carry him, when you carry him, in whatever way your interior has already decided to carry him. That is the carrying I cannot do from here. That is yours. The other carrying — the food, the roof, the watch at the consulate — is mine."

"Yes, ma'am."

"Good."

She stood. She put one hand on his shoulder. The hand was, Olav registered, the second hand to be on his shoulder in eighteen hours. The widow's hand was a different hand from Thomas's hand. The widow's hand was the hand of a woman who had been placing hands on shoulders for twenty-five years, and who had learned, across those years, what a hand on a shoulder could do when it was the right hand at the right second. The hand was warm through the wool of his sweater.

She took her hand off.

"Go to Engelstad. Say goodbye."

He went to Thomas.

Thomas was in the back room at the bench. He had a small leather-backed book open on the bench in front of him. He closed it when Olav came in.

"Olav."

"Thomas."

"She has told you."

"Yes."

"Good."

"Thomas."

"Yes."

"If I do not see you again."

Thomas looked at him.

"We may or may not. That is between the winter and the berth. Do not promise anything today you cannot keep. I will not. You will not. That is how we will leave it."

"Yes."

Thomas stood. He put one hand briefly on the back of Olav's neck — the Norwegian place — and did not say *go.* He said, instead:

"Olav."

"Yes."

"The three shapes. Remember the three shapes."

"Yes."

"Good."

He took his hand off.

Olav went downstairs. He said goodbye to the widow at the door. She did not embrace him. She gave him a piece of bread to take to the ship. She said:

"Back at noon. Today and tomorrow."

"Yes, ma'am."

He went out.

---

The *Anna Maria* lifted on the morning tide of the twenty-eighth.

Olav Vaageland, age eighteen, stood at the mainmast pinrail and watched the Cape Fear River fall away behind him. He did not look back toward the small lane with the white door at the second house. He had been back for Christmas dinner and for the dinner the day after, as the widow had asked. He had said his goodbyes. He had said them completely.

He looked forward at the channel.

On the second night out of Wilmington, on a long reach under the stars, in the small gray half-hour of the middle watch, he thought once of the hand on the shoulder at the cypress root. He thought of it without choosing to. He did not name what the thinking was. He put it back in the drawer and went on with the watch.

The drawer, that winter, was the size it had always been.
