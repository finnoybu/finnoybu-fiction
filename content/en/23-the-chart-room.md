By the second week of November Olav had settled into the rhythm of Mrs. Kindingstad's house, which was the rhythm of supper.

Supper was at half past five. The table was set at a quarter past. The four lodgers — the two gymnasium boys at the foot, Lønningen at the side under the window, Olav across from Lønningen with his back to the kitchen door — took their places when Mrs. Kindingstad set the bread down. Mr. Kindingstad, in his chair by the front window with the Stavanger newspaper, did not come to the table; his supper was carried in to him on a tray when the lodgers had finished. The arrangement was not, as far as Olav could tell, a matter of rule. It was a matter of long agreement between a husband and a wife who had decided, after thirty-one years, what they could and could not bear of each other at certain hours of the day.

The two gymnasium boys — Knut, fifteen, and Anton, seventeen — were brothers from a farm at Egersund whose father had paid for their schooling out of a herring-boat he had bought a quarter share of in '74. They were polite the way boys raised by Norwegian farmers are polite, which is to say they did not speak unless spoken to and they did not eat the last of any dish without asking. Knut was the smaller and the quieter of the two. Anton, who was nearly grown, had a habit of looking at Olav at the table the way younger boys look at older men they hope, in some particular not-yet-named way, to become.

Olav, who had not until that autumn been older than anyone in any room he had been in for any length of time, found that he was being older now whether he meant to or not. He answered Anton's questions about the school. He answered Anton's questions about the sea. He did not, when Anton asked him whether he had ever been afraid on a yard at night, lie to the boy; he said *yes,* and he said *every man who has been on a yard at night has been afraid at least once,* and he said *the difference is what a man does with the fear.* Anton wrote that down, after supper, on a piece of slate beside his bed; Olav saw it the next morning, the slate left out by accident, and had the brief unfair sensation that he had been quoted at himself.

Lønningen, the clerk, did not speak much. He held his fork at the precise position it had been in when he started chewing his bite, until the bite was swallowed; then he raised the fork the small distance to his mouth for the next bite, with no movement of the wrist that did not need to happen. Olav had come, after the first fortnight, to like Lønningen for the precision. He had learned at sea to like men who did not move their hands more than they needed to.

Mrs. Kindingstad, at the head of the table, asked one question of each lodger by turn during the meal. She asked the gymnasium boys about their lessons; she asked Lønningen about the day at his firm; she asked Olav about the school. The questions were short. The answers were expected to be short. The point of the exchange, Olav understood by the third week, was not the information. The point was that a household which spoke at supper for a quarter of an hour had, over time, the texture of a household; a household which did not had, over time, the texture of a boarding-house whose lodgers did not yet quite live there. Mrs. Kindingstad ran a household. The questions were the rent.

---

Olava's letter that week came on the Wednesday afternoon mail.

It was longer than her September letters had been. He had noticed, since the first week of October, that her letters had begun to lengthen — at first by a sentence or two, then by a paragraph, and now, in November, by a full half-page beyond the form. The lengthening was a thing she did not announce. It was a thing he noticed by the weight of the envelope before he opened it, and by the count of his own breaths that the reading took.

He took the letter to his room at the back of the house. He sat at the desk with the small iron stove a foot from his knee and read.

> *Dear Olav,*
>
> *The northwest has held nine days. Father is at the boats with the new garboard; the cold is hard on his right hand at the wrist where the wood meets the leather. Mother has begun the late kale and is asking me to ask you to ask Mrs. Kindingstad whether her late kale this year has done what ours has done, which is to say has gone soft at the center when it ought to have stayed firm. I do not know, Olav, why this is a question my mother considers urgent enough to send you in a letter. I am sending it because she has asked me to send it. You may answer or you may not. The kale will not, either way, change.*
>
> *Marit has decided that she will not after all marry the schoolmaster's son in the spring. She has told no one but me. The schoolmaster's son does not yet know. The schoolmaster does not yet know. I am not telling you what to do with this information; I am telling you because I told myself, the day you left, that I would not have a piece of news in my house that I did not pass to you. The piece of news will, in time, become not-news. Until it does it is news, and I am sending it.*
>
> *Asbjørn writes from Bergen that the* President Daae *has finished her petroleum run and is in for a refit at the Solheimsviken yard. He saw her from the wharf and says she is bigger than he had thought a Norwegian bark could be. He is, I think, considering signing on her for next spring. I do not know what this means for the family at Lindøy. Father has not, yet, said anything about it. Mother has, in the way she will not directly, asked the kitchen cat what it thinks.*
>
> *I read your fourth letter twice. The first time was for the form. The second time was for the line about Hamre. I am noting the line. I will, when you next mention him, note the next line.*
>
> *Yours,*
>
> *Olava*

He read the letter twice. Then he read the second-to-last paragraph a third time.

He had not, he understood with a small flat clarity that took him by surprise, been aware until that third reading that he had mentioned Hamre to her in the previous Wednesday's letter. He had not, that Wednesday, set out to mention Hamre. He had been writing about the school week. The mention had come in among the other lines.

He had told Olava — without yet naming what he was telling — that there was a man in his class whose stillness he was watching.

She had read the line. She had noted the line. She had, in writing back, told him that she had noted the line, and that the next mention of the man would also be noted.

He folded the letter. He set it on the desk. He sat with his hands on his knees for a long minute, looking at the small iron stove. The stove was burning down. The room was warm. He understood, sitting at the desk in the warm room with Olava's letter folded on it, that the woman he was going to marry was going to read every letter he wrote her for the rest of his life with the attention she had read the Hamre line with, and that he was not going to be able, in any letter, to mention any man in any way that did not also tell her something he had not yet decided whether to tell her.

He got up. He set a small piece of birch in the stove. He sat down again.

He took out paper. He wrote the Sunday letter early.

In the Sunday letter he did not mention Hamre.

In the Sunday letter he wrote about the kale.

The school in the second half of the autumn term moved into dead-reckoning.

Prof. Nilson taught dead-reckoning the way he taught everything — by drawing two lines of chalk on the blackboard and naming what was in the angle between them, and then by giving the class a problem to solve and walking among the desks with his spectacles on the black ribbon around his neck and his hands behind his back, looking at slates and not, ordinarily, speaking. He spoke when a student got it wrong; he spoke when a student got it right by accident. He did not speak when a student got it right. The not-speaking was, the students learned by the third week, the highest praise the man gave.

Olav got the first dead-reckoning problem right by knowing the answer before he had finished the work. He had done dead-reckoning by hand for three years on three different ships under three different captains; the answer was in his hands the way the brig's set of the swell had been in Haakon's. He wrote the work cleanly and put down the pen and sat with his slate on his desk waiting for Prof. Nilson to come past. Prof. Nilson came past. Prof. Nilson looked at the slate. Prof. Nilson did not speak. Prof. Nilson moved on.

Olav was, for the first time since he had walked into the school, certain that he belonged in it.

He understood, also — as the term went on and the problems went deeper and the men in the room around him began to separate into the men who had a navigator's hand and the men who did not — that the school was not, for him, going to teach him much that he did not already know. The school was going to give him names for what his hands had been doing since he was eighteen. The names were what the certificate was made of, and the certificate was what the marriage would be paid for with. He was not at the school to learn navigation. He was at the school to be paid for navigation he had already learned.

This was a thing he did not, at twenty-one, allow himself to say out loud to anyone, because to say it would have been to make himself the kind of man who, in a Stavanger boarding-house, told the gymnasium boys at supper that the school the gymnasium boys were paying to attend was not, perhaps, the school it claimed to be. He did not say it. He held it in his head where the not-saying did not cost him.

---

The chart room was at the back of the school, behind the chart cabinet that ran the length of the south wall.

He had been in it twice in October on errands for Prof. Nilson and once more on his own, fetching a chart of the approaches to Hamburg through the Elbe. By the second week of November he was in the chart room three afternoons a week, on the chart-work the school assigned the better students to do unsupervised because they could be trusted with the charts.

The chart room had a long table in the middle and the chart cabinet on the wall and a single window that looked out at the courtyard where the students kept their bicycles. There was a kerosene lamp on a hook above the table; on the dark afternoons of November he lit it before he started work, and the light fell yellow on the white of the chart and the brown of his hand on the dividers.

His hand had begun to do things the school had not assigned it.

He had noticed it the first week. He had been working an exercise on the southwestern approaches to the English Channel, and his hand, while the part of him that was working the exercise was at the chronometer page, had drawn — with the side of the thumbnail, in a line that did not quite leave a mark but left enough to be read by a man who had drawn it — the course down the Norwegian coast and across the Bay of Biscay and into the approaches at Lisbon. He had noticed the line afterward. He had stared at it. He had wiped the chart clean with the heel of his hand. He had gone back to the assigned exercise.

The next afternoon he had done it again. A different chart. A different course. The course of the *Allegro* north out of Stavanger to Vardøy, drawn by his thumbnail along the inside of his other hand's path with the dividers.

The third afternoon — a chart of the Carolina coast, for an exercise on the use of the lead in shallow approaches — his hand had drawn the line into Wilmington that the *Sigrid* had come in on, around the bar and up the river past the place where the two American men-of-war had lain at anchor, all the way to the lower wharf where the brig had loaded her cotton and where, two months later, he had gone over the lee rail at midnight with his sea-chest at his shoulder.

He had stared at the line.

He had wiped it.

He had, that afternoon, walked back to Mrs. Kindingstad's by a longer street than he usually walked.

He understood, by the third week, that the hand was doing what the rest of him was not yet ready to do, which was to take inventory. The hand was making a chart of the man it had become. The chart had three years of voyages on it. The chart was being drawn whether or not he watched the drawing.

He let the hand draw. He did not, after the third week, wipe the lines with the heel of his hand. He let the lines stay. He left the chart room when the bell rang at five and he walked back to Bredalmendingen in the cold and he sat at the desk in the warm room with the small iron stove and he opened his book to the next chapter, and he did not, in the boarding-house room, allow himself to think about the lines he had left on the chart-room charts.

He thought, instead, about Olava's late kale.

---

Hamre was at the desk two seats over and one row back, as he had been since the first day of October.

Olav had not, in the eight weeks since the term began, spoken to him about anything except, twice, the small courtesies of a chart-room shared. He had not approached him. Hamre had not approached Olav. The silence was, by November, the kind of silence a man stops noticing the way a man stops noticing a beam above his head he has learned not to walk into.

On the Thursday of the eleventh week, after the afternoon session, Hamre was the last man at his desk when the rest of the class went out. Olav, who had stayed back to copy a figure he had not finished copying during the lecture, was the second-to-last. He was at his own desk by the window. Hamre was at his desk in the third row. They were perhaps eight feet apart. Neither of them, when the door closed behind the second-to-last student to leave, had moved to leave.

Hamre did not look up from his slate. After perhaps a minute he said, without looking at Olav:

"Hestby."

"Hamre."

"You did the haversine without referring to your tables."

"I did."

"How."

"I have done it on a brig under a captain who would have hit me with a marlinspike if I had stopped to refer to the tables."

Hamre looked up then.

It was the first time Olav had seen Hamre's face do anything that was not the carefully-still nothing it did at lessons. It did not do much, even then. The eyes met Olav's; the mouth moved a small half-inch at the corner, not into a smile but into the shape a man's mouth makes when he has just understood a thing he had not understood a moment before. Then the eyes went back to the slate. The mouth went back to nothing.

"Thank you," Hamre said.

"For what."

"For telling me you have done it. I have not done it. I had been thinking that the men who do it without the tables were a different kind of men than I am, and that if I kept at it long enough I would become one of them. You have just told me that the difference is the marlinspike. I am, after this, going to do it without the tables until I cannot remember what the tables looked like. Thank you."

"You are welcome."

Hamre said nothing more. He did not look up again. Olav finished copying the figure he had stayed to copy, and he packed his books, and he left the chart room. At the door he glanced back, once. Hamre was at his desk with his slate in front of him and his eyes on the slate, doing the haversine without the tables.

Olav walked back to Bredalmendingen in the cold November dusk.

He thought, on the walk, that he had done a small thing for Hamre. He thought, also, that he had not, in the small thing, told Hamre anything about himself he had not told anyone else; he had said only that a captain on a brig had threatened to hit him with a marlinspike, which was a fact a sailor could give another sailor without giving up anything. The smallness of what he had given was the thing, he understood as he turned into Bredalmendingen, that had made the giving possible. He had given Hamre the marlinspike. He had not given Hamre any of the rest.

He thought, after a moment: *Olava will note this when I write it to her.*

He thought: *I am going to write it to her anyway.*

---

That night at Mrs. Kindingstad's he wrote the Wednesday letter.

He wrote about the kale (Mrs. Kindingstad's kale was, she had told him, doing what Olava's mother's was doing). He wrote about Marit and the schoolmaster's son, in the careful indirect way a man writes about another man's daughter to that man's daughter's brother's intended. He wrote three lines about a lecture Prof. Nilson had given on the use of the lead. He wrote, after these, two sentences about a classmate he had begun, slowly, to know — *a man named Hamre, from the Hardangerfjord, who has been managing alone a thing he does not need to be managing alone any longer.*

He sealed the letter. He addressed it. He set it on the desk under the kerosene lamp.

The room was warm. The stove was burning down. The brig of his eighteenth year was a thing he had walked through that afternoon at the chart-room table without wiping. The boy in the third row was a thing he had spoken to. The woman who would, in two years, be his wife was reading every letter he wrote her with attention enough to note the line about a man he had not yet finished thinking about.

He understood, sitting at the desk, that this was the year of his life in which the inventories were being made.

He understood, also, that the inventories were going to take longer than the year.

He blew out the lamp. He went to bed.

The bell of the domkirke rang ten as he closed his eyes, and he was asleep before the eleventh stroke.
