title: "Hogganvik"

---

Olav had the midwatch.

He came up out of the forecastle into a cold that surprised him even though he had been warned. They had crossed south of the Skagerrak two days before; by the reckoning at noon they were off the Dutch coast, running before a wind that had been freshening since the afternoon, and the sea, when he climbed onto the quarterdeck and looked over the rail, had the low black shine of something that was about to weather.

It was Haakon's watch too. Olav had not known this when he signed on. He knew it now because Haakon was at the wheel.

"Mr. Hestby."

"Haakon."

"Boatswain, to you. Until four bells."

"Boatswain."

Haakon's face in the binnacle-light was half-gold and half-dark. He was not smiling; he had the ship-face he wore on deck, which was not the face Olav had seen leaning at the rail with coffee in his hand. On watch he was another man. Olav had learned this in the first week — that a man at sea could be two men between one bell and the next, and no one thought anything of it. It was the nature of the work.

"Go forward," Haakon said. "Check the lashings on the boat. The mate wants them tightened. Be quick."

Olav went forward. He checked the lashings. They were fine; the mate had told Haakon to have the new man tighten them so that the new man would learn to tighten them, not because they needed tightening. Olav tightened them anyway. He came back aft.

"Done."

"Good. Stand by the wheel. I will teach you to steer."

Olav stood by the wheel. Haakon put Olav's hands on the spokes beside his own.

"Keep her there," Haakon said. "South-southwest. You feel where the wind is?"

"Yes."

"Let her ride. Do not chase her. If she wants to come up, let her come up a little, and bring her back with your hand, not with your shoulder. Your shoulder is for later."

Olav let her ride. The wheel was warm where Haakon's hands had been on it. This was an accident of the physical world that Olav thought, in his eighteenth year, might have been put there to kill him. He did not move his hands. He kept them where Haakon's had been.

The brig rose and fell. She did not chase; she did not wander. She rode.

Haakon stepped back and let him steer alone. Olav steered for a quarter of an hour, then half an hour, then an hour. The wind freshened. He felt it in his hands before he heard it in the rigging. He bore off a little to ease her; the brig went easy under him; he brought her back.

"Good," Haakon said, from the rail behind him. "You have the feel."

"I had it from a boat at home."

"That is where most men get it."

They did not speak again for some time.

---

At three bells the squall came. It came the way squalls come in the North Sea — first a darker line of cloud to the southwest, then a smell of wet on the wind, then a drop in temperature you could feel against your teeth. Haakon called the watch and they took in the royals, two men to the yard, one on the bunt, quick work with hands that had done it a thousand times. Olav was on the starboard yardarm. Mathis the galley boy, he noticed, was on the larboard. They worked the sail in in three minutes flat. Olav's hands, going through the motion, were becoming hands he recognized.

The squall hit. It hit hard for perhaps ten minutes — wind that staggered the brig on her course, rain that came sideways, a quality of noise in the rigging that was new to Olav — and then, as squalls will, it was past. The wind fell. The rain thinned to a mist. The brig came back to her steady ride.

Olav, on deck, was soaked through. The rain had gone straight through his wool shirt and was now a cold layer between his skin and the air. He had not noticed, while he was working, how cold he was. He noticed it now. His teeth started on him.

Haakon, passing him, looked at his face.

"Forward," he said. "Lee of the galley. I'll come."

Olav went. The lee of the galley, where the wind broke around the deckhouse and there was a pocket of stillness between the galley door and the foremast, was the warmest place on the deck at that hour. It was not warm. It was only less cold than the rest.

Haakon came a moment later. He had a blanket — one of the brown wool crew blankets — and a mug. He handed the mug to Olav.

"Drink."

Olav drank. It was coffee and rum, hot. The rum hit his stomach before the coffee did. His teeth stopped.

Haakon sat down on the deck with his back against the galley wall and gestured to the space beside him. Olav sat. The blanket went over both of their knees.

"Shoulder to mine," Haakon said, as if explaining a piece of rigging. "Your wool is wet. Mine is not. We will share the dry of me until the watch is out."

Olav put his shoulder to Haakon's. Through two layers of wool — one wet, one dry — and the space under the blanket where their knees were, he could feel the other man's body as a warm shape beside him. He understood, almost at once, that he was not going to sleep that night even after the watch ended.

He decided, almost at once, that this was a thing Haakon had done before — this sharing of warmth in a lee, this practical use of one body against another — and that Haakon did not think anything of it. He had to decide it. There was no other way to sit beside Haakon under the blanket without going under, and he was not, tonight, going under. He held to the decision the way he had held to the rope at the wheel, with both hands and his whole attention. He did not let go.

It was what you did when a man's wool was wet. It was what Kristoffersen would have done, or Solvaag, or Tolvsen. It was seamanship.

Olav made himself believe it. He made himself breathe slowly.

"You are from Hogganvik," he said.

"Yes."

"I have never been there."

"You are not missing much. It is a farm. Fifty head of sheep. A cliff that drops into a fjord. A house that my mother painted red twenty years ago and has not painted since."

"Brothers?"

"Two."

"Older?"

"Younger. That is why I am on this brig and not on the cliff."

Olav thought about this. He turned it over: the youngest brother goes to sea. On Finnøy he was his father's only son, and he went because his father had gone, and because the sea had left his father with a story he did not tell. The reasons were different. The result was the same; men went.

"Did you want to be a sailor," Olav said.

"I wanted to be out of Hogganvik."

"And now?"

"Now I am a sailor."

He said it without irony. Olav understood, slowly, that this was a truth about Haakon. What a man wanted at sixteen did not govern what a man was at twenty-five. The life had its own gravity. One obeyed it.

"I am glad you are," Olav said, and only realized after he had said it that he had said something he had not meant to say.

Haakon did not respond for a moment. Olav felt the other man's shoulder rise and fall, once, through the two layers of wool.

"Why," Haakon said.

Olav did not know what to say. His mouth moved. Nothing sensible came to it. The rum was very warm in his stomach and the night was very cold on his face and Haakon's shoulder was very steady against his, and somewhere on the edge of sleep he thought he was going to say a thing that would be impossible to take back.

"Because you told me when to come down," he said.

Haakon's mouth moved in the binnacle-light glow that came through the galley window. It was, again, not quite a smile.

"Good reason."

"Yes."

"That is seamanship."

"Yes."

"That is all it is."

"Yes."

Haakon looked at him, briefly, and Olav was looking at him too, and for the length of one breath, perhaps two, they did not look away. In the half-light Haakon's eyes were not the pale noon-blue of that first afternoon at the foretop; they were a darker thing, the color of a sea an hour before morning. Olav could see the small scars at the bridge of his nose; a whitened line at his temple; the way his jaw was set under a three-day beard that had come in reddish, almost orange, at the chin.

Then Haakon looked away. He looked at the sea. He did not take his shoulder from Olav's.

"Drink the rest of your coffee," he said. "We have an hour still."

Olav drank. He drank slowly. He made the mug last.

Toward the end of the hour, when the rim of the cup had gone cold against his thumb and the bell had not yet rung, he felt, under the blanket, Haakon's hand find his.

It was not a fumble. It was the deliberate movement of a man who had known exactly where the other hand was. The fingers closed. The bare thumb — the one without the nail — pressed against the back of Olav's hand, where the bone ran from wrist to knuckle. The hold was firm. It was the hold of a man who had decided, for the length of three breaths, to do a thing he did not, in the rest of his life, do.

Olav counted. One. Two. Three.

At the third breath the bell rang. Haakon let go. He stood. He went forward to relieve the man at the wheel.

---

They did not speak again for the rest of the watch. Olav was sent to the main braces at six bells. At eight bells — four in the morning — the mate of the watch relieved them and they went below.

In the forecastle Olav took off his wet shirt and hung it on the foremast stringer. He climbed into his hammock. He did not sleep. He lay there and watched the grey of the morning come up through the forescuttle, and he heard Haakon three hammocks down stop moving — that particular stillness of a man going out — and knew, without being told, that Haakon was asleep.

He thought about the blanket, and Haakon's shoulder, and the small white scar at the temple, and the sentence *that is all it is.*

He thought: *I am eighteen, and I have no word for this, because no one has ever taught me one.*

He thought: *whatever it is, Haakon has a word. Haakon has put a word on it and put it in a drawer and shut the drawer, and the drawer is called seamanship.*

He thought: *I will learn to put my words in drawers too. That is what the men here have learned. That is how they live.*

He did not sleep.

At six bells of the morning watch the steward rang the bell for breakfast, and Olav climbed down from his hammock and went aft with the rest, and when Haakon passed him in the waist they did not speak, did not nod, did not acknowledge each other's presence at all — and Olav understood, precisely and without bitterness, that this was the answer to the question his mouth had almost asked.

The answer was a drawer.

Olav accepted the answer. He went to his breakfast. The bread was bad; he ate it. The coffee was excellent; he drank it. He did not look at Haakon again for the rest of the watch.

But he would know, for the rest of his life, the weight of another man's shoulder against his own, and the smell of wool that had stayed dry under a blanket in a squall at the edge of the North Sea, and the exact color of a sea an hour before morning, seen in a man's eye.

These things, too, he put in a drawer.

The drawer was his own.
