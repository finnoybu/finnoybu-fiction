# Finnoybu — Trilogy Plan of Record

Series brand name: **Finnoybu** (ties to Ken's author/publisher brand). Each book carries its own subtitle.

**Working author:** E. A. Westbo (pseud. Kenneth Tannenbaum)
**Source scaffolding:** memoir of Oliver Vullum Bjørnson Vestbø, *A Sailor's Reminiscences from the Days of the Sailships*
**Genre:** queer period historical romance (commercial romantasy tier — Outlander / Fourth Wing / Song of Achilles / Red, White & Royal Blue crossover)
**Narration:** dual-POV third-person limited, alternating chapters at emotional peaks
**Heat level:** Outlander-tier explicit at peak beats; Song-of-Achilles literary restraint between
**Orientation:** Oliver is multisexual — loves his wife Olivia genuinely AND loves men genuinely

---

## Commercial positioning

- TikTok/BookTok compatible (#romantasy, #historicalromance, #queerromance)
- Book of the Month Club / Reese's Book Club strike zone
- Crossover appeal: literary-fiction buyers (Song of Achilles readership) + commercial romance readers
- Period setting is a **marketing moat** — most romantasy is secondary-world; authentic Norwegian maritime 1880s–1900s is genuinely underserved
- Multi-book series = reader LTV (backlist sales once Book II lands)

## Shift from memoir timeline

**Memoir's Oliver:** first voyage at 15 (cook on *Nanna*). Meets Olivia at 16. Dies/survives TB around 17. Navigation school 18–19. East India voyage 20–21. Marriage at 22 (Jan 1882).

**Novel's Oliver:** opens at 18, on his second voyage — first deep-sea. Has one short coastal training voyage in backstory (referenced, not shown). Meets Olivia at 19 during summer ashore. Navigation school 20. East India voyage 21–22. Marriage at 22–23.

**Rationale:** Ken's explicit direction — protagonist must be 18+ throughout for adult romance content. Compressing the teen memoir years into backstory preserves the arc without shifting the source geography/chronology meaningfully.

## Hestby church — a motif to weave

**Hestby is a real church on Finnøy.** Oliver's surname is the name of the stone where his people were baptized, married, and buried. Every time his surname is spoken aboard ship, it is calling a piece of an island he is not on. This is the keystone metaphor — work it in at natural points, do not over-use it:

- **Book I, Act I departure:** one brief glimpse of the church as the brig clears the roads — a white shape on Finnøy's hills, visible for maybe twenty minutes before it drops below the horizon. Oliver does not name it for the reader; the reader will catch it later.
- **Book I, Act II homecoming:** when Oliver walks up from the Roalsøy landing with Olivia, they pass Hestby churchyard. Silent weight of lineage. His grandparents' stones.
- **Book II:** in Rangoon or Copenhagen, when Oliver is farthest from home, someone mispronounces his name. The correction is its own small grief. *(Possible scene.)*
- **Book III wedding:** Oliver and Olivia are married AT Hestby. The name closes around the life. This is where the homeward arc lands.

Use the church as a motif of **rootedness that he cannot outrun and cannot fully inhabit**. Do not belabor it. One sentence where a paragraph would be tempting.

## Book I — *Finnoybu: Salt and Silence*

**Arc:** Ages 18–20. First major voyage → desertion → sickness → healing → home → meets Olivia → navigation school → departure for East India. Ends on gut-punch: Oliver leaves Olivia (and a man from the first voyage who is out there somewhere) to ship for two years.

**Target length:** 25 chapters, ~100–110k words

### Three-act structure

#### Act I — The First Voyage (Chapters 1–8)
The opening voyage is **expanded with invented detail** per Ken's note ("he didn't cover much detail, we can fill it in"). Full male-world immersion; the queer awakening happens here.

- **Ch. 1 — "Cast Off"** — Stavanger, summer 1878. Oliver, 18, boards the schoonerbrig. Crew boarding: mates, boatswain, carpenter, fellow hands. First notice of ***H——*** (the boatswain from Hogganvik; named in the novel, unnamed in memoir). Departure, opsang, tears on dock, the promise of adventure.
- **Ch. 2 — "The Captain's Smile"** — First days at sea. Captain appears warm. Food turns bad. Captain's true nature begins to emerge.
- **Ch. 3 — "Hogganvik"** — First close scene between Oliver and H——. Shared watch, cold night, shared blanket. Nothing happens. Everything happens.
- **Ch. 4 — "Lisbon"** — Discharge of coal. Drunken captain abandons Oliver and Nils ashore. Oliver watches the captain beat Nils unconscious from the rigging. First trauma bond. Captain attempts to kill Oliver with a salt-shovel.
- **Ch. 5 — "The Rotten Mast"** — Atlantic crossing. Oliver ordered aloft to fasten flag; discovers mast is rotten. Near-death. H—— is the one waiting at the deck with coffee when he comes down shaking.
- **Ch. 6 — "The Yard"** — Captain sends Oliver aloft in bad weather as punishment. 125-foot fall waiting. H—— covers for him on deck. After, they do not speak of it.
- **Ch. 7 — "Wilmington"** — Arrival. Oliver is sent aloft daily with no stool, tarred rigging. Defiance. Decides to desert. Confides only in Thomas (a friend from a different Finnøy family). H—— is not part of the plan — Oliver cannot reveal himself that far.
- **Ch. 8 — "The Boat at Midnight"** — Thomas and Oliver slip away. Boarding house. The next morning, Oliver looks back at the ship still in the harbor and wonders which hammock H—— slept in, and whether he noticed Oliver gone. (End Act I.)

#### Act II — Exile and Healing (Chapters 9–15)

- **Ch. 9 — "A Cough"** — Oliver, somehow, makes his way home. Tuberculosis suspicion. His older brother is already sick. Dr. Due is kind and evasive.
- **Ch. 10 — "Vardøy"** — Desperate voyage north on the *Allegro*. Northernmost Norway. Grey and endless light. Sickness deepens.
- **Ch. 11 — "The White Ship"** — The *Diana*, Captain Sars. Kindly older man who sees Oliver's illness and cares for him without demand. Oliver's first experience of being truly seen, by an older man, without cruelty. (Not sexual — parental-erotic, but close.)
- **Ch. 12 — "Cured"** — The cough stops. Oliver never knows when. He returns to Bergen whole.
- **Ch. 13 — "Landa"** — Summer on Finnøy. Reunion with his family. Restless.
- **Ch. 14 — "The Tag Game"** — He meets Olivia. Age 19. She hands him the tag repeatedly, runs and grabs his arm. *Hit by the arrows of Amor.* She is luminous. She knows he is a sailor. Her brother died of TB; she nursed him. She understands.
- **Ch. 15 — "Roalsøy"** — He visits her island. Her family. Her cooking. Silent understanding. They are never alone but they are always together. He has not told her about H——; he would not know how. (End Act II.)

#### Act III — The Sea Again (Chapters 16–25)

- **Ch. 16 — "The School"** — Navigation school, Stavanger. 120 students. Prof. Nilson. Olivia visits often. Distraction.
- **Ch. 17 — "The Bed in Her Parents' House"** — Staying at Roalsøy between terms. Olivia brings him breakfast in bed. Wordless intimacy.
- **Ch. 18 — "The Cough Returns"** — Exam time. His body betrays him. Despair.
- **Ch. 19 — "Hamre"** — Exam day. Navigation school classmate Hamre, struggling. Oliver helps him. Hamre thanks him heartily. They part and never see each other again. The scene codes the queer subtext: two young men, a kindness, an unspoken recognition, a farewell.
- **Ch. 20 — "Certificate"** — Oliver passes. Useless certificate — too sick, too poor to use it. Olivia understands without him saying.
- **Ch. 21 — "The Silent Engagement"** — Neither has said *I love you*. Neither says *I want to marry you*. They simply know. Her family knows. His family knows. Nothing is written down.
- **Ch. 22 — "Letter from Shields"** — A letter arrives at Finnøy. It is from H——. It has found him somehow. It is short. It says almost nothing. But he reads it seven times before burning it.
- **Ch. 23 — "The *Semiramis* is Hiring"** — East India voyage. Two years at sea. Pay is decent. He would have to leave Olivia. He would be gone so long she might marry another. But he cannot stay here sick and penniless. He must heal at sea.
- **Ch. 24 — "The Goodbye"** — The parting with Olivia. No words. Her hand on his face. The steamship to Stavanger. His family on the dock.
- **Ch. 25 — "Sails Set"** — The *Semiramis* makes way out of Stavanger harbor. Oliver at the rail. Norwegian mountains receding. Two years. He thinks of Olivia. He thinks of H——. He does not know, at this moment, which one haunts him more.

**Gut-punch ending:** Oliver sails not toward one love but away from two. The reader turns the last page knowing Book II will reunite him with one of them — and must choose. The dual-love architecture is set.

## Book II — *Finnoybu: [Working subtitle: The Monsoon Letter]*

**Arc:** Ages 20–21. East India voyage on the *Semiramis*. The Swedish man in Copenhagen. The passionate letter that forces a choice.

**Central inversion:** In the memoir, a *Swedish woman* writes Oliver a passionate love letter offering unconditional devotion; he refuses her by ghosting her. In the novel, this is a **Swedish man** — a ship's mate or merchant's son — met briefly in Copenhagen during the homeward leg, who sees Oliver unguarded once and recognizes him. His letter arrives on the same day as Olivia's. Oliver chooses duty; he buries the letter, and the self it represents, unread after the first reading.

**Key beats drawn from memoir:**
- Rangoon, the Shwedagon Pagoda, the river swim (near-drowning)
- Wilhelm Jacobson and Sven — crewmates. Sven's bunk-fight with Oliver becomes a charged intimacy.
- Teaching navigation to other sailors on the homeward leg — Oliver as mentor
- The apes caught by sugar in coconuts — metaphor for trapped desire
- Captain Hamre (*Semiramis*) — proud, demanding; sees something in Oliver he cannot name
- Arrival in Copenhagen on return — the meeting with the Swedish man
- The two letters arriving together after marriage

**Target length:** 25 chapters, ~100–110k words

## Book III — *Finnoybu: [Working subtitle: The Captain's Burden]*

**Arc:** Ages 21–22. First mate on the *President Daae*. New York, Brooklyn, petroleum runs. Steward who attacks him and becomes devoted. Marriage to Olivia. The convergence.

**Key beats drawn from memoir:**
- Brooklyn dives and gangsters (Peder Gjesdal)
- Lost on Manhattan Island in the Bowery — near-murder
- Captain Thoresen's transformation from cruel to kind
- The steward-fight that ends in the steward's devotion (the masochistic inversion of Book I's captain-violence)
- Wedding at Finnøy, January 22, 1882 (keep the date)
- Captain's drunkenness at the homecoming dinner — Oliver's outburst ("you tried to murder men") — his discharge
- The Swedish man resurfaces? Or is dead? Or Oliver learns he died at sea — the queer self extinguished by circumstance, not by Oliver's choice
- Ending: Oliver settled at Finnøy with Olivia. Children possibly. The sea behind him. But something in him still looks at the horizon. **Bittersweet — not triumphal.**

**Target length:** 25 chapters, ~100–110k words

## Voice (for Claude authoring)

Oliver's narrative voice:
- Short, declarative sentences with occasional lyric longer ones
- Sensory precision — smell, weight, temperature, sound
- Wry self-deprecation (inherited from memoir) used as armor against feeling
- Never flowery. Never self-pitying. Never explicit in naming emotion.
- **Shows desire through attention to detail** — the line of a man's jaw lit by lamplight, the callus on a palm, the way fabric shifts when a shirt is pulled off, the temperature of breath
- When the emotion is largest, the sentence is shortest

H——'s voice (dual POV):
- Slightly older (25 to Oliver's 18 in Book I)
- More at home in his body
- Still not fluent in what he is
- Warmer syntax, more humor, but guarded
- Has done this before. Oliver has not.

## Hard craft rules for Claude

1. **No modern idiom.** "Going to" not "gonna." "Cannot" not "can't" in narration (dialogue can be looser). No 21st-century emotional vocabulary ("boundaries," "processing," "vibes," "toxic").
2. **Period-appropriate sensuality.** Bodies, skin, breath, heat, want — yes. Anatomical medical vocabulary — no. When explicit, use muscular Anglo-Saxon diction, not clinical and not purple.
3. **Show queer awakening through the body's noticing, not through interior monologue about orientation.** Oliver cannot label himself; the reader can.
4. **Olivia is not a foil. She is a full character.** She is the love of his life. The book's tragedy is not that he does not love her enough — it is that he loves her AND someone else, in an era with no room for both.
5. **Every chapter earns its place in the three-act structure.** No filler. No scene without a purpose.
