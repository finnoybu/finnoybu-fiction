# Voice Target — Per-POV Sample Paragraphs

> **Purpose:** Concrete prose-texture referents for every POV register in the trilogy. Before drafting any chapter, re-read the samples for that character. During drafting, if a paragraph sounds "off," compare against the samples here to locate the drift.
> **Authority:** Drafted by Claude 2026-04-21; Olav section revised 2026-04-22 after cross-instance register review (Claude.ai, ChatGPT) argued convincingly that the Book I register is Scandinavian-plain, not Miller-lyric. Updated per that redefinition.
> **Status:** These are *targets*, not *proofs.* They demonstrate what each register should sound like in this project. Whether Claude can hit them sustainably across 30+ chapters per book is a separate empirical question the chapter-gate will answer.
> **Olav register note (important):** Olav's register **evolves across the three books** — see [voice-register.md](voice-register.md). Book I is austere saga-plain (Hamsun, Knausgaard's *A Time for Everything*, the sagas in plain translation). Book III closes by approaching Miller-adjacent interior lyric, earned by the arc. Sample 1 and Sample 2 below are Book I baseline. Sample 3 is Book III end-of-arc and is INTENTIONALLY lusher than Sample 1/2; do not use Sample 3 as a reference when drafting Book I chapters — Ch 1–3 drafts in `content/en/` are the primary Book I reference.

## Tic-pathology vocabulary to avoid in ALL registers

The prior 267K-word draft developed specific rhythmic tics. The restart's value depends on these not recurring:

- **"Small particular X" compounds** — "the small particular weight," "a small particular attention." Adjective-pairs of this shape are banned.
- **"He did not, in X-ing, Y" constructions** — subordinate gerund wedged before main clause as a breathing-tic. Banned.
- **"X was the X" tautology** — "The sea was the sea," "the weight was the weight." Banned.
- **Over-dense em-dash nesting** — more than one em-dash pair per paragraph is a flag. More than two per paragraph is a stop.
- **Ritual cadence** — any rhythm that becomes detectable as a rhythm across consecutive paragraphs. Variety is the discipline.
- **"Without X-ing, Y" subordinates stacked** — one per paragraph is fine; two is drift; three is pathology.

All samples below should pass a grep for the banned bigrams/trigrams and should not visibly repeat rhythmic shapes.

# Olav Hestby — Scandinavian-plain register (Book I baseline), evolving toward Miller-adjacent interior by Book III

**Core discipline (Book I baseline):** Close third-person. **Refuses to name interiority** — emotion lives in objects, gestures, and what the body does without telling the reader why. Plain past tense. Norwegian-translation cadence (the "and X. And Y." construction, paratactic chains). Sparing use of the retrospective-voice accent ("whose name Olav never learned"). No lyric metaphor, no abstract formulations of feeling, no extended similes. The reader earns the emotion the way Olav earns his place on the ship.

## Sample 1 — Olav at 19, *Asta* (memoir *Faina*), first morning out of Stavanger, spring 1876 — Book I baseline

The boatswain was from Hogganvik. His name was Haakon. He came up the rigging behind Olav and had an ease on the ratlines that Olav did not yet have. A year before either of them had signed the ship's articles, Haakon had laid her planks himself in a boatshed at the head of the fjord. Olav had been told this when he came aboard. He had not known what to do with the information at the time and had put it away.

On the ratlines now he found himself watching the man's hands on the rope. The hands were brown from the work, and the knuckles were marked where the skin had split in the winter and healed over the split. The rope moved through them and they moved on the rope, and neither seemed to need telling. Olav turned his eyes to the sea. The sea was what a man was supposed to look at from a shroud in fair weather off Shields.

## Sample 2 — Olav at 20, St. Vincent, Cape Verde, receiving Olava's "yes" letter, April 1877 — Book I baseline

The letter reached him in a harbor that smelled of coal and seabirds. He read it once, sitting on a watercask in the cookhouse shade, and then he folded it along the crease she had made, and put it against the skin of his chest under his shirt, and went back to work. He did not read it again for the rest of the day. Olava had said yes. He did not know how to carry the news. He carried it anyway. When the watch changed and the cook came to find him for the evening meal, he had been lashing and unlashing the same halyard for the better part of an hour. The cook looked at him without speaking and went away. Below deck, eating his soup, Olav was a man with a fiancée, and did not yet know what else he was.

## Sample 3 — Olav at 24, Boston, the oil-painted portrait of Olava delivered aboard the *Lykke*, spring 1881 — **Book III end-of-arc, NOT a Book I reference**

*This sample is INTENTIONALLY lusher than Sample 1 and Sample 2. It represents the register Olav's prose reaches by Book III, after six years of sea and interior growth — not the register of Ch 1. Use Sample 1 and Sample 2 (plus the Ch 1–3 drafts) for Book I work.*

The painter had kept his word. The portrait stood at the door of the cabin in a frame carved out of some pale wood Olav did not recognize, oiled and smelling of turpentine, and the face inside the frame was Olava's and was also not quite Olava's. It was what a man who had never seen her had made of a photograph. She was looking away from the viewer and slightly down. Her mouth was set. The painter had caught the small line at the corner of it that came out when she was holding back a smile she did not want to give the room. Olav sat down on the edge of his bunk with the painting on his knees and held it there for a long time, looking at the mouth. He thought of the afternoon at Landa, four summers back, and of the walk home through the ravine, and of the promise he had made in his own language without any word of it having to be said. Then he set the painting carefully against the cabin wall where he would see it first on waking, and he went out to take his watch.

# Olava Lindøy — Tóibín register

**Core discipline:** Precise, domestic, ledger-keeping mind. Careful observation. Quiet psychological depth. The interior register of a literate rural Norwegian woman tracking her household, her correspondence, her marriage's architecture. Book II voice is contained-waiting. Book III voice is settled-having. Do not let them blur.

## Sample 1 — Olava at 19, Roalsøy, reading Olav's "Verdensposten" letter from Rangoon, Book II (autumn 1880)

She had taken the letter up to her room to read it without her mother or her brother watching her read it. It was a very large letter. He had written it on a single sheet of rice-paper that was thirty inches wide by thirty-six, in columns, and had folded it down into an envelope of the usual size, and the folds had left faint cross-lines on the page so that she had to smooth it on the table before her with the flat of both hands before she could read what he had written. He told her about the pagoda and about the elephants at the sawmill. He told her that he had gone swimming in a river that was warm as bathwater. He told her that he was well. He had underlined *well* once. He did not tell her what month of the voyage this was, or when he expected to be home, or anything that she could use in the keeping of the calendar in her ledger. She sat with the letter for some time, and then she refolded it along the man-made creases, and she went downstairs to help her mother with the supper.

## Sample 2 — Olava at 22, the morning of the wedding, Roalsøy, January 22, 1882

The day came clear. She had been awake since before the light. She had stood in the kitchen while it was still dark and drunk a cup of coffee her mother had made her, and then she had gone up to dress. There was a small stain on the hem of her dress from the wetting she had taken at the landing yesterday. The stain would not show. Her father had coughed once in his sleep, and she had listened for the second cough through the floor, and the second cough had not come. She thought: today is not a day for counting coughs. Today is a day for standing up in a church with a man I have been engaged to marry for five years and seven months and some days I have not counted. She went to the window and looked at the crossing. The boat was there. Olav would have been up for an hour by now. She had a very clear picture of him looking out at the same strait from his own window, and she was surprised to find that the picture did not bring any of the old grief with it, but only a sort of brightness, as though a door had been opened in the upstairs of the house and the weather had come in.

# Haakon Berg — Proulx register

**Core discipline:** Spare, working-class masculinity, physical-labor cadence. Shorter sentences than Miller. Concrete nouns. Less interior monologue, more what-he-sees-and-does. Maritime/rural register. Reserved about feeling; feelings carried in gesture and landscape. Haakon is solitary discipline — distinct from Sven's family-at-a-distance discipline.

## Sample 1 — Haakon at 25, on the *Asta*, the first evening Olav took second dog-watch at the wheel, spring 1876 (Book I joyful)

The boy from Finnøy took the wheel at four bells and stood as if he knew how. He did not know how. He was watching the compass too hard. The boat was wanting a little to starboard and the boy had not felt it yet. Haakon went over from the lee-rail and put his hand flat across the top of the wheel, above the boy's two hands, and turned it a spoke and held it there. He said nothing. The boy looked at him and nodded once. Haakon took his hand off and stepped back to the rail and lit his pipe. The smell of the pipe went over the half-deck and out to sea. He did not look at the boy again that watch. He looked at the water, which was grey, and at the rim of it, which was darker grey, and at the pipe-smoke, which was lifting off to leeward. It was a fair evening. It was the sort of evening a man could remember a long time afterward without having to say why.

## Sample 2 — Haakon at 38, a Bergen-Tyne coal-coaster at anchor in Shields, winter night, Book III (morose)

The mate had put out the oil-lamp and gone to his bunk. Haakon sat at the cabin table in the dark. His pipe had gone cold on the saucer. He had been reading the paper from Stavanger that had come three days back with the mail, and in it was the notice of a wedding at Lindøy in Roalsøy parish, names and witnesses listed, and he had put the paper down and not picked it up again. Outside, the coal-dust on the dock was freezing into a crust. A man coughed two ships down. Haakon had a daughter of his own now, he reminded himself; Margit. She would be six in May. He had a daughter and a boat and a berth at Bergen. The paper was not news. It was a date he had known about for some time. But in the dark cabin, at two o'clock in the morning, in Shields, with the mate asleep aft and the cold coming through the boards, the date had a weight he had not expected. He put the saucer and the pipe into the locker, and he went on deck for a while. The lights of the town were as they had been. He went back in and to his bunk. He did not sleep.

# Jens Hestby — Robinson register

**Core discipline:** Meditative older-man reflection. Elegiac, quiet, letter-like contemplation. Possibly framed as a not-sent letter to the absent son. Lutheran undercurrent without sermon. The thing-he-learned-that-he-cannot-say-directly. Home-front counterpart to the sea-voyage narration.

## Sample — Jens at 59, Vestbø, a winter evening with Olav gone on the *Hjemkomst*, Book I (late 1876)

I walked out to the cattle-shed after supper, and Peder went with me, carrying the lantern. The frost had made a crust on the yard and the crust broke underfoot at every step, and neither of us spoke. I remember thinking, as we came to the shed door, that a man can tell a great deal about his sons by the way they carry a lantern beside him in the dark. Peder carries it in front and a little to the left, so that the light falls where his feet will be put next. Olav, when he was home last summer before he signed with Tollefson, carried the lantern in front and a little to the right, because he is handed differently from his brother, and I do not think he knew that his way meant I was walking in my own shadow the whole distance. I did not mind. It is a father's place to walk in his son's shadow for a while. The boys they send out to sea are not the men they send home — the Lord sends them home as someone else, and I have come to think we must not grieve too hard for the first of them when the second arrives, but I will confess that I grieve all the same. I came back to the house cold. I sat with his chair at the table a minute before I sat in mine.

# Sven Haugen — Proulx register (distinct from Haakon)

**Core discipline:** Same register-family as Haakon — spare, working-class, physical cadence — but the interior discipline reads different because Sven has more to protect. Wife Hanne. Son Jonas, three. A small house in Sandviken with a stove that wants blacking. His restraint is a married man's restraint: not the monk's solitary refusal but the householder's awareness of what is waiting at the other end of the voyage.

## Sample — Sven at 31, a lodging-house room at Calcutta, late night, Book II (summer 1878)

The boy from the Stavanger ship had brought him up the stairs and paid for the room and put a bottle down on the little table by the wash-basin and had not sat down. He had been smiling. He was still smiling, but differently now, in the way of a man who has understood that his guest is not going to take the chair he has pulled out. Sven stood by the window and looked at the bottle and did not look at the boy. He thought of Hanne in the kitchen at Sandviken, wiping Jonas's face with a corner of her apron. He thought of that apron. It had been blue, with a stain near the pocket that would not come out. He had been away seven months. The boy said something in English that Sven did not catch, or did not choose to catch, and he said no, thanking him, and put a small coin on the table next to the bottle and went back out into the corridor. On the stairs he did not hurry. It was not a thing to hurry from. At the door of his own lodging he found he had been holding his pipe in his hand the whole time without knowing it, and he put it in his mouth and lit it on the step before he went in.

# Captain Thoresen — Hemingway register

**Core discipline:** Terse authority. No lyric. No interior emotional vocabulary. Short declarative sentences. Action and observation over reflection. Older captain register — professional, hard-won, austere. The withheld thing is not withheld by a technique of prose; it is simply not said because he is not a man who says it. One short aside chapter in Book III, "The Photograph." ~800–1500 words target.

## Sample — Thoresen at 60, alone in his cabin on the *Nordlys* at night, New York harbor, October 1881

He took the photograph out of the tin box and put it on the table. It was small. The tin was scratched. The photograph had been in the tin for two years. He looked at it in the lamplight. She was wearing the dark dress. She had not liked the dress when it was made. She had worn it for the photograph because the photographer had said dark. She was holding a book. She had not been reading it. The photograph had been taken in Bergen in the autumn of '79. It was the only one she had let be made. He was sixty years old. She was fifty-eight. They had been married thirty-three years in December. He did not turn the photograph over to read what was written on the back. He had read it once and remembered it. He put the photograph back in the tin, and the tin in the drawer, and the drawer into the desk, and he turned up the lamp and took the ship's book and began the day's entry. *Wind SSW fresh. Anchored off pier 14. All hands signed off except steward. Weather holding.* He wrote one more line. Then he closed the book and put out the lamp.

# Discipline notes for drafting

1. **Opening sentence test.** The first sentence of any chapter must land in the intended register. If you can't hear whose voice it is from that sentence alone, re-write. Olav opens lyrically; Olava opens with a domestic datum; Haakon opens with a concrete action or object; Jens opens with a past-tense recollection; Sven opens with a person or place name; Thoresen opens with a short declarative fact.
2. **Read aloud.** Every POV-chapter draft gets a read-aloud pass before being shown. If the ear says "default narrator," you have drifted.
3. **Tic grep.** Before any draft is shown to Ken, run the automated tic-check grep ([tic-check.md](tic-check.md) — to be built) over the draft. No matches on banned bigrams.
4. **One-chapter-one-register.** Do not mix registers within a chapter. A Haakon chapter is Haakon. An Olav chapter is Olav. The one permitted move is brief-quoted-dialogue by another character, which is in their speech not their interior.
5. **The "carry" rule for Proulx characters.** Both Haakon and Sven move in a world of objects and gestures. If a Haakon or Sven paragraph has more than two clauses of interior reflection, cut one of them. Whatever was interior gets converted to a gesture or a thing.
6. **Miller's restraint on intimacy.** For Olav, physical attention is noticed but not named at the moment of first noticing. The naming comes chapters later, sometimes books later.
7. **Tóibín's calendar.** For Olava, the mind tracks time and household. Grief and joy both register through the ledger's concrete details (weather, coughing, letters arriving or not arriving, the line at the corner of a mouth).
8. **Robinson's elegy.** For Jens, the past tense and the letter-voice. A Jens passage often reads like it is being written to someone — even when it is not.
9. **Hemingway's withheld thing.** For Thoresen, the withheld thing is not gestured at. It simply is not there. The reader infers its weight from the surrounding actions.
