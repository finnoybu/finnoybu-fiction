# Voice Register — Per-POV Mapping

**Status:** Locked. Ken 2026-04-21 decisions. Multi-register voice design for the restarted trilogy with emotional-mirror structure.

## Core principle

Each POV character has a distinct, period-authentic voice. All registers stay within 19th-century-literary-English-compatible range — no contemporary idiom, no anachronistic emotional vocabulary, no modern-American banter. Reader should hear whose chapter it is from the first paragraph without needing the label.

**What this is NOT:** contemporary-voice-in-period-setting (Hamilton-style anachronism). Every POV stays period-authentic.

## Emotional mirror — the trilogy's structural spine

The two lovers trace opposite emotional trajectories:

- **Haakon:** joy in Book I (secretly loved, secretly loving) → grief in Book III (Olav married, gone to him, aging at sea)
- **Olava:** grief in Book II (waiting through Olav's 18-month absence) → joy in Book III (Olav returned, settled, hers)

Book III resolves by showing both simultaneously. The man who had Olav first ends up the man who had him longest in memory and least in life. The woman who waited ends up the woman who had him most in life and least in the interior drawer. Both loved him, neither fully had him.

This mirror is the trilogy's central thesis about queer-love-in-a-time-with-no-word-for-it rendered as shape. It is the architectural spine. Other structural choices must serve it.

## Character-to-register mapping

### Olav Hestby — core protagonist POV (all three books)

**Register (evolves across the trilogy as a structural enactment of Olav's arc into self-knowledge).**

- **Baseline (Book I):** Scandinavian plain-style, saga-filtered, restraint-as-argument, Norwegian-translation cadence. Close third-person that **refuses to name interiority** — emotion lives in objects and gestures, not in reported feeling. Plain past tense. Sparing use of the retrospective-voice accent ("whose name Olav never learned," "he would later") as the one permitted lyric concession.
- **Primary comps (Book I baseline):** Knut Hamsun (*Growth of the Soil*), the Icelandic sagas in plain translation, early Karl Ove Knausgaard (*A Time for Everything*), Kirsten Bakis. Cormac McCarthy's plainer passages are **adjacent** but NOT a direct model — his tautological-cosmic moves ("doing the thing the sea did") have already surfaced as drift-risk and are to be avoided.
- **Primary comp (trilogy end-point):** Madeline Miller (*Song of Achilles*) — as a **destination**, not as a baseline. The Book III closest-approach to Miller-interior is *earned* by Olav's arc; the Olav of Ch 1 cannot produce the sentences the Olav of the wedding chapter can.

**Evolution across the three books:**

- **Book I (ages 18–20):** register at its most austere. Olav has no language for what he feels and neither does the prose. Emotion lives in objects (the knife, the coin, the pipe, the letter under the shirt) and in gestures (the nod across the yard, the father's wet eyes, the coin placed back in the palm). The reader earns the emotion the way Olav earns his place on the ship.
- **Book II (ages 20–22):** gradual loosening. Tuberculosis scare, homecoming, navigation school, Olava at close range. Olav develops interior language he did not have. Sentences can get slightly longer; body-noticing can become slightly more explicit without tipping into lyric-lush. The retrospective-voice accent can appear a little more often. The register is still plainly plain, but the plainness has begun to carry more weight of recognized feeling.
- **Book III (ages 22–24):** closest approach to interior lyric — earned. By the *Zenomia* East India voyage Olav has partial language for himself (though not names for it in any public vocabulary). By the Anders Lindqvist cutoff in Helsingør he can think in terms the Ch 1 prose cannot have accessed. The wedding chapter can afford a single sentence of explicit emotional recognition that would have been unthinkable in Book I. Even here, though, the Scandinavian underlay holds — the ceiling is Miller-adjacent, not Miller.

**Design thesis.** A boy who has no words for what he is gets a prose that matches him; a man who has slowly learned the words gets a prose that can carry them. The register itself becomes part of the trilogy's argument about self-knowledge.

**Prior-draft note.** The 267K-word rejected draft aimed at Miller-lyric and hit tic-pathology instead. The restart tilted austere partly in reaction; the austere register is the correct one for Book I and was validated by cross-instance review 2026-04-22 (Ch 1 passed to an external Claude instance which made a better argument for the current register than we would have). The prior draft's Miller-target becomes the trilogy's eventual end-point rather than its baseline.

### Olava Lindøy — secondary POV (Book II morose; Book III joyful)
- **Register:** Colm Tóibín
- **Primary comp:** *Nora Webster*, *Brooklyn*
- **Characteristics:** Precise, domestic, documentary, measured. Ledger-keeping mind, careful observation, quiet psychological depth. Interior register of a literate rural Norwegian woman tracking her household, her correspondence, her marriage's architecture.
- **Book II voice (morose):** The not-knowing during voyage absence. Ledger-as-architecture. Forecast discipline. Contained, waiting, holding the form.
- **Book III voice (joyful):** Olav returned. Settled household. The arithmetic of what-she-has-always-known. Peaks at deathbed chapter.

### Haakon Berg — secondary POV (Book I joyful; Book III morose)
- **Register:** Annie Proulx
- **Primary comp:** *Brokeback Mountain* (novella), *The Shipping News*
- **Characteristics:** Spare, working-class masculinity, physical-labor cadence. Shorter sentences, concrete nouns, less interior monologue, more what-he-sees-and-does. Maritime/rural register. Reserved about feeling; feelings carried in gesture and landscape.
- **Book I voice (joyful):** Secret happiness during the Sigrid voyage. What he notices about Olav. The small gifts of touch he permits himself. The door closing from his own side at Shields.
- **Book III voice (morose):** Aging Bergen-Tyne coal-coaster captain. Olav married to Olava for decades. The never-again of it. Physical world keeps going; the interior has become a drawer.

### Jens Hestby (Olav's father) — secondary POV in Book I
- **Register:** Marilynne Robinson
- **Primary comp:** *Gilead*, *Home*
- **Characteristics:** Meditative older-man reflection. Possibly framed as ledger-entry or letter-never-sent format. Elegiac, quiet, looking-back register.
- **Purpose:** Home-front counterpart to the sea-voyage narration. Father thinking about his son at sea, and about his own 1857 Aase-walkoff year at Newcastle. Father-son emotional weight, intergenerational register, the thing-he-learned-that-he-cannot-tell-his-son-directly.
- **Book I role:** 1-2 chapters. One during Olav's absence (maybe during the cough return or the winter Olav is not yet home), one possibly at homecoming. Mirrors Olava's Book II role structurally — the waiter-at-home whose interior we enter.

### Sven Haugen — secondary POV in Book II
- **Register:** Annie Proulx (same family as Haakon, distinct character register)
- **Primary comp:** *Brokeback Mountain*, *The Shipping News*
- **Characteristics:** Working-class Bergen sailor. Proulx register, but distinguished from Haakon by specifics — Sven has a wife (Hanne) and a young son (Jonas, ~3 years old in 1881) in Sandviken. The discipline of what he will not let happen reads differently because he has more to protect. Haakon's Proulx is solitary; Sven's is domestic-at-a-distance.
- **Purpose:** At-sea counterpart to Olava's home-front vigil in Book II. The Calcutta lodging-house moment from inside — the not-quite-doing-it-but-knowing-what-would-have-happened. Echoes Haakon's Book I register but with different life context.
- **Book II role:** 1-2 chapters. One during or after the Calcutta lodging-house moment.

### Captain Thoresen — one short aside chapter in Book III
- **Register:** Ernest Hemingway
- **Primary comp:** *In Our Time*, *Men Without Women*
- **Characteristics:** Terse authority, no lyric, no interior emotional vocabulary. Older captain register — professional, hard-won, austere. Short declarative sentences. Action and observation over reflection.
- **Format:** Short aside, not a full POV arc. ~800-1500 words. Does not advance the Brooklyn plot. Character-pivotal illumination only.
- **Proposed scene — "The Photograph":** Thoresen alone in his cabin at night on the *President Daae* looking at his wife's small framed portrait from Bergen '79. The particulars of her pose. The arithmetic of their 33-year marriage. A private thing he's never told her and never will. Ends without resolution. Reader gets Thoresen in a way Olav never will.
- **Why this works:** Thematically resonant with the trilogy's spine. Marriage-as-form, carrying-what-you-cannot-say, drawer-as-architecture. Thoresen at 60 is what Olav-at-60 might become if he chose the long professional life. An unintended mirror of Olav's possible future.

## Removed from POV (voices reader never hears)

### Anders Lindqvist
The Swedish clerk from Copenhagen. Seen only through Olav's close third. His architectural weight is that he was a stranger Olav encountered briefly and didn't follow through with. Entering his interior would collapse the ambiguity of what might-have-been. He's at his most powerful when we only know what Olav knows about him — one short letter, one evening, one address memorized and burned.

### Thomas Engelstad
The deserter-partner from Wilmington who disappeared March 1877. Architecturally significant — "the fourth name" in Olav's drawer. His disappearance is more powerful if we never hear him as subject. He exists only as Olav's memory of him. The reader cannot follow him out of Mrs. Hovland's lodging-house on the Friday morning of the third week of March. That is the point.

## Book-by-book POV distribution

### Book I (ages 18-22)
- Olav (main)
- Haakon (joyful) — 1-2 chapters
- Jens Hestby — 1-2 chapters
- Total: 2-4 non-Olav chapters

### Book II (ages 22-24)
- Olav (main)
- Olava (morose) — 1-2 chapters
- Sven Haugen — 1-2 chapters
- Total: 2-4 non-Olav chapters

### Book III (ages 25+)
- Olav (main)
- Haakon (morose) — 1-2 chapters
- Olava (joyful → deathbed) — 2-3 chapters
- Thoresen aside ("The Photograph") — 1 short chapter
- Total: 4-6 non-Olav chapters

**Trilogy total:** ~8-14 non-Olav chapters across ~95 chapters. Roughly 9-15% of the book. Disciplined POV architecture, mirror structure preserved.

## Verification rules

Before drafting any POV chapter:
1. Re-read the 2-3 sample paragraphs for that register (to be added when voice-target.md is built)
2. Check: does the opening sentence land in the right register for the character?
3. After drafting: read the chapter aloud. Does it sound like the intended voice, or has it drifted toward default narrator?
4. For Haakon and Sven (both Proulx): verify their specific characterizations don't blur — Haakon is solitary discipline; Sven is family-at-a-distance discipline.
5. For Olava across books: Book II voice is contained-waiting; Book III voice is settled-having. Don't let them blur.
