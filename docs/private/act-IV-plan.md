# Act IV plan — Ch 26-35

> **Scope:** August 1876 → November 1877. Ch 26 opens the *Dronningen* voyage at the North Sea passage out of Stavanger; Act IV closes at the *Dronningen*'s return to Wilmington in November 1877, with Olav at the tar-mat job that becomes Act V's desertion-trigger.
> **Status:** locked 2026-04-26 after Act III close (Ch 25) and act-level review. Plan-of-record for the 18-month *Dronningen* voyage that occupies the middle of Book I.
> **Authority:** supplements [trilogy-outline.md](trilogy-outline.md) Book I Act IV. Does not supersede.
> **Position in Book I:** Acts I-III drafted (Ch 0-25, ~63.9K words / ~55% of 115K target). Act IV ahead = ~30-32K words; Act V then = ~12-15K words. Book I closes on *Cito* leaving Wilmington Christmas 1877.

## Names — already locked

- **The *Dronningen*** ("the Queen") — bark, oak from Bremerhaven, Australian-passenger-trade-built. Capt Tollefson; bound Stavanger → Archangel and onward.
- **Captain Tollefson** — first appearance Ch 25. The anti-Gjermund. Plays organ in cabin (memoir-real); sings hymns; *Den signede dag* at every departure (Bertel anchored as habit Ch 25).
- **Bertel** — carpenter, from Tau, ~25, married four months when *Dronningen* sailed (wife Hanne — verify in names-index — "took parting hard" per memoir-digest). **Olav's closest friend on this ship.** Already on stage Ch 25. Memoir-real.
- **Pallesen** — 2nd mate, ~30, good-natured, conscious of dignity, ingratiating to captain. Memoir-real. First appearance pending Act IV opening.
- **The 1st mate** (unnamed in fiction) — middle-aged, heavy brown full beard, mostly agreeable but capricious and hasty. Memoir gives no name; trilogy may keep unnamed or invent. Later strikes Thomas in face so blood flows (memoir-sourced).
- **Theodor Halversen** — 17-18, "wild but hale fellow, well-met." Son of Capt Halversen of *Harald Haarfagre* (Pensacola pitch-pine trade — already memoir-flagged as renamed *Kong Olaf* per [names-index.md](names-index.md)). Reappears later as nav-school classmate Book II.
- **Boatswain** (unnamed) — 45+, stooped, thin, wrinkled, jolly except when called out at night; big eater; **excellent opsang-starter** (already on stage Ch 25 leading *Jeg vil meg Herren love*).
- **Thomas** (memoir name kept) — from Stjernarøy/Finnøy, did farm work at Finnøy before this voyage. "Wild chap." **Co-deserter with Olav at Wilmington** (Act V opening trigger).
- **Old Man James** — Bristol shore-figure, distributes tracts and books, takes sailors to the Good Templars' Hall (temperance society). Memoir-real Ch 17.

- **Haakon-POV (Ch 32):** Proulx register continued from Ch 14. Heat level Outlander-tier at the moment itself; Song-of-Achilles restraint around it. Voice register at upper-Book-I / lower-Book-II tolerance. **The chapter that proves the trilogy can render M/M physical activity at the Hamsun-mode register without lapsing into reportage or melodrama.** This is the trilogy's hardest single chapter and may need multiple revision passes.

  **Strategic note (Claude.ai post-plan review):** Ch 32 cannot do what Ch 14 did at the same level. Ch 14's discipline was withholding; Ch 32's central event is the *release* of withholding. The chapter has to find a different discipline. Three candidate strategies: **(a) brevity** — the encounter taking less chapter-space than the surrounding registration; **(b) aftermath-emphasis** — the chapter being mostly Haakon after, with the encounter rendered briefly in the middle; **(c) external interruption-bounding** — something outside (a sound from the street, a third person's footstep, the coming of dawn) bounding the encounter at the chapter level so both men feel the bounding rather than the trilogy stating it. **Recommend (b) at draft** — Haakon's after-registration is closer to the trilogy's grammar than Olav's during-registration would be, and the aftermath gives the bounding-as-felt that the bounded design requires. Author judgment at draft.
- **Olava-POV (Ch 33):** Tóibín register continued from Ch 21. The household at Lindøy in summer 1877; the proposal letter arrives; the asking-and-deciding. Match Ch 21's discipline of interior-named-without-feeling-named.

  **Length discipline (Claude.ai post-plan review):** Ch 33 should be **shorter than Ch 21**. Ch 21 carried three movements (ravine-walk + gate-arrangement + kitchen close). Ch 33 has three parallel movements (proposal arriving + deciding + writing) and should let those three breathe rather than packing more in. The yes-letter going is the chapter's natural close; do not extend into the wait-for-reply (that beat may want a separate Olava-POV later, or may stay implicit). ### Drafting-phase rules added 2026-04-26 (per ChatGPT Act IV plan reviews)

**Rule 1 — General pattern firewall.** After drafting each chapter, scan for any sentence structure used 3+ times within 200-300 words. **Alter 1 instance only.** This is the firewall against pattern-lock at Act IV's Olav-heavy length and length-extended ship-environment register. Apply before submitting the chapter to cross-instance review.

**Rule 2 — "Registered" micro-rule.** The cognitive verb "registered" / "registering" was at saturation by Act III close. **If "registered" appears twice within 200 words, force one substitution.** Substitutions: "saw," "knew," "noticed," "took in," or — preferred — restructure the clause to remove the cognitive-verb framing entirely (the trilogy's grammar of object-and-action carries the registration without naming the registering).

**Rule 3 — Olav-voice expansion guardrail.** Olav-yes saturation requires Acts IV-V to let Olav speak more than yes. **But:** keep his speech functional, context-driven, never reflective. The danger of overcorrecting is unnatural dialogue length — Olav saying multi-sentence interior-thoughts to Bertel or Tollefson would break the trilogy's grammar harder than the yes-saturation does. Allowed expansion sites: writing letters (length without breaking POV-restraint), responding to direct technical questions (work-talk in full sentences), addressing Tollefson when called to the quarterdeck (deferential but completing his thought). **Not allowed:** Olav speaking about feeling, about Karsten, about Olava's letters, or about himself in reflection. The discipline is: he can speak about *what he is doing* and *what is happening* but not about *what he is*.

### High-risk chapters (chapter-specific watchpoints)

- **Ch 26** — setup exposition risk. Watch for explanatory clauses, repeated intro structures.
- **Ch 28** — *highest* repetition risk. Ship-action sequences are a syntactic-repetition engine. Vary sentence construction aggressively within register.
- **Ch 30** — storm-sequence repetition. Storm chapters have a known register-flattening failure mode. Vary both sentence-shape and pacing.
- **Ch 31** — letter-writing as a built-in opportunity for variation. Use it.
- **Ch 32** — *different* risk. Not pattern-lock; over-writing / out-of-register. Watch for register-drift at the encounter beat itself. Single revision pass minimum.
- **Ch 35** — long-duration fatigue + reflective compression. Risk of summarization and theme-naming. Hold the discipline of object-and-action.

### Letter-pattern repetition risk

Five letter beats in Act IV (Ch 26 first letter posted, Ch 29 first letter received, Ch 31 proposal letter, Ch 33 acceptance letter written, Ch 34 acceptance letter received). **Each must read structurally different from the others.** Ch 26 is procedural-Archangel; Ch 29 is reading-while-at-port; Ch 31 is writing-as-action with Bertel-parallel; Ch 33 is writing-as-decision in Olava-POV; Ch 34 is reading-with-immediate-consequence (the jib-guy fall follows). Differentiation by both *what the letter does* and *what the chapter renders around the reading or writing*. Avoid the structural shape: arrival → reading → restrained-reaction → continuation. By the third instance the reader anticipates that pattern.

### Bristol pair distinction (Ch 31 vs Ch 32)

- **Ch 31** — forward-directed: writing the proposal, sending it, the city as the place from which the letter goes. Olav-POV. Action-mode prose: writing, posting, walking with Old Man James, the Good Templars.
- **Ch 32** — present-tense embodied experience. Haakon-POV. The encounter happens in the present of the chapter; the registration is during-and-after rather than retrospective.

If both chapters read as reflective/interior-toned, the pairing collapses. The distinction is the act's central craft challenge.

## Act-V bridge note

Act IV closes at the *Dronningen*'s second arrival at Wilmington (November 1877). **Act V opens with the tar-mat punishment** that becomes the desertion-trigger. The Wilmington return in Ch 35's close should establish the second Wilmington as a place that is recognizably the same Wilmington from Ch 29 but charged differently — Olav has been engaged-and-letter-acknowledged for months, has had the Bristol encounter behind him, has carried the parcel through the voyage. The same port reads differently at the second visit because the man arriving is not the man who left.

## What this act is, in one sentence

Act IV is the act of the long voyage where Olav is formed by what he carries — the proposal-and-acceptance correspondence with Olava, the bounded encounter with Haakon at Bristol, the not-thinking-about-Karsten discipline at sea, the parcel in the chest, the mother's restored memory — and the trilogy's voice-register loosens at the rate that lets Olav by Act V's homecoming be a man who can speak, write, and be silent at the levels Book II will require.
