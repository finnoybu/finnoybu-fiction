## Memoir source material (for Ch 4-8 span)

Memoir content for this stretch is **thin**. Most of Ch 4-8 is invention built on memoir scaffolding.

- **Memoir Ch 1 (First Lessons):** the whole 1875 *Nanna* voyage in two paragraphs — already adapted as new-prose Ch 1-3.
- **Memoir Ch 2 (Coming Home):** opens with a single sentence on the winter at home: *"In the autumn, I spent the winter with my folks. But no sooner did the spring, 1876, open than my longing for the sea awoke, - I must away."* Then moves to the sign-on. Provides:
  - Father-and-son travel to Stavanger together

  - Dinner at Captain G.K.'s home — captain, captain's wife (quiet, refined, contrast to captain's "seadog" appearance), Olav
  - Post-dinner ship inspection — captain "in the brightest of humor"
  - Articles signed at office of registry the following day
  - 11-man crew roster including the unnamed Hogganvik boatswain (= Haakon Berg fictional)
  - Departure "with the customary chorusing (opsang)"

## Old-prose conceptual harvest

From old prose Ch 1-3 (at `D:\Users\Finnoybu\Desktop\Finnoyby Trilogy OLD\`) — **keep concept, do not emulate style.**

**Concepts worth carrying:**

1. **Letter arriving at the farmhouse** as inciting event for the decision. Old-prose Ch 1 uses an April letter from "Tolvsen" announcing the *Sigrid* offer. New-prose Ch 5 or 6 can use a similar letter-from-Stavanger device — sized down, via a figure who functions as Jens's old-shipmate contact.
2. **Captain's smile as mask / rigging** motif. Not in memoir literally, but earned from memoir's later G.K. cruelty. Gjermund's first meeting at Ch 6 dinner and his eight-bells quarterdeck appearance at Ch 8 departure can use a deliberate-friendliness texture.
3. **Captain's refined wife at the sign-on dinner** — memoir-sourced. The only clean hand in the sign-on; she then vanishes. Ch 6 beat.
4. **Age-arithmetic in household speech** — e.g. "he will be nineteen on the Asta / he will come home twenty." Concrete parental thinking-mode for Ch 4 or Ch 5.
5. **Hesby graveyard stones** as pre-departure Sunday. Old prose Ch 1 structured this around great-grandfather Gunnar Hestby's stone (fictional: went to sea at 15 in 1775). Conceptually useful for Ch 7 "Two Weeks."

**Style pitfalls — DO NOT emulate (old-prose has these heavily; new-prose must not):**

- Narrator theme-statements as opening lines. Old-prose Ch 2 opens: *"The brig lay at the quay like a promise already half broken, and Olav Hestby was eighteen years old and still young enough to believe a ship could keep a man's word for him."* That kind of thesis-sentence announces the chapter's meaning before the story starts. New-prose openings are object-and-weather, not epigrammatic thesis.
- Authorial interpretation naming body-noticing beats explicitly. Old-prose Ch 2: *"he had not known, until this morning, that a man's thumb could leave a shape on him. / He stopped thinking it because he did not know what to do with the thought."* Tells the reader exactly what the body-noticing means. New-prose Ch 2 Rørvik is correctly restrained: *"he had turned away when he became aware, and it had become one of the ordinary facts of the ship."*
- Repetitive ritual dialogue as tic — *"Jens." / "Yes." / "Jens." / "Yes."* Once is a technique; five times is a mannerism.
- Italic internal monologue broken into short lines — *"I am going. / I am gone."*
- Epigrammatic scene-payoffs — *"the smile is a piece of rigging; the eyes are the wind."*
- Queer-awakening beats written loud and fast. Old prose had Haakon touching Olav's wrist twice on day one (foretop + hammock at night); new prose places the first seed at four months in (Ch 2 Rørvik-scar), quietly, as an "ordinary fact of the ship." Pacing is deliberate.
- Over-specified parentheticals — *"in the small arithmetic of a kitchen in a Finnøy farmhouse in the spring of 1876."*

## Divergence from old prose, locked

Caretaking work Peder does was mother's work in old prose.
